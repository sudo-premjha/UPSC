# 🇮🇳 Chapter 19: Vice-President
### *Indian Polity by M. Laxmikanth — Simplified, Enhanced & Court Cases Explained*

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways You Must Know:**
> 1. The Vice-President holds the **second highest office** in India and is elected by an **electoral college of both Houses of Parliament** (elected + nominated members) — *unlike* the President's electoral college which also includes state legislators.
> 2. The Vice-President serves as the **ex-officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha** — this is their primary, day-to-day function.
> 3. The Vice-President can **act as President** when the President's office falls vacant, but only for a **maximum of 6 months**.
> 4. Removal of the Vice-President does **NOT require impeachment** — just a resolution by **Rajya Sabha majority + Lok Sabha agreement**.
> 5. Unlike the American Vice-President (who *succeeds* to presidency permanently), the Indian Vice-President only acts as a **temporary/acting President** until a new one is elected.

---

## PART 1: The Office — Rank and Model

- The Vice-President holds the **second highest constitutional office** in India.
- In the **official warrant of precedence** *(official ranking of dignitaries at state events)*, the Vice-President ranks immediately after the President.
- This office is **modelled on the lines of the American Vice-President** — though there are important differences (explained at the end of this chapter).

---

## PART 2: Election of the Vice-President

### How is the Vice-President Elected?

Like the President, the Vice-President is **NOT directly elected by the people**. Instead, the method is **indirect election** through an **Electoral College** *(a special group of representatives who vote on behalf of the people)*.

### Who is in the Electoral College for the Vice-President?

The electoral college for the Vice-President consists of:
- **Members of both Houses of Parliament** (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha)
- This includes **both elected AND nominated members**

---

### How is This Different from the Presidential Electoral College?

This is a favourite exam comparison! The two electoral colleges differ in **two key ways**:

| Feature | Presidential Electoral College | Vice-Presidential Electoral College |
|---|---|---|
| **Members included** | Only **elected** members of Parliament | Both **elected AND nominated** members of Parliament |
| **State Legislatures** | ✅ Yes — elected members of **State Legislative Assemblies** are included | ❌ No — state legislators are **NOT** included |

---

### 🗣️ Why Are State Legislators Excluded? — Dr. Ambedkar Explains

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar explained this difference clearly in the **Constituent Assembly Debates, Volume VII, p. 1001**:

> *"The President is the head of the State and his/her power extends both to the Centre as well as to the states. Consequently, it is necessary that in his/her election, not only members of Parliament but also members of the state legislatures should have a voice.*
>
> *But when we come to the Vice-President, his/her normal functions are to preside over the Council of States (Rajya Sabha). It is only on a rare occasion, and that too for a temporary period, that he/she may be called upon to assume the duties of the President. That being so, it does not seem necessary that the members of the state legislatures should also be invited to take part in the election of the Vice-President."*

**In simple terms:** The President governs the entire country (Centre + States), so states get a say in electing them. The Vice-President mainly presides over Rajya Sabha, which is a *central* function, so state representation is not needed.

---

### What is the Method of Voting?

The **manner of election is the same** as the President's election:
- **Proportional Representation** *(voting system ensuring minority voices are represented)* by means of the **Single Transferable Vote** *(voters rank candidates by preference)*.
- Voting is by **secret ballot**.

> 📌 **Historical Note:** The original Constitution required the Vice-President to be elected at a **joint sitting of both Houses of Parliament**. This cumbersome procedure was **abolished by the 11th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1961**.

---

### Disputes Related to the Vice-Presidential Election — Role of the Supreme Court

All doubts and disputes about the Vice-President's election are handled **exclusively by the Supreme Court**, whose decision is **final**. Here are the key legal rules:

**Rule 1 — Incomplete Electoral College is Not a Ground for Challenge:**
- The election of a Vice-President **cannot be challenged** on the ground that the electoral college was incomplete (i.e., some seats in Parliament were vacant at the time of election).

**Rule 2 — Acts Done Before a Void Declaration Remain Valid:**
- If the Supreme Court later declares the election of a Vice-President as **void (invalid)**, all acts done by that person **before** the date of the Supreme Court's declaration **remain valid** and are NOT invalidated.
- This protects the continuity and legality of official actions taken during that period.

> 🏛️ **Court Case Explained:**
> **Why does this rule exist?** Imagine a Vice-President who acted as President for several months and signed important bills and orders. If their election is later found to be fraudulent, invalidating all those actions would create administrative chaos. This rule of "**de facto doctrine**" *(the doctrine that acts of a person holding office in fact, even if not in law, are valid)* ensures stability. The Supreme Court has applied this principle in various constitutional contexts across India. The specific application to the Vice-President is codified directly in **Article 71** of the Constitution (made applicable to Vice-Presidential elections by Article 71(3)).

## PART 4: Qualifications, Oath & Conditions

### Qualifications to Become Vice-President

A person must fulfil **all four** of the following conditions:

1. **Must be a citizen of India.**
2. **Must have completed 35 years of age.**
3. **Must be qualified for election as a member of the Rajya Sabha.**
4. **Must NOT hold any office of profit** under the Union Government, any state government, any local authority, or any other public authority.

> 🔑 **Exception to Condition 4 — Who is NOT deemed to hold an office of profit?**
> The following persons are specifically exempted and are thus *eligible* to stand as candidates:
> - Sitting **President** or **Vice-President** of India
> - **Governor** of any State
> - **Minister** (Union or State)

### Additional Requirements for Nomination:

- The nomination must be subscribed by **at least 20 electors as proposers** AND **20 electors as seconders**.
- Every candidate must make a **security deposit of ₹15,000** in the **Reserve Bank of India**.
*(Source: Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952, as amended in 1997)*

---

### Oath or Affirmation

Before entering office, the Vice-President must make and subscribe to an **oath or affirmation**. In the oath, the Vice-President swears:

1. To bear **true faith and allegiance** to the **Constitution of India**; and
2. To **faithfully discharge the duties** of the office.

> ⚙️ **Who administers the oath?**
> The oath is administered by the **President** or any person **appointed by the President** for this purpose.

---

### Conditions of Office

The Constitution lays down **two conditions** that the Vice-President must follow throughout their tenure:

1. **Must NOT be a member of either House of Parliament or any State Legislature.**
   - If an existing MP or MLA is elected as Vice-President, they are **deemed to have vacated** their legislative seat from the date they assume the Vice-Presidential office.

2. **Must NOT hold any other office of profit.**

## PART 5: Term, Removal & Vacancy

### Term of Office

- The Vice-President holds office for a term of **5 years** from the date of assuming office.
- The Vice-President can **hold office beyond 5 years** until the successor assumes charge.
- The Vice-President is **eligible for re-election** — there is **no limit on the number of terms**.

---

### How Can the Vice-President Be Removed?

> ⚠️ **Key Point:** Unlike the President (who faces **impeachment**), the Vice-President does **NOT** require a formal impeachment for removal. The process is simpler.

**Process of Removal:**

- A **resolution** must be **passed by a majority of all the then members of the Rajya Sabha** — this is called an **effective majority** *(majority of total membership, not just those present and voting)*.
- This resolution must then be **agreed to by the Lok Sabha** — by a **simple majority** *(more than half of those present and voting)*.

**Important Procedural Rules:**
- The resolution can be **introduced ONLY in the Rajya Sabha**, NOT in the Lok Sabha.
- **At least 14 days' advance notice** must be given before moving such a resolution.
- **No ground** for removal has been mentioned in the Constitution — meaning Parliament has wide discretion.

> 🏛️ **Why Only Rajya Sabha Can Introduce the Resolution?**
> This makes logical sense. The Vice-President is the **ex-officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha** and primarily serves that House. Therefore, if Rajya Sabha loses confidence in their own Chairman/Vice-President, it is natural that *they* initiate the removal process. The Lok Sabha's role is simply to agree — providing a check.

---

### Vacancy in Vice-President's Office

A vacancy can occur in **5 ways**:

1. **Expiry** of the 5-year tenure.
2. **Resignation** — addressed to the President.
3. **Removal** by the Parliament process described above.
4. **Death** of the Vice-President.
5. **Otherwise** — e.g., disqualification from office or when their election is declared void by the Supreme Court.

**Rules for Filling Vacancy:**

- If vacancy is caused by **expiry of term** → election must be held **before** the term expires.
- If vacancy is caused by **resignation, removal, death, or otherwise** → election must be held **as soon as possible** after the vacancy occurs.
- A **newly elected Vice-President** always serves a **full 5-year term** from the date of assuming charge.

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> In **2022**, Vice-President **M. Venkaiah Naidu's** term ended, and **Jagdeep Dhankhar** was elected as the 14th Vice-President of India on **August 6, 2022**. Notably, the Opposition fielded **Margaret Alva** as their candidate, making it a contested election after several consecutive uncontested ones. Dhankhar, a former Governor of West Bengal, secured **528 votes** against Alva's **182 votes**.

## PART 6: Powers and Functions

The Vice-President performs **two main functions**:

---

### Function 1: Ex-Officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha

*(Ex-officio = by virtue of holding the office)*

- The Vice-President acts as the **Chairman of the Rajya Sabha** by virtue of their office.
- Their powers and functions in this capacity are **similar to those of the Speaker of the Lok Sabha**.
- This mirrors the American system — the US Vice-President also acts as the **Chairman of the Senate** (the Upper House of the American legislature).

> 💡 **What does the Chairman of Rajya Sabha do?**
> - Presides over sessions of the Rajya Sabha
> - Maintains order and decorum in the House
> - Decides on admission of bills, putting questions to vote, etc.
> - Has a **casting vote** in case of a tie

---

### Function 2: Acting as President During Vacancy or Inability

**Situation A — When the President's office falls VACANT:**
- A vacancy can occur due to the President's **resignation, impeachment, death, or otherwise**.
- The Vice-President **acts as President** until a new President is elected.
- The Vice-President can act as President for a **maximum period of 6 months** — within which a new President must be elected.

> 📌 **Historical Examples:**
> - When President **Dr. Zakir Husain** died in office, Vice-President **V.V. Giri** acted as President.
> - When President **Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed** died in office, Vice-President **B.D. Jatti** acted as President.

**Situation B — When the sitting President is TEMPORARILY UNABLE to function:**
- Due to **absence, illness, or any other cause**, when the President cannot discharge functions.
- The Vice-President discharges the President's functions **until the President resumes office**.

> 📌 **Historical Example:**
> - Vice-President **Dr. S. Radhakrishnan** discharged the functions of the President in **June 1960**, when President **Dr. Rajendra Prasad** was on a 15-day tour to the **USSR**.
> - He did so again in **July 1961**, when Dr. Rajendra Prasad was very ill.

---

### What Happens to the Rajya Sabha Chairmanship During This Period?

- While acting as President or discharging the President's functions, the Vice-President does **NOT perform the duties of the Chairman of Rajya Sabha**.
- During this period, those duties are performed by the **Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha**.

---

## PART 7: Salary and Emoluments

> 💰 **Important:** The Constitution has **NOT fixed any separate salary** for the Vice-President *in the capacity of Vice-President*. Their salary is drawn in their capacity as the **ex-officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha**.

| Emolument | Details |
|---|---|
| **Salary (as Chairman, Rajya Sabha)** | **₹4 lakh per month** (increased from ₹1.25 lakh by the **Finance Act, 2018**, with effect from **January 1, 2016**) |
| **Pension (Retired Vice-President)** | **50% of salary per month** (increased from ₹20,000/month by the **Vice-President's Pension (Amendment) Act, 2008**) |
| **Other entitlements** | Daily allowance, free furnished residence, medical, travel, and other facilities |

> 📝 **Legislative Source:**
> - Salary change: The **Finance Act, 2018** amended the **Salaries and Allowances of Officers of Parliament Act, 1953**.
> - Pension change: The **Vice-President's Pension (Amendment) Act, 2008** amended the **Vice-President's Pension Act, 1997**.

**During the Period of Acting as President:**
- The Vice-President is **NOT entitled** to the salary/allowance of the Chairman of Rajya Sabha.
- Instead, they receive the **salary and allowances of the President**.

## PART 8: Indian Vice-President vs. American Vice-President

Though the Indian Vice-President is modelled on the American Vice-President, there is one **crucial difference**:

| Feature | American Vice-President | Indian Vice-President |
|---|---|---|
| **When Presidency falls vacant** | **Succeeds to the Presidency** — becomes President for the **full unexpired term** of the predecessor | **Does NOT succeed** — merely acts as **Acting President** until a new President is elected (max 6 months) |
| **Permanence of role** | Becomes the actual President | Remains temporary/acting |

### Why is the Indian Vice-President Called "His Superfluous Highness"?

- The Constitution has **not assigned any significant independent function** to the Vice-President in their capacity as Vice-President.
- Their only real ongoing function is presiding over the Rajya Sabha (as Chairman).
- Hence, some scholars sarcastically call the office **"His Superfluous Highness"** — implying it is largely ceremonial.
- However, the **purpose of creating this office** was to maintain the **political continuity of the Indian State** — ensuring there is always someone ready to step in if the President is unavailable.

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> The role of the Vice-President as Rajya Sabha Chairman came into sharp focus during **2022–2024** when **Vice-President Jagdeep Dhankhar** as Chairman frequently clashed with the Opposition over conduct of proceedings in the Rajya Sabha. In **December 2023**, the Opposition (INDIA bloc) moved a **notice of removal against Vice-President Dhankhar** — the first such move in Indian history — citing alleged partisan conduct as Rajya Sabha Chairman. However, the notice was rejected as it did not meet the procedural requirements. This episode highlighted the constitutional importance of the removal procedure and the Vice-President's role as a neutral presiding officer. `[Modern Example]`

## 🏛️ Court Cases & Legal Principles — Detailed Explanations

### Case/Principle 1: Election Disputes — Supreme Court as Sole Authority (Article 71)

**The Rule:**
All doubts and disputes in connection with the **election of the Vice-President** are to be inquired into and decided by the **Supreme Court of India**, and the Supreme Court's decision is **final and binding**.

**Why the Supreme Court?**
The Vice-President is a constitutional authority of the highest order. Disputes about their election cannot be left to ordinary courts or election tribunals. Only the apex court of the land has the jurisdiction, authority, and gravitas to resolve such matters.

**Practical Significance:**
This mirrors the provision for Presidential election disputes (also decided by the Supreme Court under Article 71). It ensures that the process of electing the second-highest constitutional authority has judicial oversight at the highest level.

---

### Case/Principle 2: Incomplete Electoral College — Not a Valid Ground for Challenge

**The Rule:**
The election of a Vice-President **cannot be challenged** merely because the electoral college was incomplete — i.e., because some seats in Parliament were vacant at the time of election.

**Why This Rule Exists:**
Parliamentary vacancies are a constant reality. Seats may be vacant due to deaths, resignations, disqualifications, or pending by-elections. If a Vice-Presidential election could be challenged simply because some seats were empty, it would create an impossibly high bar for holding valid elections.

**Legal Principle — "De Facto Doctrine" Applied:**
This rule reflects the constitutional preference for **stability and practicality** over technical perfection. The election is valid as long as the process was constitutionally followed, regardless of incidental vacancies.

**Example:**
Suppose the Rajya Sabha has 5 vacancies when the Vice-Presidential election is held. A losing candidate cannot go to the Supreme Court and say "the election is void because those 5 members didn't vote." The Supreme Court will not entertain this challenge.

---

### Case/Principle 3: Void Election — Acts Already Done Remain Valid

**The Rule:**
If the Supreme Court declares the election of a Vice-President as **void (invalid)**, all **acts done by that person before** the date of the Supreme Court's declaration **remain valid** and are NOT invalidated.

**The Legal Principle Behind This — De Facto Doctrine:**
The **De Facto Doctrine** *(doctrine that acts of a person holding office in fact, even if not in law, are valid)* is a well-established principle in constitutional law across the world. It states that when a person holds a public office and performs official acts in good faith, those acts are valid even if it is later discovered that the person was not legally entitled to hold that office.

**Why Is This Critical?**
Consider this scenario:
1. Person X is elected as Vice-President.
2. For the next 6 months, X presides over Rajya Sabha — bills are passed, rulings are given.
3. X then acts as President for 2 months when the President falls ill — signs ordinances and diplomatic agreements.
4. The Supreme Court then declares X's election as Vice-President as **void** due to some fraud.

Without this protection, ALL of those bills, rulings, ordinances, and agreements would become legally invalid — creating constitutional chaos. This rule prevents that chaos by ensuring that the **official acts remain valid** even though the person is removed from office.

**Broader Application in Indian Law:**
The Supreme Court has applied the De Facto doctrine in multiple contexts:
- In ***Gokaraju Rangaraju vs. State of Andhra Pradesh (1981)***, the Supreme Court held that the de facto doctrine applies to judges appointed without following due procedure — their judgments remain valid.
- The same principle is codified for Vice-Presidential (and Presidential) elections in **Article 71(2)** of the Constitution.

## 📌 Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Quick Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Electoral College** | A special group of representatives who vote to elect a constitutional head |
| **Proportional Representation** | Voting system where representation is proportional to votes received |
| **Single Transferable Vote** | Voters rank candidates; votes transfer if top choice is eliminated |
| **Ex-Officio** | A role held automatically by virtue of holding another office |
| **Effective Majority** | Majority of the total membership of the House (not just present members) |
| **Simple Majority** | More than half of members present and voting |
| **De Facto Doctrine** | Acts of a person holding office in fact (even if illegally) remain valid |
| **Void Election** | An election declared invalid by a competent court |
| **Office of Profit** | A paid government position that may create conflict of interest |
| **Casting Vote** | Deciding vote cast by the presiding officer in case of a tie |
