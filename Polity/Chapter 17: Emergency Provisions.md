## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this box first. These are your 5 core takeaways.**

1. Emergency provisions are in **Part XVIII of the Constitution (Articles 352–360)**. They allow the Centre to handle abnormal situations while safeguarding sovereignty, unity, integrity, and security.
2. There are **three types of emergencies**: National Emergency (Art. 352), President's Rule (Art. 356), and Financial Emergency (Art. 360).
3. During a National Emergency, India transforms from a **federal to a unitary state** — without formally amending the Constitution. This is a **unique feature** of the Indian Constitution.
4. The **44th Amendment Act (1978)** introduced the most important safeguards against misuse of emergency powers — triggered by the controversial **1975 Internal Emergency**.
5. The **S.R. Bommai case (1994)** is the most landmark judgment on President's Rule — it listed when Article 356 can and cannot be used, and declared **secularism a basic feature** of the Constitution.

---

## 🧭 Introduction: Why Emergency Provisions?

- Emergency provisions are in **Part XVIII** of the Constitution, from **Articles 352 to 360**.
- Their purpose: to enable the **Central government to effectively handle any abnormal situation** while protecting:
  - Sovereignty, unity, integrity, and security of the country
  - The democratic political system
  - The Constitution itself

- During an Emergency, the **Centre becomes all-powerful** and states come under total Central control.
- Remarkably, this happens **without formally amending the Constitution** — the system just shifts from federal to unitary temporarily.

### 🇮🇳 Dr. Ambedkar's Vision (Constituent Assembly):
> *"All federal systems including American are placed in a tight mould of federalism. No matter what the circumstances, it cannot change its form and shape... On the other hand, the Constitution of India can be both unitary as well as federal according to the requirements of time and circumstances."*

This flexibility — **federal in normal times, unitary in emergencies** — is a **unique feature** of the Indian Constitution.

---

## 📊 Three Types of Emergencies — Quick Overview

| # | Type | Article | Popular Name | Trigger |
|---|------|---------|--------------|---------|
| 1 | **National Emergency** | **Article 352** | Proclamation of Emergency | War, External Aggression, Armed Rebellion |
| 2 | **State Emergency** | **Article 356** | **President's Rule** / Constitutional Emergency | Failure of constitutional machinery in a state |
| 3 | **Financial Emergency** | **Article 360** | Financial Emergency | Threat to financial stability or credit of India |

> ⚠️ **Note:** The Constitution does **NOT** use the word "emergency" for the second type (President's Rule).

# PART 1: NATIONAL EMERGENCY (Article 352)

---

## 🔴 Grounds for Declaration

Under **Article 352**, the President can declare a National Emergency when the **security of India or a part of it** is threatened by:
- **War**
- **External Aggression**
- **Armed Rebellion** *(violent internal uprising against the state)*

### 🔑 Key Points on Grounds:

- The President can declare a National Emergency **even before actual occurrence** — if there is **imminent danger** of war, external aggression, or armed rebellion.
- The President can issue **different simultaneous proclamations** on different grounds (added by **38th Amendment Act, 1975**).
- **External Emergency** = declared on grounds of war or external aggression.
- **Internal Emergency** = declared on grounds of armed rebellion.
- The Emergency can apply to the **entire country OR only a part of it** (the **42nd Amendment Act, 1976** enabled this partial application).

### 🔄 Change in Terminology — Important!

| Before 44th Amendment | After 44th Amendment (1978) |
|----------------------|----------------------------|
| 'Internal disturbance' was a ground | Replaced by **'Armed Rebellion'** |
| Very vague — could mean almost anything | Specific — requires actual armed revolt |

> 💡 This change was crucial because the **1975 Emergency** was declared on the ground of "internal disturbance" — a term so vague it was misused. The **44th Amendment Act (1978)** fixed this.

---

## 🖊️ Cabinet's Written Recommendation — A Critical Safeguard

- The President can proclaim a National Emergency **only after receiving a written recommendation from the Cabinet** *(the Council of the Prime Minister and Cabinet-rank Ministers — as defined in Article 352)*.
- This means the **entire Cabinet must concur** — the PM alone cannot decide.

### ⚠️ The 1975 Abuse:
- **Indira Gandhi** advised the President to proclaim Emergency in 1975 **without consulting her Cabinet**.
- The Cabinet was informed **after** the proclamation was already made — as a *fait accompli* *(done deal, no going back)*.
- The **44th Amendment Act (1978)** introduced the written Cabinet recommendation requirement specifically to **prevent this kind of abuse**.

---

## ⚖️ Judicial Review — Can Courts Question It?

- The **38th Amendment Act (1975)** made the declaration of National Emergency **immune from judicial review** *(courts cannot question it)*.
- The **44th Amendment Act (1978)** deleted this immunity.
- In the **Minerva Mills case (1980)**, the Supreme Court held that a proclamation of National Emergency **CAN be challenged in court** on the ground that:
  - It was based on **malafide** *(bad faith, improper motive)* intentions, OR
  - The declaration was based on **wholly extraneous and irrelevant facts**, OR
  - It is **absurd or perverse** *(completely unreasonable)*.

---

### 🏛️ CASE EXPLAINED: Minerva Mills vs. Union of India (1980)

**Background:**
The Minerva Mills Ltd. case is one of the most landmark constitutional cases in Indian history. It arose after the Nationalization of Minerva Mills (a textile company in Karnataka) under the Sick Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1974. The company challenged this nationalization.

**But the bigger constitutional question was:**
The case became the battleground for challenging the **42nd Constitutional Amendment Act (1976)** — passed during the Emergency by Indira Gandhi's government — which had:
1. Given Parliament **unlimited power** to amend the Constitution (removed judicial review of amendments)
2. Made **Directive Principles supreme** over Fundamental Rights
3. Greatly expanded the scope of Emergency powers

**What the Supreme Court Held:**
- The Supreme Court (Constitution Bench) **struck down** the key provisions of the 42nd Amendment that gave Parliament unlimited amending power.
- The Court reaffirmed the **Basic Structure Doctrine** from Kesavananda Bharati (1973).
- Key ruling: **"Parliament cannot abrogate its amendatory power under Article 368."** Limited amending power is itself a basic feature.
- On Emergency: The Court held that a proclamation of National Emergency **can be judicially reviewed** — it is not immune from challenge.
- The Court declared: **Harmony between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles** is a basic feature of the Constitution — one cannot be made completely subservient to the other.

**Why it matters for UPSC:**
- Reaffirmed Basic Structure Doctrine
- Restored judicial review over constitutional amendments AND Emergency proclamations
- A direct check on Emergency-era excesses

---

## 📅 Parliamentary Approval and Duration

### Timeline for Approval:
- Must be approved by **both Houses of Parliament** within **one month** from the date of issue.
  - *(Originally 2 months — reduced to 1 month by 44th Amendment, 1978)*

### What if Lok Sabha is Dissolved Before Approval?
- The proclamation **survives until 30 days** from the first sitting of the **reconstituted Lok Sabha**, provided the **Rajya Sabha has in the meantime approved** it.

### Duration After Approval:
- If approved → continues for **six months**.
- Can be **extended indefinitely**, with Parliament's approval **every six months**.
- *(The requirement of periodic 6-monthly approval was added by 44th Amendment, 1978. Before that, once approved, Emergency could last as long as the Executive wanted.)*

### Special Majority Required:
Every resolution approving or continuing the Emergency must be passed by **special majority** in each House:
- **(a)** Majority of the **total membership** of the House, AND
- **(b)** Majority of **not less than two-thirds** of members **present and voting**.

> 💡 Previously, a **simple majority** was enough. The **44th Amendment (1978)** raised this to special majority — another safeguard against misuse.

---

## 🔙 Revocation of National Emergency

- The President can revoke a proclamation **at any time** by a subsequent proclamation — **no parliamentary approval needed**.
- **BUT** — the President **must revoke** a proclamation if the **Lok Sabha passes a resolution disapproving** its continuation.

### Special Provision for Revocation (44th Amendment, 1978):
- If **one-tenth of total Lok Sabha members** give a **written notice** to the Speaker (or to the President if House is not in session) → a **special sitting must be held within 14 days** to consider a disapproval resolution.

### Disapproval vs. Approval Resolution — Key Differences:

| Feature | Disapproval Resolution | Approval/Continuation Resolution |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------------|
| Passed by | **Lok Sabha only** | **Both Houses** of Parliament |
| Majority needed | **Simple majority** | **Special majority** |

---

## 🌊 Effects of National Emergency

Effects can be grouped into **three categories**:

---

### 1️⃣ Effect on Centre-State Relations

#### (a) Executive:
- Centre's executive power **extends to directing any state** on **ANY matter** regarding how its executive power is to be exercised.
- In normal times, Centre can direct states only on **certain specified matters**.
- State governments are **brought under complete Central control** — but are **NOT suspended**.

#### (b) Legislative:
- Parliament becomes **empowered to make laws on any subject in the State List** *(matters normally only states can legislate on)*.
- State legislatures are **NOT suspended** — but their powers become **subject to Parliament's overriding power**.
- **Constitution becomes unitary rather than federal.**
- Laws made by Parliament on state subjects become **inoperative 6 months after the Emergency ceases**.
- During Emergency, if Parliament is not in session, **President can issue Ordinances on state subjects** too.
- Parliament can also **confer powers and impose duties** on the Centre/its officers for matters outside the Union List.
- **42nd Amendment Act (1976):** These executive and legislative consequences extend **not only to the state where Emergency operates, but also to ANY other state**.

#### (c) Financial:
- President can **modify the constitutional distribution of revenues** between Centre and states.
- This means the President can **reduce or cancel the transfer of finances** from Centre to states.
- Such modification continues till the **end of the financial year** in which the Emergency ceases.
- Every such Presidential order must be **laid before both Houses of Parliament**.

---

### 2️⃣ Effect on Life of Lok Sabha and State Assembly

- Life of **Lok Sabha** may be **extended beyond 5 years** by Parliament → for **one year at a time** (for any length of time).
- BUT this extension **cannot continue beyond 6 months** after Emergency ceases.
- **Example:** The **Fifth Lok Sabha (1971–1977)** had its term extended **twice** — by one year each time.
- Similarly, the **normal 5-year tenure** of a **state legislative assembly** can be extended by **one year at a time** during National Emergency, subject to **maximum 6 months after Emergency ceases**.

---

### 3️⃣ Effect on Fundamental Rights

This is governed by **Articles 358 and 359** — two different provisions with important differences.

---

#### (a) Article 358 — Suspension of Article 19 Fundamental Rights

- When National Emergency is proclaimed, the **six Fundamental Rights under Article 19** are **automatically suspended** — no separate order needed.
- The state can make any law or take any executive action that **abridges or takes away** these rights, and it **cannot be challenged**.
- When Emergency ceases → **Article 19 automatically revives**.
- Laws made during Emergency that are inconsistent with Article 19 **cease to have effect** after Emergency.
- **BUT:** No remedy lies for anything done **during** the Emergency — even after it ends. Legislative and executive actions taken during Emergency **cannot be challenged retroactively**.

**44th Amendment (1978) restricted Article 358 in two ways:**
1. Article 19 is suspended **only during External Emergency** (war/external aggression) — **NOT during Internal Emergency** (armed rebellion).
2. **Only laws related to the Emergency** are protected from challenge — not all laws. Executive action is protected only under such emergency-related laws.

---

#### (b) Article 359 — Suspension of Enforcement of Other Fundamental Rights

- Article 359 authorises the **President to suspend, by Order, the right to move any court** for enforcement of **specified Fundamental Rights**.
- Key distinction: Under Article 359, **Fundamental Rights are NOT suspended** — only their **enforcement** is suspended. The rights theoretically remain alive.
- The suspension covers **only those Fundamental Rights specified in the Presidential Order**.
- The suspension can be for the **entire duration of Emergency or a shorter period** — as mentioned in the order.
- The order may extend to the **whole country or a part of it**.
- The Order must be **laid before each House of Parliament** for approval.
- When Order ceases: laws made during it (inconsistent with specified rights) **cease to have effect** — but no remedy for actions taken during the Order's operation.

**44th Amendment (1978) restricted Article 359 in two ways:**
1. The President **CANNOT** suspend the right to move court for enforcement of **Articles 20 and 21** *(Right to protection against conviction, and Right to Life and Personal Liberty)* — these remain enforceable **even during Emergency**.
2. Only laws related to the Emergency are protected — not all laws.

---

### 📊 Distinction Between Articles 358 and 359 — Master Table

| Point | Article 358 | Article 359 |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Scope** | Only Article 19 rights | All Fundamental Rights specified in Presidential Order |
| **Automatic?** | ✅ Yes — automatic on Emergency declaration | ❌ No — requires Presidential Order |
| **Type of Emergency** | Only **External Emergency** (war/external aggression) | **Both External AND Internal** Emergency |
| **Duration** | Entire duration of Emergency | Entire duration OR shorter period (President decides) |
| **Territorial extent** | Entire country | Entire country OR part of it |
| **Articles 20 & 21** | Not applicable (deals with Art. 19) | ❌ Cannot suspend Articles 20 & 21 |
| **Effect** | Art. 19 rights **suspended** | Rights NOT suspended — only **enforcement** suspended |
| **State power** | State can make laws inconsistent with Art. 19 | State can make laws inconsistent with **specified** rights |

**Similarity:** Both Articles protect **only emergency-related laws** from challenge — not all laws.

## 📜 National Emergency — Declarations Made So Far

| # | When | Ground | Duration | Notes |
|---|------|--------|----------|-------|
| **1st** | **October 1962** | External aggression (Chinese aggression in NEFA — now Arunachal Pradesh) | Till **January 1968** | No fresh proclamation needed for 1965 war with Pakistan as this was still in force |
| **2nd** | **December 1971** | External aggression (Pakistan attack) | Revoked **March 1977** | — |
| **3rd** | **June 1975** | Internal disturbance (most controversial) | Revoked **March 1977** | Led to 44th Amendment safeguards |

### The 1975 Emergency — Why It Was So Controversial:
- Declared by Indira Gandhi's government on grounds of "internal disturbance" — claiming certain persons were inciting police and armed forces against their duties.
- **Widespread criticism** of misuse of Emergency powers.
- In **1977 Lok Sabha elections** after Emergency → **Congress Party lost; Janata Party came to power**.
- Janata government appointed the **Shah Commission** to investigate the circumstances of the 1975 Emergency.
- The **Shah Commission did NOT justify** the declaration of Emergency.
- Result: **44th Amendment Act (1978)** introduced numerous safeguards against misuse.

> 🌍 **[Modern Example] Modern Context:**
> The 1975 Emergency's shadow continues to shape Indian democracy. In **2021**, the Supreme Court in *Anup Barnwal vs. Union of India* reiterated the importance of free and fair elections — a right trampled in 1975. Every year on **June 25** (the day Emergency was declared in 1975), it is commemorated as **"Samvidhaan Hatya Diwas"** (Constitution Murder Day) — the Government of India officially declared this in **2024**, making it a significant political-constitutional event. The Emergency's legacy also influences debates about **UAPA, sedition law (Section 124A IPC — now BNS)**, and **preventive detention laws** — all connected to the balance between state security and individual liberty.

# PART 2: PRESIDENT'S RULE (Article 356)

---

## 📌 Grounds for Imposition

First, understand the **constitutional duty** behind it:

**Article 355** imposes a **duty on the Centre** to:
- Protect every state against **external aggression and internal disturbance**
- Ensure that every state government is carried on **in accordance with the Constitution**

It is in performance of this duty that President's Rule is imposed under Article 356.

### Two Legal Grounds:

**Ground 1 — Article 356:**
- President is **satisfied** that a situation has arisen where the **government of a state cannot be carried on in accordance with the Constitution**.
- President can act on a **Governor's report OR even without it** ("otherwise too").

**Ground 2 — Article 365:**
- Whenever a state **fails to comply with or give effect to any direction from the Centre** → President can hold that a situation has arisen making normal governance impossible.

---

## 📅 Parliamentary Approval and Duration

- Must be approved by **both Houses of Parliament** within **two months** from date of issue.
- If Lok Sabha is dissolved during this period without approving → proclamation survives **30 days from first sitting** of reconstituted Lok Sabha, provided Rajya Sabha approved it.
- Once approved → continues for **six months**.
  - *(42nd Amendment 1976 had raised this to 1 year; 44th Amendment 1978 reduced it back to 6 months)*
- Can be extended to a **maximum of 3 years**, with Parliament's approval every 6 months.
  - *(Exception: Punjab — President's Rule from May 1987 was extended to 5 years under 68th Amendment Act, 1991)*

### Approval Majority:
Only **simple majority** in each House (majority of members present and voting).
*(Unlike National Emergency which requires Special Majority)*

### Special Restriction Beyond 1 Year (44th Amendment, 1978):
Beyond one year, President's Rule can be extended **only if BOTH** these conditions are met:
1. A **National Emergency is in operation** in the whole of India or the whole/part of that state, AND
2. The **Election Commission certifies** that general elections to the state assembly **cannot be held**.

### Revocation:
- President can revoke at **any time** by subsequent proclamation — **no parliamentary approval needed**.

---

## 🔧 Consequences of President's Rule

When President's Rule is imposed:

1. President **takes up the functions** of the state government and powers of the Governor and other executive authorities.
2. President can declare that **state legislature's powers are to be exercised by Parliament**.
3. President can **suspend constitutional provisions** relating to any body or authority in the state.

### In Practice:
- **State Council of Ministers** headed by Chief Minister is **dismissed**.
- **State Governor** carries on administration on behalf of the President, with help of the **Chief Secretary** or **Presidential Advisors**.
- State legislative assembly is either **suspended or dissolved**.
  - If dissolved → fresh elections are held.

### Parliamentary Powers During President's Rule:
When state legislature is suspended/dissolved:
1. Parliament can **delegate law-making power for the state** to the President or any other specified authority.
2. Parliament or (if delegated) President/specified authority can confer powers and impose duties on the Centre/its officers.
3. President can **authorise expenditure from State Consolidated Fund** pending Parliament's sanction (when Lok Sabha is not in session).

Laws made during President's Rule **continue to operate even after** President's Rule ends — they are **not automatically repealed**. But the state legislature can repeal, alter, or re-enact them.

### Important Limitation:
> ⚠️ The President **CANNOT** assume powers of the **State High Court** or suspend constitutional provisions relating to it. The High Court's position, status, powers, and functions **remain completely unchanged** during President's Rule.

---

## 📊 National Emergency vs. President's Rule — Master Comparison

| Feature | National Emergency (Art. 352) | President's Rule (Art. 356) |
|---------|------------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Grounds** | War, External Aggression, Armed Rebellion | Failure of constitutional machinery in state |
| **State Exec/Legislature** | Continue to function; Centre gets concurrent powers | State executive dismissed; legislature suspended/dissolved |
| **Law-making** | Parliament makes laws on State List — **cannot delegate** | Parliament **can delegate** to President or specified authority |
| **Maximum Period** | **No maximum** — indefinite with 6-monthly approval | **Maximum 3 years** |
| **Approval Majority** | **Special Majority** | **Simple Majority** |
| **Centre-State** | ALL states' relations modified | Only the **affected state's** relation modified |
| **Fundamental Rights** | **Affected** (Articles 358 & 359) | **No effect** on Fundamental Rights |
| **Revocation by Lok Sabha** | ✅ Yes — Lok Sabha can pass disapproval resolution | ❌ No such provision — only President can revoke |

---

## 🏛️ Use of Article 356 — History and Controversy

- President's Rule has been imposed **more than 125 occasions** since 1950.
- First imposition: **Punjab in 1951**.
- Almost **all states** have been placed under President's Rule at least once.
- Article 356 has become **one of the most controversial and criticised provisions** of the Constitution.

### Political Misuse — Notable Instances:

**1977 — Janata Party government:**
- After winning elections post-Emergency, the Janata government imposed President's Rule in **nine states** where Congress was in power: *Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Bihar, Himachal Pradesh, Orissa, West Bengal, and Haryana*.
- Ground: Assemblies "no longer represented the wishes of the electorate."

**1980 — Congress returned to power:**
- Imposed President's Rule in **nine states** on the same ground: *Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Orissa, Gujarat, Maharashtra, and Tamil Nadu*.

**1992 — Three BJP states:**
- Congress government imposed President's Rule in **Madhya Pradesh, Himachal Pradesh, and Rajasthan**.
- Ground: Not implementing the Centre's ban on religious organisations sincerely.
- The Supreme Court upheld this in the **Bommai case (1994)** — secularism is a basic feature.
- BUT the Court did NOT uphold the imposition in **Nagaland (1988), Karnataka (1989), and Meghalaya (1991)**.

### Dr. Ambedkar's Hope vs. Reality:
> *Dr. Ambedkar hoped Article 356 would remain a "dead letter" and be used only as a last resort.*
> *H.V. Kamath's sad commentary: "Dr. Ambedkar is dead and the Articles are very much alive."*

---

## 🏛️ CASE EXPLAINED IN DETAIL: S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994) ⭐ MOST IMPORTANT

**This is arguably the single most important case on federalism and emergency provisions in Indian constitutional law.**

### Background:
**S.R. Bommai** was the Chief Minister of Karnataka (Janata Dal government). In 1989, the Karnataka Governor sent a report to the Centre claiming Bommai's government had lost its majority. President's Rule was imposed under Article 356 — **without giving Bommai a chance to prove his majority on the floor of the House**.

Bommai challenged this in court, arguing the dismissal was politically motivated and procedurally wrong.

The case eventually went to the Supreme Court, which used it as an opportunity to lay down comprehensive guidelines on the use of Article 356.

### The 9-Judge Constitution Bench Held:

1. **Judicial Review is Available:** Presidential proclamations imposing President's Rule are **subject to judicial review**. Courts can examine them.

2. **Relevant Material Required:** The President's satisfaction must be **based on relevant material**. If based on irrelevant/extraneous grounds, malafide reasons, or is perverse → courts can **strike it down**.

3. **Burden on Centre:** The Centre must **prove** that relevant material existed to justify the proclamation.

4. **Courts Check Relevance, Not Adequacy:** Courts cannot examine the *correctness* or *adequacy* of the material — but can check whether it is **relevant** to the action.

5. **Power to Restore:** If the court holds the proclamation **unconstitutional**, it has the **power to restore** the dismissed government and **revive the state legislature** (whether suspended or dissolved).

6. **Dissolve Only After Parliament Approves:** The state assembly should be **dissolved only AFTER Parliament approves** the proclamation. Until then, the President can only **suspend** it. If Parliament fails to approve → assembly gets **reactivated**.

7. **Secularism is Basic Feature:** A state government pursuing **anti-secular politics** is liable to action under Article 356. Secularism is a **basic feature** of the Constitution.

8. **Floor Test Required:** The question of a government **losing confidence** must be decided **on the floor of the House**. Until that is done, the ministry should **not be unseated**.

9. **New Central Government Cannot Dismiss State Governments:** Where a new party assumes power at the Centre, it **does NOT have authority to dismiss ministries** formed by other parties in states (directly overruling the 1977 and 1980 political misuse).

10. **Article 356 is Exceptional Power:** It should be used **only occasionally** to meet **special situations** — not as a routine political tool.

### Why the Bommai Case is a Landmark:
- It effectively **ended the casual, political misuse** of Article 356.
- It **federalized** the interpretation of President's Rule.
- It connected **secularism** directly to the use (and misuse) of emergency powers.
- It gave courts the power to **undo** a President's Rule and restore elected governments.

---

## ✅ Proper vs. Improper Use of Article 356

*(Based on Sarkaria Commission Report, 1988 and Bommai case, 1994)*

### ✅ PROPER Use — President's Rule is justified when:
1. After general elections, **no party secures majority** → **Hung Assembly**
2. Party with majority **declines to form ministry** AND no coalition can be formed
3. Ministry **resigns after defeat** AND no other party can form a majority government
4. State government **disregards a constitutional direction** from the Centre
5. **Internal subversion** — government is deliberately acting against Constitution, fomenting violent revolt
6. **Physical breakdown** — government wilfully refuses to discharge constitutional obligations, endangering state security

### ❌ IMPROPER Use — President's Rule is NOT justified when:
1. Ministry resigns/dismissed but Governor recommends President's Rule **without exploring alternatives**
2. Governor makes **his/her own assessment** of support without allowing ministry to **prove majority on the floor**
3. Ruling party **suffered massive defeat in Lok Sabha elections** (as happened in 1977 and 1980)
4. **Internal disturbances** that don't amount to subversion or physical breakdown
5. **Maladministration, corruption allegations, or financial difficulties** in the state
6. State government not given **prior warning** to rectify itself (except in extreme urgency)
7. Power used to **sort out intra-party problems** of the ruling party or for extraneous purposes

> 🌍 **[Modern Example] Modern Context:**
> The Bommai guidelines were applied in real time when President's Rule was imposed in **Uttarakhand (2016)**. The Harish Rawat government challenged it — the Uttarakhand High Court restored the government, and the **Supreme Court directed a floor test**. Rawat's government survived. This was a direct application of the Bommai principle that confidence must be tested on the floor of the House. Similarly, the **Maharashtra political crisis (2022)** — involving the Uddhav Thackeray vs. Eknath Shinde factions — while not directly invoking Article 356, was adjudicated by the Supreme Court in *Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary (2023)* which reaffirmed that **anti-defection and floor tests are central to democratic legitimacy** — principles rooted in Bommai.

# PART 3: FINANCIAL EMERGENCY (Article 360)

---

## 💰 Grounds for Declaration

- **Article 360** empowers the President to proclaim Financial Emergency if satisfied that a situation has arisen due to which the **financial stability or credit of India or any part of its territory is threatened**.
- The **38th Amendment Act (1975)** made the President's satisfaction final and non-justiciable — but this was **deleted by 44th Amendment (1978)**, meaning it is now **subject to judicial review**.

---

## 📅 Parliamentary Approval and Duration

- Must be approved by **both Houses of Parliament** within **two months** from date of issue.
- If Lok Sabha is dissolved → proclamation survives **30 days from first sitting** of reconstituted Lok Sabha, provided Rajya Sabha approved it.
- Once approved → **continues indefinitely until revoked**. This means:
  - ✅ **No maximum period** prescribed
  - ✅ **No repeated parliamentary approval** needed
- Approved by **simple majority** in each House.
- President can revoke at **any time** — no parliamentary approval needed for revocation.

---

## 📉 Effects of Financial Emergency

1. **Centre's executive authority extends** to giving directions to states to:
   - (a) Observe **canons of financial propriety** *(principles of sound financial management)*
   - (b) Any other directions the **President deems necessary**

2. Directions may include:
   - (a) **Reduction of salaries and allowances** of all or any class of persons serving in the state
   - (b) **Reservation of all Money Bills** or other financial bills for **President's consideration** after being passed by state legislature

3. President may issue directions for **reduction of salaries and allowances** of:
   - (a) All or any class of persons serving the **Union**
   - (b) **Judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts** *(this is a drastic power)*

> During Financial Emergency, the Centre acquires **full control over states in financial matters**.

### Historical Reference — Dr. Ambedkar on Financial Emergency:
> *"This Article more or less follows the pattern of what is called the National Recovery Act of the United States passed in 1933, which gave the president power to make similar provisions in order to remove difficulties, both economic and financial, that had overtaken the American people, as a result of the Great Depression."*

**H.N. Kunzru** (Constituent Assembly member) warned that financial emergency provisions "pose a serious threat to the financial autonomy of the states."

### Has Financial Emergency Ever Been Declared?
> ❌ **NO Financial Emergency has ever been declared in India** — not even during the **severe financial crisis of 1991** (when India had to pledge its gold reserves to the IMF).

> 🌍 **[Modern Example] Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` India faced an economic crisis during **COVID-19 (2020–21)** — GDP contracted by **7.3% in FY21**, the worst since Independence. Despite this, the government did **NOT invoke Article 360**. Instead, it used fiscal tools like the **Atmanirbhar Bharat Package (₹20 lakh crore)**, RBI's monetary interventions, and **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana**. This shows that Article 360 is truly a "nuclear option" that remains dormant. However, concerns about **states' fiscal autonomy** — particularly regarding GST compensation dues, and the 15th Finance Commission's recommendations (2021–26) — keep financial federalism and Article 360 contextually relevant.

# PART 4: CRITICISM OF EMERGENCY PROVISIONS

---

## 🚨 Critics in the Constituent Assembly

Several members strongly criticised the Emergency provisions:

**H.V. Kamath** (most vocal critic):
> *"I fear that by this single chapter we are seeking to lay the foundation of a totalitarian state, a police state... It will be a day of shame and sorrow when the President makes use of these powers having no parallel in any Constitution of the democratic countries of the world."*

**K.T. Shah** described them as:
> *"A chapter of reaction and retrogression... two distinct currents: (a) to arm the Centre with special powers against the units and (b) to arm the government against the people."*

**T.T. Krishnamachari** feared:
> *"By means of these provisions the President and the Executive would be exercising a form of constitutional dictatorship."*

**H.N. Kunzru** opined:
> *"The emergency financial provisions pose a serious threat to the financial autonomy of the States."*

### Five Main Criticisms:
1. Federal character of the Constitution will be **destroyed** and the Union will become **all powerful**.
2. Powers of the State — both Union and units — will be **entirely concentrated** in the Union executive.
3. The President will become a **dictator**.
4. **Financial autonomy** of states will be **nullified**.
5. **Fundamental Rights will become meaningless** → democratic foundations destroyed.

## 🛡️ Defenders of Emergency Provisions

**Sir Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar** labelled them as **"the very life-breath of the Constitution."**

**Mahabir Tyagi** opined they would work as a **"safety-valve"** to maintain the Constitution.

**Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** — while defending them — also honestly accepted:
> *"I do not altogether deny that there is a possibility of the Articles being abused or employed for political purposes."*

## 🔑 Key Amendments Summary

| Amendment | Year | Key Changes |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **38th Amendment Act** | 1975 | Made Presidential satisfaction in declaring Emergency immune from judicial review; allowed simultaneous proclamations |
| **42nd Amendment Act** | 1976 | Allowed partial Emergency; extended President's Rule duration; extended legislative/executive effects to all states |
| **44th Amendment Act** | 1978 | Replaced 'internal disturbance' with 'armed rebellion'; required Cabinet's written recommendation; reduced approval time from 2 months to 1 month; introduced special majority; added periodic 6-monthly approval; made Presidential satisfaction subject to judicial review; protected Articles 20 & 21 even during Emergency; gave Lok Sabha power to disapprove Emergency |
| **68th Amendment Act** | 1991 | Extended President's Rule in Punjab to 5 years |

## 🌍 Final Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]`

- **Jammu & Kashmir Reorganisation (2019):** The revocation of **Article 370** and conversion of J&K into two Union Territories raised questions about federalism and Emergency-like powers. While Article 356 was not formally used, the **President's Rule was in place in J&K since 2018** before the reorganisation, and the **Supreme Court in 'In Re: Article 370' (December 2023)** upheld the reorganisation while directing Assembly elections by September 2024 — a case touching the very heart of Chapter 17's themes.

- **Floor Tests and Governor's Role (2022–2024):** The Supreme Court in **Nabam Rebia vs. Deputy Speaker (2016)** and subsequent cases like **Maharashtra political crisis (2023)** and **Himachal Pradesh floor test (2023)** have repeatedly enforced the Bommai principle that **government confidence must be tested on the House floor** — not decided by Governors or the Centre.

- **Governors' Discretionary Actions:** In 2023–24, multiple opposition-ruled states (**Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Punjab**) faced tensions with their Governors over bill assent and Speaker appointments. The Supreme Court in **State of Punjab vs. Principal Secretary to Governor of Punjab (2023)** ruled that **Governors cannot indefinitely withhold assent to bills** — a direct check on potential misuse of powers that could create grounds for Article 356.
