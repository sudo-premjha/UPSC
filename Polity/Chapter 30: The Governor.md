# Chapter 30: The Governor

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **The Governor is a nominal/constitutional head** of the state — real power lies with the Council of Ministers (CoM) headed by the Chief Minister.
> 2. **The Governor is appointed by the President** (not elected) — making the office a link between the Centre and the State.
> 3. **The Governor has a dual role** — constitutional head of the state AND representative/agent of the Central government.
> 4. **Discretionary powers** make the Governor unique compared to the President, who has no such express discretion after the 42nd Amendment.
> 5. **Key Articles to remember:** 153–167, 174–176, 200–201, 213, 217, 233–234.

---

## 1. Introduction: Setting the Stage

India follows a **parliamentary system of government** (a system where the executive is responsible to the legislature) in its states — the same pattern as at the Centre.

- **Part VI** of the Constitution deals with government in the states.
- **Articles 153 to 167** (in Part VI) deal specifically with the **State Executive**.

### Who Makes Up the State Executive?
The state executive has **four components**:
1. The **Governor**
2. The **Chief Minister**
3. The **Council of Ministers**
4. The **Advocate General** of the state

> ⚠️ **Note:** There is **no office of Vice-Governor** in a state, unlike the Vice-President at the Centre.

### The Governor's Dual Role
The Governor serves two roles simultaneously:
- **Constitutional/Nominal Head** of the state (like the President at the Centre — a "figurehead" who acts on ministerial advice)
- **Agent of the Central Government** — representing the Centre's interests in the state

> 📌 **Usually**, one governor is appointed per state. But the **7th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1956** allows the **same person to be appointed as governor for two or more states**.

---

## 2. Appointment of the Governor

### How is the Governor Appointed?
The Governor is **neither directly elected by the people** nor **indirectly elected** through an electoral college (like the President).

Instead, the Governor is **appointed by the President** by a warrant under the President's hand and seal. In effect, the Governor is a **nominee of the Central Government**.

### Is the Governor an Employee of the Central Government?
> **No.** The Supreme Court clarified this in a landmark case:

---

### ⚖️ Court Case 1: Hargovind Pant vs. Raghukul Tilak (1979)

**Background:**
A question arose whether the office of the Governor was "an employment under the Central Government" — which would have had implications for who could be appointed governor and what legal protections applied.

**What the Supreme Court Held:**
The Supreme Court clearly ruled that the **office of the Governor of a state is NOT an employment under the Central Government**. It is:
- An **independent constitutional office**
- **Not under the control of** or **subordinate to** the Central Government

**Why it Matters:**
This judgment establishes that while the President appoints the Governor, the Governor is not a servant or subordinate of the Centre. The office has its own constitutional standing and dignity.

---

### Why Was Direct Election of the Governor Rejected?

The **Draft Constitution** originally proposed **direct election** of the Governor through universal adult suffrage. However, the **Constituent Assembly rejected this** and opted for the present system of Presidential appointment.

The reasons given (as recorded in **Constituent Assembly Debates, Volume IV, pp. 588–607**) are:

1. Direct election is **incompatible** with the parliamentary system established in states (since the Governor is a constitutional/nominal head, not a real executive).
2. Direct election would likely **create conflicts** between the Governor and the Chief Minister.
3. The Governor is only a **nominal/constitutional head** — making elaborate (and expensive) election arrangements for such a figurehead is pointless.
4. A gubernatorial election would revolve around **personal issues**, not national interest — involving a huge voter base for this is undesirable.
5. An elected Governor would **belong to a party** and therefore **cannot be neutral or impartial**.
6. Electing governors could create **separatist tendencies**, harming political stability and national unity.
7. Presidential nomination allows the **Centre to maintain control** over states.
8. Direct election creates **leadership confusion** during a general election in the state.
9. The Chief Minister would push for **his/her own nominee** — resulting in a second-rate party member becoming Governor.

### The Model Chosen: Canadian, Not American
- **American model** (where state governors are directly elected) → **Rejected**
- **Canadian model** (where provincial governors are appointed by the Governor-General/Centre) → **Accepted**

## 3. Qualifications, Oath, and Conditions of Office

### 3.1 Constitutional Qualifications

The Constitution lays down **only two qualifications** for appointment as Governor:

1. Must be a **citizen of India**
2. Must have **completed the age of 35 years**

### 3.2 Sarkaria Commission Recommendations on Appointment (1983–88)

The **Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1983–88)** made the following additional recommendations (beyond the bare constitutional qualifications):

**Criteria for the person to be appointed:**
- (i) Should be **eminent in some walk of life**
- (ii) Should be a person **from outside the state**
- (iii) Should be a **detached figure** — not too closely connected with local state politics
- (iv) Should **not have been too active in politics**, particularly in the recent past

**On Political Neutrality:**
- It is **desirable** that a politician from the **ruling party at the Centre** is NOT appointed as Governor of a state run by a **different party or coalition**.

**On Consultation with the Chief Minister:**
- The procedure of **consulting the state Chief Minister** in the selection of Governor should be **prescribed in the Constitution itself** by suitably amending **Article 155**.

**On Who Should Be Consulted by the Prime Minister:**
- The **Vice-President of India** and the **Speaker of the Lok Sabha** may be consulted by the Prime Minister while selecting a Governor.
- However, this consultation should be **informal and confidential** — NOT a constitutional obligation.

---

### 3.3 Oath or Affirmation

Before assuming office, the Governor must take an oath or affirmation. In the oath, the Governor swears to:

- **(a)** Faithfully execute the office
- **(b)** Preserve, protect and defend the Constitution and the law
- **(c)** Devote himself/herself to the service and well-being of the people of the state

**Who administers the oath?**
- The **Chief Justice of the concerned State High Court**
- In the Chief Justice's absence → the **senior-most judge** of that court who is available

> Every person **discharging the functions** of the Governor (e.g., during the Governor's absence) also takes a similar oath.

---

### 3.4 Conditions of Office

The Constitution prescribes the following conditions for the Governor's office:

1. **No dual membership:** The Governor must **NOT be a member** of either House of Parliament or any House of the state legislature. If such a person is appointed, they are deemed to have **vacated their seat** in that House on the date they enter office as Governor.

2. **No other office of profit:** The Governor must **not hold any other office of profit** (a position with financial gain from government).

3. **Official Residence:** The Governor is entitled to the use of the **Raj Bhavan** (official residence) **without payment of rent**.

4. **Emoluments:** The Governor is entitled to emoluments (salary), allowances, and privileges as determined by **Parliament**.
   > 💰 In **2018**, Parliament increased the Governor's salary from **₹1.10 lakh to ₹3.50 lakh per month** (via the Finance Act, 2018, with effect from **1st January, 2016**, amending the Governor's (Emoluments, Allowances and Privileges) Act, 1982).

5. **Shared emoluments (two or more states):** When the same person is Governor of two or more states, their emoluments are **shared by those states** in proportions determined by the President.

6. **No reduction in emoluments:** The Governor's emoluments and allowances **cannot be reduced** during their term of office.

**Privileges and Immunities:**
- Like the President, the Governor enjoys **personal immunity** from legal liability for **official acts**.
- During their term, the Governor is immune from **criminal proceedings** — even for personal acts.
- The Governor **cannot be arrested or imprisoned** during their term.
- However, **civil proceedings** for personal acts **can** be initiated after giving a **two months' notice** during their term.

---

## 4. Term of the Governor's Office

- The Governor holds office for a **term of five years** from the date of entering office.
- However, this five-year term is **subject to the pleasure of the President** — the President can remove the Governor at any time.
- The Governor can **resign at any time** by writing to the President.

> ⚠️ **The Constitution does NOT lay down any specific grounds** for removal of a Governor. This has led to political controversies.

---

### ⚖️ Court Case 2: Surya Narain vs. Union of India (1981)

**Background:**
The question arose whether the "pleasure of the President" — which determines the Governor's tenure — could be challenged in a court of law.

**What the Supreme Court Held:**
The Supreme Court ruled that the **pleasure of the President is NOT justiciable** (i.e., courts cannot examine or question whether the President was right in removing a Governor).

**Implications:**
- The Governor has **no security of tenure** and **no fixed term** in practice.
- The President can remove a Governor at any time, and this action **cannot be challenged in court**.

**Why it Matters:**
This judgment has been criticized because it gives the Centre vast, unchecked power over Governors, which has historically been misused during political transitions.

---

### Historical Examples of Governors Being Replaced:

- **1989:** The **National Front Government (V.P. Singh)** asked all governors appointed by the Congress government to resign. Some were replaced; some continued.
- **1991:** The **Congress Government (P.V. Narasimha Rao)** changed **14 governors** appointed by the V.P. Singh and Chandra Sekhar governments.

### Other Provisions on Term:
- The President may **transfer a Governor** from one state to another for the remainder of the term.
- A Governor whose term has **expired may be reappointed** in the same or a different state.
- A Governor can **hold office beyond five years** until a successor takes charge (to prevent a vacancy/"interregnum").
- If a sitting Governor **dies in office**, the President can appoint the **Chief Justice of the concerned State High Court** to temporarily discharge the Governor's functions.

---

## 5. Powers and Functions of the Governor

The Governor's powers are broadly **analogous to those of the President** at the Centre. However, unlike the President, the Governor has:

- ✅ Executive Powers
- ✅ Legislative Powers
- ✅ Financial Powers
- ✅ Judicial Powers
- ❌ NO Diplomatic Powers
- ❌ NO Military Powers
- ❌ NO Emergency Powers (unlike the President)

---

### 5.1 Executive Powers

1. All **executive actions** of the state government are formally taken **in the Governor's name**.
2. The Governor can make **rules** specifying how orders and instruments executed in their name shall be **authenticated** (officially verified).
3. The Governor can make rules for **convenient transaction of state government business** and for **allocating business among ministers**.
4. The Governor **appoints the Chief Minister and other ministers**. Ministers hold office during the Governor's **pleasure**.
   - **Special provision:** States of **Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, and Odisha** must have a **Tribal Welfare Minister** appointed by the Governor.
   - Bihar was **excluded** from this provision by the **94th Amendment Act of 2006**.
5. The Governor **appoints the Advocate General** of the state and determines their remuneration. The Advocate General holds office during the Governor's pleasure.
6. The Governor **appoints the State Election Commissioner** and determines their service conditions and tenure.
   - However, the State Election Commissioner can be removed **only** in the same manner as a **High Court judge** (not at the Governor's whim).
7. The Governor **appoints the Chairman and Members of the State Public Service Commission (SPSC)**.
   - However, they can be **removed only by the President**, not by the Governor.
8. The Governor can **seek information** from the Chief Minister regarding state administration and legislative proposals.
9. The Governor can **require the Chief Minister** to place before the full Council of Ministers any matter on which a single minister has taken a decision but the full CoM has not considered it.
10. The Governor can **recommend the imposition of Constitutional Emergency (President's Rule)** in the state to the President.
    - During President's Rule, the Governor enjoys **extensive executive powers** as the President's agent.
11. The Governor acts as the **Chancellor of Universities** in the state and **appoints Vice-Chancellors**.

---

### 5.2 Legislative Powers

The Governor is an **integral part of the state legislature**. Key legislative powers:

1. Can **summon or prorogue** (suspend session of) the state legislature and **dissolve** the State Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha).
2. Can **address the state legislature** at:
   - The commencement of the **first session after each general election**
   - The **first session of each year**
3. Can **send messages** to the legislature regarding a bill pending before it or on any other matter.
4. Can **appoint any member of the State Legislative Assembly** to preside over proceedings when **both the Speaker and Deputy Speaker posts are vacant**. Similarly for the Legislative Council (when both Chairman and Deputy Chairman posts are vacant).
5. **Nominates one-sixth of the members of the State Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad)** from persons with special knowledge/experience in:
   - Literature
   - Science
   - Art
   - Cooperative movement
   - Social service
6. **Previously nominated one Anglo-Indian member** to the State Legislative Assembly. However, the **104th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2019** has **discontinued this provision**.
7. **Decides on disqualification** of members of the state legislature, in consultation with the **Election Commission**.
8. **Four options when a bill is sent to the Governor** after being passed by the state legislature:
   - **(a) Give assent** → Bill becomes an Act.
   - **(b) Withhold assent** → Bill ends; does not become an Act.
   - **(c) Return the bill (for non-money bills only)** for reconsideration. If the legislature passes it again (with or without amendments), the Governor **must give assent**.
   - **(d) Reserve the bill for the President's consideration**. Reservation is **obligatory** when the bill **endangers the position of the State High Court**.

   **Additional grounds for reservation** (as identified by **Soli Sorabji**, former Attorney-General of India):
   - (i) **Ultra-vires** (against the Constitution)
   - (ii) **Against the Directive Principles** of State Policy
   - (iii) Against the **larger national interest**
   - (iv) Of **grave national importance**
   - (v) Deals with **compulsory acquisition of property** under **Article 31A**

9. **Can promulgate Ordinances** when the state legislature is not in session.
   - Ordinances must be approved by the state legislature within **six weeks** from its reassembly.
   - The Governor can **withdraw an ordinance** at any time.
   - This is the **most important legislative power** of the Governor.

---

### ⚖️ Court Case 3: D.C. Wadhwa vs. State of Bihar (1986) — Ordinance Re-promulgation Case

**Background:**
Between **1967 and 1981**, the **Governor of Bihar** promulgated a staggering **256 ordinances**. These ordinances were repeatedly re-promulgated (re-issued with the same text) without being placed before the legislature — some were kept alive for **one to fourteen years** through this mechanism, bypassing the state legislature entirely.

D.C. Wadhwa, a political science professor, challenged this practice in the Supreme Court, arguing it was a constitutional fraud.

**What the Supreme Court Held:**
The Supreme Court ruled that:
- **Successive re-promulgation** of ordinances with the same text, **without any genuine attempt to get corresponding bills passed** by the legislature, is a **violation of the Constitution**.
- An ordinance re-promulgated in this manner is **liable to be struck down** (declared invalid).
- The **exceptional power of law-making through ordinances cannot be used as a substitute** for the normal legislative power of the state legislature.

**Why it Matters:**
This is a landmark case on the **misuse of ordinance-making power**. The ordinance power is meant to handle genuine emergencies when the legislature is not in session — it cannot be used as a permanent alternative to legislation. This judgment protects the supremacy of the legislature.

---

10. The Governor **lays the following reports** before the state legislature:
    - Report of the **State Finance Commission**
    - Report of the **State Public Service Commission**
    - Report of the **Comptroller and Auditor-General** (relating to state accounts)

---

### 5.3 Financial Powers

1. Ensures that the **Annual Financial Statement (State Budget)** is laid before the state legislature.
2. **Money bills** can be introduced in the state legislature **only with the Governor's prior recommendation**.
3. **No demand for a grant** can be made except on the Governor's recommendation.
4. Can make **advances from the Contingency Fund** of the state to meet unforeseen expenditure.
5. **Constitutes a Finance Commission** every five years to review the financial position of **Panchayats and Municipalities**.

---

### 5.4 Judicial Powers

1. Can **grant pardons, reprieves, respites, and remissions** of punishment, or **suspend, remit, and commute** the sentence of any person convicted of an offence against a **state law**.
2. Is **consulted by the President** while appointing judges of the concerned **State High Court**.
3. Makes **appointments, postings, and promotions** of **district judges** in consultation with the State High Court.
4. **Appoints persons to the judicial service** (other than district judges) in consultation with both the State High Court and the **State Public Service Commission**.

## 6. Detailed Comparison: Governor vs. President

### 6.1 Veto Powers Compared (Table 30.1)

#### Regarding Ordinary Bills:

| Aspect | President | Governor |
|---|---|---|
| Options available | 3 options | 4 options |
| Give assent | ✅ Bill becomes Act | ✅ Bill becomes Act |
| Withhold assent | ✅ Bill ends | ✅ Bill ends |
| Return for reconsideration | ✅ If passed again, MUST give assent | ✅ If passed again, MUST give assent |
| Reserve for President | ❌ N/A | ✅ Additional 4th option |
| Type of veto | **Suspensive Veto** (can only delay, not permanently block) | **Suspensive Veto** |

**What happens when a Governor reserves an ordinary bill for the President?**
- The Governor has **no further role** after reservation.
- The President can:
  - **(a)** Give assent → Bill becomes Act
  - **(b)** Withhold assent → Bill ends
  - **(c)** Return for reconsideration → Legislature must reconsider within **6 months**. If passed again and presented to the President, the **President is NOT bound to give assent** (may still withhold).
- If the bill is returned by the President and passed again by the state legislature, it must be presented **only to the President** (not to the Governor again). Governor's assent is no longer required.

#### Regarding Money Bills:

| Aspect | President | Governor |
|---|---|---|
| Options available | 2 options | 3 options |
| Give assent | ✅ Bill becomes Act | ✅ Bill becomes Act |
| Withhold assent | ✅ Bill ends | ✅ Bill ends |
| Return for reconsideration | ❌ Cannot return a Money Bill | ❌ Cannot return a Money Bill |
| Reserve for President | ❌ N/A | ✅ Can reserve for President |

**Normally**, the Governor gives assent to a money bill since it is introduced with their **prior permission** in the first place.

When Governor reserves a Money Bill for the President:
- President can give or withhold assent.
- President **cannot return** a Money Bill for reconsideration of the state legislature.
- Governor has no further role after reservation.

---

### 6.2 Ordinance-Making Powers Compared (Table 30.2)

| Aspect | President | Governor |
|---|---|---|
| When can promulgate | When either or both Houses of Parliament are not in session | When Legislative Assembly (unicameral) is not in session, OR both/either House(s) of bicameral legislature not in session |
| Condition | Satisfied that circumstances require immediate action | Same — satisfied that circumstances require immediate action |
| Scope | Co-extensive with Parliament's legislative power | Co-extensive with state legislature's legislative power |
| Legal force | Same as an Act of Parliament | Same as an Act of the state legislature |
| Subject to limitations | Same as Parliament's limitations | Same as state legislature's limitations |
| Can withdraw | Yes, at any time | Yes, at any time |
| Discretionary? | **Not discretionary** — acts on advice of Council of Ministers (PM) | **Not discretionary** — acts on advice of Council of Ministers (CM) |
| Must be laid before | Both Houses of Parliament on reassembly | Legislative Assembly / both Houses (bicameral) on reassembly |
| Ceases to operate | After 6 weeks of Parliament's reassembly, or earlier if both Houses pass disapproval resolution | After 6 weeks of state legislature's reassembly, or earlier if Legislative Assembly passes disapproval resolution agreed to by Legislative Council (bicameral) |
| Instructions needed? | **No** — needs no instructions | **Yes** — needs President's instructions in 3 cases (see below) |

**The Governor CANNOT issue an ordinance without President's instructions in these 3 cases:**
- **(a)** If a bill with the same provisions would have required the **President's prior sanction** for introduction in the state legislature.
- **(b)** If the Governor would have found it necessary to **reserve a bill** with the same provisions for the President's consideration.
- **(c)** If a state Act with the same provisions would have been **invalid without the President's assent**.

---

### 6.3 Pardoning Powers Compared (Table 30.3)

| Aspect | President | Governor |
|---|---|---|
| Pardon for offences against | **Central law** | **State law** |
| Can pardon death sentence? | ✅ **YES** — Only authority to pardon death sentence | ❌ **NO** — Cannot pardon death sentence (even if state law prescribes it) |
| Can suspend/remit/commute death sentence? | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes — but cannot PARDON |
| Court-martial (military court) sentences | ✅ Can pardon/commute | ❌ No such power |

> 📌 **Key Distinction to Remember:** Even if a **state law** prescribes the death penalty, the power to **grant pardon** for that death sentence lies with the **President, not the Governor**.

## 7. Constitutional Position of the Governor

The Indian Constitution establishes a **parliamentary form of government** in the states, just as at the Centre. As a result:

- The Governor is a **nominal executive** — a "figurehead."
- **Real executive power** lies with the **Council of Ministers headed by the Chief Minister.**
- The Governor exercises powers on the **aid and advice** of the Council of Ministers — **except** in matters where the Governor is required to act in **personal discretion**.

### Key Constitutional Articles on the Governor's Position:

- **Article 154:** Executive power of the state is vested in the Governor, exercised directly or through subordinate officers.
- **Article 163:** There shall be a Council of Ministers with the CM as head to **aid and advise** the Governor — except where the Governor must exercise **discretion**.
- **Article 164:** The Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible** to the State Legislative Assembly — this is the **foundation of parliamentary government** in the state.

### How the Governor's Position Differs from the President's:

1. The Constitution **envisages the possibility of the Governor acting in personal discretion** — no such express discretion exists for the President.
2. After the **42nd Constitutional Amendment (1976)**, ministerial advice became **binding on the President**. But **no such binding provision** exists for the Governor.

> ⚠️ **Important Rule:** If a question arises whether a matter falls within the Governor's discretion, **the Governor's own decision is final** — and this cannot be challenged in court.

## 8. Discretionary Powers of the Governor

### 8.1 Constitutional Discretion (Expressly Mentioned in the Constitution)

1. **Reservation of a bill** for the consideration of the President.
2. **Recommendation for imposition of President's Rule** in the state.
3. **While functioning as administrator** of an adjoining Union Territory (when given additional charge).
4. **Determining royalty amounts** payable by the Governments of **Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram** to an autonomous Tribal District Council from mineral exploration licenses.
   - (*Under Paragraph 9(2) of the Sixth Schedule — The Governor's decision is final and not subject to judicial review.*)

### 8.2 Situational Discretion (Hidden — Derived from Political Circumstances)

1. **Appointment of Chief Minister** when no party has a clear majority in the assembly, or when the sitting CM dies suddenly with no obvious successor.
2. **Dismissal of the Council of Ministers** when it cannot prove the confidence of the state legislature.
3. **Dissolution of the State Legislative Assembly** when the Council of Ministers has lost its majority.

### 8.3 Special Responsibilities Under Presidential Directions (State-Specific)

In these cases, the Governor consults the Council of Ministers but **acts in personal discretion** as per Presidential directions:

| State | Special Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Maharashtra** | Establishment of separate development boards for **Vidarbha** and **Marathwada** |
| **Gujarat** | Establishment of separate development boards for **Saurashtra** and **Kutch** |
| **Nagaland** | Law and order — as long as internal disturbance in **Naga Hills-Tuensang Area** continues |
| **Assam** | Administration of **tribal areas** |
| **Manipur** | Administration of **hill areas** |
| **Sikkim** | Peace and ensuring **social and economic advancement** of different sections |
| **Arunachal Pradesh** | **Law and order** in the state |
| **Karnataka** | Establishment of a separate development board for **Hyderabad-Karnataka region** (Added by the **98th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2012**) |

## 9. Issues in the Governor's Functioning (Sarkaria Commission, 1983–88)

The **Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1983–88)** identified several recurring and controversial issues:

### Issue 1: Choosing the Chief Minister
- When a single party gets a clear majority → **No problem.**
- When **no single party has an absolute majority** → Governor must use **discretion** to choose the CM.
- Best practice: Call the leader of the **largest party/group** to form the government and let the Assembly decide confidence — minimizing allegations of bias.

### Issue 2: Testing the Majority
- Different Governors have used different methods to verify which party commands majority.
- Some relied on **lists of supporters** submitted by rival claimants.
- Others conducted **physical verification** (counting heads — "floor test").

### Issue 3: Dismissal of Chief Minister
- **No uniform criteria** across states.
- A CM who loses majority must either **resign** or **face the Assembly** to prove majority.
- Inconsistency in the approach taken by different Governors has been criticized.

### Issue 4: Dissolving the Legislative Assembly
- When a CM **commands majority** → Governor is **normally bound** to accept the CM's advice to dissolve.
- When a CM has **lost majority** → Some Governors refused dissolution; others accepted the advice and dissolved. Inconsistency criticized.

### Issue 5: Recommending President's Rule
- Many Governors recommended **President's Rule (Article 356)** without exhausting all constitutional options first.
- Governors didn't give contending parties a fair chance to form a government or allow fresh elections.
- These recommendations have been widely criticized as **politically motivated and partisan**.

### Issue 6: Reserving State Bills for the President
- Reservation of state bills for Presidential consideration has created **controversy** in several cases.

### Issue 7: Nominations to the Legislative Council
- Governors' use of discretion in **nominating members to the Legislative Council** has been criticized.
- The criticism: Governors have **no discretion** in such matters and must follow ministerial advice.

### Issue 8: Exercise of Discretion as Chancellor of University
- Several states have laws making the Governor the **Chancellor of state universities** by virtue of the office.
- Governors have exercised powers as **Chancellor under the statute** (not as Governor per se).
- Criticism: Even in this capacity, Governors should abide by the advice of the Council of Ministers.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> *[Modern Example — AI Added for contemporary relevance]*

### 🔹 The Ongoing Debate: Governor vs. Elected State Governments

The role of the Governor has been a **flashpoint** in Centre-State relations in recent years, particularly in states governed by opposition parties.

**Recent Controversies (2019–2024):**

- **Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Telangana, West Bengal, and Punjab** saw significant friction between their elected state governments and the Governor's office. In several cases, Governors delayed giving assent to bills passed by state legislatures — sometimes for months or years — creating a constitutional deadlock.

- **[Modern Example]** In **2023**, the **Supreme Court of India** in the case of **State of Punjab vs. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab** issued a significant ruling. The Supreme Court held that the Governor cannot **indefinitely withhold assent** to bills passed by the state legislature and directed that the Governor must act expeditiously. The Court observed that the Governor cannot "sit on" bills indefinitely as this would paralyze constitutional governance.

- **[Modern Example]** In **November 2023**, the Supreme Court (in a case involving the **Kerala Governor**) strongly reaffirmed that Governors cannot use **pocket veto** (indefinite withholding of assent) on state bills. It reiterated the constitutional principle that the Governor acts as a constitutional head and must work within constitutional bounds.

### 🔹 Vice-Chancellors and Governors: A New Battleground

**[Modern Example]** A major 2022–2024 controversy emerged over **gubernatorial powers as Chancellor of State Universities**:

- States like **Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and West Bengal** passed or proposed laws to **strip the Governor of the Chancellor's role** and transfer it to the Chief Minister or another state authority.
- **Tamil Nadu's amendment (2023)** to replace the Governor as Chancellor with a state-appointed authority was reserved by the Governor for Presidential consideration.
- This has rekindled the Sarkaria Commission's issue no. 8 (Governor as Chancellor) with new urgency.

---

### 🔹 The B.P. Singhal Case (2010) — Also Relevant

**[Modern Example]** While technically decided in 2010, this case remains highly relevant:

In **B.P. Singhal vs. Union of India (2010)**, the **Supreme Court** held that while the President can remove a Governor at any time (confirming the Surya Narain ruling), the **removal cannot be done arbitrarily or for political reasons**. There must be some justifiable ground even if the grounds don't need to be communicated to the Governor. This was a progressive step toward protecting the constitutional neutrality of the office.

---

### 🔹 Punchhi Commission (2007–2010) Recommendations

**[Modern Example]** The **Punchhi Commission on Centre-State Relations** (set up in 2007, report submitted in 2010) built upon Sarkaria's work and made additional recommendations:

- The **practice of using Governors as instruments of the ruling party at the Centre** should be stopped.
- Governors should be given a **fixed tenure** with removal only through **impeachment** (similar to a High Court judge).
- The **"doctrine of pleasure"** (the ability to remove the Governor arbitrarily) should be curtailed.
