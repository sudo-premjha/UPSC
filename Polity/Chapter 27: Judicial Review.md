## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> *Read this before diving in. These are your 5 core takeaways.*

1. **Judicial Review** is the power of courts to strike down laws and executive orders that violate the Constitution.
2. It originated in the **USA (1803)** via *Marbury v. Madison*, but in India, it is **explicitly written into the Constitution itself**.
3. The Supreme Court has declared Judicial Review a **Basic Feature of the Constitution** — meaning even Parliament cannot remove it via amendment.
4. India's scope of Judicial Review is **narrower than the USA's** because India uses "procedure established by law" while the USA uses "due process of law."
5. Laws placed in the **Ninth Schedule** are **not fully immune** from Judicial Review — especially those added after **April 24, 1973**.

---

## 🌱 Origin of Judicial Review

### Where Did It Come From?

The doctrine of **Judicial Review** was born in the **United States of America**. It was propounded (formally put forward) for the first time in the landmark case of **Marbury versus Madison (1803)** by **John Marshall**, who was the Chief Justice of the American Supreme Court at the time.

### India is Different

In India, the situation is different and, arguably, stronger. Here, **the Constitution itself directly grants the power of Judicial Review** to the judiciary — both the **Supreme Court** and the **High Courts**.

Going even further, the Supreme Court has declared Judicial Review to be a **Basic Feature of the Constitution** (part of the **Basic Structure Doctrine**). This has a very important consequence:

> 🔑 **Even a Constitutional Amendment cannot curtail or remove the power of Judicial Review.**

---

## 📖 What Does "Judicial Review" Actually Mean?

**Judicial Review** is the power of the judiciary to examine whether laws passed by the legislature or orders issued by the executive are in line with the Constitution — for both the **Central Government** and **State Governments**.

**What happens if a law fails the test?**
- It is declared **illegal, unconstitutional, and invalid (null and void)**
- The government **cannot enforce** that law anymore
- It is said to be **ultra vires** (Latin for "beyond power" — i.e., exceeding legal authority)

---

## 🗂️ Three Categories of Judicial Review

Justice **Syed Shah Mohamed Quadri** classified Judicial Review into **three categories**:

| # | Category | What It Covers |
|---|----------|----------------|
| 1 | **Review of Constitutional Amendments** | Checking if amendments to the Constitution are valid |
| 2 | **Review of Legislation** | Checking laws of Parliament, State Legislatures, and subordinate bodies |
| 3 | **Review of Administrative Action** | Checking executive/administrative actions of Union, State, and authorities under the State |

---

## ⚖️ Key Cases Where Judicial Review Was Used

The Supreme Court has exercised this power in many famous cases. Here is a detailed breakdown of each:

---

### 🏛️ Case 1: Marbury v. Madison (1803) — USA
**Court:** US Supreme Court
**Judge:** Chief Justice John Marshall

**Background:**
William Marbury was appointed as a Justice of the Peace by outgoing President John Adams. The new Secretary of State, James Madison, refused to deliver his commission. Marbury petitioned the Supreme Court directly.

**Judgment:**
Chief Justice Marshall ruled that while Marbury had a right to his commission, the section of the *Judiciary Act of 1789* that gave the Supreme Court power to issue the order was **unconstitutional**. The Court struck down that part of the Act.

**Why It Matters:**
This was the **first time any court in the world formally asserted the power to strike down legislation** as unconstitutional. It established the foundational principle of Judicial Review.

---

### 🏛️ Case 2: Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Bench:** 11-judge Constitutional Bench

**Background:**
The Golaknath family held large tracts of agricultural land in Punjab. The state government sought to enforce land ceiling laws under the Punjab Security of Land Tenures Act, which was placed in the Ninth Schedule. The family argued that Parliament could not take away Fundamental Rights.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court, by a 6:5 majority, held that **Parliament cannot amend Part III (Fundamental Rights) of the Constitution** to take away or abridge Fundamental Rights. It held that Constitutional Amendments are "laws" under Article 13 and are therefore subject to Fundamental Rights.

**Why It Matters:**
This was a direct exercise of Judicial Review over a Constitutional Amendment itself. It set up a major confrontation between Parliament and the Judiciary that eventually led to the Kesavananda Bharati case.

---

### 🏛️ Case 3: R.C. Cooper v. Union of India — Bank Nationalisation Case (1970)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India

**Background:**
The government of **Indira Gandhi** passed the **Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act, 1969**, nationalising 14 major private banks. Rustom Cavasjee Cooper, a shareholder of one of the banks, challenged this as a violation of his Fundamental Rights (Right to Property, Right to carry on business under Article 19, and Right to equality under Article 14).

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court struck down the Bank Nationalisation Act, holding that:
- The compensation offered was illusory and not "adequate"
- It violated Article 14 (equality) and Article 19(1)(f) (right to property)
- The Act was **unconstitutional**

**Why It Matters:**
This was a landmark use of Judicial Review to strike down a major government policy. It added to the tension between Parliament and the Judiciary.

---

### 🏛️ Case 4: Madhav Rao Scindia v. Union of India — Privy Purses Abolition Case (1970)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India

**Background:**
After Independence, former rulers (princes) of princely states were given special payments called **Privy Purses** and special privileges as part of the merger agreements. In 1970, President V.V. Giri issued an order **de-recognising** all the rulers (on the advice of PM Indira Gandhi), effectively abolishing privy purses.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court held that the **Presidential Order was unconstitutional** because:
- It violated the agreements made at the time of merger
- The correct procedure would have been to amend the Constitution (Article 291 and 362), which had not been done

**Why It Matters:**
This is another example of Judicial Review being used to strike down an executive order. (Note: Parliament subsequently passed the **26th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1971** which formally abolished privy purses.)

---

### 🏛️ Case 5: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) ⭐ *Most Important*
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Bench:** 13-judge Constitutional Bench (the largest ever in Indian history)

**Background:**
Swami Kesavananda Bharati, head of a Hindu mutt (monastery) in Kerala, challenged the **Kerala Land Reforms Act** which restricted the management of his religious property. More broadly, this case became the platform to decide: **Can Parliament amend ANY part of the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights?**

**Judgment:**
By a thin 7:6 majority, the Supreme Court delivered one of the most important judgments in Indian legal history. It held:

- Parliament **can amend** any part of the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights (overruling Golaknath)
- **BUT**, Parliament **cannot destroy the Basic Structure** (also called Basic Features) of the Constitution
- **Judicial Review** was declared a **Basic Feature** of the Constitution

The "Basic Structure" was not given an exhaustive list, but was said to include features like:
- Supremacy of the Constitution
- Sovereign, democratic, republican nature
- Secular character
- Federal character
- Separation of powers
- Judicial Review

**Modern Connection to Ninth Schedule:**
The Court also ruled that acts included in the **Ninth Schedule are open to challenge if they violate the Basic Structure**. The date of this judgment — **April 24, 1973** — became a critical cutoff date.

**Why It Matters:**
This is arguably **the single most important case in Indian constitutional history**. It established the **Basic Structure Doctrine** which acts as an unbreakable limit on Parliament's amending power.

---

### 🏛️ Case 6: Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980) ⭐
**Court:** Supreme Court of India

**Background:**
The **Minerva Mills** in Karnataka was a textile company nationalised by the government under the Sick Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1974. The company challenged this. More importantly, this case challenged the **42nd Constitutional Amendment Act (1976)** — passed during the Emergency — which had:
- Declared Parliament's amending power to be **unlimited and unchallengeable**
- Made Directive Principles superior to Fundamental Rights

**Judgment:**
Chief Justice Y.V. Chandrachud, writing the judgment, struck down key provisions of the 42nd Amendment and held:

- **Judicial Review is a Basic Feature** of the Constitution (famously quoted in the chapter)
- Parliament's power to amend the Constitution is **limited**, not unlimited
- The **balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles** is itself a Basic Feature
- "Rights without remedies are as writ in water" — meaning Fundamental Rights are meaningless if courts cannot enforce them

**Why It Matters:**
It reaffirmed and strengthened Kesavananda Bharati. It also restored the balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles that the 42nd Amendment had disturbed.

---

### 🏛️ Case 7: A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Judge:** Chief Justice Kania

**Background:**
A.K. Gopalan, a communist leader, was detained under the **Preventive Detention Act, 1950**. He challenged his detention as a violation of his Fundamental Rights, particularly the right to personal liberty.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court upheld the detention, ruling that as long as a law followed the procedure prescribed, it was valid. Chief Justice Kania made the famous observation (quoted in the chapter) that **the Constitution is supreme and statute law must conform to it**, and it is for the judiciary to decide constitutional validity.

**Why It Matters:**
While the immediate outcome upheld the detention, the case established early foundational principles about the role of Judicial Review in India.

---

### 🏛️ Case 8: State of Madras v. V.G. Row (1952)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Judge:** Chief Justice Patanjali Shastri

**Background:**
The State of Madras banned an organisation called the "People's Education Society" under the Criminal Law Amendment Act. V.G. Row challenged the ban as unconstitutional.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court struck down the ban, holding that the law imposed **unreasonable restrictions on freedom of association** (Article 19). Chief Justice Shastri made the important observation (quoted in the chapter) that the Court is the **"sentinel on the qui vive"** (watchful guardian) of Fundamental Rights.

**Why It Matters:**
It established the principle of judicial scrutiny of restrictions on Fundamental Rights and the Court's role as a protector of citizens' freedoms.

---

### 🏛️ Case 9: State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Judge:** Justice P.N. Bhagwati

**Background:**
The Janata Party government at the Centre sought to dissolve nine State Legislative Assemblies (held by the Congress party). The State of Rajasthan challenged this in the Supreme Court.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court upheld the Centre's power but Justice Bhagwati made the landmark observation (quoted in the chapter) that **no authority, however high, can claim to be the sole judge of its own powers** under the Constitution, and that the Supreme Court is the ultimate interpreter.

**Why It Matters:**
It reinforced the supremacy of the Constitution and the role of Judicial Review in keeping all organs of government within their constitutional limits.

---

### 🏛️ Case 10: L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Judge:** Chief Justice A.M. Ahmadi

**Background:**
This case challenged the jurisdiction of **Administrative Tribunals** set up under the 42nd Amendment (Article 323A and 323B). The question was: Can these tribunals completely exclude the jurisdiction of High Courts?

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court held that:
- The power of **High Courts under Articles 226 and 227** (to issue writs) is a **Basic Feature** of the Constitution
- Administrative Tribunals **cannot completely exclude** the supervisory jurisdiction of High Courts
- Decisions of Tribunals are subject to review by High Courts

Chief Justice Ahmadi made the quoted observation about judges being "entrusted with the task of upholding the Constitution."

**Why It Matters:**
It protected the judicial review powers of High Courts and prevented the executive from creating parallel systems that bypass courts entirely.

---

### 🏛️ Case 11: S.S. Bola v. B.D. Sharma (1997)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Judge:** Justice Ramaswami

**Background:**
This case dealt with service matters and seniority issues in government employment.

**Judgment:**
Justice Ramaswami made the important observation (quoted in the chapter) about the **function of Judicial Review being part of constitutional interpretation itself**, and its role in adjusting the Constitution to meet new conditions and needs.

**Why It Matters:**
It articulates Judicial Review not just as a power, but as a **living constitutional tool** that helps the Constitution stay relevant over time.

---

### 🏛️ Case 12: Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India

**Background:**
Raj Narain challenged the election of Indira Gandhi from Rae Bareli constituency on grounds of electoral malpractice. The Allahabad High Court found her guilty and declared her election void. In response, the government passed the **39th Constitutional Amendment** which, among other things, placed disputes relating to elections of the President, Vice President, PM, and Speaker in a special category beyond court jurisdiction.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court upheld the High Court's finding but struck down the relevant clause of the 39th Amendment that immunised the PM's election from judicial scrutiny, holding it violated the **Basic Structure** (specifically, free and fair elections and rule of law).

**Why It Matters:**
It reaffirmed the Basic Structure doctrine in a highly political context and showed that even legislation designed to protect a specific individual could be struck down.

---

### 🏛️ Case 13: Waman Rao v. Union of India (1980)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India

**Background:**
This case challenged several land reform laws placed in the Ninth Schedule.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court clarified the Kesavananda Bharati ruling by holding that:
- Laws placed in the Ninth Schedule **before April 24, 1973** get full protection
- Laws placed **after April 24, 1973** are valid **only if they do not damage the Basic Structure**

**Why It Matters:**
It operationalised the April 24, 1973 cutoff date as a binding rule for Ninth Schedule challenges.

---

### 🏛️ Case 14: I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007) ⭐
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Bench:** 9-judge Constitutional Bench

**Background:**
I.R. Coelho challenged several Tamil Nadu state laws placed in the Ninth Schedule, arguing they violated Fundamental Rights.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court delivered a comprehensive ruling that **reaffirmed and expanded** the earlier positions. Key conclusions (as stated in the chapter):

1. A law placed in the Ninth Schedule that **abrogates or abridges Part III rights** must be tested for Basic Structure violation. The test is **"direct impact and effect"** — it's the **consequence** of the law, not its form, that matters.

2. The validity of each Constitutional Amendment placing a law in the Ninth Schedule must be **judged on its own merits** — using the **"impact test"**.

3. All Ninth Schedule amendments **on or after April 24, 1973** must be tested on the touchstone of **Article 21 read with Articles 14 and 19** and their underlying principles.

4. Protection for Ninth Schedule laws is **not blanket** — it must be justified by examining the **nature and extent of infraction** of Fundamental Rights using:
   - The **"Rights Test"** — does the law take away rights guaranteed under Articles 14, 15, 19, 21?
   - The **"Essence of the Right" Test** — does it destroy the core/essence of those rights?
   
   When the **"triangle"** of Articles 14, 19, and 21 is sought to be eliminated, **both tests** must apply.

5. Laws already upheld by the Supreme Court **cannot be challenged again** on these principles. But if a law held to violate Part III rights is **subsequently** placed in the Ninth Schedule after April 24, 1973, that inclusion can be challenged.

6. **Past transactions** completed under impugned Acts will **not be reopened**.

**Why It Matters:**
This is the definitive modern statement on Ninth Schedule immunity. It conclusively established that **no law has absolute immunity from Judicial Review** in India.

---

### 🏛️ Case 15: M. Nagaraj v. Union of India (2006)
**Court:** Supreme Court of India

**Background:**
This case dealt with reservations in promotions for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in government jobs, specifically the **77th, 81st, 82nd, and 85th Constitutional Amendments**.

**Judgment:**
The Supreme Court upheld the amendments but held that the State must collect **quantifiable data** on backwardness and inadequate representation before providing reservation in promotions. The case developed and applied the **"Essence of the Right" test** in the context of the Basic Structure doctrine.

**Why It Matters:**
It is referenced in the I.R. Coelho case as the source of the "Essence of the Right" test.

---

### 🏛️ Case 16: Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015) ⭐
**Court:** Supreme Court of India
**Bench:** 5-judge Constitutional Bench

**Background:**
The **99th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2014** and the **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) Act, 2014** sought to replace the existing **Collegium system** (where judges appoint judges) with a new body called the NJAC, which would include:
- The Chief Justice of India
- Two senior Supreme Court judges
- The Union Law Minister
- Two eminent persons (nominated jointly)

The Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association challenged this as violating judicial independence and the Basic Structure.

**Judgment:**
By a 4:1 majority, the Supreme Court:
- **Struck down** both the 99th Amendment and the NJAC Act as **unconstitutional and null and void**
- Held that the **independence of the judiciary** is a Basic Feature of the Constitution
- The inclusion of the Law Minister and eminent persons (with government influence) in judicial appointments would compromise judicial independence
- Restored the **Collegium system**

**Why It Matters:**
This is the most recent and high-profile use of Judicial Review in the chapter. It shows Judicial Review being used to strike down a Constitutional Amendment itself — the ultimate expression of the Basic Structure doctrine.

## 📋 Why Is Judicial Review Important?

There are three core reasons why Judicial Review is needed:

- **(a)** To uphold the principle of the **supremacy of the Constitution**
- **(b)** To maintain **federal equilibrium** (balance of power between the Centre and the States)
- **(c)** To **protect the Fundamental Rights** of citizens

### 📜 Key Judicial Observations on Importance

Here are the most important quotes from judges — memorise the case names they came from:

> **"In India it is the Constitution that is supreme and a statute law to be valid, must be in conformity with the constitutional requirements and it is for the judiciary to decide whether any enactment is constitutional or not."**
> — *Chief Justice Kania, A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950)*

> **"This is especially true as regards the Fundamental Rights, to which the court has been assigned the role of sentinel on the qui vive."** *(Watchful guardian)*
> — *Chief Justice Patanjali Shastri, State of Madras v. V.G. Row (1952)*

> **"As long as some Fundamental Rights exist and are a part of the Constitution, the power of judicial review has also to be exercised."**
> — *Justice Khanna, Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)*

> **"No one, however highly placed... can claim that it shall be the sole judge of the extent of its power under the Constitution."**
> — *Justice Bhagwati, State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)*

> **"Rights without remedies are as writ in water. A controlled Constitution will then become uncontrolled."**
> — *Chief Justice Chandrachud, Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980)*

> **"The judges of the Supreme Court have been entrusted with the task of upholding the Constitution... to ensure that the balance of power envisaged by the Constitution is maintained."**
> — *Chief Justice Ahmadi, L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997)*

> **"The function of judicial review is a part of the constitutional interpretation itself. It adjusts the Constitution to meet new conditions and needs of the time."**
> — *Justice Ramaswami, S.S. Bola v. B.D. Sharma (1997)*

## 📜 Constitutional Provisions for Judicial Review

> *Note: The phrase "Judicial Review" is NEVER explicitly mentioned in the Constitution. But many Articles together give courts this power.*

| Article | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| **Article 13** | Laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights are **null and void** |
| **Article 32** | Right to move Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights; SC can issue **writs** |
| **Article 131** | **Original jurisdiction** of SC in Centre-State and inter-state disputes |
| **Article 132** | **Appellate jurisdiction** of SC in **constitutional cases** |
| **Article 133** | **Appellate jurisdiction** of SC in **civil cases** |
| **Article 134** | **Appellate jurisdiction** of SC in **criminal cases** |
| **Article 134-A** | Certificate for appeal to SC from High Courts *(added by 44th Amendment, 1978)* |
| **Article 135** | SC can exercise jurisdiction of Federal Court under pre-Constitution law |
| **Article 136** | SC can grant **Special Leave to Appeal** from any court/tribunal *(except military tribunal/court martial)* |
| **Article 143** | President can seek **advisory opinion** of SC on questions of law or fact |
| **Article 226** | **High Courts** can issue writs for Fundamental Rights and **other purposes** |
| **Article 227** | High Courts have **superintendence** over all courts/tribunals in their territory *(except military courts)* |
| **Article 245** | Deals with **territorial extent** of laws made by Parliament and State Legislatures |
| **Article 246** | Deals with **subject matter** of laws (Union List, State List, Concurrent List) |
| **Articles 251 & 254** | In case of conflict between Central law and State law, **Central law prevails**; State law is void |
| **Article 372** | Continuance in force of **pre-Constitution laws** |

## 🔍 Scope of Judicial Review

### When Can a Law Be Challenged?

The constitutional validity of a legislative act or executive order can be challenged in the Supreme Court or High Courts on **three grounds**:

- **(a)** It **infringes Fundamental Rights** (Part III of the Constitution)
- **(b)** It is **outside the competence** of the authority that made it (e.g., a state law on a Union List subject)
- **(c)** It is **repugnant** (contradictory) to other constitutional provisions

---

### 🇮🇳 India vs. 🇺🇸 USA: Scope of Judicial Review Compared

India's scope is **narrower than the USA's**. Here's why:

| Feature | India | USA |
|---------|-------|-----|
| **Standard used** | **"Procedure Established by Law"** | **"Due Process of Law"** |
| **What courts check** | *Only* whether a law is within the authority's power (substantive validity) | Both whether a law is valid *AND* whether it is reasonable/fair (procedural validity too) |
| **Scope** | **Narrower** | **Wider** |

**Explanation of the Key Difference:**

- **"Due Process of Law"** (USA): Gives the US Supreme Court very wide power. It can strike down laws not only if they are unlawful but also if they are **unreasonable** in procedure. This is why critics call the US Supreme Court a **"third chamber" of the Legislature**, a **"superlegislature"**, or an **"arbiter of social policy"**.

- **"Procedure Established by Law"** (India): The Indian Supreme Court mainly examines whether a law is within the power of the authority concerned. It is **not expected to go into the question of its reasonableness, suitability, or policy implications**.

### 🇮🇳 India's Unique Position: A Synthesis

India does not follow either extreme:

- It does NOT fully follow the **American principle of judicial supremacy**
- It does NOT fully follow the **British principle of parliamentary supremacy** (where Parliament is absolutely sovereign)

India has several limits on Parliament's sovereignty:
- **Written Constitution**
- **Federalism** with division of powers
- **Fundamental Rights**
- **Judicial Review**

> ✅ **What India has is a SYNTHESIS** — a combination of both the American principle of judicial supremacy *and* the British principle of parliamentary supremacy.

## 📂 Judicial Review of the Ninth Schedule

### What is the Ninth Schedule?

The **Ninth Schedule** is a special list of laws that were originally intended to be **protected from being challenged in courts** on the ground that they violate Fundamental Rights.

- **Article 31B** saves acts in the Ninth Schedule from being challenged for violating any Fundamental Right
- **Article 31B and the Ninth Schedule** were added by the **1st Constitutional Amendment Act of 1951**
- **Originally (1951):** The Schedule had only **13 acts and regulations**
- **Currently:** It contains **282 acts and regulations** (last entry is numbered 284, but entries 87, 92, and 130 were deleted; one entry is numbered 257A)

**What types of laws are in it?**
- **State legislature laws:** Mainly deal with **land reforms and abolition of the zamindari system** (landlord system)
- **Parliament laws:** Deal with various other matters

### Is the Ninth Schedule Fully Immune? NO.

The evolution of this question happened across three key cases:

#### 🔹 Step 1: Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973)
The Supreme Court ruled that Ninth Schedule laws **can be challenged** if they violate the **Basic Structure of the Constitution**.

#### 🔹 Step 2: Waman Rao Case (1980)
The Court clarified: Laws placed in the Ninth Schedule **after April 24, 1973** (date of the Kesavananda Bharati judgment) are valid **only if they do not damage the Basic Structure**.

#### 🔹 Step 3: I.R. Coelho Case (2007) — The Definitive Ruling
The Supreme Court reaffirmed the above and laid down the final comprehensive rules (see detailed case explanation above).

**The Core Ruling of I.R. Coelho:**
- **No blanket immunity** from Judicial Review for Ninth Schedule laws
- Judicial Review is a **Basic Feature** — it cannot be taken away by putting a law in the Ninth Schedule
- Laws placed in the Ninth Schedule **after April 24, 1973** can be challenged if they violate rights under **Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21** or the **Basic Structure**

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *These connections are added to link the chapter to contemporary events.*

### 1. NJAC Judgment (2015) — Most Recent High-Profile Use of Judicial Review
`[Modern Example]` In **2015**, the Supreme Court used Judicial Review to strike down the **99th Constitutional Amendment** and the **NJAC Act** in the case **Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India**. This is the most dramatic recent example of Judicial Review nullifying a Constitutional Amendment, showing the Basic Structure doctrine in live action. The Court restored the Collegium system.

### 2. Judicial Review and Fundamental Rights — Puttuswamy Case (2017)
`[Modern Example]` In **Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)**, a 9-judge bench unanimously held that the **Right to Privacy** is a Fundamental Right under Article 21. This was a classic exercise of Judicial Review — the Court interpreted the Constitution to expand the scope of Fundamental Rights, affecting major government programs like **Aadhaar**.

### 3. Electoral Bonds Scheme — Judicial Review in Action (2024)
`[Modern Example]` In **Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (February 2024)**, the Supreme Court struck down the **Electoral Bonds Scheme** as unconstitutional, holding it violated voters' **Right to Information** (part of Article 19) and the principle of free and fair elections. This is a powerful recent example of Judicial Review striking down a government policy/scheme.

### 4. Ninth Schedule and Land Reforms — Contemporary Relevance
`[Modern Example]` Several state land reform laws continue to remain in the Ninth Schedule. Post the I.R. Coelho ruling, any new land-related legislation being pushed into the Ninth Schedule (like some amendments related to tribal land rights) must pass the **Basic Structure test** and can be challenged under Articles 14, 19, and 21.

### 5. Scope of Judicial Review — CAA Challenge
`[Modern Example]` The **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA), 2019** challenges currently pending before the Supreme Court are a live example of Judicial Review in action — petitioners argue the law violates **Article 14** (equality) and **Article 21** (right to life/dignity). The eventual ruling will be another major chapter in India's Judicial Review history.

| Concept | India | USA |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Judicial Review basis | **Explicitly in Constitution** | Derived by court (Marbury v. Madison) |
| Standard of review | "Procedure Established by Law" | "Due Process of Law" |
| Scope | Narrower | Wider |
| Can Judicial Review be removed? | **No — Basic Feature** | No established equivalent rule |
| Ninth Schedule immunity | **Partial — not blanket** | Not applicable |
