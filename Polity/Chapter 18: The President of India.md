# 📘 Chapter 18: The President of India
### (Articles 52–78 | Part V of the Constitution)

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. The **President is the constitutional head** of India — a nominal (in name only) executive, NOT the real one. Real power rests with the **Council of Ministers**.
> 2. The President is elected by an **indirect election** through an Electoral College — NOT directly by the people.
> 3. Parliament has enormous **legislative, financial, judicial, military, and emergency powers** exercised *through* the President.
> 4. The President holds **three types of veto powers** — Absolute, Suspensive, and Pocket — but **NOT a Qualified Veto**.
> 5. The President's **pardoning power** (Article 72) is independent of the judiciary but exercised on the advice of the Cabinet.

---

## 🏛️ Section 1: The Union Executive — Who's Who?

**Articles 52 to 78** under **Part V** of the Constitution deal with the **Union Executive**.

The Union Executive consists of:
- The **President**
- The **Vice-President**
- The **Prime Minister**
- The **Council of Ministers**
- The **Attorney General of India**

> The **President** is the **head of the Indian State**, the **first citizen of India**, and acts as the **symbol of unity, integrity and solidarity** of the nation.

---

## 🗳️ Section 2: Election of the President

### Who Elects the President?

The President is **NOT elected directly** by the people. Instead, he/she is elected by an **Electoral College** consisting of:

1. The **elected members** of both Houses of Parliament (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha)
2. The **elected members** of the Legislative Assemblies of the **States**
3. The **elected members** of the Legislative Assemblies of the **Union Territories of Delhi and Puducherry**
   *(This provision was added by the **70th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1992**, effective June 1, 1995)*

---

### ❌ Who Does NOT Participate in the Presidential Election?

The following categories are **excluded** from voting:

- **Nominated members** of both Houses of Parliament
- **Nominated members** of State Legislative Assemblies
- **Members (elected AND nominated)** of State **Legislative Councils** (the upper houses in bicameral states)
- **Nominated members** of the Legislative Assembly of Puducherry
- **Members of a dissolved Assembly** — even if fresh elections have not been held

> 📌 **Note on Anglo-Indian Nomination:** The **104th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2019** discontinued the special provision of nominating Anglo-Indian members to the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies. This provision **ceased to have effect on 25th January 2020**.

> 📌 The **Legislative Assembly of Delhi** has **no nominated members**.

---

### ⚖️ How Are Votes Calculated? (The Uniformity Formula)

The Constitution ensures **uniformity** in representation across states and **parity** between the states as a whole and the Union.

**Formula 1 — Value of Vote of Each MLA:**

Value of Vote of Each MLA = Population of the State (1971 Census) ───────────────────────────────────── Total Elected Members of the Assembly × 1000

> Only **multiples of 1000** are counted. Remainders of 500 or more are rounded up by adding 1.

**Formula 2 — Value of Vote of Each MP:**

Value of Vote of Each MP = Total Value of Votes of All State Legislative Assembly Members ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Total Elected Members of Both Houses of Parliament

> 📌 **Important:** According to the **84th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2001**, the word **"population"** means the population as per the **1971 Census**, until the figures from the **first census after 2026** are published.

### 🗳️ How Is the President Elected? (The Voting System)

The President's election uses:
- **Proportional Representation** by means of **Single Transferable Vote** (STV)
- **Secret Ballot** voting

**How it works — Step by Step:**

1. Each elector gets **one ballot paper** and marks preferences as **1, 2, 3, 4...** against candidates.
2. The voter can indicate **as many preferences as there are candidates**.
3. A candidate must secure a **fixed quota** (minimum required votes) to win.

**The Quota Formula:**

Quota = Total Valid Votes Polled ──────────────────────── + 1 (Number of Candidates + 1)

4. In **Phase 1**: First preference votes are counted. If a candidate reaches the quota → **declared elected**.
5. If not: The candidate with the **least first-preference votes is eliminated**. His/her **second-preference votes** are transferred to remaining candidates.
6. This process **continues** until one candidate reaches the quota.

---

### 🔍 Constituent Assembly Criticism — Why Indirect Election?

Some members of the Constituent Assembly called indirect election **undemocratic** and demanded direct election. The Constitution makers rejected this for the following reasons:

- **Reason 1 — Harmony with Parliamentary System:** India follows a **parliamentary system** where the President is only a **nominal executive**. It would be absurd to elect him/her directly by millions of voters but give him/her no real power.
- **Reason 2 — Cost and Impracticality:** Direct election would be enormously costly, time-consuming, and energy-draining given India's vast electorate — unwarranted for a **symbolic head**.
- **Reason 3 — Avoiding Party Dominance:** If only Parliament elected the President, a party with a majority would always pick its own candidate. The **current system** ensures the President represents **both the Union and the States equally**.

> 💡 **Terminological Note (from the Constituent Assembly itself):**
> - The term **'proportional representation'** is technically a **misnomer** here, because proportional representation is meant for filling **multiple seats** — here there is only **one seat**.
> - The term **'single transferable vote'** was also criticized — because no voter has *a single vote*; every voter has **plural preferences**.
> - It would more accurately be called a **"preferential"** or **"alternative vote system."**

---

### ⚖️ Disputes in Presidential Elections

- All doubts and disputes regarding the President's election are **inquired into and decided by the Supreme Court**, whose decision is **final**.
- The election **cannot be challenged** on the ground that the electoral college was **incomplete** (due to vacancies).
- If a President's election is **declared void** by the Supreme Court, all **acts done before that declaration remain valid**.

## 📜 Section 3: Qualifications, Oath, and Conditions of Office

### ✅ Qualifications to Become President

A person must fulfil **ALL** of the following:

1. Must be a **citizen of India**
2. Must have completed **35 years of age**
3. Must be qualified for election as a **member of the Lok Sabha**
4. Must **not hold any office of profit** under the Union, State, Local, or any other public authority

> **Exception:** The following are **not deemed** to hold an office of profit and are thus **eligible**:
> - Sitting **President or Vice-President**
> - **Governor** of any state
> - **Minister** of the Union or any state

**Additional Requirements:**
- Nomination must be subscribed by **at least 50 electors as proposers** and **50 electors as seconders**
- Candidate must deposit **₹15,000** as security deposit in the **Reserve Bank of India**
- Security deposit is **forfeited** if the candidate fails to secure **one-sixth of the votes polled**

> 📌 **Before 1997:** proposers/seconders were **10 each** and deposit was **₹2,500**. These were increased in 1997 via the *Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act of 1952, as amended in 1997*, to **discourage non-serious candidates**.

---

### 🙏 Oath or Affirmation by the President

Before entering office, the President swears to:

1. **Faithfully execute** the office of President
2. **Preserve, protect and defend** the Constitution and the law
3. **Devote himself/herself to the service and well-being** of the people of India

> The oath is administered by the **Chief Justice of India**, and in his/her absence, by the **seniormost judge of the Supreme Court available**.

---

### 📋 Conditions of the President's Office

The Constitution lays down the following conditions:

1. The President **cannot be a member** of either House of Parliament or any State Legislature. If elected while being a member, he/she is deemed to have **vacated that seat** on the date of assuming office.
2. The President **cannot hold any other office of profit**.
3. The President is entitled, **without payment of rent**, to use the official residence — **Rashtrapati Bhavan**.
4. The President is entitled to **emoluments, allowances, and privileges** as determined by Parliament.
5. **Emoluments and allowances cannot be diminished** during the term of office.

**Current Salary and Benefits:**
- **Salary:** ₹5 lakh per month *(increased from ₹1.50 lakh by Parliament in 2018, via the Finance Act 2018, effective 1st January 2016)*
- **Pension of Retired President:** 50% of salary per month *(increased from ₹3 lakh per annum in 2008)*
- **Former Presidents are also entitled to:**
  - Furnished residence
  - Phone facilities
  - Car
  - Medical treatment
  - Travel facility
  - Secretarial staff
  - Office expenses up to **₹1,00,000 per annum**

**Spouse of a Deceased President is entitled to:**
- Family pension at **50% of pension** of a retired President
- Furnished residence, phone, car, medical, travel, secretarial staff
- Office expenses up to **₹20,000 per annum**

---

### 🛡️ Immunities of the President

- **Personal immunity** from legal liability for **official acts**
- **Immune from criminal proceedings** during the term of office (even for personal acts)
- **Cannot be arrested or imprisoned** during term
- **Civil proceedings** for personal acts *can* be instituted, but only after giving **two months' notice**

---

## ⏳ Section 4: Term, Impeachment, and Vacancy

### Term of Office

- The President holds office for **5 years** from the date of assuming office.
- Can **resign** by addressing resignation to the **Vice-President**.
- Can be **removed** by **impeachment**.
- Can hold office **beyond 5 years** until the successor assumes charge (to prevent an **'interregnum'** — gap without a head of state).
- **Eligible for re-election** for any number of terms.
  > 📌 In the USA, a President **cannot be elected more than twice**. In India, there is **no such limit**. *(Only **Dr. Rajendra Prasad** has served two terms.)*

---

### ⚖️ Impeachment of the President

The President can be removed for **"violation of the Constitution"**.

> 📌 The Constitution **does NOT define** what "violation of the Constitution" means.

**The Impeachment Process — Step by Step:**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **Step 1** | Charges can be **initiated by either House** of Parliament |
| **Step 2** | Charges must be **signed by ¼ (one-fourth) of the members** of that House |
| **Step 3** | A **14-day notice** must be given to the President |
| **Step 4** | The **initiating House passes** the impeachment resolution by **2/3rd majority of its total membership** |
| **Step 5** | The resolution goes to the **other House**, which **investigates** the charges |
| **Step 6** | The President has the **right to appear and be represented** during this investigation |
| **Step 7** | If the **other House also passes** the resolution by **2/3rd majority of its total membership** → President is **removed from the date of that resolution** |

> **Impeachment is a quasi-judicial procedure** (part legal, part political process in Parliament).

**Two Special Points:**
- **Nominated members of Parliament CAN participate** in impeachment — even though they **cannot vote** in the President's election.
- **Elected MLAs of States and UTs of Delhi & Puducherry CANNOT participate** in impeachment — even though they **can vote** in the election.

> 📌 **No President has ever been impeached in India** so far.

---

### 🔄 Vacancy in the President's Office

A vacancy can occur due to:

1. **Expiry** of the 5-year term
2. **Resignation**
3. **Removal** by impeachment
4. **Death** *(Two Presidents have died in office — **Dr. Zakir Husain** in 1969 and **Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed** in 1977)*
5. **Otherwise** — e.g., disqualification or election declared void

**Rules for Filling Vacancies:**

| Type of Vacancy | Timeline | Who Acts as President? |
|---|---|---|
| Term expiry | Election must be held **before** expiry | Outgoing President continues till successor takes charge |
| Resignation/Removal/Death | Election within **6 months** | **Vice-President** acts as President |
| VP also vacant | — | **Chief Justice of India** acts as President |
| CJI also vacant | — | **Seniormost judge of Supreme Court** acts as President |

> 📌 **Historical Example:** When President Dr. Zakir Husain died (May 1969), VP V.V. Giri became acting President. When Giri resigned to contest the election, **Chief Justice M. Hidayatullah** served as officiating President from **20 July 1969 to 24 August 1969**.

> The newly elected President serves a **full fresh term of 5 years** from the date of assuming charge.

> Any person acting as President **enjoys all powers, immunities, and entitled emoluments** of the President.

## 💼 Section 5: Powers and Functions of the President

The President's powers are classified under **7 heads**:

---

### 1. 🔧 Executive Powers

**(a)** All executive actions of the Government of India are **formally taken in the President's name**.

**(b)** Can make **rules for authentication** of orders and instruments made in his/her name.

**(c)** Can make **rules for the transaction of government business** and allocation of business among ministers.

**(d)** **Appoints the Prime Minister** and other ministers (who hold office during the President's pleasure).

**(e)** **Appoints the Attorney General of India** and determines his/her remuneration. The AG holds office at the President's pleasure.

**(f)** Appoints the following key constitutional authorities:
- **Comptroller and Auditor General of India**
- **Chief Election Commissioner** and other Election Commissioners
- **Chairman and Members of the Union Public Service Commission**
- **Governors of States**
- **Chairman and Members of the Finance Commission**
- And others

**(g)** Can **seek information** relating to administration of Union affairs and proposals for legislation from the Prime Minister.

**(h)** Can require the PM to submit, for consideration of the **full Council of Ministers**, any matter on which a minister has taken a decision **without the Council's consideration**.

**(i)** Can **appoint a commission** to investigate into the conditions of **backward classes**.

**(j)** Can **appoint an Inter-State Council** to promote Centre-State and inter-State cooperation.

**(k)** **Directly administers Union Territories** through administrators appointed by him/her.

**(l)** Can declare any area as a **Scheduled Area** and has powers regarding **Scheduled Areas and Tribal Areas**.

---

### 2. 📜 Legislative Powers

The President is an **integral part of the Parliament** and has the following legislative powers:

**(a)** Can **summon or prorogue** Parliament and **dissolve the Lok Sabha**. Can also summon a **joint sitting** of both Houses (presided over by the **Speaker of the Lok Sabha**).

**(b)** Can **address Parliament** at the commencement of the first session after each general election and the first session of each year.

**(c)** Can **send messages** to the Houses of Parliament regarding pending bills or otherwise.

**(d)** Can **appoint a member of Lok Sabha** to preside over its proceedings if both the Speaker and Deputy Speaker posts fall vacant. Similarly for **Rajya Sabha** when both Chairman and Deputy Chairman posts fall vacant.

**(e)** **Nominates 12 members** of the Rajya Sabha from persons with special knowledge or practical experience in **literature, science, art, and social service**.

**(f)** **Nominated 2 Anglo-Indian members** to the Lok Sabha — but this was **discontinued by the 104th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2019**.

**(g)** **Decides on disqualification** of members of Parliament, in consultation with the **Election Commission**.

**(h)** **Prior recommendation/permission** of the President is required to introduce the following bills in Parliament:
   - A bill involving expenditure from the **Consolidated Fund of India**
   - A bill for **alteration of state boundaries or creation of a new state**
   - A **money bill**
   - A bill that **imposes or varies any tax/duty in which states are interested**
   - A bill varying the meaning of **'agricultural income'** for income tax purposes
   - A bill **affecting the principles of revenue distribution to states**
   - A bill imposing any **surcharge on any specified tax or duty**
   - A **state bill** imposing restrictions on freedom of trade/commerce (needs Presidential sanction even before the state legislature)

**(i)** When a bill passed by Parliament is sent to the President, he/she may:
   - **Give assent** → Bill becomes law
   - **Withhold assent** → Bill does not become law (Absolute Veto)
   - **Return the bill** *(only if it is NOT a Money Bill)* for reconsideration → If Parliament passes it again (with or without amendments), the President **must give assent** (Suspensive Veto)

**(j)** When a bill passed by a **State Legislature** is reserved by the Governor for the President's consideration:
   - **Give assent** → Bill becomes law
   - **Withhold assent** → Bill does not become law
   - **Direct Governor to return** *(only if NOT a Money Bill)* for reconsideration
   - If the state legislature passes it again and sends it back to the President → the President is **NOT bound** to give assent (unlike in the case of Central legislation). The **state legislature CANNOT override the President's veto**.

**(k)** Can **promulgate Ordinances** when Parliament is not in session. These must be approved by Parliament within **6 weeks** of its reassembly. Can also **withdraw** an ordinance at any time.

**(l)** Lays the following reports before Parliament:
   - Report of the **Comptroller and Auditor General**
   - Report of the **Union Public Service Commission**
   - Report of the **Finance Commission**
   - And others

**(m)** Can make **regulations** for peace, progress and good government of:
   - Andaman and Nicobar Islands
   - Lakshadweep
   - Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu
   - Ladakh
   - Puducherry *(only when its assembly is suspended or dissolved)*

---

### 3. 💰 Financial Powers

**(a)** **Money bills** can only be introduced in Parliament with the **President's prior recommendation**.

**(b)** Causes the **Union Budget (Annual Financial Statement)** to be laid before Parliament.

**(c)** **No demand for a grant** can be made except on the President's recommendation.

**(d)** Can make **advances out of the Contingency Fund of India** to meet unforeseen expenditure.

**(e)** **Constitutes a Finance Commission** every 5 years to recommend the distribution of revenues between the Centre and the States.

---

### 4. ⚖️ Judicial Powers

**(a)** **Appoints** the Chief Justice and judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts.

**(b)** Can seek **advisory opinion from the Supreme Court** on any question of law or fact. However, the Supreme Court's advice is **NOT binding** on the President.

**(c)** Can **grant pardon, reprieve, respite and remission** of punishment or **suspend, remit or commute** the sentence of any person convicted of:
   - An offence against a **Union Law**
   - A punishment/sentence by a **Court Martial** (military court)
   - A **death sentence**

*(Detailed explanation of pardoning powers is in Section 7 below.)*

---

### 5. 🌐 Diplomatic Powers

- International **treaties and agreements** are negotiated and concluded on behalf of the President, **subject to Parliamentary approval**.
- **Represents India** in international forums.
- **Sends and receives diplomats** — ambassadors, high commissioners, etc.

---

### 6. 🎖️ Military Powers

- The President is the **Supreme Commander of the Defence Forces of India**.
- **Appoints** the Chiefs of the Army, Navy, and Air Force.
- Can **declare war or conclude peace**, subject to Parliamentary approval.

---

### 7. 🚨 Emergency Powers

The Constitution gives the President **extraordinary powers** to deal with three types of emergencies:

#### (a) National Emergency — Article 352

Can be proclaimed on the grounds of:
- **War**
- **External Aggression**
- **Armed Rebellion**

During a National Emergency, the President acquires:
- Power to **modify the distribution of financial resources** between Union and States
- Power to **suspend Fundamental Rights** of citizens — EXCEPT:
  - **Article 21** (Right to Life and Personal Liberty)
  - **Article 20** (Protection against conviction for offences)

#### (b) President's Rule — Articles 356 & 365

Also called **State Emergency** or **Constitutional Emergency**. Can be proclaimed on:
- **Failure of constitutional machinery** in a state (Article 356)
- **Failure to comply with or give effect to Union's directions** (Article 365)

During President's Rule, the President can:
- **Assign to himself all functions** of the state government and powers of the Governor
- Declare that the powers of the **state legislature shall be exercised by Parliament**
- Authorise expenditure from the **Consolidated Fund of the State** (when Lok Sabha is not in session), pending Parliamentary sanction

#### (c) Financial Emergency — Article 360

Can be proclaimed if the **financial stability or credit of India** or any part thereof is **threatened**.

During a Financial Emergency, the President can:
- Issue directions for **reduction of salaries and allowances** of all persons serving the Union — including **judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts**

## 🚫 Section 6: Veto Power of the President

> **'Veto'** is a Latin word meaning **'I forbid'**.

A bill passed by Parliament becomes an act **only** if it receives the **President's assent** (under **Article 111**).

### Types of Veto Power in Modern States

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Absolute Veto** | Permanently withhold assent — bill is dead |
| **Qualified Veto** | Can be overridden by the legislature with a **higher majority** |
| **Suspensive Veto** | Can be overridden by the legislature with an **ordinary majority** |
| **Pocket Veto** | Take **no action** — bill is kept pending indefinitely |

### What Vetos Does the Indian President Have?

> The Indian President has **THREE** types of veto: **Absolute, Suspensive, and Pocket**.
> The Indian President does **NOT** have a **Qualified Veto** — that is held by the **American President**.

---

### 🔴 1. Absolute Veto

The President **permanently withholds assent** — the bill dies and does not become an act.

**Typically used in two situations:**
- Bills introduced by **private members** (non-ministers)
- **Government bills** where the Cabinet resigned after passage but before Presidential assent, and the **new Cabinet advises against assent**

**Real Examples:**

> 🗂️ **Example 1 (1954):** President **Dr. Rajendra Prasad** withheld assent to the **PEPSU Appropriation Bill**. The bill was passed when President's Rule was in operation in PEPSU. But by the time it reached the President for assent, President's Rule had been revoked. *(PEPSU = Patiala and East Punjab States Union, merged into Punjab in 1956)*

> 🗂️ **Example 2 (1991):** President **R. Venkataraman** withheld assent to the **Salary, Allowances and Pension of Members of Parliament (Amendment) Bill**. The bill was passed by Parliament on the last day before dissolution of the Lok Sabha, **without obtaining the President's prior recommendation** as required.

---

### 🟡 2. Suspensive Veto

The President **returns the bill for reconsideration**. If Parliament passes it **again** (with or without amendments), the President **must give assent**.

- The veto is overridden by the **same ordinary majority** (unlike USA, where a higher 2/3rd majority is needed).
- The President **does NOT have this veto** for **Money Bills** — he/she can only assent or withhold assent on a Money Bill. (Normally assents because it was introduced with his/her prior permission.)

---

### 🟢 3. Pocket Veto

The President **neither approves, nor rejects, nor returns** the bill — he/she simply **keeps it pending** for an indefinite period.

- This is possible because the **Constitution prescribes NO time limit** within which the President must act on a bill.
- In the **USA**, the President must return the bill within **10 days** — so the Indian President's pocket is said to be **"bigger than the American President's pocket."**

**Real Example:**

> 🗂️ **Example (1986):** President **Zail Singh** exercised the Pocket Veto on the **Indian Post Office (Amendment) Bill** — a bill passed by the **Rajiv Gandhi Government** that imposed restrictions on the **freedom of the press** and was widely criticized.
> After 3 years, in **1989**, the next President **R. Venkataraman** sent it back for reconsideration. The new **National Front Government** then decided to **drop the bill entirely**.

---

### 🚫 No Veto on Constitutional Amendment Bills

The President has **NO veto power** on **Constitutional Amendment Bills**. The **24th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1971** made it **obligatory** for the President to give assent to a constitutional amendment bill.

---

### 📊 Veto Power at a Glance (Table 18.2)

| | **Central Legislation** | **State Legislation** |
|---|---|---|
| **Ordinary Bills** | Can be ratified / rejected / returned | Can be ratified / rejected / returned |
| **Money Bills** | Can be ratified / rejected **(cannot be returned)** | Can be ratified / rejected **(cannot be returned)** |
| **Constitutional Amendment Bills** | **Can ONLY be ratified** (cannot reject or return) | Constitutional amendment bills cannot be introduced in state legislatures |

## 📋 Section 7: Ordinance-Making Power of the President

**Article 123** empowers the President to **promulgate ordinances** during the **recess of Parliament** (when Parliament is not in session).

> Ordinances have the **same force and effect as an Act of Parliament** but are **temporary** in nature.
> This is the **most important legislative power** of the President.

---

### ⛔ Four Limitations on Ordinance-Making Power

**Limitation 1 — Both Houses must NOT be in session:**
- An ordinance can be promulgated **only when both Houses of Parliament are NOT in session**, or when **either one House is not in session**.
- Even if **only one House** is in session, an ordinance can be issued — because a law requires **both Houses** to pass it.
- An ordinance made when **both Houses are in session is VOID**.
- This power is **NOT a parallel power of legislation** — it is a substitute only when Parliament cannot function.

**Limitation 2 — President must be "satisfied" of urgency:**
- The President can make an ordinance only when **circumstances exist necessitating immediate action**.

> 🏛️ **Cooper Case (1970) — Cooper vs. Union of India:**
> The Supreme Court held that the **President's satisfaction CAN be questioned in court** on the ground of **malafide** (bad faith).
> This means if the President **prorogued Parliament deliberately** to bypass a controversial issue and issue an ordinance instead, that can be challenged.
>
> **Important amendment history on this point:**
> - The **38th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1975** (Indira Gandhi government) made the President's satisfaction **final, conclusive, and beyond judicial review**.
> - The **44th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1978** (Janata Party/Morarji Desai government) **deleted** this provision.
> - Therefore, today, the **President's satisfaction IS justiciable** (can be examined by courts) on the ground of malafide.

**Limitation 3 — Subject matter must be within Parliament's legislative competence:**
- An ordinance can be issued **only on subjects on which Parliament can legislate**.
- An ordinance is subject to the **same constitutional limitations** as a Parliamentary Act.
- Therefore, an **ordinance cannot abridge or take away Fundamental Rights**.
  *(Article 13 expressly includes 'ordinances' in the definition of 'law'.)*

**Limitation 4 — Must be approved by Parliament within 6 weeks:**
- Every ordinance **must be laid before both Houses** when Parliament reassembles.
- If Parliament **approves it** → it becomes an Act.
- If Parliament **takes no action** → ordinance ceases to operate after **6 weeks** from reassembly.
- If **both Houses pass resolutions disapproving it** → ordinance ceases even before 6 weeks.
- If Houses reassemble on **different dates** → 6 weeks counted from the **later date**.

> 📌 **Maximum Life of an Ordinance:**
> The maximum gap between two Parliament sessions is **6 months**.
> Adding the 6-week approval window = **maximum life of an ordinance is approximately 6 months and 6 weeks**.

**Additional Points about Ordinances:**
- If an ordinance **lapses without being placed before Parliament**, all acts done under it before it ceases remain **fully valid**.
- The President **can withdraw** an ordinance at any time.
- Ordinance-making is **NOT a discretionary power** — it is exercised **only on the advice of the Council of Ministers** headed by the PM.
- An ordinance **can be retrospective** (come into force from a past date).
- Can **modify or repeal** any Act of Parliament or another ordinance.
- Can **alter or amend a tax law**.
- **CANNOT be issued** to **amend the Constitution**.

---

### 🧠 Why Does India Have Ordinance-Making Power?

> Dr. B.R. Ambedkar explained in the Constituent Assembly *(Debates, Volume VIII, p. 213)*:
> *"The mechanism of issuing an ordinance has been devised to enable the Executive to deal with a situation that may suddenly and immediately arise when the Parliament is not in session."*

> 📌 The ordinance-making power has **no connection** with **National Emergency under Article 352**. The President can issue ordinances even when there is **no war, external aggression, or armed rebellion**.

> 📌 **Lok Sabha Rules:** Whenever a bill seeking to **replace an ordinance** is introduced in the House, a statement explaining the **circumstances** that necessitated the ordinance must be placed before the House.

---

### 🏛️ Key Court Case: D.C. Wadhwa vs. State of Bihar (1986)

> **Facts:** Between **1967 and 1981**, the **Governor of Bihar** promulgated **256 ordinances**. These were kept alive for periods ranging from **1 to 14 years** by **re-promulgation** (issuing the same ordinance again and again) without ever attempting to pass them as laws through the state legislature.

> **Supreme Court Ruling:**
> - **Successive re-promulgation** of ordinances with the same text, without any attempt to get the bills passed by the legislature, amounts to a **violation of the Constitution**.
> - Such re-promulgated ordinances are **liable to be struck down**.
> - The **exceptional power of ordinance-making CANNOT be used as a substitute** for the legislature's lawmaking power.

## 🙏 Section 8: Pardoning Power of the President

**Article 72** empowers the President to grant pardons to persons who have been **tried and convicted** of any offence in cases where:

1. Punishment/sentence is for an **offence against a Union Law**
2. Punishment/sentence is by a **Court Martial** (military court)
3. Sentence is a **death sentence**

> The pardoning power is **independent of the Judiciary**. The President does **not** sit as a court of appeal. The object is:
> **(a)** To correct **judicial errors** in the operation of law
> **(b)** To provide **relief from sentences** the President considers unduly harsh

---

### Types of Pardoning Power

| Type | Meaning | Simple Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Pardon** | Removes sentence AND conviction completely | The convict is fully absolved — as if the crime never happened |
| **Commutation** | Substitutes one form of punishment for a **lighter form** | E.g., death sentence → rigorous imprisonment → simple imprisonment |
| **Remission** | Reduces the **period** of sentence without changing its character | E.g., 2 years rigorous imprisonment → 1 year rigorous imprisonment |
| **Respite** | Awards a **lesser sentence** due to special circumstances | E.g., physical disability or pregnancy of a woman offender |
| **Reprieve** | **Temporary stay** of execution (especially death sentence) | Gives the convict time to seek pardon or commutation from the President |

---

### Governor's Pardoning Power vs. President's Pardoning Power

Under **Article 161**, the **Governor of a state** also has pardoning power for offences against **state laws**.

| Feature | **President** | **Governor** |
|---|---|---|
| Pardon for **Court Martial sentences** | ✅ Yes | ❌ No |
| Pardon for **death sentences** | ✅ Yes | ❌ No (cannot pardon death sentence) |
| Suspend/Remit/Commute **death sentence** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes (concurrent power) |

> 📌 Even if a **state law prescribes death sentence**, the power to grant **pardon** lies with the **President, NOT the Governor**. However, the Governor CAN suspend, remit, or commute a death sentence.
> Both the President and Governor have **concurrent power** for suspension, remission, and commutation of death sentences.

---

### 🏛️ Key Court Cases on Pardoning Power

---

#### 📌 Case 1: Maru Ram vs. Union of India (1980)

> **Issue:** Whether the President's pardoning power is exercised personally or on ministerial advice?

> **Supreme Court Held:**
> 1. The pardoning power **must be exercised on the advice of the Union Cabinet** — not personally by the President.
> 2. The exercise of pardoning power **is subject to judicial review** — but only in limited circumstances (where the decision is irrational, malafide, or discriminatory).

---

#### 📌 Case 2: Kehar Singh vs. Union of India (1988)

> **Background:** Kehar Singh was convicted and sentenced to death for his role in the **assassination of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi** (1984). He filed a mercy petition before the President. When his plea was rejected, he challenged the President's power in the Supreme Court.

> **Issue:** What are the limits and nature of the President's pardoning power under Article 72?

> **Supreme Court Held (5 key principles):**
>
> 1. The **petitioner for mercy has NO right to an oral hearing** before the President.
> 2. The **President CAN re-examine the evidence** and take a view **different from the court's view**.
> 3. The power **must be exercised on the advice of the Union Cabinet** (not personally).
> 4. There is **no need for the Supreme Court to lay down specific guidelines** for how the President should exercise this power.
> 5. The exercise of Presidential pardoning power **is subject to judicial review** ONLY where the decision is:
>    - **Irrelevant**
>    - **Irrational**
>    - **Malafide** (in bad faith)
>    - **Discriminatory**

> 📌 **Kehar Singh confirmed and built upon the Maru Ram ruling.**

## 👑 Section 9: Constitutional Position of the President

India has a **Parliamentary form of government**. Therefore:

> The President is a **nominal executive** (executive in name only).
> The **real executive** is the **Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister**.

---

### Dr. Ambedkar's Explanation (Constituent Assembly Debates, Volume VII, pp. 32–34)

> *"The President of the Indian Union will be generally bound by the advice of his ministers. He can do nothing contrary to their advice nor can he do anything without their advice. The President of the United States can dismiss any secretary at any time. The President of the Indian Union has no power to do so, so long as his ministers command a majority in Parliament."*

**The Key Contrast:**

| Feature | **India** | **USA** |
|---|---|---|
| System | **Parliamentary** | **Presidential** |
| Executive Head | **Prime Minister** (real) | **President** (real) |
| President's Role | **Symbolic/Nominal head** | **Chief Executive** |
| Position of President | Like the **King in English Constitution** | Chief head of administration |

> *"He represents the nation but does not rule the nation. He is the symbol of the nation. His place in administration is that of a ceremonial device or a seal by which the nation's decisions are made known."* — Dr. Ambedkar

---

### Key Constitutional Articles Governing the President's Position

1. **Article 53** — The executive power of the Union shall be **vested in the President** and exercised by him/her directly or through officers.

2. **Article 74** — There shall be a **Council of Ministers with the PM at the head** to aid and advise the President, who **'shall' act in accordance** with such advice.

3. **Article 75** — The Council of Ministers shall be **collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha** — the foundation of the parliamentary system.

---

### History of Article 74 — Evolution of Presidential Advice

| Amendment | Government | Change Made |
|---|---|---|
| **Original Constitution** | — | No specific provision making President bound by ministerial advice |
| **42nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1976** | Indira Gandhi Government | Made the President **bound by the advice** of the Council of Ministers |
| **44th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1978** | Janata Party/Morarji Desai | Added that the President **may return the matter ONCE** for reconsideration. But **reconsidered advice is binding**. |

---

### Real Examples of President Returning Cabinet Advice

> 🗂️ **Example 1 (October 1997):** The Cabinet recommended President **K.R. Narayanan** to impose **President's Rule in Uttar Pradesh**. The President returned the matter for reconsideration. The Cabinet then **decided not to proceed**. This saved the **BJP-led Kalyan Singh government**.

> 🗂️ **Example 2 (September 1998):** President **K.R. Narayanan** returned a Cabinet recommendation seeking **President's Rule in Bihar**. The Cabinet re-advised after a couple of months. Only then was President's Rule imposed in Bihar in **February 1999**.

---

### Presidential Discretion — When Can the President Act Without Ministerial Advice?

Though the President has **no constitutional discretion**, he/she has some **situational discretion** in the following cases:

1. **Appointment of Prime Minister** — when no party has a clear majority in the Lok Sabha, or when the sitting PM dies suddenly with no obvious successor.
2. **Dismissal of the Council of Ministers** — when it cannot prove the confidence of the Lok Sabha.
3. **Dissolution of the Lok Sabha** — when the Council of Ministers has lost its majority.

### 🔹 India's First Tribal Woman President (2022)

Droupadi Murmu** was elected as India's **15th President** in July 2022, becoming the **first tribal woman** and the **second woman** (after Pratibha Patil) to hold the office. ### 🔹 Ordinance Raj — A Continuing Debate
`[Modern Example]` The use of ordinances has remained a contested issue. Between **2014 and 2022**, the Central Government issued several key ordinances including:
- **The Farmers' Ordinances (2020)** — later passed as laws and subsequently **repealed in 2021** after widespread protests. This was a landmark instance where ordinances were used for significant economic reform, sparking the **year-long farmers' protest**.
- **The Jammu & Kashmir Reorganisation (Passing of Bills) Amendment Ordinance, 2021** — promulgated to address governance issues in the new UT.
The **D.C. Wadhwa principle** (no re-promulgation as a substitute for legislation) continues to be cited in debates on executive overreach.

### 🔹 Presidential Pardon and Death Penalty — An Active Area
`[Modern Example]` The pardoning power under Article 72 continues to be litigated:
- In **Shatrughan Chauhan vs. Union of India (2014)**, the Supreme Court held that **unexplained and inordinate delay** in disposing of mercy petitions can be a ground to commute a death sentence. This refined the **Kehar Singh** and **Maru Ram** principles.
- As of 2023, mercy petitions from several death row convicts remain pending before the President — highlighting the importance of **timely exercise** of pardoning power.

---

### 🔹 104th Constitutional Amendment — End of Anglo-Indian Nomination (2020)
`[Modern Example]` The **104th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2019** discontinued the **reserved seats and nomination** of Anglo-Indian members to the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies, effective **25th January 2020**. This directly affected the composition of the **electoral college** for Presidential elections, as nominated members do not participate in the election.

---

### 🔹 President's Situational Discretion — Hung Parliament Scenarios
`[Modern Example]` The President's discretionary power in appointing a PM during a hung parliament (no party with clear majority) is increasingly relevant in India's coalition era. The **2019 Maharashtra political crisis** and the **2023 Karnataka election** both involved intense negotiations over government formation, underlining how the Governor (and by extension, the President's framework) must exercise discretion carefully while inviting the party most likely to command a majority — as laid down by the Supreme Court in **S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)**.
