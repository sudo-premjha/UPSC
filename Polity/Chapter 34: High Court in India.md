# 📚 Chapter 34: High Court in India
### Based on M. Laxmikanth's Indian Polity, 8th Edition

---

## 🗂️ Chapter at a Glance (Top 5 Takeaways)

> 1. **High Courts** sit at the apex of state judicial administration, operating below the Supreme Court but above all subordinate courts.
> 2. Judges are appointed by the **President** through the **Collegium System** — a consultation process involving senior judges, not politicians.
> 3. A High Court has **7 types of jurisdiction**: Original, Writ, Appellate, Supervisory, Control over Subordinate Courts, Court of Record, and Judicial Review.
> 4. **Article 226** gives High Courts the power to issue writs — and this power is *wider* than even the Supreme Court's writ power.
> 5. Four landmark cases — the **2nd, 3rd, 4th Judges Cases** and the **Chandra Kumar Case** — fundamentally shape how High Court judges are appointed and how writ jurisdiction works.

---

## 🏛️ Section 1: Introduction — What is a High Court?

India has a **single, integrated judicial system**. Think of it as a pyramid:
- **Supreme Court** → at the top
- **High Courts** → in the middle (one for each state)
- **Subordinate Courts** → at the bottom (district courts, magistrate courts, etc.)

The **High Court** is the **highest court of judicial administration in a state**.

### 📅 Historical Origin

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1862** | First three High Courts set up at **Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras** under the Indian High Courts Act, 1861 |
| **1866** | Fourth High Court established at **Allahabad** |
| **After 1950** | Each province's High Court became the High Court of the corresponding **state** |

### 🗺️ How Many High Courts Exist Today?

- Currently, India has **25 High Courts**.
- The **Seventh Amendment Act of 1956** allowed Parliament to create a **common High Court** for two or more states, or for states and union territories together.
- The **territorial jurisdiction** (the geographic area a court has authority over) of a High Court is **co-terminus** (exactly matching in boundary) with the territory of its state(s).

### 🏙️ Union Territories and High Courts

- Among the **8 Union Territories**, only **Delhi** has its own separate High Court (since **1966**).
- **Jammu & Kashmir** and **Ladakh** share a **common High Court**.
- All other Union Territories fall under the jurisdiction of nearby state High Courts.
- Parliament can **extend or exclude** the jurisdiction of a High Court over any Union Territory.

### 📜 Constitutional Provisions

**Articles 214 to 231** in **Part VI** of the Constitution deal with the organisation, independence, jurisdiction, powers, and procedures of High Courts.

## 👨‍⚖️ Section 2: Composition and Appointment of Judges

### Who Sits in a High Court?

Every High Court consists of:
1. A **Chief Justice**
2. **Other Judges** — as many as the **President** thinks necessary from time to time

> 💡 **Key Point:** The Constitution does **NOT** fix the number of judges in a High Court. The President determines the strength based on the **workload** of the court.

---

### 🔑 How Are Judges Appointed?

All judges are appointed by the **President of India**, but the process is different for the Chief Justice vs. other judges.

#### For Chief Justice of a High Court:
The President consults:
- The **Chief Justice of India (CJI)**
- The **Governor of the concerned state**
- *(In case of a common High Court: Governors of **all** concerned states)*

#### For Other Judges of a High Court:
The President consults:
- The **Chief Justice of India (CJI)**
- The **Governor of the concerned state**
- The **Chief Justice of the concerned High Court**

---

### ⚖️ The Landmark Cases on Appointment (Read These Carefully!)

These four cases are absolutely critical for UPSC. Each one changed how judges are appointed.

---

#### 📌 Case 1: The Second Judges Case (1993)
**Full Name:** *Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India (1993)*

**Background:**
Before this case, there was a dispute about what "consultation" with the Chief Justice of India meant during judicial appointments. Did the government *have* to follow the CJI's advice, or was it just a formality?

**What the Court Ruled:**
The Supreme Court ruled that **no appointment of a High Court judge can be made unless it is in conformity with the opinion of the Chief Justice of India.** In other words, the CJI's opinion is **binding**, not merely advisory.

**Impact:**
This ruling effectively shifted the power of judicial appointments away from the executive (government) and towards the judiciary itself. This was the foundation of what we now call the **Collegium System**.

---

#### 📌 Case 2: The Third Judges Case (1998)
**Full Name:** *In re Presidential Reference (1998)*

**Background:**
After the Second Judges Case, a new question arose: What does "consultation with the CJI" actually mean? Can the CJI alone decide, or must he consult other judges too? The President sent a reference (a formal question) to the Supreme Court under **Article 143** asking for clarification.

**What the Court Ruled:**
The Supreme Court opined that for **appointing** High Court judges, the CJI must consult a **collegium** (a group) of the **two senior-most judges** of the Supreme Court. For **transferring** High Court judges, the CJI must consult a collegium of **four senior-most judges** of the Supreme Court, **plus** the Chief Justices of both the High Courts involved (the one the judge is leaving and the one receiving the judge).

**Impact:**
This case firmly established that the **sole opinion of the CJI alone** is NOT sufficient. It must be a collective decision of the collegium. The **Collegium System** was thus fully formalised.

---

#### 📌 Case 3: The Fourth Judges Case (2015)
**Full Name:** *Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India (2015)*

**Background:**
The **99th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2014** and the **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) Act of 2014** were passed by Parliament to **replace** the Collegium System with a new body called the **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC)**. The NJAC would have included:
- The Chief Justice of India (Chairperson)
- Two senior-most SC judges
- The Union Law Minister
- Two eminent persons (chosen by a committee)

The inclusion of the Law Minister and eminent persons was seen by many as giving the **executive** a say in judicial appointments, potentially threatening judicial independence.

**What the Court Ruled:**
The Supreme Court, by a **4:1 majority**, declared **both** the 99th Constitutional Amendment Act **and** the NJAC Act as **unconstitutional and void**. The Court opined that the NJAC system would affect the **independence of the judiciary**, which is part of the **Basic Structure** of the Constitution (meaning it cannot be altered even by a constitutional amendment).

**Impact:**
The **Collegium System was restored** and continues to operate to this day.

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The debate over the Collegium System is **very much alive today**. In **2022-23**, former Law Minister **Kiren Rijiju** publicly criticised the Collegium System, calling it "opaque" and a system not contemplated by the Constitution. He stated that the government has a right to have a say in appointments.
> - The Supreme Court and the government have been in a tug-of-war, with the SC reiterating (in cases like the **reiteration by CJI D.Y. Chandrachud in 2023**) that the Collegium System is the law of the land until it is constitutionally changed.
> - This remains one of the most contested areas of Indian constitutional law and a key UPSC topic.

---

#### 📌 Case 4: The Chandra Kumar Case (1997)
**Full Name:** *L. Chandra Kumar vs. Union of India (1997)*

**Background:**
There was a question about whether tribunals (special bodies set up to handle specific disputes, like service matters or tax disputes) could **bypass** the High Court's writ jurisdiction. Some laws had set up tribunals with appeals going directly to the Supreme Court, seemingly cutting out the High Court.

**What the Court Ruled:**
The Supreme Court ruled that the **writ jurisdiction of both the High Court (under Article 226) and the Supreme Court (under Article 32)** are part of the **Basic Structure of the Constitution**. Therefore, they **cannot be taken away or excluded**, even by a constitutional amendment. Tribunals are **subject to** the writ jurisdiction of the High Courts.

**Impact:**
- An aggrieved person **cannot** directly approach the Supreme Court against a tribunal's decision without first going to the High Court.
- This case protects the High Court's role as an essential check on all types of adjudicatory bodies.

## 📋 Section 3: Qualifications, Oath, and Salaries

### ✅ Qualifications to Become a High Court Judge

A person must fulfil **both** of the following:

1. **Must be a citizen of India.**
2. **Must have EITHER:**
   - Held a **judicial office** in India for **ten years**, OR
   - Been an **advocate of a High Court** (or succession of High Courts) for **ten years**.

> 💡 **Important Distinctions:**
> - The Constitution does **NOT** prescribe a minimum age for appointment.
> - **Unlike the Supreme Court**, the Constitution makes **no provision** for appointing a *distinguished jurist* (an eminent legal scholar) as a High Court judge.

---

### 🙏 Oath or Affirmation

Before taking office, a High Court judge takes an oath before the **Governor of the state** (or a person appointed by the Governor).

In the oath, the judge swears to:
1. Bear **true faith and allegiance** to the Constitution of India.
2. Uphold the **sovereignty and integrity** of India.
3. Perform the **duties of the office** faithfully, to the best of ability, knowledge, and judgement, **without fear or favour, affection or ill-will**.
4. **Uphold the Constitution and the laws**.

---

### 💰 Salaries and Allowances

- Salaries, allowances, privileges, leave, and pension are **determined by Parliament**.
- They **cannot be reduced** to the disadvantage of a judge after appointment, **except** during a **financial emergency** (under Article 360).

#### Salary History of High Court Judges:

| Year | Chief Justice (per month) | Other Judges (per month) |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| 1950 | ₹4,000 | ₹3,500 |
| 1986 | ₹9,000 | ₹8,000 |
| 1998 | ₹30,000 | ₹26,000 |
| 2009 | ₹90,000 | ₹80,000 |
| **2018** | **₹2,50,000 (₹2.50 lakh)** | **₹2,25,000 (₹2.25 lakh)** |

- Judges also receive **sumptuary allowance** and free **accommodation, medical, car, and telephone** facilities.
- **Retired** Chief Justices and judges get **50% of their last drawn salary** as monthly pension.

> 💡 Note: Pension is charged on the **Consolidated Fund of India** (not the state).

## 🔄 Section 4: Tenure, Removal, and Transfer

### ⏳ Tenure of Judges

The Constitution does **not fix a tenure** for High Court judges. However, it provides these four rules:

1. **Retirement Age:** A judge holds office until the age of **62 years**. (This was raised from 60 to 62 years by the **15th Amendment Act of 1963**.) Any dispute about age is decided by the President after consulting the CJI — the President's decision is **final**.
2. **Resignation:** A judge can resign by writing to the **President**.
3. **Removal:** A judge can be removed by the President on the recommendation of Parliament (see below).
4. **Vacation of office:** A judge vacates office when:
   - Appointed as a **Supreme Court judge**, OR
   - **Transferred** to another High Court.

---

### 🔨 Removal of Judges (Impeachment Process)

A High Court judge can only be removed by an **order of the President**, but only after Parliament passes an address by a **Special Majority** in the **same session**.

**Special Majority means:**
- Majority of the **total membership** of that House, **AND**
- Majority of not less than **two-thirds** of the members **present and voting**.

**Grounds for removal:** Only two:
1. **Proved misbehaviour**, OR
2. **Incapacity**

#### Step-by-Step Impeachment Procedure (under The Judges Enquiry Act, 1968):

| Step | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **Step 1** | A removal motion is signed by **100 members (Lok Sabha)** or **50 members (Rajya Sabha)** and given to the Speaker/Chairman. |
| **Step 2** | The **Speaker/Chairman** decides to **admit or refuse** the motion. |
| **Step 3** | If admitted, a **three-member committee** is constituted to investigate the charges. |
| **Step 4** | The committee consists of: **(a)** a Chief Justice or judge of the Supreme Court, **(b)** Chief Justice of a High Court, and **(c)** a distinguished jurist. |
| **Step 5** | If the committee finds the judge **guilty**, the House takes up consideration of the motion. |
| **Step 6** | The motion is passed by **each House** by **special majority**. |
| **Step 7** | An address is presented to the **President**, who then passes the **removal order**. |

> 🌟 **Historical Fact:** **No judge of a High Court has ever been successfully impeached** in India's history.

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - In **2022**, a motion was initiated in the Rajya Sabha against **Justice S.K. Gangele** of the Madhya Pradesh High Court on allegations of sexual harassment. The motion, however, did not proceed to the investigation committee stage, illustrating how rare and difficult the impeachment process is.

---

### 🔁 Transfer of Judges

- The **President** can transfer a judge from one High Court to another **after consulting the Chief Justice of India**.
- On transfer, the judge gets a **compensatory allowance** (in addition to salary) as determined by Parliament.

#### Key Court Rulings on Transfer:

| Year | Ruling |
|------|--------|
| **1977** | Supreme Court ruled that transfer of HC judges can only be done as an **exceptional measure** in **public interest** — **not as punishment**. |
| **1994** | Supreme Court held that **judicial review** is necessary to check arbitrariness in transfers. But only the **transferred judge** can challenge it. |
| **Third Judges Case (1998)** | For transferring HC judges, the CJI must consult: **(a)** collegium of **four senior-most SC judges**, and **(b)** Chief Justices of **both** the concerned High Courts. Sole opinion of CJI alone is NOT sufficient. |

---

## 🔄 Section 5: Acting, Additional, and Retired Judges

### 👤 Acting Chief Justice

The **President** can appoint a judge of a High Court as **Acting Chief Justice** when:
1. The office of Chief Justice is **vacant**.
2. The Chief Justice is **temporarily absent**.
3. The Chief Justice is **unable to perform duties**.

---

### ➕ Additional and Acting Judges

#### Additional Judges:
The President can appoint **additional judges** for a **temporary period not exceeding 2 years** when:
1. There is a **temporary increase in business** of the High Court, OR
2. There are **arrears of work** (backlog of pending cases).

#### Acting Judges:
The President can appoint an **acting judge** when a judge of a High Court (other than the Chief Justice):
1. Is **unable to perform duties** due to absence or any other reason, OR
2. Is appointed to **act temporarily as Chief Justice**.

> 📌 An acting judge holds office until the **permanent judge resumes** office.
> 📌 Both additional and acting judges **cannot hold office after the age of 62 years**.

---

### 🧓 Retired Judges

- The **Chief Justice of a High Court** can request a **retired judge** (of that High Court or any other) to sit as a judge **temporarily**.
- This requires:
  - **Previous consent of the President**, AND
  - **Consent of the retired judge** themselves.
- Such a judge gets **allowances** as determined by the President.
- They enjoy all **jurisdiction, powers, and privileges** of a regular judge, but are **not deemed to be** a regular judge of that court.

---

## 🛡️ Section 6: Independence of the High Court

The independence of the High Court is essential for it to deliver justice without fear or favour. The Constitution provides **9 safeguards** to protect it:

---

### 1. 🔒 Mode of Appointment
Judges are appointed by the President **in consultation with the judiciary itself** (CJI and Chief Justice of HC). This **limits absolute executive discretion** and prevents purely political appointments.

---

### 2. 🔐 Security of Tenure
Judges can be removed **only** in the manner and on the grounds specified in the Constitution. They do **not** hold office "at the pleasure of the President." This is proven by the fact that **no High Court judge has ever been removed**.

---

### 3. 📊 Fixed Service Conditions
Salaries, allowances, and pensions are set by Parliament and **cannot be reduced** to a judge's disadvantage after appointment (except during a financial emergency). This ensures judges cannot be pressured by cutting their pay.

---

### 4. 💳 Expenses Charged on Consolidated Fund
- Salaries, allowances of judges and staff, and administrative expenses of the High Court are charged on the **Consolidated Fund of the State**.
- This means they are **non-votable** by the state legislature (though they can be *discussed*).
- **Exception:** The **pension** of a High Court judge is charged on the **Consolidated Fund of India** (not the state).

---

### 5. 🔇 Conduct of Judges Cannot Be Discussed
The Constitution **prohibits any discussion** in Parliament or state legislature regarding the conduct of High Court judges in the discharge of their duties — **except** when an impeachment motion is under consideration.

---

### 6. 🚫 Ban on Practice After Retirement
Retired permanent judges of a High Court **cannot plead or act** in any court or before any authority in India, **EXCEPT:**
- The **Supreme Court**, and
- **Other High Courts**.
This prevents judges from favouring parties during their tenure in the hope of getting lucrative briefs after retirement.

---

### 7. 🔨 Power to Punish for Contempt
A High Court can punish **anyone for contempt of court**. This protects the court's **authority, dignity, and honour**.

---

### 8. 🧑‍💼 Freedom to Appoint its Staff
The **Chief Justice of the High Court** can appoint officers and servants of the court **without executive interference** and can also prescribe their service conditions.

---

### 9. 🚧 Jurisdiction Cannot Be Curtailed
The jurisdiction and powers of a High Court as specified in the Constitution **cannot be curtailed** by either Parliament or the state legislature.

---

## ⚖️ Section 7: Jurisdiction and Powers of the High Court

The High Court has **extensive and effective powers**. It is:
- The **highest court of appeal** in the state.
- The **protector of Fundamental Rights** of citizens.
- Vested with the power to **interpret the Constitution**.
- Exercises **supervisory and consultative roles**.

> 💡 Unlike the Supreme Court, the Constitution does **not** contain detailed provisions on HC jurisdiction. It simply states that the HC's jurisdiction shall be the **same as it was just before the Constitution commenced** — with some additions (like revenue jurisdiction and writ jurisdiction).

The jurisdiction of High Courts is governed by:
- **(a)** Constitutional provisions
- **(b)** The Letters Patent (old royal charters)
- **(c)** Acts of Parliament
- **(d)** Acts of State Legislature
- **(e)** Indian Penal Code, 1860
- **(f)** Criminal Procedure Code, 1973
- **(g)** Civil Procedure Code, 1908

---

### 1. 📑 Original Jurisdiction
*(Power to hear a case for the first time, not by way of appeal)*

A High Court hears original disputes in:
- **Election disputes** relating to members of Parliament and state legislatures.
- **Revenue matters** or acts done in the course of revenue collection.
- **Enforcement of Fundamental Rights** of citizens.
- Cases **transferred from subordinate courts** involving interpretation of the Constitution.
- **Civil cases of higher value**: The four High Courts — **Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, and Delhi** — have original civil jurisdiction in high-value civil cases.

> 📌 Before 1973, Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras also had **original criminal jurisdiction**, but this was **fully abolished** by the Criminal Procedure Code, 1973.

---

### 2. 📝 Writ Jurisdiction

**Article 226** empowers a High Court to issue **five types of writs**:

| Writ | Plain English Meaning |
|------|----------------------|
| **Habeas Corpus** | "Produce the body" — to free an illegally detained person |
| **Mandamus** | "We command" — order to a public authority to do its legal duty |
| **Certiorari** | "To be certified" — to quash an illegal order of a lower court/tribunal |
| **Prohibition** | To stop a lower court from exceeding its jurisdiction |
| **Quo Warranto** | "By what authority" — to challenge illegal occupation of a public office |

**For what purposes?**
- Enforcement of **Fundamental Rights**, AND
- **Any other purpose** — i.e., enforcement of any **ordinary legal right** (this is a key difference from the Supreme Court).

**Territorial Reach:**
The High Court can issue writs to any person, authority, or government:
- **Within** its territorial jurisdiction, OR
- **Outside** its territorial jurisdiction if the **cause of action** (the event that led to the dispute) **arises within** its territorial jurisdiction. *(This provision was added by the 15th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1963.)*

#### High Court Writ vs. Supreme Court Writ:

| Feature | High Court (Article 226) | Supreme Court (Article 32) |
|---------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| For Fundamental Rights? | Yes | Yes |
| For ordinary legal rights? | **Yes** | No |
| Jurisdiction | Concurrent with SC | Concurrent with HC |
| Wider scope? | **YES — Wider** | Narrower |

> 📌 The writ jurisdictions of both courts are **concurrent** (running side by side), not exclusive. A citizen can approach **either** court when Fundamental Rights are violated.

#### 📌 Chandra Kumar Case (1997) — Writ Jurisdiction is Part of Basic Structure:
As detailed in Section 2, the Supreme Court in *L. Chandra Kumar vs. Union of India (1997)* held that writ jurisdiction under **Articles 226 and 32** forms part of the **Basic Structure** of the Constitution and **cannot be excluded** even by a constitutional amendment. Tribunals are subject to High Court supervision.

---

### 3. 📢 Appellate Jurisdiction
*(Power to hear and decide appeals against decisions of lower courts)*

The High Court is **primarily a court of appeal**. Its appellate jurisdiction covers **both civil and criminal matters** and is **wider than its original jurisdiction**.

#### (a) Civil Appellate Jurisdiction:

| Type of Appeal | From Which Court | On What Grounds |
|---------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **First Appeal** | District Courts, Additional District Courts, other subordinate courts | Both **questions of law AND fact** (if amount exceeds stipulated limit) |
| **Second Appeal** | District Courts or subordinate courts | Only **questions of law** (not fact) |
| **Intra-Court Appeal** | Single judge of the High Court (original or appellate) | Goes to a **Division Bench** of the same HC |
| **Tribunal Appeals** | Administrative and other tribunals | Goes to **Division Bench** of the HC |

> 📌 In **1997**, the Supreme Court ruled that tribunals are subject to HC writ jurisdiction. Therefore, an aggrieved person **cannot** directly approach the Supreme Court against tribunal decisions — they must first go to the High Court.

#### (b) Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction:

- Appeals from judgements of **Sessions Court** and **Additional Sessions Court** go to the HC if the sentence is **more than 7 years of imprisonment**.
- A **death sentence** (capital punishment) awarded by a Sessions Court or Additional Sessions Court **MUST be confirmed by the High Court** before execution — whether or not the convicted person appeals.
- In some cases specified under **CrPC 1973**, appeals from **Assistant Sessions Judges, Metropolitan Magistrates, or other Judicial Magistrates** also lie to the High Court.

---

### 4. 🔍 Supervisory Jurisdiction

*(Power to oversee and regulate all lower courts and tribunals in the state)*

Under this power, a High Court may:
1. **Call for returns** (records/reports) from lower courts.
2. **Make and issue general rules** and prescribe forms for regulating practice and proceedings of subordinate courts.
3. **Prescribe formats** for books, entries, and accounts kept by them.
4. **Settle fees** payable to sheriffs, clerks, officers, and legal practitioners.

**Why is this power considered very broad?**
- It extends to **all courts and tribunals** — whether or not they fall under the HC's appellate jurisdiction.
- It covers **both administrative AND judicial** superintendence.
- It is a **revisional jurisdiction** (can review and revise).
- It can be exercised **suo-motu** (on the court's own initiative, without anyone asking for it).

**BUT — it has limits!**
This is an **extraordinary** power and must be used **sparingly**. It is generally limited to:
- Excess of jurisdiction
- Gross violation of natural justice (basic fairness)
- Error of law
- Disregard to law of superior courts
- Perverse findings (completely unreasonable conclusions)
- Manifest injustice (obvious, clear injustice)

> 📌 Notably, this supervisory power does **NOT** extend to **military courts or tribunals**.

---

### 5. 🏛️ Control over Subordinate Courts

Beyond appellate and supervisory jurisdiction, the High Court has **direct administrative control** over subordinate courts:

1. The **Governor consults the High Court** in matters of appointment, posting, and promotion of **District Judges** and appointments to the judicial service of the state.
2. The High Court handles **posting, promotion, grant of leave, transfers, and discipline** of members of the state's judicial service (other than district judges).
3. The HC can **withdraw a case** from a subordinate court if it involves a **substantial question of law** requiring constitutional interpretation. It can then either:
   - Dispose of the entire case itself, OR
   - Decide the constitutional question and return the case to the lower court with its judgment.
4. The **law declared by the High Court is binding** on all subordinate courts within its territorial jurisdiction — just as the Supreme Court's law is binding on all courts in India.

---

### 6. 📚 A Court of Record

As a **Court of Record**, a High Court has two powers:

**Power 1: Permanent Record**
- All judgements, proceedings, and acts of the HC are recorded for **perpetual memory and testimony**.
- These records have **evidentiary value** — they cannot be questioned when produced before any subordinate court.
- They serve as **legal precedents and legal references**.

**Power 2: Power to Punish for Contempt of Court**
- The term **"contempt of court"** is not defined in the Constitution, but is defined in the **Contempt of Courts Act, 1971**.
- Contempt can be:
  - **Civil contempt**: Willful disobedience of a court's order.
  - **Criminal contempt**: Scandalising the court, prejudicing a fair trial, or obstructing justice.
- A High Court has the power to punish for contempt of:
  - **Itself**, AND
  - **Subordinate courts** (lower courts under it).
- **Exception:** The HC cannot take cognizance of contempt of a subordinate court if that contempt is **already punishable under the Indian Penal Code, 1860**.

> 📌 **Important Distinction:** As a court of record, the HC also has the power to **review and correct its own judgements**. Unlike the Supreme Court (which is *specifically granted* the power of review by the Constitution), the HC's review power flows **inherently** from its status as a court of record.

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - In **2021**, the Supreme Court in *In Re: Prashant Bhushan* clarified standards for criminal contempt of court in the context of social media criticism of judges — a growing concern in the digital age.
> - The **Contempt of Courts (Amendment) Act** debate has been ongoing, with many legal experts calling for reform to the 1971 Act to protect free speech while maintaining judicial dignity.

---

### 7. 🔎 Power of Judicial Review

**Judicial Review** is the power of the High Court to **examine whether laws and executive actions are constitutional**. If found to be:
- **Ultra-vires** (beyond the powers given by the Constitution) → they can be declared **illegal, unconstitutional, invalid, and null and void**.
- Such laws/orders **cannot be enforced** by the government.

> 📌 The phrase "judicial review" is **nowhere explicitly used** in the Constitution. However, **Articles 13 and 226** explicitly confer this power on High Courts.

**On what grounds can a law be challenged?**
1. It **infringes Fundamental Rights** (Part III of the Constitution).
2. It is **outside the competence** (authority) of the body that made it.
3. It is **repugnant** (contradictory) to constitutional provisions.

#### Key Constitutional Amendments Affecting Judicial Review:

| Amendment | Year | Effect |
|-----------|------|--------|
| **42nd Amendment Act** | 1976 | **Curtailed** HC's judicial review power — debarred High Courts from considering the constitutional validity of **central laws**. |
| **43rd Amendment Act** | 1977 | **Restored** the original position — High Courts could again examine central laws. |

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - **Judicial Review in Action (2020-2024):** High Courts across India have actively used their judicial review powers in recent years:
>   - The **Kerala High Court** examined the constitutional validity of the **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) Rules in 2024** through petitions.
>   - Multiple High Courts issued orders on matters related to the **abrogation of Article 370** before the Supreme Court took up the matter.
>   - The **Delhi High Court** used judicial review to examine aspects of the **Farm Laws (now repealed)** before the Supreme Court intervened.

## 🌍 Modern Context: Connecting the Chapter to Today

> `[Modern Example]`

### 1. Collegium System Reform Debate (2022-2024)
- **What's happening:** The Union Government and the Supreme Court have been at loggerheads over the Collegium System. In late 2022, the Union Government sent back several collegium recommendations, leading to a public standoff.
- **Government's Position:** The Collegium is "opaque" and lacks accountability. Former Law Minister Kiren Rijiju (2022) stated that the Collegium System was "not contemplated" by the Constitution.
- **SC's Position:** Reaffirmed that the Collegium System is the law of the land (per the Fourth Judges Case, 2015). CJI D.Y. Chandrachud, in 2023, called for a Memorandum of Procedure (MoP) to be finalised to make the process more transparent.
- **UPSC Angle:** This is a live debate about **separation of powers** and **judicial independence**.

### 2. Pending Vacancies in High Courts (2023-2024)
- As of early 2024, High Courts across India had over **350+ judicial vacancies** against their sanctioned strength. This directly leads to the **pendency of over 60 lakh cases** in High Courts.
- This illustrates the real-world consequence of a slow appointment process and is directly linked to the debate over reforming the Collegium System.

### 3. High Courts and Fundamental Rights Protection (2020-2024)
- **COVID-19 and High Courts (2021):** The **Allahabad High Court** and the **Delhi High Court** took suo-motu cognizance of the mismanagement of oxygen supply and healthcare during the second COVID wave, directly invoking their supervisory jurisdiction and writ jurisdiction under Article 226.
- **Manipur Violence (2023):** The **Manipur High Court** and later the Supreme Court intervened in the ethnic violence cases, showcasing how High Courts act as the first line of protection for Fundamental Rights.

### 4. Judicial Infrastructure Upgradation
- The **National Mission for Justice Delivery and Legal Reforms** and the **eCourts Project (Phase III, launched 2023)** are government initiatives aimed at digitising High Court records and reducing case pendency — directly relevant to the HC's role as a court of record.
