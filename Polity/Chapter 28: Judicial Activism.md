## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. **Judicial Activism** = Judiciary proactively protecting rights & pushing other organs to do their duty.
> 2. The concept was **born in the USA (1947)** and came to India in the **mid-1970s**.
> 3. **PIL (Public Interest Litigation)** is the most visible tool of judicial activism in India.
> 4. Judicial activism must not become **"judicial adventurism"** — the Supreme Court itself warned this.
> 5. **Judicial Restraint** is the opposite philosophy — judges should stick to law, not make it.

---

## 🌱 Origin of Judicial Activism

- The concept **originated and developed in the USA**.
- The term was **first coined in 1947** by **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**, an American historian and educator.
  - *(His article "The Supreme Court: 1947" was published in **Fortune** magazine.)*
- In **India**, judicial activism was introduced in the **mid-1970s**.
- The **founding fathers** of judicial activism in India:
  - **Justice V.R. Krishna Iyer**
  - **Justice P.N. Bhagwati**
  - **Justice O. Chinnappa Reddy**
  - **Justice D.A. Desai**

---

## 📖 Meaning of Judicial Activism

In simple terms: **Judicial Activism = Judiciary stepping up when other organs of government fail.**

More specifically, it means:
- The **proactive role** played by the judiciary to **protect citizens' rights** and **promote justice**.
- The judiciary **assertively pushes** the Legislature and Executive to **perform their constitutional duties**.
- It is also called **"Judicial Dynamism"**.
- It is the **exact opposite** of **"Judicial Restraint"** (self-control exercised by the judiciary — i.e., judges holding themselves back).

---

### 📚 How Different Authorities Define Judicial Activism

Here are all five definitions as given in the chapter — simplified but complete:

| # | Source | Core Idea |
|---|--------|-----------|
| 1 | *Black's Law Dictionary* | Judges **depart from strict precedent** (past court decisions) in favour of **progressive social policies**; sometimes this means intruding into legislative/executive matters. |
| 2 | *Merriam Webster's Dictionary of Law* | Judiciary **protects or expands individual rights** through decisions that go beyond established precedent or even constitutional/legislative intent. |
| 3 | *V.G. Palishikar, AIR 1998* | Judges **actively interpret existing laws** to enhance their usefulness for **social betterment** — the opposite of "judicial pessimism" (interpreting law without trying to improve it). |
| 4 | *Black's Law Dictionary* | Judges allow their **personal views on public policy** to guide their decisions. |
| 5 | *P.B. Sawant, 1987* | A procedure to **evolve new principles, formulas, and relief** to do justice, expand who can approach courts, and entertain cases affecting entire society or sections of it. |

> 💡 **Quick Recall:** Judicial Activism = **Active Interpretation + Social Engineering + Expanded Access to Courts**

## 🔍 Two Major Aspects of Judicial Activism in India

*(As identified by Adish C. Aggarwal)*

### Aspect 1: Directions to Government Authorities
- Courts issue **specific directions to government bodies** to protect citizens' rights and serve public interest.
- **Public Interest Litigations (PILs)** — (cases filed for the benefit of the public at large, not just one person) — fall under this aspect.

### Aspect 2: Expansive Interpretation of Fundamental Rights
- Courts have **broadened the scope** of key Fundamental Rights through interpretation:
  - **Article 14** — Right to Equality
  - **Article 19** — Right to Freedom
  - **Article 21** — Right to Life and Personal Liberty
- The courts, at their discretion, have **stretched the meaning** of these rights far beyond their literal text.

> 💡 **Example:** Article 21 originally just said "no person shall be deprived of life or personal liberty." Through judicial activism, the Supreme Court has read into it the right to education, right to health, right to clean environment, right to livelihood, and much more.

## ⚖️ Judicial Review vs. Judicial Activism

These two are **closely related but NOT the same.** Here's the distinction:

| Feature | Judicial Review | Judicial Activism |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Basic Meaning** | Checking if laws conform to the Constitution | Moulding law to fit changing social/economic realities |
| **Scope** | Upholding or striking down laws based on constitutional parameters | Also involves judges exercising **policy preferences** |
| **Nature** | More passive / mechanical | More dynamic / creative |
| **Locus Standi** (legal standing — *who can approach the court*) | Traditional, strict rules | Expanded through PIL |

**Key connecting points:**
1. Since the mid-20th century, a version of judicial review earned the nickname "judicial activism," especially in the USA.
2. In India, people often mix up the two terms. Judicial activism is that **form of judicial review** where judges not only uphold/invalidate laws but also **exercise policy preferences** in doing so.
3. The term "judicial activism" came into currency in the 20th century to describe **judicial legislation** (judges making positive law).
4. The **expanded concept of locus standi** through PIL has stretched the courts' jurisdictional limits — critics of this expansion call it "judicial activism."
5. In constitutional cases, judicial activism falls under the **rubric of judicial review**, and at its broadest, it is when a court **strikes down a duly enacted piece of legislation**.

## ✅ Why is Judicial Activism Justified?

### According to Dr. B.L. Wadehra:

- **Near Collapse of Responsible Government:** When the Legislature and Executive fail to do their jobs, people lose faith in the Constitution and democracy. The judiciary must step in.
- **Citizens Look to the Judiciary:** People look to courts to protect their rights and freedoms, creating enormous pressure on the judiciary to help the suffering masses.
- **Judicial Enthusiasm:** Judges like to participate in social reform. This encourages PILs and liberalises the principle of **Locus Standi** (who has the right to file a case).
- **Legislative Vacuum:** Some areas are simply **not covered by any law**. Courts must step in and engage in judicial legislation to meet changing social needs.
- **Constitutional Provisions:** The Constitution of India itself has provisions that **give the judiciary scope** to play an active, law-making role.

---

### According to Subhash Kashyap:

These are the specific situations when the judiciary may need to **overstep its normal jurisdiction:**

- When the **legislature fails to discharge its responsibilities**.
- When there is a **'hung' legislature** (no clear majority government), resulting in a weak government that is busy only surviving — unable to take any bold or unpopular decisions.
- When those **in power fear losing power** and deliberately refer public issues to courts as legal questions — just to **delay decisions** or pass the blame of tough choices onto the judiciary.
- When the legislature and executive **fail to protect basic rights** of citizens — like the right to a decent life, healthy surroundings, and an honest administration.
- When the **court is misused** by a strong authoritarian parliamentary government for **ulterior motives** — as attempted during the Emergency (1975–77).
- When courts themselves, knowingly or unknowingly, become victims of the **craze for populism, publicity, and media attention**.

## 👥 Who Activates Judicial Activism? (Upendra Baxi's Typology)

**Upendra Baxi**, an eminent jurist, identified **15 types of social/human rights activists** who trigger judicial activism:

1. **Civil Rights Activists** — Focus on civil and political rights issues.
2. **People Rights Activists** — Focus on social and economic rights in the context of state repression of people's movements.
3. **Consumer Rights Groups** — Raise issues of consumer rights and accountability of polity and economy.
4. **Bonded Labour Groups** — Demand annihilation of wage slavery in India.
5. **Citizens for Environmental Action** — Activate the judiciary to combat environmental degradation and pollution.
6. **Citizen Groups against Large Irrigation Projects** — Ask the judiciary to stop ordering mega irrigation projects.
7. **Rights of Child Groups** — Focus on child labour, right to literacy, juveniles in custodial institutions, and rights of children born to sex workers.
8. **Custodial Rights Groups** — Include prisoners' rights groups, women under state 'protective' custody, and persons under preventive detention.
9. **Poverty Rights Groups** — Litigate issues of drought and famine relief, and urban impoverishment.
10. **Indigenous People's Rights Groups** — Agitate for forest dwellers, citizens of the **Fifth and Sixth Schedules** of the Constitution, and identity rights.
11. **Women's Rights Groups** — Agitate for gender equality, gender-based violence, rape, and dowry murders.
12. **Bar-based Groups** — Agitate for issues of autonomy and accountability of the Indian judiciary.
13. **Media Autonomy Groups** — Focus on autonomy and accountability of the press and State-owned mass media.
14. **Assorted Lawyer-Based Groups** — Critically influential lawyers' groups agitating for various causes.
15. **Assorted Individual Petitioners** — Freelance activist individuals filing cases on their own.

## 😰 Fears & Apprehensions About Judicial Activism

**Upendra Baxi** also identified a **typology of fears** generated by judicial activism — these are concerns even the most conscientious judges feel:

1. **Ideological Fears** — *"Are we usurping the powers of the legislature, executive, or other autonomous institutions in civil society?"*
2. **Epistemic Fears** — *"Do we have enough knowledge in economics (like Manmohan Singh), or in science (like the heads of atomic energy or CSIR), to rule on these matters?"*
3. **Management Fears** — *"Are we doing justice by adding to an already staggering backlog of cases?"*
4. **Legitimation Fears** — *"Are we depleting our own authority by passing orders that the executive simply ignores? Will people lose faith in the judiciary?"*
5. **Democratic Fears** — *"Is a flood of PILs nurturing democracy — or depleting it?"*
6. **Biographic Fears** — *"What will my place in national affairs be after retirement if I overdo this kind of litigation?"*

## ⚔️ Judicial Activism vs. Judicial Restraint

### What is Judicial Restraint?

**Judicial Restraint** (self-control exercised by the judiciary — judges limiting themselves to interpreting law, not making it) is the **opposing philosophy** to judicial activism.

Its core belief:
- The role of judges should be **scrupulously limited** — their job is to **say what the law IS**, not what it should be.
- Law-making belongs to **legislators and executives**.
- Judges must **NEVER** let personal political values or policy agendas colour their opinions.
- Courts must follow the **"original intent"** of the framers of the Constitution.

---

### Six Assumptions of Judicial Restraint (in the USA)

*(Source: Joel B. Grossman and Richard S. Wells, 1972)*

> **Note:** All assumptions except No. 2 (about judicial review) are relevant to the Indian context as well.

1. **Undemocratic Court** — The court is non-elected and non-responsive to popular will. Being "oligarchic" in composition, it should defer to the more democratic branches.
2. **Questionable Origins of Judicial Review** — Judicial review is a powerful tool not specifically granted by the Constitution — its origins are debatable.
3. **Separation of Powers** — Each organ has its own domain; courts should not cross over.
4. **Federalism** — Division of powers between the nation and states requires the Court to defer to state government actions.
5. **Pragmatic Assumption** — Since the Court depends on Congress/Parliament for jurisdiction and resources, and on public acceptance for effectiveness, it should not overstep without considering the risks.
6. **Aristocratic Notion** — As a court of law and custodian of the Anglo-American legal tradition, it should not descend into the realm of politics (which is about power and influence).

## 🏛️ LANDMARK CASE / COURT OBSERVATION: Supreme Court on Judicial Restraint (December 2007)

> **📌 This is a critical Supreme Court observation — treat it as a case study.**

### Background
In **December 2007**, the Supreme Court of India delivered a landmark judgement in which it **strongly called for judicial restraint**. The Court warned judges not to take over the functions of the legislature or executive, emphasising the broad **separation of powers** under the Constitution.

---

### What Did the Supreme Court Bench Say? (All 9 Points)

**Observation 1 — On Overstepping:**
> *"We are repeatedly coming across cases where judges are unjustifiably trying to perform executive or legislative functions. This is clearly unconstitutional. In the name of judicial activism, judges cannot cross their limits and try to take over functions which belong to another organ of the state."*

**Observation 2 — On Judicial Humility:**
> *"Judges must know their limits and must not try to run the government. They must have modesty and humility, and not behave like emperors."*

**Observation 3 — Quoting Montesquieu:**
- The Bench quoted **Montesquieu's** book *'The Spirit of Laws'* on the dangers of not maintaining separation of powers.
- It said Montesquieu's *"warning is particularly apt and timely for the Indian judiciary today, since very often it is rightly criticised for 'overreach' and encroachment on the domain of the other two organs."*

> 💡 **Who is Montesquieu?** A French political philosopher who first clearly articulated the doctrine of separation of powers — the idea that legislative, executive, and judicial powers must be held by separate institutions.

**Observation 4 — On Judicial Adventurism:**
> *"Judicial activism must not become judicial adventurism."*
- Adjudication must be done within a **system of historically validated restraints** and with **conscious minimisation of judges' preferences**.

**Observation 5 — On Administrative Expertise:**
> *"The courts must not embarrass administrative authorities and must realise that administrative authorities have expertise in the field of administration while the court does not."*

**Observation 6 — On the "Other Organs Are Failing" Argument:**
> *"The justification often given for judicial encroachment on the domain of the executive or the legislature is that the other two organs are not doing their jobs properly. Even assuming this is so, the same allegations can be made against the judiciary too because there are cases pending in courts for half-a-century."*

**Observation 7 — On Democratic Remedy:**
- If the legislature or executive is not functioning properly, the **remedy lies with the people** — by exercising their franchise correctly in the next elections, voting for better candidates, or through other lawful methods like **peaceful demonstrations**.

**Observation 8 — On Why Courts Can't Replace Other Organs:**
> *"The remedy is not in the judiciary taking over the legislative or the executive functions, because that will not only violate the delicate balance of power enshrined in the Constitution but also (because) the judiciary has neither the expertise nor the resources to perform these functions."*

**Observation 9 — On Why Judicial Restraint Protects the Judiciary Itself:**
> *"Judicial restraint is consistent with and complementary to the balance of power among the three independent branches of the state. It accomplishes this in two ways: first, judicial restraint not only recognises the equality of the other two branches with the judiciary, it also fosters that equality by minimising inter-branch interference by the judiciary. Second, judicial restraint tends to protect the independence of the judiciary. When courts encroach on the legislative or administrative fields almost inevitably voters, legislators, and other elected officials will conclude that the activities of judges should be closely monitored."*

---

> ### 🔑 Summary of the 2007 SC Observation in One Line:
> **"Be active enough to protect rights, but humble enough not to become the government."**

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` *The following examples are added to connect chapter concepts to current affairs.*

### 1. PIL & Judicial Activism — Still Alive and Kicking
- **Suo Motu Cognizance during COVID-19 (2021):** The Supreme Court took suo motu (on its own motion) cognizance of the COVID-19 crisis — directing the Central government on oxygen supply, vaccine policy, and NDMA guidelines. This is a textbook example of judicial activism under **Article 21 (Right to Life)**.
- **[Modern Example]** The Supreme Court in *In Re: Distribution of Essential Supplies and Services During Pandemic* (2021) directly questioned the Central government's vaccine pricing policy, forcing a revision — a clear instance of courts "forcing the executive to discharge its constitutional duties."

### 2. Article 21 Expanded Even Further
- **[Modern Example]** In **2022**, the Supreme Court in *Justice K.S. Puttaswamy vs Union of India* (Privacy Judgment follow-up matters) continued expanding Article 21 to include **digital privacy and data protection**, ultimately leading to the **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023**.

### 3. Judicial Activism on Environment
- **[Modern Example]** The National Green Tribunal (NGT), itself a product of judicial activism, has passed thousands of orders since 2010 on air quality, the Yamuna river, and stubble burning — connecting directly to Baxi's "Citizens for Environmental Action" category.
- The **Delhi Air Quality Crisis (2021–2024)**: The Supreme Court repeatedly intervened to direct the CAQM (Commission for Air Quality Management) — a modern example of courts compelling executive action.

### 4. Judicial Activism vs. Judicial Restraint Debate — Today
- **[Modern Example]** The debate got renewed in the **NJAC Judgment (Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs Union of India, 2015)** — where the Supreme Court struck down the **99th Constitutional Amendment** and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act, restoring the Collegium system. Critics called this peak judicial activism; supporters called it constitutional guardianship.
- **[Modern Example]** In **2023**, the Supreme Court's Constitution Bench in the **Maharashtra Political Crisis case** (Shinde vs. Thackeray) and the **Delhi vs. Centre (GNCTD)** case showed the tension between judicial intervention and restraint in live political disputes.

### 5. Relevant Government Schemes Linked to Activist Categories (Baxi's List)
| Baxi's Category | Related Modern Scheme/Policy |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| Poverty Rights Groups | **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana** (free foodgrains for 81 crore people, extended through 2028) |
| Rights of Child Groups | **POCSO Act enforcement**, **National Commission for Protection of Child Rights (NCPCR)** |
| Women's Rights Groups | **Beti Bachao Beti Padhao**, amendments to **rape laws post-Nirbhaya (2013)** |
| Indigenous People's Rights | **Forest Rights Act 2006** and Supreme Court orders on tribal land rights |
| Environmental Groups | **Environment Protection (Amendment) Rules 2021**, NGT orders on Yamuna |

| Parameter | Judicial Activism | Judicial Restraint |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Approach** | Proactive, interventionist | Passive, deferential |
| **Law-making** | Judges can make positive law | Law-making is for legislature only |
