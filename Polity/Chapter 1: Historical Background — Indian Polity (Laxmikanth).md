# 📘 Chapter 1: Historical Background — Indian Polity (Laxmikanth)

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Main Takeaways you MUST remember:**
>
> 1. 🏢 The British came as **traders in 1608** but gradually became a **territorial power from 1765** (after getting 'Diwani' rights).
> 2. ⚖️ The journey from **Company Rule (1773–1858)** to **Crown Rule (1858–1947)** was marked by a series of landmark Acts that shaped India's governance.
> 3. 🏛️ Many features of our **Constitution — federalism, bicameralism, public service commissions, judiciary** — have direct roots in these colonial-era Acts.
> 4. 🗳️ The introduction of **communal electorates** (1909) and the **Poona Pact (1932)** left a deep mark on India's political identity.
> 5. 🇮🇳 The **Indian Independence Act of 1947** formally ended British rule and set the stage for India's own Constitution, which came into force on **January 26, 1950**.

---

## 🕰️ Setting the Stage: How Britain Came to Rule India

- The British first arrived in India in **1608** as **traders**, operating through the **East India Company** — a trading firm given exclusive rights to trade in India by **Queen Elizabeth I in 1600**.
- In **1765**, after winning the **Battle of Buxar (1764)**, the Company obtained **'Diwani'** (i.e., rights over revenue collection and civil justice) of **Bengal, Bihar and Orissa** from the Mughal Emperor **Shah Alam**.
  - This marked the Company's shift from a trading firm to a **territorial (ruling) power**.
- In **1858**, following the **Sepoy Mutiny**, the **British Crown** took direct control over India's governance.
- British rule lasted until **August 15, 1947**, when India gained independence.
- A **Constituent Assembly** was formed in **1946** to write India's Constitution, which came into force on **January 26, 1950**.

> 💡 **Key Idea:** Many features of the Indian Constitution have roots in British rule. Understanding these colonial laws helps us understand *why* our Constitution is the way it is.

---

The entire constitutional history of British India is divided into **two major phases:**

| Phase | Period |
|---|---|
| **The Company Rule** | 1773–1858 |
| **The Crown Rule** | 1858–1947 |

# PART 1: THE COMPANY RULE (1773–1858)

---

## 1️⃣ Regulating Act of 1773

### Why It Matters
This was the **first ever** attempt by the British Government to **control and regulate** the East India Company. It is a **landmark in constitutional history** for three reasons:
- First step by British Government to oversee the Company's functioning.
- First recognition of the Company's **political and administrative** (not just trading) role.
- Laid the **foundations of central administration** in India.

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Created the post of Governor-General of Bengal** — with an **Executive Council of four members**. The first holder was **Lord Warren Hastings**.
- 🔹 Made the **Governors of Bombay and Madras** subordinate to the Governor-General of Bengal. (Earlier, all three presidencies were independent of each other.)
- 🔹 Provided for the establishment of a **Supreme Court at Calcutta (1774)** — with **one Chief Justice** and **three other judges**.
- 🔹 **Prohibited** Company servants from engaging in **private trade or accepting bribes/gifts** from 'natives'.
- 🔹 Required the **Court of Directors** (the Company's governing body) to **report** on revenue, civil, and military affairs in India to the British Government — strengthening oversight.

---

## 2️⃣ Amending Act of 1781 *(Also called the Act of Settlement)*

### Why It Was Needed
The Regulating Act of 1773 had created conflicts between the Supreme Court and the Governor-General's Council. This Act was passed to **fix those defects**.

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Exempted** the Governor-General, Council members, and Company servants from the **Supreme Court's jurisdiction** for their **official acts**.
- 🔹 **Excluded** revenue matters and matters related to revenue collection from the Supreme Court's jurisdiction.
- 🔹 The Supreme Court would have jurisdiction over **all inhabitants of Calcutta**. It would follow **personal law** — Hindus tried under Hindu law, Muslims under Mohammedan law.
- 🔹 **Appeals** from Provincial Courts could now go to the **Governor-General-in-Council**, not to the Supreme Court.
- 🔹 **Empowered** the Governor-General-in-Council to frame regulations for Provincial Courts and Councils.

---

## 3️⃣ Pitt's India Act of 1784

> 📌 *Named after British Prime Minister William Pitt, who introduced it in Parliament.*

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Distinguished** between the Company's **commercial** and **political** functions for the first time.
- 🔹 Created a new body called the **Board of Control** to manage **political affairs**, while the **Court of Directors** continued to handle **commercial affairs**.
  - This established a **"Double Government"** system (two parallel bodies controlling India).
- 🔹 **Empowered the Board of Control** to supervise all civil, military, and revenue operations of British territories in India.

### Two Big Firsts:
1. India's territories were **for the first time called "British possessions in India"**.
2. The **British Government** was given **supreme control** over the Company's affairs and Indian administration.

---

## 4️⃣ Act of 1786

### Background
**Lord Cornwallis** was appointed Governor-General of Bengal but agreed only on two conditions:
1. He should be able to **override his council's decisions** in special circumstances.
2. He should also serve as **Commander-in-Chief**.

The **Act of 1786** was passed to fulfil both these demands.

---

## 5️⃣ Charter Act of 1793

### Key Features:
- 🔹 Extended the **overriding power** (power to override council decisions) to **all future Governor-Generals** and **Governors of Presidencies** — not just Cornwallis.
- 🔹 Gave the Governor-General **more powers and control** over Bombay and Madras.
- 🔹 **Extended the Company's trade monopoly** in India for **another 20 years**.
- 🔹 The Commander-in-Chief was **not** to be a member of the Governor-General's council unless specifically appointed.
- 🔹 Members of the **Board of Control and their staff** were now to be paid from **Indian revenues** (not British funds).

---

## 6️⃣ Charter Act of 1813

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Abolished the Company's trade monopoly** — Indian trade was thrown open to **all British merchants**. (Exception: tea trade and trade with China remained with the Company.)
- 🔹 **Asserted the sovereignty of the British Crown** over the Company's territories in India.
- 🔹 Allowed **Christian missionaries** to come to India to "enlighten" people.
- 🔹 Provided for **spread of Western education** among people in British territories.
- 🔹 Authorised **Local Governments in India to impose taxes** on persons and punish those who didn't pay.

---

## 7️⃣ Charter Act of 1833

> 📌 This Act was the **final step toward complete centralisation** in British India.

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Made the Governor-General of Bengal the Governor-General of India** — vested with all civil and military powers. **Lord William Bentinck** was the **first Governor-General of India**.
  - For the **first time**, the Government of India had authority over **all British territories** in India.
- 🔹 **Deprived Governors of Bombay and Madras** of their legislative powers. The Governor-General of India became the **sole legislative authority** for all of British India.
  - Laws made earlier = called **Regulations**. Laws made under this Act = called **Acts**.
- 🔹 **Ended the Company's commercial activities** — it became a **purely administrative body**, holding Indian territories "in trust for His Majesty, His heirs and successors."
- 🔹 Attempted to introduce **open competition** for civil servants and stated Indians should **not be barred** from any office under the Company. *(However, this was blocked by the Court of Directors.)*

---

## 8️⃣ Charter Act of 1853

> 📌 This was the **last Charter Act** in the series (1793–1853). It was a **significant constitutional landmark**.

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Separated legislative and executive functions** of the Governor-General's Council for the **first time**.
  - Added **6 new "legislative councillors"** — creating a separate **Governor-General's Legislative Council**, also called the **Indian (Central) Legislative Council**.
  - This body functioned like a **mini-Parliament**, following the same procedures as the British Parliament.
  - This was the first time **legislation** was treated as a **special function** requiring special machinery and process.
- 🔹 Introduced an **open competition system** for selecting civil servants — opening the **covenanted civil service** (higher civil services, created by Company law) to Indians too. The **Macaulay Committee** (Committee on the Indian Civil Service) was set up in **1854** for this purpose.
- 🔹 Extended the Company's rule **without specifying a fixed time period** — a signal that Parliament could end it anytime.
- 🔹 Introduced **local/provincial representation** in the Central Legislative Council — **4 of the 6** new legislative members were appointed by local governments of **Madras, Bombay, Bengal, and Agra**.

# PART 2: THE CROWN RULE (1858–1947)

---

## 9️⃣ Government of India Act of 1858

> 📌 *Enacted in the wake of the **Revolt of 1857** (also known as the First War of Independence or the 'Sepoy Mutiny'). Officially called the "Act for the Good Government of India."*

### Key Features:
- 🔹 India was to be governed by, and **in the name of, Her Majesty** (the British Crown).
- 🔹 **Changed the title** of the Governor-General of India to **Viceroy of India** — the direct representative of the British Crown. **Lord Canning** became the **first Viceroy of India**.
- 🔹 **Ended Double Government** — abolished the **Board of Control** and the **Court of Directors**.
- 🔹 Created the **Secretary of State for India** — a member of the British Cabinet, with complete authority over Indian administration, responsible to the British Parliament.
- 🔹 Established a **15-member Council of India** to assist the Secretary of State (advisory body). The Secretary of State was its Chairman.
- 🔹 Constituted the **Secretary of State-in-Council** as a corporate body — capable of suing and being sued in India and England.

> ⚠️ *Note: This Act did not substantially change how India was governed — it mainly improved the machinery for British oversight from London.*

---

## 🔟 Indian Councils Act of 1861

> 📌 After 1857, the British felt they needed Indian cooperation in administration. This Act was the **first in a series** (1861, 1892, 1909) aimed at bringing Indians into governance — a policy called **"association."**

### Key Features:
- 🔹 Made a **beginning of representative institutions** — the Viceroy was to **nominate some Indians** as non-official members of his legislative council. In **1862**, **Lord Canning** nominated three Indians: **Raja of Benaras, Maharaja of Patiala**, and **Sir Dinkar Rao**.
- 🔹 **Restored legislative powers** to Bombay and Madras — beginning **decentralisation**. This reversed the centralising trend that began with the Regulating Act of 1773 and peaked with the Charter Act of 1833. This trend ultimately led to **near-complete provincial autonomy in 1937**.
- 🔹 Provided for **new legislative councils** for Bengal, North-Western Provinces, and Punjab — established in **1862, 1886, and 1897** respectively.
- 🔹 Empowered the Viceroy to make rules for conducting business in council. Also gave **official recognition to the 'Portfolio System'** (introduced by Lord Canning in 1859), under which each council member was in charge of specific government departments.
- 🔹 Empowered the Viceroy to issue **ordinances during emergencies** without the legislative council's approval — valid for **6 months**.

---

## 1️⃣1️⃣ Indian Councils Act of 1892

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Increased the number of non-official members** in Central and provincial legislative councils — but **official majority was maintained**.
- 🔹 Increased the **functions of legislative councils** — members could now **discuss the budget** (the Budget system was introduced in British India in 1860) and **ask questions** of the executive.
- 🔹 Provided for **nomination of non-official members** on the recommendation of:
  - **Central Legislative Council:** Provincial legislative councils and the Bengal Chamber of Commerce.
  - **Provincial Councils:** District boards, municipalities, universities, trade associations, zamindars, and chambers.

> 📌 **Important Note:** This Act introduced a **limited and indirect form of elections**, though the word "election" was not used. The process was called **"nomination made on the recommendation of certain bodies."**

---

## 1️⃣2️⃣ Indian Councils Act of 1909 *(Morley-Minto Reforms)*

> 📌 *Lord **Morley** = Secretary of State for India. Lord **Minto** = Viceroy of India.*

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Considerably increased the size** of legislative councils at Central and Provincial levels. Central Legislative Council: membership raised from **16 to 60**.
- 🔹 Retained **official majority** in the Central council, but allowed **non-official majority** in provincial councils.
- 🔹 **Enlarged deliberative functions** — members could ask **supplementary questions**, move **resolutions on the budget**, etc.
- 🔹 **For the first time**, Indians were associated with **executive councils** of the Viceroy and Governors. **Satyendra Prasad Sinha** became the **first Indian** to join the Viceroy's Executive Council as the **Law Member**.
- 🔹 **Introduced Communal Representation for Muslims** — Muslims were to be elected **only by Muslim voters** ("separate electorate"). This **legalised communalism**. Lord Minto is known as the **Father of Communal Electorate**.
- 🔹 Also provided for **separate representation** of presidency corporations, chambers of commerce, universities, and zamindars.

---

## 1️⃣3️⃣ Government of India Act of 1919 *(Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms)*

> 📌 On **August 20, 1917**, the British Government declared for the **first time** that its objective was the **gradual introduction of responsible Government** in India. *[Responsible Government = government accountable to elected representatives.]*
>
> The Act came into force in **1921**. **Montagu** = Secretary of State for India. **Lord Chelmsford** = Viceroy.

### Key Features:

**1. Division of Subjects (Centre vs. Province):**
- All subjects were divided into **Central subjects** and **Provincial subjects** through **Devolution Rules**.
- This relaxed central control over provinces, though government structure remained **centralised and unitary**.

**2. Dyarchy in Provinces:**
- Provincial subjects were further split into:
  - **Transferred subjects** (e.g., public health, education, local self-government, agriculture) — administered by the **Governor with ministers responsible to the legislature**.
  - **Reserved subjects** (e.g., police, justice, prisons, land revenue, finance) — administered by the **Governor and his executive council, NOT accountable to the legislature**.
- This dual scheme was called **'Dyarchy'** *(double rule, from the Greek 'di-arche')*. However, it was **largely unsuccessful**.

**3. Bicameralism and Direct Elections:**
- **First time** in India — introduced **bicameralism** (two-house legislature) and **direct elections**.
- The Indian Legislative Council was replaced by a **bicameral legislature**:
  - **Upper House: Council of State**
  - **Lower House: Legislative Assembly**
- Majority of members of both houses were **directly elected**.

**4. Indian Representation in Viceroy's Council:**
- At least **3 of 6 members** of the Viceroy's Executive Council (other than the Commander-in-Chief) were to be **Indian**.

**5. Extended Communal Representation:**
- Separate electorates extended to **Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians, and Europeans**.

**6. Limited Franchise:**
- Granted voting rights to a **limited number of people** based on property, tax, or other qualifications.

**7. High Commissioner for India:**
- Created the office of the **High Commissioner for India** in London — took over some functions previously done by the Secretary of State.

**8. Public Service Commission:**
- Provided for a **Central Public Service Commission** — set up in **1926** (based on the recommendation of the Lee Commission on Superior Civil Services in India, 1923–24).

**9. Separation of Provincial Budgets:**
- **For the first time**, **provincial budgets were separated** from the Central budget. Provincial legislatures could now enact their own budgets.

**10. Statutory Commission:**
- Provided for appointment of a **statutory commission** to review the Act's working after **10 years**.

**11. Chamber of Princes:**
- Proposed the establishment of a **Chamber of Princes** (also known as **Narendra Mandal**) — inaugurated in **1921**.
- Consisted of **120 members** (108 Princes + 12 representatives of other states). Headed by the **Viceroy (Governor-General)**.

---

## 📋 Simon Commission (1927)

- In **November 1927** (2 years ahead of schedule), the British Government appointed a **7-member statutory commission** under **Sir John Simon** to review India's Constitution.
- **All members were British** — so **all Indian parties boycotted** the commission.
- The Commission submitted its report in **1930**, recommending:
  - Abolition of Dyarchy
  - Extension of responsible Government in provinces
  - Establishment of a federation of British India and Princely States
  - Continuation of communal electorates
- Three **Round Table Conferences** followed, involving British Government, British India, and Indian Princely States.
- A **'White Paper on Constitutional Reforms'** was prepared and sent to the **Joint Select Committee of the British Parliament**.
- The Committee's recommendations (with changes) became the basis for the **Government of India Act of 1935**.

---

## 📋 Communal Award (1932)

- In **August 1932**, British Prime Minister **Ramsay MacDonald** announced the **Communal Award** — a scheme for minority representation.
- **Continued** separate electorates for Muslims, Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians, and Europeans.
- **Extended** separate electorates to the **depressed classes (Scheduled Castes)** — this was the new and controversial addition.
- **Gandhiji** was deeply distressed at this extension and went on a **fast unto death** at **Yerawada Jail, Poona**.
- This led to a historic agreement between **Congress** and **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** (leader of the depressed classes) — called the **Poona Pact**:
  - **Hindu joint electorate** was retained.
  - **Reserved seats** were given to the depressed classes within the joint electorate.
- The British Government **accepted the Poona Pact** and modified the Communal Award — dropping separate electorates for the depressed classes.
- The Poona Pact's provisions were later **incorporated in the Government of India Act of 1935**.

---

## 1️⃣4️⃣ Government of India Act of 1935

> 📌 This was a **second major milestone** toward responsible government in India. It was an **enormous document** — **321 Sections and 10 Schedules**. Many provisions of this Act were directly borrowed into the Indian Constitution.

### Key Features:

**1. All-India Federation:**
- Provided for an **All-India Federation** of provinces and princely states.
- Powers divided into **three lists:**
  - **Federal List** (Centre) — 59 items
  - **Provincial List** (Provinces) — 54 items
  - **Concurrent List** (Both) — 36 items
  - **Residuary powers** given to the **Viceroy**.
- ⚠️ The federation **never came into being** because the princely states refused to join.

**2. Provincial Autonomy:**
- **Abolished Dyarchy** in provinces. Replaced with **'Provincial Autonomy'** — provinces could act as autonomous units within their defined spheres.
- Introduced **Responsible Government** in provinces — Governor was required to act on the **advice of ministers** accountable to the legislature.
- Came into effect in **1937**, discontinued in **1939**.

**3. Dyarchy at the Centre:**
- Introduced Dyarchy at the Centre (federal subjects divided into reserved and transferred).
- ⚠️ This provision **never came into operation**.

**4. Bicameralism in Provinces:**
- Introduced **bicameral legislatures** in **6 out of 11 provinces**: Bengal, Bombay, Madras, Bihar, Assam, and the United Provinces.

**5. Representation for Depressed Classes:**
- Reserved seats for **depressed classes (Scheduled Castes)**.
- Extended special representation to **women** and **labour (workers)**.

**6. Abolished Council of India:**
- Abolished the Council of India (established in 1858). The Secretary of State for India was now assisted by a team of **advisors**.

**7. Extended Franchise:**
- **About 14% of the total population** got voting rights.

**8. Reserve Bank of India:**
- Provided for the establishment of the **Reserve Bank of India** to control currency and credit.

**9. Public Service Commissions:**
- Provided for:
  - **Federal Public Service Commission**
  - **Provincial Public Service Commission**
  - **Joint Public Service Commission** for two or more provinces.

**10. Federal Court:**
- Provided for the establishment of a **Federal Court** — set up in **1937**.

**11. Separation of Burma:**
- **Separated Burma (now Myanmar)** from India.

**12. New Provinces:**
- Created two new provinces: **Orissa** and **Sind**.

**13. Safeguards for Minorities:**
- Provided for safeguards to protect minority interests.

**14. Federal Railway Authority:**
- Provided for a **Federal Railway Authority** to administer railways.

**15. Auditor-General of India:**
- Provided for an **Auditor-General of India** to audit accounts of the federation and provinces.

## 1️⃣5️⃣ Indian Independence Act of 1947

### Background:
- On **February 20, 1947**, British PM **Clement Atlee** declared that British rule would end by **June 30, 1948** and power would be transferred to Indian hands.
- The **Muslim League's demand** for partition followed.
- On **June 3, 1947**, Britain clarified that the Constitution being framed by the Constituent Assembly could not apply to areas unwilling to accept it.
- On the **same day**, **Lord Mountbatten** (Viceroy) announced the **Mountbatten Plan** for partition — accepted by both Congress and the Muslim League.
- The **Indian Independence Act (1947)** was then passed to give it immediate legal effect.
  - *(The Bill was introduced in British Parliament on July 4, 1947; received Royal Assent on July 18, 1947; came into force on August 15, 1947.)*

### Key Features:
- 🔹 **Ended British rule** from **August 15, 1947**. India became an independent state.
- 🔹 **Partitioned India** into **two independent dominions** — **India** and **Pakistan** — both with the right to **secede from the British Commonwealth**.
- 🔹 **Abolished the office of Viceroy**. Each dominion would have a **Governor-General** appointed by the British King on the **advice of the dominion cabinet**. Britain had **no further responsibility** over governance of India or Pakistan.
- 🔹 **Empowered the Constituent Assemblies** of both dominions to **frame and adopt their own Constitutions** and to **repeal any British Parliament Act**, including the Independence Act itself.
- 🔹 Both Constituent Assemblies were empowered to **legislate for their territories** until new constitutions came into force. No British Act passed after August 15, 1947 would extend to either dominion unless the dominion itself chose to adopt it.
- 🔹 **Abolished the office of Secretary of State for India** — his functions were transferred to the **Secretary of State for Commonwealth Affairs**.
- 🔹 **British paramountcy** over Indian Princely States and **treaty relations with tribal areas lapsed** from August 15, 1947.
- 🔹 **Princely states were free** to join India, join Pakistan, or **remain independent**.
- 🔹 Both dominions would be governed by the **Government of India Act of 1935** (with modifications allowed) until new constitutions were adopted.
- 🔹 **Deprived the British Monarch** of the right to veto bills or reserve bills for approval — but this right was **reserved for the Governor-General**, who could assent to any bill in the name of His Majesty.
- 🔹 **Governor-General of India and provincial Governors** were designated as **constitutional (nominal) heads** — required to act on the advice of the Council of Ministers in all matters.
- 🔹 **Dropped the title 'Emperor of India'** from the royal titles of the King of England.
- 🔹 **Discontinued** the appointment to civil services and reservation of posts by the Secretary of State for India. Civil servants appointed before August 15, 1947 continued to enjoy their earlier benefits.

> 🕛 At the stroke of midnight of **14–15 August 1947**, the British rule ended and power was transferred to India and Pakistan.
>
> - **Lord Mountbatten** became the **first Governor-General** of independent India.
> - **Jawaharlal Nehru** was sworn in as the **first Prime Minister**.
> - The **Constituent Assembly of 1946** became the **Parliament of the Indian Dominion**.
> - The boundaries between the two dominions were determined by a **Boundary Commission headed by Radcliffe**. Pakistan included: West Punjab, Sind, Baluchistan, East Bengal, North-Western Frontier Province, and the district of Sylhet in Assam.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *Added to connect historical concepts to present-day relevance.*

### 🔗 1. From Dyarchy to Cooperative Federalism
The concept of dividing subjects between the Centre and states — pioneered in the **Government of India Act of 1919 and 1935** — lives on in the **7th Schedule of the Indian Constitution** (Union List, State List, Concurrent List).

Today, the idea of **Cooperative Federalism** (Centre and states working together) is central to schemes like:
- **NITI Aayog** (replaced Planning Commission in 2015) — acts as a forum for Centre-State policy dialogue.
- **GST Council** (formed under Article 279A) — a constitutionally mandated body where Centre and states collectively decide tax rates, reflecting shared legislative power.

---

### 🔗 2. The Legacy of Open Competition in Civil Services
The **Charter Act of 1853** and the **Macaulay Committee of 1854** introduced open competition for civil services — a principle that gave birth to the modern **UPSC (Union Public Service Commission)**, which traces its origins to the **Central Public Service Commission set up in 1926** under the Government of India Act of 1919.

`[Modern Example]` — In **2023**, the Government of India introduced **Lateral Entry** into the IAS, allowing domain experts to be appointed directly to Joint Secretary-level posts, sparking a debate about the original vision of an open, merit-based civil service — the same debate that began in 1853!

---

### 🔗 3. Communal Representation and its Modern Echoes
The **Indian Councils Act of 1909** introduced **separate electorates**, and the **Poona Pact of 1932** introduced **reserved seats** for Scheduled Castes — a system that continues today.

`[Modern Example]` — The **104th Constitutional Amendment Act (2019)** extended the **reservation of seats for SCs and STs in the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies by another 10 years** (until 2030), directly continuing the legacy of the Poona Pact. The **Supreme Court's judgment in Jarnail Singh vs. Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018)** also reaffirmed reservation in promotions for SCs/STs, keeping this debate alive.

---

### 🔗 4. Provincial Autonomy → State Autonomy Today
The **Government of India Act of 1935** introduced **Provincial Autonomy** and a **Federal Court**. The Federal Court was the precursor to the **Supreme Court of India**, established in 1950.

`[Modern Example]` — The debate over **State autonomy** remains relevant. In **2023**, the Supreme Court in **Government of NCT of Delhi vs. Union of India** (the Delhi services case) reaffirmed that elected governments must have control over their bureaucracy — echoing the 1935 Act's principle that Governors must act on ministerial advice.

---

### 🔗 5. Reserve Bank of India
`[Modern Example]` — The **Reserve Bank of India**, established under the **Government of India Act of 1935**, turns **90 years old in 2025**. Today, its role in controlling inflation (via **Monetary Policy Committee**, set up in 2016) and managing digital currency (**e-RUPI, CBDC pilot since 2022**) far exceeds anything envisioned by the colonial architects.
