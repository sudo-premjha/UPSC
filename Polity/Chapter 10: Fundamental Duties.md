## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways before you begin reading:**
>
> 1. **Fundamental Duties were NOT in the original Constitution** — they were added in **1976** by the **42nd Amendment Act** based on the **Swaran Singh Committee's** recommendations.
> 2. The duties are listed under **Article 51A** in **Part IVA** — originally **10 duties**, later **11th duty added in 2002** by the **86th Amendment Act**.
> 3. Fundamental Duties are **non-justiciable** (cannot be directly enforced by courts), but Parliament *can* make laws to enforce them.
> 4. They are **inspired by the Constitution of erstwhile USSR** — not by any Western democracy.
> 5. The **Verma Committee (1999)** identified existing laws that already give legal backing to several Fundamental Duties.

---

## 🔹 Section 1: Introduction — Why Were Duties Added Later?

### Rights Without Duties: A Gap in the Original Constitution

Rights and duties are two sides of the same coin — you cannot truly separate them. Yet, when the Indian Constitution was originally drafted, it included **Fundamental Rights** but **did not include Fundamental Duties** for citizens.

The framers of the Constitution did not feel this was necessary at the time. However, they *did* incorporate the **duties of the State** in the form of **Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV)**.

This gap was filled much later:
- **1976** → **10 Fundamental Duties** added by the **42nd Constitutional Amendment Act**
- **2002** → **11th Fundamental Duty** added by the **86th Constitutional Amendment Act**

### 🌐 Global Comparison: Who Else Has Duties in Their Constitution?

This is a **unique and important fact for UPSC:**

| Country | Has Fundamental Duties Listed? |
|---|---|
| USA | ❌ No |
| Canada | ❌ No |
| France | ❌ No |
| Germany | ❌ No |
| Australia | ❌ No |
| **Japan** | ✅ Yes (only major democracy) |
| **India** | ✅ Yes (added in 1976) |
| **USSR (erstwhile)** | ✅ Yes (India's inspiration) |

> **Key Point:** India's Fundamental Duties are **inspired by the Constitution of the erstwhile USSR** (the old Soviet Union). Socialist countries gave *equal importance* to both rights and duties. The USSR Constitution declared that the exercise of rights and freedoms by citizens was **inseparable from the performance of their duties and obligations**.

> Notably, **none of the major democratic constitutions** (USA, Canada, France, Germany, Australia) specifically list duties of citizens. **Japan** is perhaps the only major democratic constitution that does.

## 🔹 Section 2: The Swaran Singh Committee (1976)

### Why Was This Committee Formed?

In **1976**, the **Congress Party** set up the **Sardar Swaran Singh Committee** to make recommendations about Fundamental Duties. The need was strongly felt during the **Internal Emergency (1975–1977)** — a period when anti-national activities had increased and citizens seemed to ignore their obligations to the State.

### What Did the Committee Recommend?

The committee's core message was:
> *"Citizens must realise that along with enjoying their rights, they also have duties to perform."*

It recommended adding a **separate chapter** on Fundamental Duties to the Constitution.

### What Happened Next?

- The **Congress Government at the Centre accepted** these recommendations.
- The **42nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1976** was passed.
- This added a brand new part — **Part IVA** — to the Constitution.
- Part IVA has only **one Article: Article 51A**, which lists the Fundamental Duties.

> **Interesting Contrast:** The Swaran Singh Committee recommended **8 Fundamental Duties**. But the **42nd Amendment Act included 10** — two more than suggested!

---

### ⚠️ Recommendations of the Swaran Singh Committee That Were *NOT* Accepted

These three recommendations were specifically **rejected** and never incorporated into the Constitution:

1. **Penalty/Punishment Clause:** Parliament may provide for imposition of suitable penalty or punishment for non-compliance with or refusal to observe any of the duties.

2. **No Judicial Review of Penalty:** No law imposing such penalty or punishment shall be questioned in any court on the grounds of infringement of Fundamental Rights or repugnancy to any other Constitutional provision.

3. **Duty to Pay Taxes:** This should also be a Fundamental Duty of citizens.

> 💡 **UPSC Tip:** The fact that the Swaran Singh Committee recommended a "duty to pay taxes" but it was *not* included is frequently asked in exams.

## 🔹 Section 3: The Complete List of Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

According to **Article 51A**, it shall be the **duty of every citizen of India:**

| # | Fundamental Duty | Simple Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | To **abide by the Constitution** and respect its ideals and institutions, the **National Flag** and the **National Anthem** | Follow the Constitution, respect the flag and anthem |
| **2** | To **cherish and follow the noble ideals** that inspired the national struggle for freedom | Remember and live by the values of our freedom fighters |
| **3** | To **uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity and integrity** of India | Keep India united and strong |
| **4** | To **defend the country** and render national service when called upon | Be ready to serve the nation when needed |
| **5** | To **promote harmony and common brotherhood** among all people, transcending religious, linguistic, regional or sectional diversities; and to **renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women** | Live in harmony; respect all communities; respect women |
| **6** | To **value and preserve the rich heritage** of the country's **composite culture** (mixed/plural cultural identity of India) | Protect India's diverse cultural heritage |
| **7** | To **protect and improve the natural environment** including forests, lakes, rivers and wildlife; and to have **compassion for living creatures** | Care for the environment and animals |
| **8** | To **develop scientific temper, humanism** and the **spirit of inquiry and reform** | Think rationally, be humane, and question for progress |
| **9** | To **safeguard public property** and to **abjure violence** (give up violence) | Don't destroy public property; reject violence |
| **10** | To **strive towards excellence** in all spheres of individual and collective activity | Work hard and aim for the best, individually and collectively |
| **11** ⭐ | To **provide opportunities for education** to his/her child or ward between the **age of 6 and 14 years** | Educate your children — *Added by 86th Amendment Act, 2002* |

> ⭐ **Duty 11 is special** — it was added **26 years after** the original 10 duties, by the **86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> The **11th Fundamental Duty** (education for children aged 6–14) is directly linked to **Article 21A** (Right to Education), also inserted by the 86th Amendment Act, 2002. This was operationalised through the **Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education (RTE) Act, 2009**. As per government data (2022–23), India's **Gross Enrolment Ratio (GER)** at the elementary level has crossed **99%**, reflecting the long-term impact of this constitutional duty.

## 🔹 Section 4: Features of Fundamental Duties

Here are the key characteristics you must know:

- **Moral + Civic Mix:**
  - Some duties are **moral duties** — e.g., cherishing the noble ideals of the freedom struggle is a *moral precept*.
  - Some are **civic duties** — e.g., respecting the Constitution, National Flag, and National Anthem is a *civic duty*.

- **Rooted in Indian Tradition:**
  - They refer to values that have been a part of **Indian tradition, mythology, religions and practices**.
  - They are essentially a **codification of tasks integral to the Indian way of life** — not imported Western ideas.

- **Only for Citizens, Not Foreigners:**
  - Unlike some **Fundamental Rights** (Articles 14, 20, 21, 21A, 22, 23, 24, 25, 26, 27, 28) which apply to *all persons* including foreigners, **Fundamental Duties apply ONLY to citizens of India**.

- **Non-Justiciable** (cannot be directly enforced in court):
  - Like the **Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP)**, Fundamental Duties are also **non-justiciable** — the Constitution does not provide for their *direct* enforcement by courts.
  - There is **no legal sanction** for violating them.
  - However, **Parliament is free to enforce them through suitable legislation**.

## 🔹 Section 5: Criticism of Fundamental Duties

Critics have raised the following objections:

1. **Incomplete List:**
   The list is **not exhaustive**. It does not include other important duties such as:
   - Casting vote
   - Paying taxes (actually recommended by Swaran Singh Committee)
   - Family planning

2. **Vague and Ambiguous Language:**
   Several phrases are difficult for ordinary citizens to understand. For example:
   - *"Noble ideals"* — What exactly counts as a "noble ideal"? (Even the **Bhagat Singh cult** could be interpreted by some as such)
   - *"Composite culture"* (India's mixed, plural cultural identity)
   - *"Scientific temper"*
   - *"Humanism"*

   > As **D.D. Chawla** (then President of the National Forum of Lawyers and Legal Aid, Delhi) observed: *"The duties may be spelt out in a more concrete form; one is left guessing the noble ideals... The values are beyond the ken of the general run of the people and carry no meaning to them."* *(Socialist India, October 23, 1976)*

3. **Mere Moral Precepts (Code of Conduct without Teeth):**
   Critics called them a **"code of moral precepts"** because they are non-justiciable. Ironically, the **Swaran Singh Committee had itself suggested penalties** for non-performance of Fundamental Duties — a suggestion the Government did not accept.

4. **Superfluous (Unnecessary) Inclusion:**
   - **C.K. Daphtary**, former Attorney General of India, argued that **more than 99.9% of citizens were law-abiding** and didn't need to be told about their duties.
   - He said: *"To tell them what their duties are implies that they are not content. If that is the case after 26 years, it is not their fault."*
   - **A.K. Sen** also opposed the inclusion, arguing: *"A democratic set-up, instead of thriving on the willing cooperation and confidence of people, is reduced to the position of a harsh school master asking the student to stand up on the classroom bench because he has not done the home work."*

5. **Wrong Placement in the Constitution:**
   Critics argued that placing Fundamental Duties as an **appendage (attachment) to Part IV** (which deals with DPSPs) has **reduced their value and significance**. They should have been placed **after Part III** (Fundamental Rights) to give them equal standing.

## 🔹 Section 6: Significance of Fundamental Duties

Despite criticisms, Fundamental Duties hold great importance:

1. **Reminder of Responsibility:**
   They remind citizens that while enjoying rights, they must also be conscious of **duties they owe to the country, society, and fellow citizens**.

2. **Warning Against Anti-National Activities:**
   They serve as a **warning against anti-national and anti-social activities** such as:
   - Burning the national flag
   - Destroying public property

3. **Source of Inspiration and Discipline:**
   - They inspire citizens and promote a **sense of discipline and commitment**.
   - They create a feeling that citizens are **not mere spectators** but **active participants** in achieving national goals.

4. **Aid to Courts in Constitutional Interpretation:**
   This is a very important point for UPSC:

   - **Mohan Kumar Singhania vs. Union of India (1991):** The **Supreme Court held** that **Article 51A (Fundamental Duties) can be used to interpret ambiguous laws** to determine their constitutionality.

   - **Re, Ramlila Maidan Incident (2012):** The court observed that *"a common thread runs through Parts III, IV and IVA of the Constitution."*
     - **Part III** → Fundamental Rights
     - **Part IV** → Directive Principles (fundamental principles of governance)
     - **Part IVA** → Fundamental Duties
     The court said that while interpreting any of these provisions, it will always be advisable to **examine the scope and impact of such interpretation on all three constitutional aspects** together.

5. **Enforceable Through Legislation:**
   Parliament *can* provide for **imposition of appropriate penalty or punishment** for failure to fulfil any Fundamental Duty.

---

### 💬 Justification by Political Leaders

**H.R. Gokhale** (then Law Minister) explained why duties were added 26 years after the Constitution's inauguration:
> *"In post-independent India, particularly on the eve of emergency in June 1975, a section of the people showed no anxiety to fulfil their fundamental obligations of respecting the established legal order... the provisions of the chapter on fundamental duties would have a sobering effect on these restless spirits who have had a host of anti-national subversive and unconstitutional agitations in the past."*

**Indira Gandhi** (then Prime Minister) justified the inclusion by saying:
> *"The moral value of fundamental duties would be not to smother rights but to establish a democratic balance by making the people conscious of their duties equally as they are conscious of their rights."*

---

### 🤝 Political Consensus — Even Opposition Eventually Agreed

- The **Opposition in Parliament strongly opposed** the inclusion of Fundamental Duties when the Congress government introduced them.
- However, the **new Janata Government under Morarji Desai** (post-Emergency period) **did NOT remove the Fundamental Duties**, even though it sought to undo many other changes made by the 42nd Amendment Act through the:
  - **43rd Amendment Act (1977)**
  - **44th Amendment Act (1978)**
- This shows **eventual cross-party consensus** on the necessity and desirability of Fundamental Duties.
- This consensus was further confirmed when **one more Fundamental Duty was added in 2002** by the **86th Amendment Act**.

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> The **86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002** which added the 11th Fundamental Duty was also the same amendment that inserted **Article 21A** (Right to Education as a Fundamental Right) and amended **Article 45** (DPSP on early childhood care and education). This was one of the most comprehensive single-amendment changes in recent constitutional history. The **RTE Act, 2009** remains the operative law giving force to both the right and the corresponding duty.

## 🔹 Section 7: The Verma Committee (1999) — Existing Laws That Support Fundamental Duties

The **Justice J.S. Verma Committee on Fundamental Duties of the Citizens (1999)** identified existing **legal provisions** that already give force to several Fundamental Duties:

| # | Law | What It Does | Related Duty |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act (1971)** | Prevents disrespect to the Constitution, National Flag, and National Anthem | Duty 1 |
| **2** | **Various Criminal Laws** | Punish for encouraging enmity between people on grounds of language, race, religion, place of birth, etc. | Duty 5 |
| **3** | **Protection of Civil Rights Act (1955)** *(formerly known as the Untouchability (Offences) Act till 1976)* | Provides punishment for offences related to caste and religion | Duty 5 |
| **4** | **Indian Penal Code (IPC)** | Declares imputations and assertions prejudicial to **national integration** as punishable offences | Duty 3 |
| **5** | **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, 1967** | Provides for declaration of a **communal organisation** as an **unlawful association** | Duty 5 |
| **6** | **Representation of People Act (1951)** | Disqualifies MPs/MLAs for **corrupt practices** like soliciting votes on grounds of religion or promoting enmity between communities | Duty 5 |
| **7** | **Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972** | **Prohibits trade** in rare and endangered species | Duty 7 |
| **8** | **Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980** | Checks **indiscriminate deforestation** and diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes | Duty 7 |

> 💡 **UPSC Tip:** The Verma Committee essentially showed that Fundamental Duties are **not toothless** — existing laws already enforce many of them, even if Article 51A itself is non-justiciable.

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> Several laws have been strengthened in recent years in direct alignment with Fundamental Duties:
> - The **Wildlife Protection (Amendment) Act, 2022** significantly expanded the list of protected species and increased penalties — directly reinforcing **Duty 7** (protection of wildlife).
> - The **Environment Protection (Amendment) Rules, 2021** and the **Forest Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2023** represent recent legislative actions connected to **Duty 7**.
> - The **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA)** has been extensively used in the 2019–2024 period to act against organisations deemed threats to national integrity, reinforcing **Duty 3**.
> - In **Aruna Roy vs. Union of India (2002)**, the Supreme Court upheld the **value-based education system** in schools as consistent with Fundamental Duties, particularly **Duty 8** (scientific temper) and **Duty 6** (composite culture). `[Modern Example]`

## 📋 Important Cases Explained

### 🔴 Case 1: Mohan Kumar Singhania vs. Union of India (1991)

**What happened:** A question arose about whether a law related to government service conditions was constitutionally valid.

**What the Supreme Court held:**
- The Supreme Court held that **Article 51A (Fundamental Duties) can be actively used by courts to interpret ambiguous laws**.
- If a law's meaning is unclear, courts should look at the Fundamental Duties to determine whether the law upholds or violates the spirit of the Constitution.
- This was a landmark ruling because it gave **judicial teeth** to Fundamental Duties even though they are non-justiciable in a direct sense.

**Why it matters for UPSC:** It established that Fundamental Duties are **not merely decorative text** — they have interpretive value and can influence judicial decisions.

---

### 🔴 Case 2: Re, Ramlila Maidan Incident (2012)

**Background:** This case arose from the midnight police crackdown on **Baba Ramdev's anti-corruption protest at Ramlila Maidan, New Delhi, in June 2011**. The police used force to disperse sleeping protesters, raising issues of Fundamental Rights violations.

**What the Supreme Court observed:**
- The court made an important constitutional observation: *"A common thread runs through Parts III, IV and IVA of the Constitution."*
- It said that **Fundamental Rights (Part III)**, **Directive Principles (Part IV)**, and **Fundamental Duties (Part IVA)** are all **interconnected and must be read together**.
- While interpreting any provision from these parts, courts must examine its impact on **all three constitutional dimensions** simultaneously.

**Why it matters for UPSC:** This case established the **holistic/integrated approach** to constitutional interpretation — Parts III, IV, and IVA are a unified constitutional vision, not isolated provisions.

## 📊 Quick Comparison: Fundamental Rights vs. Fundamental Duties

| Feature | Fundamental Rights | Fundamental Duties |
|---|---|---|
| **Part of Constitution** | Part III | Part IVA |
| **Article** | Articles 12–35 | Article 51A |
| **Added** | Original Constitution (1950) | 42nd Amendment (1976) + 86th Amendment (2002) |
| **Applicable to** | All persons (citizens + foreigners for some) | Only Citizens |
| **Justiciable?** | ✅ Yes (Courts can enforce) | ❌ No (Non-justiciable) |
| **Number** | 6 categories | 11 duties |
| **Inspiration** | Western Liberal Democracies | Constitution of erstwhile USSR |
