# Chapter: Parliamentary System

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **India follows a Parliamentary System** — the executive (Cabinet) is responsible to the legislature (Parliament). Articles **74 & 75** cover the Centre; Articles **163 & 164** cover States.
> 2. The **President is the nominal executive** (titular head); the **Prime Minister is the real executive** (actual power-holder).
> 3. **Collective Responsibility** (Article 75) is the *bedrock* of the parliamentary system — ministers swim and sink together.
> 4. India's founding fathers chose the parliamentary system over the presidential system for **4 key reasons**: familiarity, more responsibility, avoiding legislature-executive conflict, and India's plural society.
> 5. India's parliamentary model is **inspired by Britain but is NOT a copy** — 6 important differences exist between them.

---

## 1. What is a Parliamentary System? — The Basic Idea

Modern democratic governments are divided into two types based on how the **executive** (the ruling government) and the **legislature** (Parliament) relate to each other:

| Type | Definition | Also Known As | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Parliamentary** | Executive IS responsible to the legislature | Cabinet Govt / Responsible Govt / Westminster Model | India, Britain, Japan, Canada |
| **Presidential** | Executive is NOT responsible to the legislature; has fixed tenure | Non-responsible / Fixed Executive System | USA, Brazil, Russia, Sri Lanka |

### 🔑 What Does "Responsible" Mean Here?

In a parliamentary system, the government (Cabinet) can be **thrown out of power** by Parliament if it loses the Parliament's trust (via a **no-confidence motion**). In a presidential system, this cannot happen — the President has a fixed term.

### 📖 Why the Name "Westminster Model"?

The parliamentary system is called the **Westminster model** because it originated in the **British Parliament**, which is located in Westminster, London.

### 📖 Why is it Called "Cabinet System"?

Political thinker **Ivor Jennings** called it the *"cabinet system"* because the **Cabinet is the nucleus (core centre) of power** in a parliamentary setup.

## 2. The Evolution: From "First Among Equals" to "Prime Ministerial Government"

- **Earlier view (British tradition):** The Prime Minister was called **"primus inter pares"** (Latin: *first among equals*) — meaning the PM was just one among the other ministers, slightly senior but not dominant.
- **Modern view:** The PM's power has grown **enormously** compared to the Cabinet. Political analysts like **Crossman** and **Mackintosh** now call the British system a **"Prime Ministerial Government"** — recognising the PM's dominant role.
- **This applies equally to India** — the Indian PM too exercises dominant leadership.

## 3. Features of Parliamentary Government in India

*(These are the 8 core principles — memorise all 8)*

---

### Feature 1: Nominal and Real Executives

- **President** = **Nominal Executive** (also called *de jure* executive or *titular* executive) → Head of the **State**
- **Prime Minister** = **Real Executive** (also called *de facto* executive) → Head of the **Government**

> 💡 *Think of it this way:* The President is like a company's "Chairman" who gives approval, while the PM is like the "CEO" who actually runs things.

**Article 74** says a **Council of Ministers, headed by the PM**, shall *aid and advise* the President. This advice is **binding on the President** *(the President must follow it)*.

> 📌 *Footnote:* The **42nd Amendment Act (1976)** and **44th Amendment Act (1978)** made the ministerial advice *legally binding* on the President.

---

### Feature 2: Majority Party Rule

- The political party that wins **majority seats in the Lok Sabha** forms the government.
- The **leader of that party** is appointed **Prime Minister** by the President.
- Other ministers are appointed by the President **on the advice of the PM**.
- If **no single party gets majority** → a **coalition** *(alliance of multiple parties)* of parties may be invited to form the government.

---

### Feature 3: Collective Responsibility ⭐

- This is the **bedrock (foundation) principle** of parliamentary government.
- **Article 75** states that ministers are **collectively responsible** to Parliament — and specifically to the **Lok Sabha**.
- Ministers act as a **team** — they *"swim and sink together"* (if one falls, all fall).
- The **Lok Sabha can remove the entire ministry** (Council of Ministers including the PM) by passing a **vote of no-confidence**.

---

### Feature 4: Political Homogeneity

- Usually, all ministers belong to the **same political party** and therefore share the **same political ideology** *(similar set of political beliefs)*.
- In a **coalition government**, ministers from different parties are **bound by consensus** *(mutual agreement)*.

---

### Feature 5: Double Membership

- Ministers are members of **both** the legislature (Parliament) and the executive (Cabinet) at the same time.
- A person **cannot be a minister without being a Member of Parliament (MP)**.
- **Exception rule:** A non-MP can be appointed as minister, but must **become an MP within 6 consecutive months**, failing which they **cease to be a minister**.

---

### Feature 6: Leadership of the Prime Minister

The Prime Minister plays a **triple leadership role**:
- Leader of the **Council of Ministers**
- Leader of the **Parliament**
- Leader of the **party in power**

---

### Feature 7: Dissolution of the Lower House

- The **President can dissolve the Lok Sabha** on the **recommendation of the Prime Minister**.
- This means the PM can advise the President to **dissolve Lok Sabha before its full term** and call fresh elections.
- This gives the **executive the right to dissolve the legislature** — a key feature of the parliamentary system.

---

### Feature 8: Secrecy

- Ministers operate on the principle of **secrecy of procedure** — they cannot publicly reveal the details of their discussions, policies, and decisions.
- All ministers take an **oath of secrecy** before entering office.
- This oath is **administered by the President**.

---

## 4. Features of the Presidential System (American Model)

*(For comparison — these are the features of the USA's system)*

1. The **American President is both** the Head of State *and* Head of Government — combining ceremonial and executive roles in one person.
2. The President is elected by an **Electoral College** for a **fixed term of 4 years** and can only be removed by **impeachment** *(formal removal process)* for a grave unconstitutional act — NOT by a no-confidence vote.
3. The President governs with a **Cabinet** (or a smaller informal body called the **"Kitchen Cabinet"**). This cabinet is purely an **advisory body** consisting of **non-elected departmental secretaries** who are:
   - Selected, appointed, **and** removed by the President
   - Responsible **only** to the President
4. The President and secretaries are **not responsible to the Congress** (US Parliament) for their acts. They are **not members of Congress** and do not attend its sessions.
5. The President **cannot dissolve** the **House of Representatives** (the lower house of Congress).
6. The **doctrine of separation of powers** *(the idea that legislative, executive, and judicial powers must be separate)* is the foundation of the American system. All three powers are vested in **three independent organs**.

## 5. Merits of the Parliamentary System

*(Why it works well — 5 advantages)*

### ✅ Merit 1: Harmony Between Legislature and Executive
- The executive is a **part of the legislature** — both are interdependent.
- Result: **Less conflict** between the two organs; more cooperation and smooth governance.

### ✅ Merit 2: Responsible Government
- Ministers are responsible to Parliament for **all their acts** — both acts of *omission* (what they didn't do) and *commission* (what they did).
- Parliament controls ministers through:
  - **Question Hour**
  - **Discussions and debates**
  - **Adjournment Motion**
  - **No-Confidence Motion**

### ✅ Merit 3: Prevents Despotism *(prevents one-person dictatorship)*
- Executive power is vested in a **group** (Council of Ministers), not one person.
- This **dispersal of authority** prevents dictatorial tendencies.
- The executive can also be removed by a no-confidence motion.

### ✅ Merit 4: Ready Alternative Government
- If the ruling party loses majority, the **opposition party can be invited to form the government** — without needing fresh elections.
- As **Dr. Jennings** says: *"The leader of the opposition is the alternative Prime Minister."*

### ✅ Merit 5: Wide Representation
- The executive (Council of Ministers) consists of elected representatives.
- The PM can ensure **representation to all sections, communities, and regions** while selecting ministers.

---

## 6. Demerits of the Parliamentary System

*(Why it has problems — 5 disadvantages)*

### ❌ Demerit 1: Unstable Government
- No guarantee that a government will **survive its full term**.
- Ministers depend on **majority legislators** for survival.
- Threats to stability:
  - **No-confidence motion**
  - **Political defection** *(switching party loyalties)*
  - **Evils of multi-party coalition**

> 📌 *Examples of unstable governments in India:*
> Morarji Desai, Charan Singh, V.P. Singh, Chandra Sekhar, Deve Gowda, and I.K. Gujral — all headed short-lived governments.

### ❌ Demerit 2: No Continuity of Policies
- Uncertain tenure means **long-term policy planning is difficult**.
- A change in the ruling party often brings **policy reversals**.

> 📌 *Example:* The Janata Government under **Morarji Desai (1977)** reversed many policies of the previous Congress government. The Congress government reversed them again after returning to power in **1980**.

### ❌ Demerit 3: Dictatorship of the Cabinet
- When the ruling party has an **absolute majority**, the Cabinet becomes **autocratic** *(all-powerful, unchecked)*.
- **H.J. Laski** warned that the parliamentary system gives the executive an opportunity for **tyranny**.
- British historian **Ramsay Muir** (in his book *"How Britain is Governed"*) complained of the **"dictatorship of the cabinet"**.

> 📌 *Indian Example:* This was witnessed during the eras of **Indira Gandhi** and **Rajiv Gandhi**.

### ❌ Demerit 4: Against Separation of Powers
- Legislature and executive are **fused together** — not separate.
- As **Walter Bagehot** pointed out: *"The Cabinet is a hyphen that joins the buckle that binds the executive and legislative departments together."*
- This goes against the theory of **separation of powers**.

> 📌 *Background on Separation of Powers:* This theory was propounded by **Montesquieu**, a French political thinker, in his book **"The Spirit of Laws" (1748)**. He argued that concentration of powers in one person would result in **despotism** and destroy **individual liberty**.

### ❌ Demerit 5: Government by Amateurs *(non-experts running government)*
- Ministers are **not experts** in their respective ministries.
- The PM's **choice of ministers is restricted to MPs only** — cannot pick outside talent.
- Ministers divide their time between:
  - Parliamentary work
  - Cabinet meetings
  - Party activities
- This affects **administrative efficiency**.

## 7. Why Did India Adopt the Parliamentary System?

*(In the Constituent Assembly, **K.T. Shah** favoured the US Presidential system — but the majority preferred the British Parliamentary system for these 4 reasons:)*

---

### Reason 1: Familiarity with the System
- The Constitution-makers had **experience with the parliamentary system** during British rule in India.
- **K.M. Munshi** argued in the Constituent Assembly:

> *"For the last thirty or forty years, some kind of responsibility has been introduced in the governance of this country. Our constitutional traditions have become Parliamentary. After this experience, why should we go back and buy a novel experience."* — Constituent Assembly Debates, Volume VII, pp. 284–5.

---

### Reason 2: Preference for More Responsibility
- **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** explained the dilemma clearly:

> *"A democratic executive must satisfy two conditions: stability and responsibility. The American system gives more stability but less responsibility. The British system gives more responsibility but less stability. The Draft Constitution in recommending the parliamentary system has preferred more responsibility to more stability."* — Constituent Assembly Debates, Volume VII, p. 32.

---

### Reason 3: Need to Avoid Legislature-Executive Conflicts
- The framers wanted to avoid **constant conflict** between the legislature and the executive — which is common in the presidential system (as seen in the USA).
- They felt an **infant democracy** could not afford perpetual conflict between these two organs.
- They wanted a system **conducive to the manifold (all-round) development** of the country.

---

### Reason 4: Nature of Indian Society
- India is one of the **most heterogeneous** *(diverse)* and **complex plural societies** in the world.
- The parliamentary system offers **greater scope for representation** of various sections, interests, and regions in the government.
- This **promotes national unity** and builds a united India.

---

## 8. Should India Switch to a Presidential System? The Debate

- This debate has been alive **since the 1970s**.
- The **Swaran Singh Committee**, appointed by the Congress government in **1975**, examined this question in detail.
- **Conclusion:** The committee opined that the parliamentary system *"has been doing well"* and **there is no need to replace it** with the presidential system.

## 9. Comparing Parliamentary vs Presidential Systems

### 📊 Feature-by-Feature Comparison

| Aspect | Parliamentary System | Presidential System |
|---|---|---|
| **Executive** | Dual executive (nominal + real) | Single executive (President = head of state + govt) |
| **Tenure** | Majority party rule; no fixed term | President elected for fixed term separately |
| **Responsibility** | Collective responsibility to legislature | Non-responsible to legislature |
| **Ideology** | Political homogeneity (same party/coalition) | Homogeneity may not exist |
| **Membership** | Double membership (minister = MP) | Single membership (executive ≠ legislator) |
| **Leadership** | Leadership of Prime Minister | Domination of President |
| **Dissolution** | Lower House can be dissolved | No dissolution of Lower House |
| **Powers** | Fusion of powers | Separation of powers |

### ⚖️ Merits & Demerits Comparison

| | Parliamentary | Presidential |
|---|---|---|
| **Merits** | Harmony between legislature & executive; Responsible govt; Prevents despotism; Wide representation | Stable govt; Definite long-term policies; Based on separation of powers; Government by experts |
| **Demerits** | Unstable govt; No continuity of policies; Against separation of powers; Govt by amateurs | Conflict between legislature & executive; Non-responsible govt; May lead to autocracy; Narrow representation |

## 10. Distinction Between Indian and British Parliamentary Models

*(India borrowed from Britain — but is NOT an exact copy. Here are the 6 key differences:)*

| # | Aspect | British Model | Indian Model |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Head of State** | **Monarch** (King/Queen) — hereditary position | **President** — elected position; India is a **republic** |
| 2 | **Supremacy of Parliament** | Based on **doctrine of Parliamentary Sovereignty** — Parliament is supreme | Parliament is **NOT supreme** — limited by written Constitution, federalism, judicial review, and Fundamental Rights |
| 3 | **PM's House membership** | PM **must** be a member of the **House of Commons** (lower house) | PM **can be a member of either house** — Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha |
| 4 | **Ministers' Parliamentary membership** | Usually, **only MPs** are appointed as ministers | A **non-MP can be appointed minister** for up to **6 months** |
| 5 | **Legal Responsibility of Ministers** | Britain has **legal responsibility** of ministers — ministers must **countersign** official acts of the Head of State | India has **no such system** — ministers need not countersign Presidential acts |
| 6 | **Shadow Cabinet** | A unique British institution — the **opposition** forms a "Shadow Cabinet" to monitor the ruling cabinet and prepare for future governance | **No Shadow Cabinet** exists in India |

> 📌 *Footnote example on PM from Rajya Sabha:* Three Prime Ministers — **Indira Gandhi (1966)**, **Deve Gowda (1996)**, and **Manmohan Singh (2004)** — were members of the **Rajya Sabha**, not the Lok Sabha.

## ⚖️ Important Court Cases Explained in Detail

> *(The original chapter does not cite specific landmark court cases directly. However, the key constitutional provisions mentioned — especially relating to the binding nature of ministerial advice — have been tested in courts. Here are the most relevant judicial interpretations:)*

---

### 🏛️ Case 1: Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)

**What was the dispute?**
This case questioned whether the President (and Governors of states) could act in their **personal discretion** or whether they were **bound by the advice of the Council of Ministers**.

**What did the Supreme Court decide?**
- The Supreme Court held that the **President and Governor must act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers** in all matters, **except where the Constitution explicitly provides for discretion**.
- The court confirmed the **nominal vs. real executive distinction** — the President/Governor is a constitutional head and not a real executive.

**Why is it relevant to this chapter?**
- This directly supports **Feature 1** (Nominal and Real Executives).
- It established that India's parliamentary system means the **elected executive (PM + Cabinet) is the real authority**.

---

### 🏛️ Case 2: S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) ⭐⭐ (Most Important)

**What was the dispute?**
S.R. Bommai was the Chief Minister of Karnataka. The Governor recommended **President's Rule** (Article 356) in Karnataka, dismissing his government without allowing him to prove majority on the floor of the house. The question was: *Can a government be dismissed without a floor test?*

**What did the Supreme Court decide?**
The Supreme Court gave a **landmark 9-judge constitutional bench judgment** with these key holdings:

1. **Floor of the House is the only place** where a government's majority should be tested — the Governor cannot rely on private reports or personal assessment to dismiss a government.
2. **President's Rule (Article 356)** is subject to **judicial review** — courts can examine whether it was imposed properly.
3. If President's Rule is found unconstitutional, the court can **restore the dismissed government**.
4. The **Proclamation of President's Rule** requires **Parliamentary approval** within 2 months, failing which it lapses.
5. The judgment strongly reinforced the principles of **federalism** and **parliamentary democracy**.

**Why is it relevant to this chapter?**
- It directly reinforces **Feature 3 (Collective Responsibility)** — the ministry must prove its majority in the legislature, not just claim it.
- It protects the **stability and integrity of the parliamentary system** at the state level.
- It prevents arbitrary use of **executive power** (President/Governor) against elected legislatures.

> 🌍 **Modern Relevance:** The Bommai judgment's principle of the "floor test" was applied in:
> - **Karnataka (2018):** Governor B.S. Yediyurappa was directed by the Supreme Court to face a floor test within 24 hours.
> - **Maharashtra (2019):** Similar direction by the Supreme Court in the Devendra Fadnavis government controversy.

---

### 🏛️ Case 3: U.N.R. Rao v. Indira Gandhi (1971)

**What was the dispute?**
After the Lok Sabha was dissolved in 1970, the question arose: *Can a caretaker Council of Ministers (which has lost its Lok Sabha majority or whose Lok Sabha is dissolved) continue to function?* Is such a ministry constitutionally valid?

**What did the Supreme Court decide?**
- The Supreme Court held that the **Council of Ministers can continue even after the dissolution of the Lok Sabha**, until a new government is formed after fresh elections.
- Article 74 requires the President to always have a **Council of Ministers to aid and advise** — there cannot be a constitutional vacuum.

**Why is it relevant to this chapter?**
- It clarifies the **continuous nature of the executive** in the parliamentary system.
- It reinforces the principle of **Article 74** — that a council of ministers must always exist.

---

### 🏛️ Case 4: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

**What was the dispute?**
This was the historic case about whether Parliament can amend the **basic structure of the Constitution**, including the parliamentary democratic system itself.

**What did the Supreme Court decide?**
- The Supreme Court by a **7:6 majority** held that while Parliament has wide powers to amend the Constitution, it **cannot alter the "basic structure"** of the Constitution.
- The **parliamentary democratic system** and the **rule of law** are considered part of this **basic structure**.

**Why is it relevant to this chapter?**
- This means India **cannot simply switch from a parliamentary to a presidential system** through a constitutional amendment — as that would alter the basic structure.
- It permanently protects the core features of India's parliamentary democracy.

---

### 🏛️ Case 5: Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)

**What was the dispute?**
This case dealt with the **disqualification of legislators** and the **dissolution of the Legislative Assembly** by the Governor of Arunachal Pradesh, which led to a political crisis in the state.

**What did the Supreme Court decide?**
- The Supreme Court **restored the dismissed government** of Nabam Tuki.
- It held that the Governor **cannot act contrary to the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers** except in very limited circumstances.
- Reinforced the **Shamsher Singh** and **Bommai** judgments — Governor is a nominal executive.

**Why is it relevant to this chapter?**
- Reinforces the principle that even in states, the **real executive is the elected Chief Minister and Cabinet**, not the nominated Governor.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> *[Modern Examples added below — these connect the chapter's concepts to recent events]*

---

### [Modern Example 1] — Coalition Government & Collective Responsibility

**Concept Link:** Feature 3 (Collective Responsibility) + Demerit 1 (Unstable Government)

- After the **2024 General Elections**, the BJP did not secure an outright majority on its own. The **National Democratic Alliance (NDA)**, a coalition led by BJP, formed the government with coalition partners like TDP and JDU playing a crucial role.
- This is a live example of **coalition dynamics** — where **collective responsibility** is tested when coalition partners have differing policy preferences.
- The tension between **coalition compulsions** (keeping partners happy) and **policy continuity** remains a live challenge, validating the chapter's demerit of "No Continuity of Policies."

---

### [Modern Example 2] — Floor Test Doctrine (S.R. Bommai Applied)

**Concept Link:** Collective Responsibility + S.R. Bommai judgment

- **Maharashtra (2022 — Eknath Shinde vs. Uddhav Thackeray Crisis):** A major political crisis erupted when a faction of Shiv Sena MLAs rebelled. The Supreme Court in *Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra* (2023) examined whether the Governor's invitation to Eknath Shinde to prove majority was constitutionally valid.
  - The Court held that the Governor's action was **questionable** but did not restore Uddhav Thackeray's government as he had already resigned.
  - This case reinforced the **Bommai principle** that majority must be tested on the floor of the house.

---

### [Modern Example 3] — Presidential vs Parliamentary Debate in India (Revived)

**Concept Link:** Reasons for Adopting Parliamentary System + Swaran Singh Committee

- The debate was recently revived when voices within the ruling establishment discussed moving towards a **"One Nation, One Election"** (simultaneous elections for Lok Sabha and state assemblies) framework.
- The **High-Level Committee on Simultaneous Elections (Ram Nath Kovind Committee, 2023)** submitted its report recommending simultaneous elections.
- While this doesn't change the parliamentary system, it addresses the demerit of **instability and frequent elections** that disrupt governance and policy continuity.
- `[Modern Example]` The Cabinet accepted the report's recommendations in **2024**.

---

### [Modern Example 4] — Representation in Cabinet (Wide Representation Merit)

**Concept Link:** Merit 5 (Wide Representation)

- The **Modi Cabinet (2024)** after NDA's coalition victory consciously included ministers from coalition partner states (Andhra Pradesh — TDP; Bihar — JDU) — a direct example of how the parliamentary system allows **regional representation in the cabinet**.
- This validates the founding fathers' reasoning that India's diverse society needs a parliamentary system for inclusive governance.

---

### [Modern Example 5] — Anti-Defection Law & Political Homogeneity

**Concept Link:** Feature 4 (Political Homogeneity) + Demerit 1 (Unstable Government due to defection)

- The **Tenth Schedule (Anti-Defection Law, 1985)** was added to prevent the "evil of political defection" mentioned in the chapter.
- The **Supreme Court in Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** upheld the validity of this law but ruled that the Speaker's decision is subject to judicial review.
- In 2023, the Supreme Court in the **Maharashtra political crisis case** again examined anti-defection provisions, highlighting the ongoing tension between party discipline (political homogeneity) and democratic dissent.
