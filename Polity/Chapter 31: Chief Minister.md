## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. The **Chief Minister (CM)** is the **real executive authority (de facto)** at the state level — the Governor is only the nominal head.
> 2. **Article 164** governs the appointment of the CM, but the actual process follows **parliamentary conventions**, not a rigid written procedure.
> 3. The CM is the **pivot of the state cabinet** — his/her resignation collapses the entire council of ministers.
> 4. The **S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)** case is a landmark ruling that protects the CM from arbitrary dismissal by the Governor.
> 5. The **Sarkaria Commission (1983–88)** laid down clear guidelines for Governor's role in CM selection during hung assembly situations.

---

## 1. 🏛️ Who is the Chief Minister? — The Real vs. Nominal Executive

India follows a **parliamentary system of government** (a system where the executive is responsible to the legislature). In this system, there are two types of executive heads at the state level:

| Type | Who | What it Means |
|---|---|---|
| **Nominal Executive** (*de jure* — legally titled but limited power) | **Governor** | Head of the State |
| **Real Executive** (*de facto* — actually runs things) | **Chief Minister** | Head of the Government |

> 💡 **Simple Analogy:** Think of the Governor as the President of a company (figurehead), and the Chief Minister as the CEO (who actually runs operations).

The position of the **Chief Minister at the state level** is **directly analogous** (equivalent/parallel in role) to the position of the **Prime Minister at the Centre**.

## 2. 📋 Appointment of Chief Minister

### 2.1 What Does the Constitution Actually Say?

- **Article 164** simply states: *"The Chief Minister shall be appointed by the Governor."*
- The Constitution **does NOT prescribe a specific procedure** for how this selection happens.
- This does **NOT** mean the Governor is free to appoint anyone he/she likes.

### 2.2 The Normal Rule — When One Party Has a Clear Majority

By **parliamentary convention** (unwritten but accepted practice), the Governor **must** appoint the **leader of the majority party** in the State Legislative Assembly as the Chief Minister.

> There is no choice here — the Governor is bound by convention.

### 2.3 The Exception — When No Party Has a Clear Majority (Hung Assembly)

When **no single party wins a clear majority**, the Governor may use **personal discretion**. In such a situation:

- The Governor usually appoints the **leader of the largest party or coalition**.
- The appointed CM is then asked to **seek a vote of confidence** in the House **within one month**.

### 2.4 Other Special Situations

- **Sudden death of a sitting CM:** The Governor may use individual judgement to select a successor. However, the ruling party usually elects a new leader first, and then the Governor appoints that person — leaving little room for discretion.

- **Majority proof is NOT a pre-condition:** The Constitution does **not** require a person to prove their majority *before* being appointed CM. The Governor can first appoint, then ask for a floor test within a **reasonable period**.

- **Non-member of the Legislature:** A person who is **not a member** of the state legislature can still be appointed CM, but **must get elected to the legislature within 6 months**. If they fail to do so, they **cease to be the CM**.

- **Which House?** The CM may belong to **either House** of the state legislature (Assembly or Council, if it exists). Conventionally, CMs are from the **Lower House (Legislative Assembly)**, but members of the **Upper House (Legislative Council)** have also been appointed CM on several occasions.

## 3. 📜 Sarkaria Commission Recommendations on CM Appointment

The **Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1983–88)** was set up to examine and improve the relationship between the Centre and states. It made the following important recommendations specifically about the selection and appointment of a Chief Minister:

### Recommendation 1: Governor's Guiding Principle
- The party or **combination of parties** that commands the **widest support** in the Legislative Assembly should be called to form the government.
- The Governor's job is to **ensure a government is formed** — NOT to pick a government whose policies he personally agrees with.

### Recommendation 2: Order of Preference in a Hung Assembly

If there is a **single party with absolute majority** → its leader is automatically called to be CM.

If **no such party exists**, the Governor should go through the following **order of preference** while sounding out parties:

1. ✅ **An alliance formed BEFORE the elections** (pre-electoral coalition).
2. ✅ **The largest single party** that stakes a claim and can get outside support (including independents).
3. ✅ **A post-electoral coalition** where all partners jointly form the government.
4. ✅ **A post-electoral alliance** where some parties form the government and others support from outside.

### Recommendation 3: Most Likely to Command Majority
- While going through the above process, the Governor should select a leader who is **most likely to command a majority** in the assembly.

### Recommendation 4: Floor Test Within 30 Days
- A CM who does **not lead a party with absolute majority** must seek a **vote of confidence within 30 days** of taking office.

### Recommendation 5: Don't Decide Floor Strength Outside the House
- If several MLAs approach the Governor and question whether the sitting CM still commands majority support, the Governor **must NOT try to assess this himself/herself** outside the assembly.
- The correct course: **Force a floor test — let it be decided in the Legislative Assembly itself.**

## 4. 🤝 Oath, Term, and Salary

### 4.1 Oath

Before entering office, the **Governor administers two oaths** to the Chief Minister:

**Oath of Office** — The CM swears to:
- Bear **true faith and allegiance** to the Constitution of India.
- Uphold the **sovereignty and integrity** of India.
- Faithfully and conscientiously **discharge the duties** of office.
- Do right to all people in accordance with the **Constitution and law**, without fear or favour, affection or ill-will.

**Oath of Secrecy** — The CM swears to:
- **NOT directly or indirectly communicate or reveal** to any person any matter that comes under his/her consideration as a state minister.
- Exception: Only when it is required for the **due discharge of duties**.

> 📌 Note: The form of oath for the CM is **similar to that of any state minister**.

### 4.2 Term of Office

- The CM's term is **not fixed**.
- He/she holds office **during the pleasure of the Governor**.
- BUT — this does **not** mean the Governor can dismiss the CM anytime.
- As long as the CM **enjoys majority support** in the Legislative Assembly, the Governor **cannot dismiss** him/her. *(Ruled by the Supreme Court in **S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India, 1994** — explained in detail below.)*
- If the CM **loses the confidence of the assembly** → must resign, OR the Governor can dismiss.

### 4.3 Salary and Allowances

- Determined by the **State Legislature**.
- In addition to regular MLA salary and allowances, the CM gets:
  - **Sumptuary allowance** (a special personal-expense allowance)
  - **Free accommodation**
  - **Travelling allowance**
  - **Medical facilities**

## 5. ⚡ Powers and Functions of the Chief Minister

### 5.1 In Relation to the Council of Ministers

The CM is the **head of the state council of ministers** and enjoys the following powers:

- 🔵 The Governor appoints **only those persons as ministers** who are **recommended by the CM**.
- 🔵 The CM **allocates and reshuffles portfolios** among ministers.
- 🔵 Can ask a minister to **resign**, or advise the Governor to **dismiss** a minister in case of differences.
- 🔵 **Presides over meetings** of the council of ministers and influences its decisions.
- 🔵 **Guides, directs, controls, and coordinates** activities of all ministers.
- 🔵 Can **bring down the entire cabinet** by simply resigning.

> 🔑 **Critical Point:** Since the CM is the head of the council of ministers:
> - **CM's resignation or death = Entire Council of Ministers dissolves automatically.**
> - **Any other minister's resignation or death = Only a vacancy is created** (which the CM may or may not fill).

### 5.2 In Relation to the Governor

The CM is the **principal channel of communication** between the Governor and the Council of Ministers *(governed by **Article 167**)*.

**It is the CM's constitutional duty to:**
1. **Communicate** to the Governor all decisions of the council of ministers relating to state administration and proposals for legislation.
2. **Furnish information** relating to administration and legislation as the Governor may ask for.
3. If the Governor requires, **submit for council consideration** any matter decided by a single minister (but not yet discussed by the full cabinet).

Additionally, the CM:
- **Advises the Governor** on the appointment of important officials such as:
  - **Advocate General**
  - **Chairman and Members of the State Public Service Commission**
  - **State Election Commissioner**
  - And others.

### 5.3 In Relation to the State Legislature

As **leader of the house**, the CM:

- Advises the Governor regarding **summoning and proroguing** of sessions of the state legislature.
- Can recommend the **dissolution of the Legislative Assembly** to the Governor at any time.
- **Announces government policies** on the floor of the house.

### 5.4 Other Powers and Functions

- 🔹 **Chairman of the State Planning Board.**
- 🔹 **Vice-Chairman of the concerned Zonal Council** by rotation, for **one year at a time**. *(The Union Home Minister is the Chairman of all Zonal Councils.)*
- 🔹 **Member of the Inter-State Council** (headed by the Prime Minister).
- 🔹 **Member of the Governing Council of NITI Aayog** (headed by the Prime Minister).
- 🔹 **Chief Spokesman** of the state government.
- 🔹 **Crisis manager-in-chief** at the political level during emergencies.
- 🔹 **Leader of the state** — meets various sections of people, receives memoranda regarding public problems.
- 🔹 **Leader of the ruling party** in the state.
- 🔹 **Political head of the civil services** in the state.

> 📌 **Important Qualifier:** The **discretionary powers** of the Governor *do reduce* the overall power, authority, influence, prestige, and role of the Chief Minister to some extent.

## 6. 🔗 Constitutional Relationship Between the Governor and Chief Minister

The following **Articles** directly govern this relationship:

### Article 163
- There shall be a **Council of Ministers with the CM as head** to **aid and advise the Governor**.
- Exception: Where the Governor is required to act **in his/her own discretion**.

### Article 164
Three key provisions:
1. **CM is appointed by the Governor**; other ministers are appointed by the Governor **on the advice of the CM**.
2. Ministers hold office **during the pleasure of the Governor**.
3. The Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible** to the Legislative Assembly of the State.

### Article 167
Lays down the **duties of the CM** with respect to furnishing information to the Governor (the three duties listed in Section 5.2 above).

## ⚖️ Landmark Court Case — Explained in Detail

### 🏛️ S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)

#### Background — What Happened?
This is **one of the most important constitutional judgments in Indian history**. It arose from a series of controversial dismissals of state governments by invoking **Article 356** (President's Rule / Governor's Rule), which was being used by the Centre as a political tool to destabilize opposition-ruled states.

**S.R. Bommai** was the Chief Minister of Karnataka (Janata Dal government). In 1989, the Governor of Karnataka, Venkatasubbaiah, received letters from some legislators claiming that the Bommai government had lost its majority. Without allowing Bommai to prove his majority on the floor of the house, the Governor recommended President's Rule. The President's Rule was imposed under **Article 356** and the assembly was dissolved.

Bommai challenged this action, and the case eventually reached the **Supreme Court's nine-judge Constitutional Bench**.

#### What Did the Supreme Court Rule?

The Supreme Court delivered a **landmark unanimous verdict** with several path-breaking holdings:

**1. Floor of the House is the Only Test for Majority:**
> The Governor **cannot** decide whether a CM has lost majority support on his/her own, outside the legislative assembly. The **only legitimate place** to test majority is the **floor of the Legislative Assembly**. The CM must be given an opportunity to prove majority through a floor test.

This directly protects the Chief Minister from being arbitrarily dismissed by the Governor based on unverified claims.

**2. Article 356 Cannot Be Used Arbitrarily:**
> The imposition of **President's Rule** (Article 356) is **subject to judicial review** (courts can examine and question it). The court held that Article 356 is a **drastic and last resort measure**, to be used only when the constitutional machinery has genuinely broken down — not for political convenience.

**3. Dissolution of Assembly — Irreversible Step:**
> Before the Parliament approves the proclamation of President's Rule, the **Governor cannot dissolve the Legislative Assembly**. It can only be **kept under suspended animation**. This prevents a fait accompli (irreversible situation) before Parliament has a say.

**4. Secularism as a Basic Feature:**
> The court also held that **secularism is a basic feature of the Constitution**, and any state government that acts against secularism can face dismissal under Article 356. (This was significant in the context of post-Babri Masjid demolition governments.)

**5. Presidential Satisfaction Must be Based on Relevant Material:**
> The President's satisfaction under Article 356 must be based on **objective and relevant material**. The court can examine whether relevant material existed.

#### Why is This Case Relevant to the CM Chapter?

The footnote in the original chapter (Footnote 2) directly cites this case to establish the rule:
> *"The Governor cannot dismiss the CM as long as the CM enjoys majority support in the Legislative Assembly."*

This case converted a **convention into a judicially enforceable rule** — making it much harder for Governors to act politically.

> ⚠️ **The chapter notes:** Despite this ruling, there have been **many violations** where Governors dismissed CMs without giving them the opportunity to prove their majority in the assembly. The **Bihar (2005)** and **Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** situations are prominent examples of controversies related to this.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example — AI Added for UPSC Relevance]`

### 🔹 Hung Assembly & CM Appointment — Recent Controversies

**Karnataka 2018 & Maharashtra 2019** are the most vivid recent examples of the Sarkaria Commission's guidelines being tested:

- **Karnataka (2018):** After election results gave no party a clear majority, the Governor invited the BJP (largest single party) to form the government, bypassing a pre-election alliance of Congress-JD(S). The Supreme Court, in a midnight hearing, ordered a floor test within 24 hours. The BJP CM **B.S. Yediyurappa** resigned before the floor test, as numbers didn't add up.

- **Maharashtra (2019):** In a dramatic turn, the BJP's **Devendra Fadnavis** was sworn in as CM at 5:30 AM after the Governor invited him, without a floor test. The Supreme Court again ordered an immediate floor test. Fadnavis resigned within 80 hours. This was widely seen as violating the **Sarkaria Commission's spirit** and the **S.R. Bommai ruling**.

`[Modern Example]` These cases show that while the law is clear, **political practice often tests constitutional boundaries**.

---

### 🔹 Arunachal Pradesh Case (2016) — Nabam Rebia vs. Deputy Speaker

`[Modern Example]`

**What happened:** The Governor of Arunachal Pradesh advanced the session of the assembly (without the CM's advice) leading to a constitutional crisis and President's Rule being imposed on the Congress-led government.

**Supreme Court (2016) held:**
- The Governor's action in advancing the session **was unconstitutional**.
- President's Rule in Arunachal Pradesh was **set aside** and the original government was restored.
- This reinforced the **S.R. Bommai principle** — Governors cannot act in a politically motivated manner.

---

### 🔹 NITI Aayog Governing Council — CM's National Role

`[Modern Example]`

- Since NITI Aayog replaced the Planning Commission in **January 2015**, all Chief Ministers are members of its **Governing Council**.
- The Governing Council meetings, chaired by the **Prime Minister**, have become important platforms for Centre-State dialogue on issues like **health, education, water, and economic policy**.
- The **7th Governing Council Meeting (2023)** focused on **Viksit Bharat @2047**, with Chief Ministers presenting state-level development roadmaps — a direct real-world exercise of the CM's role as "vice-chairman of planning" at the state level.

---

### 🔹 Supreme Court on Floor Tests — Recent Reinforcement

`[Modern Example]`

- In **Shivraj Singh Chouhan vs. Speaker, Madhya Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2020)**, the Supreme Court ordered a floor test within 24 hours during the MP political crisis.
- The court reiterated that **delay in floor tests erodes democratic legitimacy** and that the Governor has the power to call for a floor test.
- This showed that the **Bommai principle is alive and actively enforced** by the judiciary.
