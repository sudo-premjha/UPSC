## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read these 5 key takeaways BEFORE you start. They are your compass.**

1. **State judiciary = High Court + Subordinate/Lower Courts** arranged in a hierarchy.
2. **District Judge** is the most powerful judicial officer at the district level, handling both civil AND criminal matters.
3. **Governor** appoints district judges *in consultation with* the High Court — a crucial constitutional safeguard.
4. **Articles 233–237** (Part VI of the Constitution) govern the entire structure of subordinate courts.
5. **Capital punishment** awarded by a Sessions Judge is NOT final — it **must be confirmed by the High Court**.

---

## 🏛️ Section 1: What is the State Judiciary?

The **state judiciary** has two main parts:
- **The High Court** — the apex court of the state.
- **Subordinate Courts (Lower Courts)** — a hierarchy of courts functioning *below and under* the High Court, at the district level and below.

> 💡 **Why "Subordinate"?** These courts are called subordinate because they are *subordinate to* (i.e., under the authority and supervision of) the state High Court.

---

## ⚖️ Section 2: Constitutional Provisions (Articles 233–237)

These articles are found in **Part VI of the Constitution**. Their purpose is **two-fold**:
1. To regulate the **organisation** of subordinate courts.
2. To ensure their **independence from the executive** (i.e., from political interference).

---

### 📌 Provision 1: Appointment of District Judges (Article 233)

The **appointment, posting, and promotion** of district judges in a state are made by:

> **The Governor of the State** — but only *in consultation with* the High Court.

#### ✅ Qualifications Required to Become a District Judge:

- ❌ The person should **NOT** already be in service of the Central or State Government.
- ✔️ Must have been an **advocate or pleader for at least 7 years**.
- ✔️ Must be **recommended by the High Court** for appointment.

> 💡 **Simple Explanation:** You cannot be a sitting government servant. You must be a practising lawyer with 7 years of experience, and the High Court must vouch for you.

---

### 📌 Provision 2: Appointment of Other Judges (Article 234)

For judges *other than* district judges (i.e., lower-level judicial officers), appointments are made by:

> **The Governor** — after consultation with **both**:
> - The **State Public Service Commission (SPSC)**, AND
> - The **High Court**

> 📝 **In Practice:** The State Public Service Commission conducts a **competitive examination** for recruitment to the state's judicial service.

---

### 📌 Provision 3: Control over Subordinate Courts (Article 235)

The **High Court** has full control over district courts and all subordinate courts. This includes:

- **Posting** (where a judge is assigned)
- **Promotion** (moving up in rank)
- **Leave** (granting leave of absence)

This applies to all judicial officers holding posts **inferior to (below) the post of District Judge**.

> 💡 **Key Takeaway:** The High Court — not the Governor, not the State Government — controls the day-to-day administration of the lower judiciary. This protects judicial independence.

---

### 📌 Provision 4: Interpretation (Article 236)

This article defines two important terms clearly:

#### 🔹 What does "District Judge" include?
The term **"District Judge"** is broader than it sounds. It includes all of the following:

| Title | Description |
|---|---|
| Judges of a City Civil Court | Civil matters in city courts |
| Additional District Judge | Assists the district judge |
| Joint District Judge | Works alongside district judge |
| Assistant District Judge | Junior to district judge |
| Chief Judge of a Small Cause Court | Heads small cause courts |
| Chief Presidency Magistrate | Top magistrate in presidency towns |
| Additional Chief Presidency Magistrate | Assists the Chief Presidency Magistrate |
| Sessions Judge | Criminal side of the district court |
| Additional Sessions Judge | Assists Sessions Judge |
| Assistant Sessions Judge | Junior to Sessions Judge |

#### 🔹 What does "Judicial Service" mean?
**"Judicial Service"** means a service consisting *exclusively* of persons meant to fill:
- The post of **District Judge**, AND
- Other **civil judicial posts inferior to** (below) the post of District Judge.

---

### 📌 Provision 5: Application to Certain Magistrates (Article 237)

The **Governor** may issue a direction that the provisions of this chapter (Articles 233–237) shall also apply to **any class or classes of Magistrates** in the state.

> 💡 **Why does this matter?** This ensures that even magistrates — not just judges — can be brought under the same constitutional framework of appointment and control, giving them similar protections and accountability.

---

### 📜 Special Constitutional Amendment: Article 233-A

> The **20th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1966** added **Article 233-A**.

**What did it do?** It **retrospectively validated** (i.e., gave legal legitimacy going back in time to) the following:
- Appointments of **certain district judges** that had earlier been made irregularly or were being challenged.
- **Judgements delivered** by those district judges, ensuring those verdicts remained legally valid and were not thrown out.

> 💡 **Simple Explanation:** Some district judges were appointed in a technically flawed manner. Their appointments — and all the court decisions they had already made — were in danger of being declared invalid. This amendment fixed that problem by saying, "Those appointments and judgements are valid, as if they were always done correctly."

---

## 🏗️ Section 3: Structure and Jurisdiction of Subordinate Courts

> ⚠️ **Important Note:** The organisational structure, jurisdiction (authority to hear cases), and **nomenclature** (names used for courts) are determined by **each individual state**. So they differ slightly from state to state.

Broadly speaking, there are **three tiers of courts** below the High Court — both on the civil and criminal side.

---

### 🗺️ The Three-Tier Structure (Visual Layout)

```
HIGH COURT
│
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                  │
CIVIL SIDE                                   CRIMINAL SIDE
│                                                  │
TIER 1: District Court (District Judge)    Sessions Court (Sessions Judge)
│                                                  │
TIER 2: Court of Subordinate Judges        Court of Chief Judicial Magistrate
│                                                  │
TIER 3: Court of Munsiff                   Court of Judicial Magistrate
```

---

### 🔹 Tier 1: District Court / Sessions Court

The **District Judge** is the **highest judicial authority in the district**.

**Key Powers:**
- Has **original jurisdiction** (can hear a case for the first time) in both civil and criminal matters.
- Has **appellate jurisdiction** (can hear appeals against lower court decisions) in both civil and criminal matters.
- Exercises both **judicial AND administrative powers**.
- Has **supervisory powers** over ALL subordinate courts in the district.
- **Appeals** against the District Judge's orders go to the **High Court**.

**The Same Person, Two Roles:**
| Role | When Used |
|---|---|
| **District Judge** | When hearing **civil** cases |
| **Sessions Judge** | When hearing **criminal** cases |

> 💡 It's the same person wearing two hats! The same judge handles both civil and criminal matters in the district.

**⚠️ Critical Fact — Capital Punishment:**
- The **Sessions Judge** has the power to impose **any sentence**, including:
  - Life imprisonment
  - **Capital punishment (death sentence)**
- However, a **death sentence passed by the Sessions Judge is NOT final** until it is **confirmed by the High Court** — regardless of whether anyone files an appeal or not.

---

### 🔹 Tier 2: Court of Subordinate Judges / Court of Chief Judicial Magistrate

**Civil Side — Court of Subordinate Judges:**
- The **Subordinate Judge** exercises **unlimited pecuniary jurisdiction** (no upper limit on the money value of civil cases they can hear) over civil suits.
- Also known as: **Civil Judge (Senior Division)** or **Civil Judge (Class I)**.
- Can also be given powers of an **Assistant Sessions Judge**, which means they can handle *both* civil and criminal matters — similar to a District Judge.

**Criminal Side — Court of Chief Judicial Magistrate:**
- Decides criminal cases punishable with **imprisonment up to 7 years**.

---

### 🔹 Tier 3: Court of Munsiff / Court of Judicial Magistrate

**Civil Side — Court of Munsiff:**
- The **Munsiff** has **limited jurisdiction** — handles civil cases of **small pecuniary (monetary) value** only.
- Also known as: **Civil Judge (Junior Division)** or **Civil Judge (Class II)**.

**Criminal Side — Court of Judicial Magistrate:**
- Tries criminal cases punishable with **imprisonment up to 3 years**.

---

### 🏙️ Special Courts in Metropolitan Cities

In some **metropolitan cities**, there are special courts:

| Side | Court Name |
|---|---|
| **Civil Side** | City Civil Courts (headed by a Chief Judge) |
| **Criminal Side** | Courts of Metropolitan Magistrates |

---

### ⚡ Small Cause Courts

Some **states and presidency towns** (formerly Delhi, Bombay, Calcutta, and Madras) have established **Small Causes Courts**.

**Features:**
- Decide **civil cases of small value** in a **summary manner** (quick, simplified procedure).
- Their decisions are **final**.
- However, the **High Court** possesses the **power of revision** (can review if the law was applied correctly, even if the decision itself is final).

---

### 🌾 Panchayat Courts (Village-Level Justice)

In some states, **Panchayat Courts** try **petty (minor) civil and criminal cases**.

They are known by different names in different states:

| State/Region | Name Used |
|---|---|
| General | Nyaya Panchayat |
| Bihar | Gram Kutchery |
| Some States | Adalati Panchayat |
| Other States | Panchayat Adalat |

## ⚖️ Section 4: Detailed Explanation of Court Cases / Legal Concepts Mentioned

> Since no specific court cases are explicitly cited by name in this chapter, the following section explains the **key legal principles and constitutional disputes** that arise from the provisions discussed in this chapter, which are frequently tested.

---

### 📜 Case Law Context 1: The Problem That Led to Article 233-A

**Background:**
After Independence, several states appointed district judges without strictly following the procedure laid down in Article 233 (i.e., without proper consultation with the High Court, or while the person was still in government service).

**The Legal Problem:**
- Challenges were filed in courts arguing these appointments were unconstitutional.
- If appointments were invalid, then ALL judgements delivered by those judges would also be invalid — affecting thousands of cases.

**The Fix — Article 233-A (via the 20th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1966):**
- Parliament stepped in and passed this amendment to **retrospectively validate** (fix backwards in time) both:
  1. The irregular **appointments** themselves.
  2. All **judgements and orders** passed by those judges.

> 💡 **Simple Explanation:** Imagine a judge had been giving verdicts for 5 years, but it was later discovered their original appointment was technically illegal. Thousands of people's cases would be thrown into chaos. Article 233-A said: "No, all those appointments and all those verdicts are hereby declared valid. Problem solved."

---

### 📜 Case Law Context 2: Governor's Consultation with High Court — Is it Binding?

**The Core Question:** When the Constitution says the Governor appoints district judges "in consultation with" the High Court — is the High Court's opinion **binding** or just **advisory**?

**The Legal Position (settled by Supreme Court interpretations):**
- The word **"consultation"** does NOT mean "concurrence" (complete agreement).
- However, the consultation must be **real and not nominal** — the Governor cannot simply ignore the High Court's view.
- The Supreme Court has held in multiple cases that the High Court's opinion carries **significant weight** and the Governor cannot act arbitrarily against it.

> 💡 **Why it matters for UPSC:** Questions often test whether "consultation" = "concurrence." The answer is NO — but it is also not merely a formality.

---

### 📜 Case Law Context 3: Confirmation of Death Sentence by High Court

**Legal Principle (Section 366 of CrPC / Section 413 of BNSS 2023):**
- When a **Sessions Court** awards the **death penalty**, it is legally mandatory for the case to go to the **High Court for confirmation** — even if the convict does NOT appeal.
- The High Court can:
  - **Confirm** the death sentence.
  - **Reduce** it to life imprisonment.
  - **Acquit** the accused entirely.

**Landmark Case: Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)**
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India (5-judge Constitutional Bench)
- **Issue:** Is the death penalty constitutional? Does it violate Article 21 (Right to Life)?
- **Judgement:** The Supreme Court upheld the **constitutionality of the death penalty** by a 4:1 majority.
- **Key Doctrine Established — "Rarest of Rare" Doctrine:** Death sentence should be imposed **only in the rarest of rare cases**, where the alternative of life imprisonment is **unquestionably foreclosed**.
- **Justice P.N. Bhagwati** dissented (disagreed), saying death penalty is unconstitutional.

> 💡 **Connection to Chapter:** The chapter states the Sessions Judge can impose capital punishment, but it must be confirmed by the High Court. The *Bachan Singh* case explains the legal and constitutional framework within which that power operates.

---

**Landmark Case: Machhi Singh v. State of Punjab (1983)**
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Issue:** What are the specific circumstances that qualify as "rarest of rare"?
- **Judgement:** The Supreme Court laid down **five categories** where the death penalty may be considered:
  1. Manner of commission of murder (extreme brutality).
  2. Motive for commission (for gain, for lust, etc.).
  3. Anti-social or socially abhorrent nature of the crime.
  4. Magnitude of the crime (multiple murders).
  5. Personality of the victim (child, helpless woman, public figure, etc.).

> 💡 **Why it matters:** Any time the Sessions Court awards death and sends it to the High Court for confirmation, judges apply the *Bachan Singh* and *Machhi Singh* principles to decide whether to confirm it.

## 🌍 Modern Context

### 🌍 Modern Context 1: Judiciary Independence & Executive Interference

**Topic Connection:** Article 235 gives the High Court control over subordinate courts to protect judicial independence from the executive.

**Recent Development:**
- `[Modern Example]` The debate over **judicial appointments** has been fierce in recent years. The **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) Act, 2014** was struck down by the Supreme Court in **Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015)** — upholding the **Collegium System** as the constitutional method for judicial appointments.
- This reinforces the principle embedded in Articles 233–235: that **the judiciary, not the executive**, must have the primary say in who gets appointed to judicial positions.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context 2: Gram Nyayalayas — Modern Panchayat Courts

**Topic Connection:** The chapter mentions Panchayat Courts (Nyaya Panchayats) for petty cases at the village level.

**Recent Development:**
- `[Modern Example]` The **Gram Nyayalayas Act, 2008** established **Gram Nyayalayas** (village courts) at the grassroots level to provide **affordable and speedy justice** in rural areas.
- As of recent data (2022–23), only about **476 Gram Nyayalayas** are operational out of the **2,500+ sanctioned**, largely due to resistance from state governments and bar associations.
- The **Supreme Court in 2023** took *suo motu* (on its own) cognizance of the poor implementation of the Gram Nyayalayas Act and directed states to explain the delays.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context 3: Pendency Crisis in Subordinate Courts

**Topic Connection:** The chapter describes the three-tier structure of subordinate courts — their efficiency directly impacts access to justice.

**Recent Data:**
- `[Modern Example]` According to the **National Judicial Data Grid (NJDG)** data (2023–24), over **4.4 crore cases** are pending across all courts in India.
- Of these, the **vast majority (~87%)** are pending in **District and Subordinate Courts** — not the High Courts or Supreme Court.
- The government's **Phase III of the eCourts Mission Mode Project** (approved with a budget of ₹7,210 crore in 2023) aims to digitise courts, reduce pendency, and improve access to justice — directly targeting the subordinate judiciary discussed in this chapter.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context 4: Death Penalty — Recent Developments

**Topic Connection:** Sessions Judge's power to award capital punishment (subject to High Court confirmation).

**Recent Development:**
- `[Modern Example]` In **Manoj v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2022)**, the Supreme Court **commuted the death sentence** to life imprisonment. The Court criticised the trial courts and High Courts for not following the *Bachan Singh* "rarest of rare" doctrine properly, especially for **not considering mitigating circumstances** (background, mental health, socio-economic conditions of the accused).
- This case is significant because it shows the **critical role of High Court confirmation** — exactly as described in this chapter.

## 🧠 Summary: Jurisdiction at a Glance

| Court | Side | Jurisdiction / Key Power |
|---|---|---|
| **District Judge / Sessions Judge** | Civil + Criminal | Unlimited; can impose life imprisonment & death penalty (death subject to HC confirmation) |
| **Subordinate Judge / Civil Judge (Sr.)** | Civil | Unlimited pecuniary jurisdiction |
| **Chief Judicial Magistrate** | Criminal | Cases with up to **7 years** imprisonment |
| **Munsiff / Civil Judge (Jr.)** | Civil | Limited — only small monetary value cases |
| **Judicial Magistrate** | Criminal | Cases with up to **3 years** imprisonment |
| **Metropolitan Magistrate** | Criminal | Cases in metropolitan cities |
| **Small Causes Court** | Civil | Small value cases, summary procedure, decisions final (HC has revision power) |
| **Panchayat / Nyaya Panchayat** | Civil + Criminal | Petty (minor) cases only |
