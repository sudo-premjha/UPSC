# 📘 Chapter 9: Directive Principles of State Policy
### *Based on M. Laxmikanth's Indian Polity, 8th Edition — Rewritten for UPSC Aspirants*

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **DPSPs are non-justiciable** (cannot be enforced in court) but are **fundamental to governance** — they carry moral and political force, not legal force.
> 2. They are borrowed from the **Irish Constitution of 1937**, which itself borrowed from the **Spanish Constitution**.
> 3. DPSPs are classified into **three types**: Socialistic, Gandhian, and Liberal-Intellectual.
> 4. There has been a long **constitutional conflict between Fundamental Rights and DPSPs**, resolved through landmark Supreme Court cases — especially **Minerva Mills (1980)**.
> 5. The current position: **Fundamental Rights have supremacy**, but Parliament *can* amend them to implement DPSPs, as long as the **Basic Structure** of the Constitution is not damaged.

---

## 🗺️ Where Are DPSPs Located?

- **Part IV of the Constitution**
- **Articles 36 to 51**
  - Article 36 → Defines "State"
  - Article 37 → Nature and significance of DPSPs
  - Articles 38–51 → The actual Directive Principles

## 🌱 Origin and Source

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| **Source** | Irish Constitution of 1937 (which borrowed from Spanish Constitution) |
| **Described by** | Dr. B.R. Ambedkar as **'novel features'** of the Indian Constitution |
| **Called by Granville Austin** | **'Conscience of the Constitution'** (along with Fundamental Rights) |

> 💡 **Simple Explanation:** DPSPs are like a *promise* the Indian State makes to its citizens — "We will work towards your social and economic welfare." They are not enforceable in court today, but they guide every law and policy the government makes.

## ⚙️ Features of Directive Principles

### Feature 1: What Are They?
- **DPSPs are constitutional instructions** to the State in **legislative, executive, and administrative** matters.
- The word **'State'** in Part IV (per Article 36) has the same meaning as in Part III (Fundamental Rights). This includes:
  - Legislative and executive organs of the **Central and State governments**
  - **All local authorities**
  - **All other public authorities** in the country

### Feature 2: Resemblance to 'Instrument of Instructions'
- DPSPs resemble the **'Instrument of Instructions'** in the **Government of India Act, 1935**.
- In Dr. Ambedkar's own words: *"The Directive Principles are like the instrument of instructions, which were issued to the Governor-General and to the Governors of the colonies of India by the British Government under the Government of India Act of 1935... The only difference is that they are instructions to the legislature and the executive."*

### Feature 3: Comprehensive Programme for a Modern State
- DPSPs form a **comprehensive economic, social and political programme** for a modern democratic State.
- They aim to realise the ideals of **justice, liberty, equality and fraternity** as stated in the **Preamble**.
- They embody the concept of a **'welfare state'** (a state that actively promotes citizens' wellbeing), *not* a **'police state'** (a state only concerned with law-and-order and defence, based on 19th-century laissez-faire individualism) that existed during colonial rule.
- In short, they seek to establish **economic and social democracy**.

### Feature 4: Non-Justiciable Nature
- DPSPs are **non-justiciable** (not legally enforceable by courts for their violation).
- The government **cannot be compelled** by courts to implement them.
- **BUT** — Article 37 itself says these principles are **"fundamental in the governance of the country"** and it shall be the **duty of the State** to apply them in making laws.

### Feature 5: Help Courts Determine Constitutional Validity
- Even though DPSPs are non-justiciable, they **help courts** in examining whether a law is constitutionally valid.
- The Supreme Court has repeatedly ruled: if a law seeks to give effect to a Directive Principle, courts may consider it **'reasonable'** in relation to:
  - **Article 14** (equality before law)
  - **Article 19** (six freedoms)
- This can **save such a law from being struck down as unconstitutional**.

## 🗂️ Classification of Directive Principles

> *Note: The Constitution does NOT officially classify DPSPs. The following classification is based on their content and direction.*

---

### 🔴 Category 1: Socialistic Principles
*(Reflect socialist ideology — aim for social and economic justice, welfare state)*

These direct the State to:

- **Article 38** → Promote welfare of people by securing a social order with **justice — social, economic, and political** — and **minimise inequalities** in income, status, facilities and opportunities.
  - *(The part about minimising inequalities was added by the **44th Amendment Act, 1978**)*

- **Article 39** → Secure the following:
  - (a) Right to **adequate means of livelihood** for all citizens
  - (b) **Equitable distribution** of material resources for the common good
  - (c) **Prevention of concentration of wealth** and means of production
  - (d) **Equal pay for equal work** for men and women
  - (e) Preservation of **health and strength of workers and children** against forcible abuse
  - (f) Opportunities for **healthy development of children** and protection of childhood/youth against exploitation and moral/material abandonment *(Part (f) was partially added by the **42nd Amendment Act, 1976**)*

- **Article 39A** → Promote **equal justice** and provide **free legal aid to the poor**.
  - *(Added by the **42nd Amendment Act, 1976**)*

- **Article 41** → Secure the **right to work, to education** and to **public assistance** in cases of unemployment, old age, sickness and disablement.

- **Article 42** → Make provision for **just and humane conditions of work** and **maternity relief**.

- **Article 43** → Secure a **living wage** *(wage covering food, shelter, clothing + education, health, insurance — more than a 'minimum wage' which only covers bare needs; a 'fair wage' is a mean between the two)*, a decent standard of life, and social and cultural opportunities for all workers.

- **Article 43A** → Take steps to secure **participation of workers in the management of industries**.
  - *(Added by the **42nd Amendment Act, 1976**)*

- **Article 47** → **Raise the level of nutrition** and standard of living of people and **improve public health**.

---

### 🟢 Category 2: Gandhian Principles
*(Based on Gandhian ideology — represent Gandhi's programme of reconstruction during the national movement)*

These require the State to:

- **Article 40** → **Organise village panchayats** and endow them with necessary powers to function as units of **self-government**.

- **Article 43** → Promote **cottage industries** on an individual or cooperative basis in rural areas.

- **Article 43B** → Promote **voluntary formation, autonomous functioning, democratic control and professional management of co-operative societies**.
  - *(Added by the **97th Amendment Act, 2011**)*

- **Article 46** → Promote the **educational and economic interests of SCs, STs and other weaker sections** and protect them from social injustice and exploitation.

- **Article 47** → **Prohibit the consumption of intoxicating drinks and drugs** which are injurious to health.

- **Article 48** → Prohibit the **slaughter of cows, calves and other milch and draught cattle** and improve their breeds.

---

### 🔵 Category 3: Liberal–Intellectual Principles
*(Represent the ideology of liberalism)*

These direct the State to:

- **Article 44** → Secure for all citizens a **Uniform Civil Code (UCC)** throughout the country.

- **Article 45** → Provide **early childhood care and education** for all children until they complete the age of **six years**.
  - *(Changed by the **86th Amendment Act, 2002** — original provision required free and compulsory education for all children up to age 14. After the amendment, elementary education became a Fundamental Right under **Article 21A**, and this directive was narrowed to under-6 care)*

- **Article 48** → Organise **agriculture and animal husbandry** on modern and scientific lines.

- **Article 48A** → **Protect and improve the environment** and safeguard **forests and wildlife**.
  - *(Added by the **42nd Amendment Act, 1976**)*

- **Article 49** → Protect **monuments, places and objects of artistic or historic interest** declared to be of national importance.

- **Article 50** → **Separate the judiciary from the executive** in public services of the State.

- **Article 51** → Promote **international peace and security**; maintain just and honourable relations between nations; foster respect for **international law and treaty obligations**; encourage settlement of international disputes by **arbitration**.

## ⚖️ Sanction Behind Directive Principles

### Who Recommended the Division?
- **Sir B.N. Rau**, the Constitutional Advisor to the Constituent Assembly, recommended dividing individual rights into:
  - **Justiciable rights** → became **Fundamental Rights (Part III)**
  - **Non-justiciable rights** → became **Directive Principles (Part IV)**

### Why Are DPSPs Non-Justiciable?
The framers made DPSPs non-enforceable because:

1. The country **did not have sufficient financial resources** to implement them immediately.
2. **Vast diversity and backwardness** would stand in the way of implementation.
3. The **newly independent State** had many preoccupations and needed freedom to decide the *order, time, place and mode* of implementing them.

### What Force Do They Have, Then?
- They impose a **moral obligation** on state authorities.
- The real force behind them is **political** — i.e., **public opinion**.
- **Alladi Krishna Swamy Ayyar** said: *"No ministry responsible to the people can afford light-heartedly to ignore the provisions in Part IV."*
- **Dr. Ambedkar** said: *"A government which rests on popular vote can hardly ignore the Directive Principles while shaping its policy. If any government ignores them, it will certainly have to answer before the electorate at election time."*

---

## 📊 Fundamental Rights vs. Directive Principles — Key Differences

| # | Fundamental Rights | Directive Principles |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Negative** in nature — prohibit the State from doing certain things | **Positive** in nature — require the State to do certain things |
| 2 | **Justiciable** — legally enforceable by courts | **Non-justiciable** — not legally enforceable by courts |
| 3 | Aim at establishing **political democracy** | Aim at establishing **social and economic democracy** |
| 4 | Have **legal sanctions** | Have **moral and political sanctions** |
| 5 | Promote welfare of the **individual** — personal and individualistic | Promote welfare of the **community** — societarian and socialistic |
| 6 | Do **not require legislation** — automatically enforced | **Require legislation** — not automatically enforced |
| 7 | Courts **must strike down** a law violating Fundamental Rights | Courts **cannot strike down** a law violating DPSPs; however, they can **uphold** a law enacted to give effect to a DPSP |

## 🔻 Criticism of Directive Principles

### 1. No Legal Force
- **K.T. Shah** called them *"pious superfluities"* and compared them to *"a cheque on a bank, payable only when the resources of the bank permit."*
- **Nasiruddin** said they are *"no better than the new year's resolutions, which are broken on the second of January."*
- **T.T. Krishnamachari** described them as *"a veritable dustbin of sentiments."*
- **K.C. Wheare** called them *"a manifesto of aims and aspirations"* serving as mere *"moral homily."*
- **Sir Ivor Jennings** called them only *"pious aspirations."*

### 2. Illogically Arranged
- **N. Srinivasan** said: *"The Directives are neither properly classified nor logically arranged. The declaration mixes up relatively unimportant issues with the most vital economic and social questions."*
- **Sir Ivor Jennings** also pointed out they have **no consistent philosophy**.

### 3. Conservative
- **Sir Ivor Jennings** said the Directives are based on the **political philosophy of 19th century England**.
- He remarked: *"The ghosts of Sydney Webb and Beatrice Webb stalk through the pages of the text. Part IV of the Constitution expresses Fabian Socialism without the socialism."*
- He warned they might be *"entirely outmoded"* by the 21st century.

### 4. Constitutional Conflict
- **K. Santhanam** pointed out that DPSPs can lead to conflicts:
  - **(a)** Between the **Centre and the States** — Centre can give directions to states on DPSP implementation; non-compliance could justify dismissal of state government.
  - **(b)** Between the **President and the Prime Minister** — If Parliament passes a bill violating DPSPs, the President may reject it since DPSPs are "fundamental to governance."
  - **(c)** Between the **Governor and the Chief Minister** — Same conflict can arise at the state level.

---

## 🌟 Utility of Directive Principles

Despite criticisms, DPSPs are not unnecessary. Here's why experts value them:

- **L.M. Singhvi** (eminent jurist): *"The Directives are the life-giving provisions of the Constitution. They constitute the stuff of the Constitution and its philosophy of social justice."*
- **M.C. Chagla** (former Chief Justice): *"If all these principles are fully carried out, our country would indeed be a heaven on earth."*
- **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar**: The Directives lay down that the goal of Indian polity is **'economic democracy'** as distinguished from **'political democracy'**.
- **Granville Austin**: DPSPs are *"aimed at furthering the goals of the social revolution."*
- **Sir B.N. Rau**: They are intended as *"moral precepts for the authorities of the state. They have at least an educative value."*

### According to M.C. Setalvad (former Attorney General), DPSPs are useful because:

1. They are like an **'Instrument of Instructions'** — reminding all authorities of the basic principles of the new social and economic order.
2. They serve as **beacon-lights to courts** — helping in **judicial review** (the power to determine the constitutional validity of a law).
3. They form the **dominating background** to all State action — legislative or executive.
4. They **amplify the Preamble** — which resolves to secure justice, liberty, equality and fraternity for all citizens.

### Additional Roles of DPSPs:

- ✅ Facilitate **stability and continuity** in domestic and foreign policies despite changes of government.
- ✅ Are **supplementary to Fundamental Rights** — fill in the vacuum in Part III by providing social and economic rights.
- ✅ Their implementation creates a **favourable atmosphere** for the full enjoyment of Fundamental Rights. (Political democracy without economic democracy has no meaning.)
- ✅ Enable the **opposition to exercise control** over the government — blame the ruling party if its actions violate DPSPs.
- ✅ Serve as a **test for the performance of the government** — citizens can examine government policies against these constitutional declarations.
- ✅ Serve as a **common political manifesto** — irrespective of ideology, every ruling party must treat these as its "guide, philosopher and friend."

## ⚔️ Conflict Between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles

> This is one of the **most important topics** for UPSC. Study all these cases carefully.

---

### 🏛️ CASE 1: State of Madras vs. Champakam Dorairajan (1951)

**What happened?**
The State of Madras had a communal Government Order (G.O.) that reserved seats in state medical and engineering colleges on the basis of religion, race, and caste. Champakam Dorairajan, a Brahmin woman, was denied admission despite having higher marks than candidates from reserved categories.

**What the Court ruled:**
- The Supreme Court ruled that in case of **any conflict between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles**, the **Fundamental Rights would prevail**.
- DPSPs must **conform to and run as subsidiary to** Fundamental Rights.
- The communal G.O. was struck down as it violated **Article 15(1)** (prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex or place of birth) and **Article 29(2)** (right of minorities regarding admission to educational institutions).
- **BUT** the Court also held that Parliament **can amend Fundamental Rights** through constitutional amendment acts to implement Directives.

**Consequence:**
Parliament enacted the **First Amendment Act (1951)**, **Fourth Amendment Act (1955)**, and **Seventeenth Amendment Act (1964)** to implement some Directives by modifying certain Fundamental Rights (particularly, by adding a 9th Schedule to protect land reform laws from judicial review).

---

### 🏛️ CASE 2: Golak Nath vs. State of Punjab (1967)

**What happened?**
Henry and William Golak Nath owned about 500 acres of land in Punjab. The state sought to acquire the surplus land under the Punjab Security and Land Tenures Act. The Golak Naths challenged the act as violating their Fundamental Rights under Articles 14, 19, and 31.

**What the Court ruled:**
- In a landmark **11-judge bench ruling**, the Supreme Court **overruled its earlier position** and held that **Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights** — they are **'sacrosanct'** in nature.
- In other words, Fundamental Rights **cannot be abridged or taken away**, even to implement Directive Principles.
- This was a significant blow to Parliament's power to carry out socio-economic reforms.

**Parliament's Reaction:**
Parliament reacted strongly by passing:

#### 🔹 24th Amendment Act (1971)
- Declared that **Parliament has the power to abridge or take away any Fundamental Right** by enacting Constitutional Amendment Acts.
- Amended Article 368 to make this explicit.

#### 🔹 25th Amendment Act (1971)
- Inserted a new **Article 31C** with **two important provisions**:
  1. **Provision 1:** No law seeking to implement the socialistic Directives specified in **Article 39(b)** *(ownership and control of material resources distributed for common good)* and **Article 39(c)** *(preventing concentration of wealth to common detriment)* shall be void on the ground of violating:
     - **Article 14** (equality before law)
     - **Article 19** (six freedoms)
     - **Article 31** (right to property)
  2. **Provision 2:** No law containing a declaration that it gives effect to such policy shall be questioned in **any court** on the ground that it does not actually do so. *(This effectively removed judicial review)*

---

### 🏛️ CASE 3: Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973)

**What happened?**
Swami Kesavananda Bharati, head of a mutt in Kerala, challenged the Kerala Land Reforms Act that sought to acquire mutt property. This case became the most significant constitutional case in Indian history — argued before a **13-judge bench** (the largest ever).

**What the Court ruled:**
- The Supreme Court upheld the **24th Amendment Act** as valid.
- Regarding the **25th Amendment Act (Article 31C)**:
  - **Provision 1** (protection of laws implementing Art. 39(b) and (c) from Art. 14, 19, 31 challenge) → **Upheld as constitutional and valid.**
  - **Provision 2** (barring courts from questioning whether a law actually gives effect to DPSP policy) → **Struck down as unconstitutional** on the ground that **judicial review is a basic feature of the Constitution** and cannot be taken away.
- Most importantly, the Court propounded the **Basic Structure Doctrine**: Parliament can amend any part of the Constitution, but **cannot alter or destroy its basic structure/essential features**.

**Significance:**
This case established the famous **Basic Structure Doctrine** — a cornerstone of Indian constitutional law — and struck a balance between Parliamentary sovereignty and constitutional supremacy.

---

### 🏛️ CASE 4: Minerva Mills vs. Union of India (1980)

**What happened?**
The Minerva Mills Ltd. in Karnataka was nationalised under the Sick Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1974. The company challenged the nationalisation and also challenged certain provisions of the Constitution inserted by the **42nd Amendment Act (1976)**.

**Background — What did the 42nd Amendment do?**
The 42nd Amendment Act (1976) had **extended the scope of Article 31C** — instead of protecting only laws implementing Art. 39(b) and (c), it now gave protection to **any law implementing ANY Directive Principle** from challenge under Art. 14 and 19. This effectively gave **legal primacy and supremacy to Directive Principles over Fundamental Rights**.

**What the Court ruled:**
- The Supreme Court struck down the **42nd Amendment's extension of Article 31C** as **unconstitutional and invalid**.
- The extension had given absolute supremacy to DPSPs over Fundamental Rights, which the Court held destroys the constitutional balance.
- The Court stated:

> *"The Indian Constitution is founded on the bedrock of the balance between the Fundamental Rights and the Directive Principles. They together constitute the core of commitment to social revolution. They are like two wheels of a chariot, one no less than the other. To give absolute primacy to one over the other is to disturb the harmony of the Constitution. This harmony and balance between the two is an essential feature of the basic structure of the Constitution."*

- The goals of DPSPs must be achieved **without abrogating the means** provided by Fundamental Rights.

**Current Position After Minerva Mills:**

| Aspect | Current Position |
|---|---|
| General supremacy | **Fundamental Rights prevail** over DPSPs |
| Art. 14 & 19 vs. Art. 39(b) & (c) | **Art. 39(b) and (c) DPSPs prevail** (original Article 31C, upheld in Kesavananda, remains valid) |
| Art. 31 (right to property) | **Omitted** by the 44th Amendment Act, 1978 |
| Can Parliament amend FRs to implement DPSPs? | **Yes**, as long as it does not damage the **Basic Structure** |

## 🕰️ Timeline of the FR-DPSP Conflict

```
1951 → Champakam Dorairajan Case: FRs prevail over DPSPs
         ↓
1951, 1955, 1964 → 1st, 4th, 17th Amendments: Parliament amends FRs to implement DPSPs
         ↓
1967 → Golaknath Case: Parliament CANNOT amend FRs at all (FRs are sacrosanct)
         ↓
1971 → 24th Amendment: Parliament CAN amend FRs
1971 → 25th Amendment: Article 31C inserted (DPSPs u/Art.39(b)(c) protected from FRs)
         ↓
1973 → Kesavananda Bharati: Basic Structure Doctrine; Provision 2 of Art.31C struck down
         ↓
1976 → 42nd Amendment: Article 31C extended — ALL DPSPs given supremacy over FRs
         ↓
1980 → Minerva Mills: 42nd Amendment extension struck down; Balance restored
         ↓
PRESENT → FRs have general supremacy; Art.39(b)(c) DPSPs prevail over Art.14 & 19
```

## 🏗️ Implementation of Directive Principles (Since 1950)

Here is a comprehensive list of all the steps taken by Central and State governments:

### 1. Planning and Development
- **Planning Commission** established in **1950** for planned development and Five Year Plans aimed at socio-economic justice.
- In **2015**, Planning Commission was replaced by **NITI Aayog** (National Institution for Transforming India).

### 2. Land Reforms
- Almost all states passed **land reform laws** including:
  - Abolition of intermediaries like **zamindars, jagirdars, inamdars**
  - **Tenancy reforms** (security of tenure, fair rents)
  - **Ceilings on land holdings**
  - **Distribution of surplus land** among landless labourers
  - **Cooperative farming**

### 3. Labour Welfare Laws
- **Minimum Wages Act (1948)**
- **Payment of Wages Act (1936)**
- **Payment of Bonus Act (1965)**
- **Contract Labour Regulation and Abolition Act (1970)**
- **Child Labour Prohibition and Regulation Act (1986)** — In **2006**, government banned child labour; in **2016**, this Act was renamed **Child and Adolescent Labour Prohibition and Regulation Act, 1986**
- **Bonded Labour System Abolition Act (1976)**
- **Trade Unions Act (1926)**
- **Factories Act (1948)**
- **Mines Act (1952)**
- **Industrial Disputes Act (1947)**
- **Workmen's Compensation Act (1923)**

### 4. Women's Welfare
- **Maternity Benefit Act (1961)**
- **Equal Remuneration Act (1976)**

### 5. Utilisation of Financial Resources for Common Good
- Nationalisation of **life insurance (1956)**
- Nationalisation of **fourteen leading commercial banks (1969)**
- Nationalisation of **general insurance (1971)**
- **Abolition of Privy Purses (1971)**

### 6. Legal Aid
- **Legal Services Authorities Act (1987)** — established a nationwide network for **free and competent legal aid** to the poor and for organising **Lok Adalats**.
  - **Lok Adalat** = statutory forum for *conciliatory settlement of legal disputes*; given the status of a civil court; its awards are enforceable, binding on parties, final — **no appeal lies before any court** against them.

### 7. Cottage Industries
- Khadi and Village Industries Board
- Khadi and Village Industries Commission
- Small-Scale Industries Board
- National Small Industries Corporation
- Handloom Board, Handicrafts Board, Coir Board, Silk Board

### 8. Rural Development and Employment Programmes
- **Community Development Programme (1952)**
- **Hill Area Development Programme (1960)**
- **Drought-Prone Area Programme (1973)**
- **Minimum Needs Programme (1974)**
- **Integrated Rural Development Programme (1978)**
- **Jawahar Rozgar Yojana (1989)**
- **Swarnajayanti Gram Swarozgar Yojana (1999)**
- **Sampoorna Grameena Rozgar Yojana (2001)**
- **National Rural Employment Guarantee Programme (2006)**

### 9. Environment and Forest Protection
- **Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972**
- **Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980**
- **Water and Air Acts** — for Central and State Pollution Control Boards
- **National Forest Policy (1988)** — protection, conservation and development of forests

### 10. Agricultural Modernisation
- Improved seeds, fertilisers, irrigation facilities
- Steps to organise **animal husbandry** on modern and scientific lines

### 11. Panchayati Raj
- **Three-tier panchayati raj system** (village, taluka, zila levels) introduced.
- **73rd Amendment Act (1992)** — gave **constitutional status and protection** to panchayati raj institutions, realising Gandhi's dream of every village being a republic.

### 12. SC/ST and Weaker Sections
- Seats reserved in **educational institutions, government services, and representative bodies**
- **Untouchability (Offences) Act, 1955** → renamed **Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1976**
- **Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989**
- **65th Constitutional Amendment Act (1990)** — established **National Commission for SCs and STs**
- **89th Constitutional Amendment Act (2003)** — bifurcated into two separate commissions:
  - **National Commission for Scheduled Castes**
  - **National Commission for Scheduled Tribes**

### 12a. National Commissions for Weaker Sections
- **National Commission for Backward Classes (1993)**
- **National Commission for Minorities (1993)**
- **National Commission for Women (1992)**
- **National Commission for Protection of Child Rights (2007)**
- **102nd Amendment Act of 2018** — conferred **constitutional status** on the National Commission for Backward Classes and enlarged its functions.

### 12b. EWS Reservation
- In **2019**, the central government issued orders providing **10% reservation** for **Economically Weaker Sections (EWSs)** in:
  - Admission to educational institutions
  - Civil posts and services in the Government of India
- This benefit is for EWS persons **not already covered** under reservations for SCs, STs, and OBCs.
- Facilitated by the **103rd Amendment Act of 2019**.

### 13. Separation of Judiciary from Executive
- **Criminal Procedure Code (1973)** separated the judiciary from the executive in public services.
- Before this, district authorities like the **collector, sub-divisional officer, tehsildar** exercised both executive and judicial powers.
- After separation, **judicial powers were vested in district judicial magistrates** working under direct control of the state High Court.

### 14. Protection of Monuments
- **Ancient and Historical Monument and Archaeological Sites and Remains Act (1951)** enacted.

### 15. Public Health
- Primary health centres and hospitals established throughout the country.
- Special programmes to eradicate diseases like **malaria, TB, leprosy, AIDS, cancer, filaria, kala-azar, guineaworm, yaws, Japanese encephalitis**, etc.

### 16. Cow Protection
- Laws to prohibit the **slaughter of cows, calves and bullocks** enacted in some states.

### 17. Old Age Pension
- Some states have initiated **old age pension schemes** for people above 65 years.

### 18. International Peace
- India follows the policy of **Non-Alignment** and **Panchsheel** to promote international peace and security.

> ⚠️ Despite all these steps, DPSPs have **not been fully implemented** due to:
> - Inadequate financial resources
> - Unfavourable socio-economic conditions
> - Population explosion
> - Strained Centre-State relations

## 🗺️ Directives Outside Part IV

Apart from the DPSPs in Part IV, there are **three other Directives** in other Parts of the Constitution. They are also **non-justiciable** but given equal importance by the judiciary (since all parts of the Constitution must be read together).

### 1. Claims of SCs and STs to Services (Article 335, Part XVI)
- The claims of members of **Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes** shall be taken into consideration, consistently with the **maintenance of efficiency of administration**, in making appointments to Union and State services.

### 2. Instruction in Mother Tongue (Article 350-A, Part XVII)
- Every State and local authority within the State shall **endeavour to provide adequate facilities for instruction in the mother-tongue** at the primary education stage for children belonging to **linguistic minority groups**.

### 3. Development of the Hindi Language (Article 351, Part XVII)
- It shall be the **duty of the Union** to promote the spread of the **Hindi language** and develop it so that it may serve as a **medium of expression for all elements of the composite culture of India**.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *These are AI-added contemporary connections to help link chapter content to current affairs.*

### 🔹 On Directive Principles and Welfare State

`[Modern Example]` **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PMGKAY):** Under this scheme launched during COVID-19 in 2020 and extended multiple times (currently extended till December 2028), the government provides **5 kg of free food grain per person per month** to over 80 crore beneficiaries under the National Food Security Act. This directly implements Article 47 (raising nutrition levels) and Article 39(a) (right to adequate means of livelihood).

`[Modern Example]` **Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA):** The scheme guarantees **100 days of wage employment** per year to rural households. In FY 2022-23, over **5.4 crore households** availed employment under this scheme, directly giving effect to Article 41 (right to work).

### 🔹 On Equal Pay for Equal Work (Art. 39(d))

`[Modern Example]` In **Randhir Singh vs. Union of India (1982)**, the Supreme Court held that the principle of equal pay for equal work is a constitutional goal under Article 39(d). More recently, the **Code on Wages, 2019** (one of the four new labour codes) consolidates wage-related laws including equal remuneration provisions, directly implementing Article 39(d).

### 🔹 On Free Legal Aid (Art. 39A)

`[Modern Example]` The **National Legal Services Authority (NALSA)** has significantly expanded legal aid. As per recent data, **over 1.7 crore people** received free legal services in 2022-23. Lok Adalats settled **over 1 crore cases** in a single National Lok Adalat in 2023.

### 🔹 On Uniform Civil Code (Art. 44)

`[Modern Example]` **Uttarakhand became the first state in India** to implement a **Uniform Civil Code** in 2024, when Governor Gurmit Singh gave assent to the Uttarakhand Uniform Civil Code Act, 2024. This is the most significant recent step toward implementing Article 44. The **Supreme Court in various cases** (including *Jose Paulo Coutinho vs. Maria Luiza Valentina Pereira*, 2019) has repeatedly urged Parliament to enact a UCC.

### 🔹 On Environment Protection (Art. 48A)

`[Modern Example]` **India's commitments at COP26 (Glasgow, 2021)** and **COP27 (Sharm el-Sheikh, 2022)** — India pledged to achieve **net zero emissions by 2070** and **50% energy from renewables by 2030**. The **National Green Tribunal (NGT)**, established under the National Green Tribunal Act, 2010, actively enforces environmental laws, directly giving teeth to Article 48A.

`[Modern Example]` The Supreme Court in **M.C. Mehta vs. Union of India** (a series of landmark judgments) has consistently used Article 48A to order measures to protect the environment, including the famous **Delhi vehicular pollution** orders.

### 🔹 On EWS Reservation (Art. 46)

`[Modern Example]` The **103rd Constitutional Amendment Act (2019)** providing 10% reservation to EWS was challenged before the Supreme Court. In **Janhit Abhiyan vs. Union of India (2022)**, the Supreme Court, by a **3:2 majority**, upheld the constitutional validity of the **103rd Amendment**, holding that EWS reservation does not violate the basic structure of the Constitution.

### 🔹 On Worker Participation in Industries (Art. 43A)

`[Modern Example]` The **Code on Industrial Relations, 2020** (one of the four labour codes) consolidates laws related to trade unions, industrial disputes, and standing orders. While the code is yet to be fully notified, it represents a step toward implementing Art. 43A.

### 🔹 On Panchayati Raj (Art. 40)

`[Modern Example]` As of 2023, **India has over 2.55 lakh gram panchayats** covering rural India. The government's push for **e-Gram Swaraj** (a unified digital platform for panchayats) under the **Digital India** initiative is a modern implementation of Art. 40.

### 🔹 On DPSP-FR Conflict — Recent Judicial Trend

`[Modern Example]` In **Property Owners' Association vs. State of Maharashtra (2024)**, a 9-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court deliberated on the interpretation of **Article 39(b)** — specifically, whether "material resources of the community" includes privately owned resources. The majority held that **not all private property** can be considered "material resources of the community" for the purpose of Article 39(b) — a significant ruling that limits the scope of State acquisition powers under this DPSP.
