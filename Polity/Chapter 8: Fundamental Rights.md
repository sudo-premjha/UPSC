## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **Fundamental Rights** are enshrined in **Part III** (Articles 12–35) of the Constitution and are more elaborate than those of any other country, including the USA.
> 2. Originally **7 Fundamental Rights** existed; after the **44th Amendment Act, 1978**, the **Right to Property** was removed, leaving **6 Fundamental Rights**.
> 3. These rights are **not absolute** — the State can impose **reasonable restrictions**, but courts decide if restrictions are truly reasonable.
> 4. **Article 32** (Right to Constitutional Remedies) is called the **"heart and soul"** of the Constitution by Dr. Ambedkar — it allows citizens to directly approach the Supreme Court.
> 5. **Key tension**: Fundamental Rights vs. Directive Principles vs. Parliament's power to amend — resolved by the **"Basic Structure" doctrine** (Kesavananda Bharati case, 1973).

## 1. Introduction: What Are Fundamental Rights?

**Fundamental Rights** are guaranteed protections written into the **Constitution of India**, found in **Part III, Articles 12 to 35**.

### Where Did the Inspiration Come From?
- The framers of our Constitution were inspired by the **Constitution of the USA** (specifically, the **Bill of Rights**).
- Part III is rightly called the **"Magna Carta of India"**.
  - 💡 *'Magna Carta'* = the Charter of Rights issued by King John of England in **1215** under pressure from barons. It is the **first written document** relating to the Fundamental Rights of citizens.

### Why Are They "Fundamental"?
The rights are called *fundamental* for **two reasons**:
- They are **guaranteed by the Constitution**, which is the supreme/fundamental law of the land.
- They are essential for the **all-round development** of individuals — material, intellectual, moral, and spiritual.

### What Purpose Do They Serve?
- **Uphold** equality of all individuals and dignity of the person.
- **Promote** political democracy and the larger public interest.
- **Prevent** authoritarian (dictatorial) and despotic (tyrannical) rule.
- **Protect** liberties and freedoms against invasion by the State.
- **Operate as limitations** on executive tyranny and arbitrary (random, unfair) laws of the legislature.
- In short: they aim at establishing **"a government of laws and not of men."**

---

## 2. The Six (Originally Seven) Fundamental Rights

Originally, the Constitution provided for **seven** Fundamental Rights. After the **44th Amendment Act, 1978**, the **Right to Property (Article 31)** was deleted from Part III and converted into a **legal right under Article 300-A** (Part XII).

**So today, there are only SIX Fundamental Rights:**

| # | Fundamental Right | Articles |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Right to Equality** | 14–18 |
| 2 | **Right to Freedom** | 19–22 |
| 3 | **Right Against Exploitation** | 23–24 |
| 4 | **Right to Freedom of Religion** | 25–28 |
| 5 | **Cultural and Educational Rights** | 29–30 |
| 6 | **Right to Constitutional Remedies** | 32 |

---

## 3. Key Features of Fundamental Rights

### Feature 1: Available to Citizens vs. All Persons
- Some rights are available **only to citizens**.
- Some rights are available to **all persons** — citizens, foreigners, and even legal persons (companies, corporations).

**Rights available ONLY to Citizens (NOT to foreigners):**
- Prohibition of discrimination (Article 15)
- Equality of opportunity in public employment (Article 16)
- Six freedoms: speech, assembly, association, movement, residence, profession (Article 19)
- Protection of minority language, script, culture (Article 29)
- Right of minorities to establish educational institutions (Article 30)

**Rights available to BOTH Citizens AND Foreigners (except enemy aliens):**
- Equality before law (Article 14)
- Protection against conviction for offences (Article 20)
- Protection of life and personal liberty (Article 21)
- Right to elementary education (Article 21A)
- Protection against arrest and detention (Article 22)
- Prohibition of traffic in human beings and forced labour (Article 23)
- Prohibition of child labour (Article 24)
- Freedom of conscience and religion (Article 25)
- Freedom to manage religious affairs (Article 26)
- Freedom from religious taxation (Article 27)
- Freedom from attending religious instruction (Article 28)

### Feature 2: Not Absolute — Subject to Reasonable Restrictions
- Fundamental Rights are **not absolute but qualified** (limited).
- The State can impose **reasonable restrictions** on them.
- Whether a restriction is truly *reasonable* is decided by **courts**.
- This creates a **balance** between individual liberty and social control.

### Feature 3: Against State Action (Mostly)
- All Fundamental Rights are available against **arbitrary action of the State**.
- However, **some** (like Articles 17 and 23) are also available against **private individuals**.

### Feature 4: Negative AND Positive in Character
- Some rights are **negative** — they *limit* the State's authority (e.g., "State shall not discriminate").
- Some rights are **positive** — they *confer privileges* on persons (e.g., right to free education).

### Feature 5: Justiciable (Enforceable in Court)
- **Justiciable** (courts can enforce them) = if your Fundamental Right is violated, you can go to court.
- This is what makes them truly "fundamental" — they are not merely moral statements.

### Feature 6: Defended by the Supreme Court
- The **Supreme Court** is the primary guardian of Fundamental Rights.
- An aggrieved person can **directly** go to the Supreme Court under **Article 32** — no need to first go to a High Court.

### Feature 7: Not Permanent — Can Be Amended
- Fundamental Rights are **not sacrosanct** (permanently untouchable) or **permanent**.
- Parliament **can curtail or repeal** them via a **Constitutional Amendment Act** (not an ordinary law).
- However, this **cannot destroy the 'basic structure'** of the Constitution (Kesavananda Bharati case, 1973).

### Feature 8: Suspension During National Emergency
- Most Fundamental Rights **can be suspended** during a **National Emergency**.
- **EXCEPTION**: Rights under **Articles 20 and 21** can **NEVER** be suspended, even during an Emergency.
- The **six rights under Article 19** are automatically suspended **only** when Emergency is declared on grounds of **war or external aggression** (external emergency) — **NOT** on the ground of **armed rebellion** (internal emergency).

### Feature 9: Scope Limited by Certain Articles
- **Article 31A**: Saves laws related to acquisition of estates, etc.
- **Article 31B**: Validates acts/regulations in the **Ninth Schedule**.
- **Article 31C**: Saves laws giving effect to certain Directive Principles.

### Feature 10: Restricted for Armed Forces
- **Parliament** (under **Article 33**) can restrict or abrogate Fundamental Rights for members of:
  - Armed forces
  - Para-military forces
  - Police forces
  - Intelligence agencies
  - Analogous services
- Objective: Ensure discipline and proper discharge of duties.

### Feature 11: Restricted Under Martial Law
- **Martial law** (military rule — imposed under abnormal circumstances to restore order) can restrict Fundamental Rights in any area (Article 34).
- Martial law is **different** from a National Emergency.

### Feature 12: Some Require Parliamentary Legislation
- Most Fundamental Rights are **directly enforceable** (self-executory).
- A few can only be enforced based on a **law made by Parliament** (not state legislatures), ensuring **uniformity** throughout India (Article 35).

## 4. Definition of 'State' Under Article 12

The term **'State'** is used throughout Part III. **Article 12** defines it for this purpose.

**The 'State' includes:**
1. **Government and Parliament of India** (Executive + Legislature of the Union)
2. **Government and Legislature of States** (Executive + Legislature of each State)
3. **All Local Authorities** — municipalities, panchayats, district boards, improvement trusts, etc.
4. **All Other Authorities** — statutory or non-statutory bodies like **LIC, ONGC, SAIL**, etc.

> 💡 **Key Point**: Even a **private body or agency** working as an **instrument of the State** falls within the meaning of 'State' under Article 12 (Supreme Court ruling).

This wide definition means the actions of all these agencies can be challenged in court for violating Fundamental Rights.

---

## 5. Laws Inconsistent with Fundamental Rights — Article 13

**Article 13** declares that any law inconsistent with or in derogation (violation) of Fundamental Rights shall be **void** (invalid).

This expressly provides for the **doctrine of judicial review** (courts' power to strike down unconstitutional laws).

This power is held by:
- **Supreme Court** (Article 32)
- **High Courts** (Article 226)

### What Does 'Law' Mean Under Article 13?
The term 'law' here is given a **wide meaning** and includes:
- **Permanent laws** enacted by Parliament or state legislatures
- **Temporary laws** like ordinances issued by the President or Governors
- **Statutory instruments** — delegated legislation like orders, bye-laws, rules, regulations, notifications
- **Non-legislative sources of law** — customs or usages having the force of law

### What About Constitutional Amendments?
- Article 13 says a **constitutional amendment is NOT a 'law'** and hence cannot be challenged.
- BUT the Supreme Court ruled in the **Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)** that a Constitutional Amendment **can** be challenged if it violates a Fundamental Right that forms part of the **'basic structure'** of the Constitution.

## 6. Right to Equality (Articles 14–18)

### 6.1 Article 14: Equality Before Law and Equal Protection of Laws

Article 14 states: *"The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India."*

- Available to **all persons** — citizens, foreigners, and legal persons (companies, corporations).

**Two Concepts:**

| Concept | Origin | Meaning | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Equality before Law** | British origin | No special privileges; everyone subject to same law; no one above the law | **Negative** concept |
| **Equal Protection of Laws** | American Constitution | Equal treatment under equal circumstances; like should be treated alike | **Positive** concept |

Both aim at establishing **equality of legal status, opportunity, and justice**.

> 💡 **Key SC Ruling**: Article 14 **forbids class legislation** but **permits reasonable classification** of persons, objects, and transactions. Classification must be based on an **intelligible differential** (logical difference) and a **substantial distinction** (meaningful difference) — not arbitrary, artificial, or evasive.

### The Rule of Law (A.V. Dicey)
The concept of 'equality before law' is part of the **Rule of Law** propounded by **A.V. Dicey**, the British jurist. It has three elements:

1. **Absence of arbitrary power** — no man can be punished except for a breach of law.
2. **Equality before law** — all citizens, rich or poor, high or low, official or non-official, are subject to the same ordinary law courts.
3. **Primacy of individual rights** — the Constitution is a *result* of individual rights (rights come first, Constitution follows).

> ⚠️ **For India**: Elements **1 and 2** apply to India. Element **3 does NOT** apply — in India, the **Constitution is the SOURCE** of individual rights.

The Supreme Court held that the **Rule of Law** as embodied in Article 14 is a **'basic feature'** of the Constitution — it cannot be destroyed even by amendment.

### Exceptions to Equality Before Law
The rule is not absolute. Constitutional and other exceptions include:

1. **President and Governors** (Article 361):
   - Not answerable to any court for exercise of their official powers.
   - No criminal proceedings can be started or continued against them during their term of office.
   - No process for arrest or imprisonment can be issued against them during their term.
   - No civil proceedings against them in their personal capacity unless 2 months' notice has been given after their term expires.

2. **Parliamentary Publications** (Article 361-A): No person is liable for civil or criminal proceedings for publishing a substantially true report of Parliament or State Legislature proceedings in a newspaper, radio, or TV.

3. **Members of Parliament** (Article 105): MPs cannot be held liable in any court for anything said or any vote given in Parliament or its committees.

4. **Members of State Legislature** (Article 194): MLAs/MLCs cannot be held liable for anything said or any vote given in the legislature or its committees.

5. **Article 31-C Exception**: Laws made to implement Directive Principles under Article 39(b) or (c) cannot be challenged on the ground of violating Article 14. As the SC said: *"Where Article 31-C comes in, Article 14 goes out."*

6. **Foreign Sovereigns, Ambassadors, Diplomats**: Enjoy immunity from criminal and civil proceedings.

7. **UNO and Its Agencies**: Enjoy diplomatic immunity.

### 6.2 Article 15: Prohibition of Discrimination on Certain Grounds

Article 15 prohibits the State from discriminating against any **citizen** on grounds **only** of:
- Religion
- Race
- Caste
- Sex
- Place of birth

> 💡 **Two crucial words**: *'discrimination'* (to make an adverse distinction) and *'only'* (discrimination on OTHER grounds is not prohibited by this article).

The **second provision** of Article 15 also prohibits discrimination by **both State AND private individuals** regarding:
- Access to shops, public restaurants, hotels, places of public entertainment
- Use of wells, tanks, bathing ghats, roads, and places of public resort maintained from State funds or dedicated to public use

### Four Exceptions (Permitted Special Provisions):
1. **Women and Children** (Article 15(3)): State can make special provisions for them. E.g., reservation of seats for women in local bodies.

2. **Socially & Educationally Backward Classes / SC / ST** (Article 15(4)): State can make special provisions for their advancement. *Added by the 1st Amendment Act, 1951.*

3. **Admission to Educational Institutions** (Article 15(5)): State can make special provisions for advancement of backward classes/SC/ST in educational institutions — including **private** institutions (aided or unaided), **except** minority educational institutions. *Added by the 93rd Amendment Act, 2005.*

4. **Economically Weaker Sections (EWSs)** (Article 15(6)): State can make special provisions for EWSs including **up to 10% reservation** in educational institutions (including private, aided or unaided), **except** minority educational institutions. This is **in addition** to existing reservations. EWSs to be identified based on family income and economic indicators. *Added by the 103rd Amendment Act, 2019.*

### Reservation for OBCs in Educational Institutions
- Exception (c) (Article 15(5)) was added by the **93rd Amendment Act of 2005**.
- The Centre enacted the **Central Educational Institutions (Reservation in Admission) Act, 2006** — providing a **27% quota for OBCs** in all central higher educational institutions including **IITs and IIMs**.
- In **April 2008**, the Supreme Court **upheld** the validity of both the Amendment Act and the OBC Quota Act.
- The Court directed the Centre to **exclude the 'creamy layer'** (advanced sections) among OBCs while implementing the law.

### Who is in the 'Creamy Layer' Among OBCs?
Children of the following people are in the 'creamy layer' and do **not** get the quota benefit:
- Persons holding **constitutional posts** (President, VP, SC/HC judges, UPSC/SPSC members, CEC, CAG, etc.)
- **Group A/Class I and Group B/Class II officers** of All India, Central, and State Services; employees in equivalent posts in PSUs, banks, insurance organisations, universities, or private employment
- Persons of **colonel rank and above** in the Army and equivalent posts in Navy, Air Force, and Paramilitary
- **Professionals** — doctors, lawyers, engineers, artists, authors, consultants, etc.
- Persons in **trade, business, and industry**
- People holding **agricultural land above a certain limit** and vacant land or buildings in urban areas
- Persons with **gross annual income of more than ₹8 lakh** or wealth above the exemption limit

**Creamy Layer Income Ceiling History:**
- 1993: ₹1 lakh → 2004: ₹2.5 lakh → 2008: ₹4.5 lakh → 2013: ₹6 lakh → **2017: ₹8 lakh**

### Reservation for EWSs in Educational Institutions
- Exception (d) (Article 15(6)) was added by the **103rd Amendment Act of 2019**.
- The Central Government issued an order in 2019 providing **10% reservation to EWSs** in admission to educational institutions.
- Available to persons **not covered** under existing reservations for SC, ST, and OBC.

**Eligibility criteria for EWS:**
- **Family income below ₹8 lakh** per annum (from all sources: salary, agriculture, business, profession, etc.) for the financial year prior to the application year.
- Persons excluded from EWS (irrespective of income) if they own:
  - 5 acres of agricultural land and above
  - Residential flat of 1000 sq. ft. and above
  - Residential plot of 100 sq. yards and above in notified municipalities
  - Residential plot of 200 sq. yards and above in areas other than notified municipalities
- Property held in different locations is **clubbed** while applying the land/property holding test.
- **'Family'** for this purpose includes the applicant, his/her parents, siblings below 18 years, spouse, and children below 18 years.

### 6.3 Article 16: Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment

Article 16 provides **equality of opportunity** for all **citizens** in employment or appointment to any office under the State.

No citizen can be discriminated against on grounds **only** of: religion, race, caste, sex, descent, place of birth, or residence.

### Four Exceptions:
1. **Residence requirement** (Article 16(3)): Parliament can prescribe residence as a condition for certain employment in a state or UT. (The Public Employment Act of 1957 expired in 1974; only **Andhra Pradesh** and **Telangana** retain this under Article 371D.)

2. **Reservation for Backward Classes / SC / ST** (Article 16(4), 16(4A), 16(4B)):
   - State can reserve posts for **backward classes not adequately represented** in state services.
   - State can provide **reservation in promotions** (with consequential seniority) for SCs/STs not adequately represented (added by 77th Amendment Act, 1995; modified by 85th Amendment Act, 2001).
   - **Backlog vacancies** (unfilled reserved vacancies) can be treated as a separate class and filled in succeeding years without counting towards the 50% ceiling (added by 81st Amendment Act, 2000).

3. **Religious qualification** (Article 16(5)): A law can require that the incumbent of an office in a religious or denominational institution must belong to that particular religion/denomination.

4. **EWS Reservation** (Article 16(6)): State can provide **up to 10% reservation** in appointments/posts for EWSs, in addition to existing reservations. *Added by the 103rd Amendment Act, 2019.*

### Mandal Commission and Its Aftermath

**Background:**
- In **1979**, the **Morarji Desai Government** appointed the **Second Backward Classes Commission** under **B.P. Mandal** (MP) under **Article 340** of the Constitution.
  - *Note: The First Backward Classes Commission was appointed in 1953 under **Kaka Kalelkar**, which submitted its report in 1955.*
- The Mandal Commission submitted its **report in 1980**.
- It identified **3,743 castes** as socially and educationally backward classes.
- These constitute nearly **52% of the population** (excluding SCs and STs).
- It recommended **27% reservation** in government jobs for OBCs so that total reservation (SCs + STs + OBCs) amounts to **50%** (as the Supreme Court in 1963 ruled that more than 50% reservation in a single year would be unconstitutional).

**Government Actions:**
- **1990**: V.P. Singh Government declared **27% reservation** for OBCs in government jobs.
- **1991**: Narasimha Rao Government introduced two changes:
  - Preference to **poorer sections** among OBCs in the 27% quota (economic criteria).
  - Additional **10% reservation** for other economically backward sections not covered by existing schemes.

**Indra Sawhney Case (1992)** — also called the **Mandal Case:**
The Supreme Court examined the scope of Article 16(4) thoroughly and ruled:
- The **additional 10% for economically backward sections** was **rejected**.
- **27% reservation for OBCs** was **upheld**, with conditions:
  1. **Creamy layer** among OBCs must be excluded from reservation benefits.
  2. **No reservation in promotions** — only in initial appointments. Existing promotion reservation to continue for only **5 more years** (till 1997).
  3. **Total reserved quota must not exceed 50%** except in extraordinary situations. Applied every year.
  4. The **'carry forward rule'** for unfilled (backlog) vacancies is valid, but the 50% rule must be observed.
  5. A **permanent statutory body** must be set up to examine complaints of over-inclusion/under-inclusion in OBC lists.
  6. **No relaxations** in qualifying marks or evaluation standards in promotion reservations (to maintain efficiency under Article 335).

**Government Responses to the Rulings:**
1. **Ram Nandan Committee** (1993): Appointed to identify creamy layer. Report accepted in 1993.
2. **National Commission for Backward Classes (NCBC)**: Established in 1993 by Act of Parliament to examine complaints about OBC list inclusions/exclusions. Later, the **102nd Amendment Act of 2018** gave it **constitutional status** and enlarged its functions by inserting **new Article 338-B**.
3. **77th Amendment Act (1995)**: Nullified the ruling on promotion reservation — empowers State to provide reservation in promotions for SCs/STs.
4. **85th Amendment Act (2001)**: Provides for **'consequential seniority'** in promotion via reservation for government servants of SCs/STs, retrospectively from **June 1995**.
5. **81st Amendment Act (2000)**: Nullified the backlog vacancies ruling — ends the 50% ceiling on backlog vacancy reservations.
6. **76th Amendment Act (1994)**: Placed the **Tamil Nadu Reservations Act (1993)** in the **Ninth Schedule** to protect its **69% reservation** from judicial review (it exceeded the 50% ceiling).
7. **82nd Amendment Act (2000)**: Nullified the ruling on relaxations in qualifying marks — empowers State to allow relaxation in qualifying marks/lowering of standards for SCs/STs in promotion reservations.

**Reservation for EWSs in Public Employment:**
- **103rd Amendment Act of 2019** (Article 16(6)): Central Government issued an order in 2019 providing **10% reservation to EWSs** in civil posts and services under the Government of India.
- Available to EWSs **not covered** under existing SC/ST/OBC reservations.
- Eligibility criteria: Same as under Article 15 (income below ₹8 lakh, no large land/property holdings).
- **Scientific and technical posts** satisfying all four conditions can be **exempted** from this reservation:
  1. Posts in grades above the lower grade in Group A.
  2. Classified as "scientific or technical" per Cabinet Secretariat Order (1961).
  3. Posts are for **conducting, organising, guiding, or directing research**.

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **103rd Constitutional Amendment Act (2019)** providing 10% EWS reservation was challenged before the Supreme Court. In **November 2022**, the Supreme Court upheld its constitutional validity in the **Janhit Abhiyan vs. Union of India case** (3:2 majority). The Court held that the EWS reservation does not violate the basic structure of the Constitution. This was a landmark ruling as it allowed economic criteria alone to be the basis for reservation for the first time.

### 6.4 Article 17: Abolition of Untouchability

- Article 17 **abolishes 'untouchability'** and forbids its practice in **any form**.
- Enforcement of any disability arising from untouchability is a **punishable offence**.
- In **1976**, the **Untouchability (Offences) Act, 1955** was comprehensively amended and renamed as the **Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955** — to enlarge scope and make penal provisions more stringent.
- The Act defines **civil right** as any right accruing to a person by reason of the abolition of untouchability under Article 17.

**Important Notes:**
- The term **'untouchability'** is **not defined** in the Constitution or the Act.
- The **Mysore High Court** held that it refers to the **social disabilities imposed on certain classes by reason of their birth in certain castes** — not literal/grammatical untouchability.
- It does **not** cover social boycott of individuals or exclusion from religious services.
- In the **People's Union for Democratic Rights case (1982)**, the SC held that the right under Article 17 is available **against private individuals** and it is the **constitutional obligation of the State** to take action to ensure this right is not violated.

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989**, amended significantly in **2015 and 2018**, strengthens the implementation of Article 17. The 2018 amendment was necessitated after the Supreme Court's controversial **Subhash Kashinath Mahajan judgment (2018)**, which diluted some provisions. Parliament swiftly reversed those dilutions, reaffirming legislative commitment to protecting Dalit rights. The National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) data regularly tracks atrocities against SCs/STs, reflecting the ongoing relevance of Article 17.

### 6.5 Article 18: Abolition of Titles

Article 18 abolishes titles and makes **four provisions**:
1. **State CANNOT confer any title** (except military or academic distinctions) on anyone — citizen or foreigner.
2. **Citizens CANNOT accept any title** from any foreign state.
3. A **foreigner holding any office of profit/trust** under the State cannot accept a title from a foreign state without **President's consent**.
4. **No citizen or foreigner** holding any office of profit/trust under the State can accept any **present, emolument, or office** from any foreign State without **President's consent**.

- Hereditary titles of nobility like **Maharaja, Raj Bahadur, Rai Bahadur, Rai Saheb, Dewan Bahadur**, etc. (conferred by colonial rulers) are **banned** as they go against the principle of equal status.

**National Awards and Article 18:**
- In the **Balaji Raghavan case (1995)**, the SC upheld the constitutional validity of **Bharat Ratna, Padma Vibhushan, Padma Bhushan, and Padma Sri**.
- These awards do **NOT** amount to 'titles' under Article 18 — Article 18 prohibits only **hereditary titles of nobility**.
- The SC ruled they **should not be used as suffixes or prefixes** to names — violating this means **forfeiture of the award**.
- National Awards were **instituted in 1954**.
- **Discontinued in 1977** by the Janata Party government under Morarji Desai.
- **Revived in 1980** by the Indira Gandhi government.
- **Padma Awards** are announced on **Republic Day** (except for brief interruptions during 1978-79 and 1993-97).
- **Maximum Padma Awards per year**: **120** (excluding posthumous and NRI/Foreigner/OCI awards).
- **Maximum Bharat Ratna per year**: **3**.

## 7. Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22)

### 7.1 Article 19: Protection of Six Rights

Article 19 guarantees six rights to all **citizens** (including shareholders of a company, but **NOT** foreigners or legal persons like companies).

These rights are protected against **State action only**, not against private individuals.

**The Six Rights and Grounds for Restrictions:**

| # | Right (Article 19(1)) | Grounds for Reasonable Restriction |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Freedom of speech and expression** (19(1)(a)) | Sovereignty & integrity of India *(16th Amendment, 1963)*; Security of State; Friendly relations with foreign states *(1st Amendment, 1951)*; Public order *(1st Amendment, 1951)*; Decency or morality; Contempt of court; Defamation; Incitement to an offence *(1st Amendment, 1951)* |
| 2 | **Freedom to assemble peaceably and without arms** (19(1)(b)) | Sovereignty & integrity of India *(16th Amendment, 1963)*; Public order |
| 3 | **Freedom to form associations/unions/co-operative societies** (19(1)(c)) | Sovereignty & integrity of India *(16th Amendment, 1963)*; Public order; Morality |
| 4 | **Freedom to move freely throughout India** (19(1)(d)) | General public interest; Protection of interests of any Scheduled Tribe |
| 5 | **Freedom to reside and settle in any part of India** (19(1)(e)) | General public interest; Protection of interests of any Scheduled Tribe |
| 6 | **Freedom to practise any profession/occupation/trade/business** (19(1)(g)) | General public interest; Professional/technical qualifications; State monopoly on trade/business/industry/service *(1st Amendment, 1951)* |

> 📝 **Note:** The **7th original right** — Right to acquire, hold and dispose of property (Article 19(1)(f)) — was **omitted by the 44th Amendment Act, 1978**.

---

#### Freedom of Speech and Expression

Every citizen can freely express views, opinions, beliefs, and convictions by **word of mouth, writing, printing, picturing, or any other manner**.

The SC has held that this freedom **includes**:
- Right to propagate one's views (Freedom of circulation)
- **Freedom of the press**
- Freedom of commercial advertisements
- Right against tapping of telephonic conversation
- Right to telecast (government has no monopoly on electronic media)
- Right against *bandh* called by a political party or organisation
- Right to know about government activities
- **Freedom of silence**
- Right against imposition of pre-censorship on a newspaper
- Right to demonstration or picketing (but **NOT** right to strike)
- **Right to fly the national flag**
- Right of voters to know antecedents of election candidates
- Right to choose the medium of instruction at primary school stage
- **Right to express gender identity**
- Right to reply (right to answer criticism)
- Right to post information/videos on the internet/social media
- Right of film-makers to exhibit their films
- **Right to access the internet** (right to access information via the internet)

---

#### Freedom of Assembly
- Every citizen has the right to **assemble peaceably and without arms**.
- Includes the right to hold **public meetings, demonstrations, and processions**.
- Must be on **public land**, peaceful, and unarmed.
- Does **NOT** protect violent, disorderly, or riotous assemblies.
- Does **NOT** include the **right to strike**.

**Legal Restrictions:**
- **Section 144, CrPC (1973)**: A magistrate can restrain an assembly if there is risk of obstruction, danger to human life/health/safety, or disturbance of public tranquillity, riot, or affray.
- **Section 141, IPC**: An assembly of **5 or more persons** becomes **unlawful** if the object is:
  - To resist execution of any law or legal process
  - To forcibly occupy another person's property
  - To commit mischief or criminal trespass
  - To force someone to do an illegal act
  - To threaten the government or its officials in exercising lawful powers

---

#### Freedom of Association
- Right to form **associations, unions, or co-operative societies** (the right to form co-operative societies was added by the **97th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2011**).
- Includes the right to form **political parties, companies, partnership firms, societies, clubs, trade unions**, etc.
- Covers both the right to **start** and the right to **continue** an association.
- Also covers the **negative right** — right NOT to form or join an association.
- The **right to obtain recognition** of an association is **NOT** a fundamental right.
- The SC held that trade unions have **no guaranteed right** to effective bargaining, right to strike, or right to declare a lock-out.

---

#### Freedom of Movement
- Every citizen can move **freely throughout the territory of the country** — from state to state or within a state.
- Promotes **national feeling** and discourages parochialism.
- Restrictions: general public interest and protection of **Scheduled Tribe** interests (to protect their culture, language, customs, and properties from exploitation).
- The SC held that the movement of **prostitutes** can be restricted on grounds of public health and public morals.
- The Bombay High Court validated restrictions on movement of persons affected by **AIDS**.

**Two Dimensions of Freedom of Movement:**
- **Internal** (movement within India) → Protected by **Article 19**
- **External** (right to travel abroad and return) → Protected by **Article 21**

---

#### Freedom of Residence
- Every citizen has the right to **reside and settle** in any part of India.
- **Right to reside** = stay temporarily anywhere
- **Right to settle** = set up a permanent home/domicile anywhere
- Removes internal barriers and promotes nationalism.
- Restrictions: same as freedom of movement (general public interest + Scheduled Tribe protection).
- The SC held that **prostitutes and habitual offenders** can be banned from certain areas.

---

#### Freedom of Profession, Occupation, Trade, or Business
- Every citizen has the right to practise any profession or carry on any occupation, trade, or business.
- Very wide right — covers **all means of earning livelihood**.
- The State can:
  - Prescribe **professional or technical qualifications**.
  - Carry on by itself any trade/business/industry/service — whether as a **complete or partial monopoly** — without needing to justify it.
- Does **NOT** include right to carry on activities that are **immoral** (e.g., trafficking) or **dangerous** (e.g., harmful drugs/explosives).

**Important SC Judgment — Anuradha Bhasin Case (2020):**
- The SC held that the **freedom to practise any profession or trade over the medium of the internet** enjoys constitutional protection under **Article 19(1)(g)**.
- Restrictions on internet must conform to **Article 19(6)**, including the test of **proportionality**.
- **An order suspending internet services indefinitely is impermissible** under the Temporary Suspension of Telecom Services (Public Emergency or Public Safety) Rules, 2017.

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **Anuradha Bhasin case (2020)** remains highly relevant. In **2023**, the internet was suspended multiple times across states (e.g., Haryana, Rajasthan during protests). The Supreme Court's ruling sets a clear standard: indefinite internet suspensions are unconstitutional, and any restriction must pass the proportionality test. India consistently ranks among the top countries for **internet shutdowns globally** (according to Access Now reports), making Article 19 protections critically important.

---

### 7.2 Article 20: Protection in Respect of Conviction for Offences

Article 20 grants **protection against arbitrary and excessive punishment** to an accused person — citizen, foreigner, or legal person (company/corporation).

It contains **three protections**:

**1. No Ex-Post-Facto Law** (retroactive punishment):
- No person can be convicted for an act unless it was an offence under the law **in force at the time** of commission.
- No penalty greater than that prescribed by the law **at the time** of the act.
- **Note**: This applies only to **criminal laws** — NOT to civil or tax laws (civil liability or tax can be imposed retrospectively).
- This prohibits **conviction or sentence** under an ex-post-facto law — but NOT the trial.
- Does **not** apply to **preventive detention** or demanding security.

**2. No Double Jeopardy** (tried twice for same offence):
- No person can be **prosecuted and punished** for the same offence more than once.
- Available only in proceedings before a **court of law or judicial tribunal** — NOT in departmental or administrative proceedings.

**3. No Self-Incrimination** (forced to be a witness against oneself):
- No accused person can be **compelled** to be a witness against himself/herself.
- Extends to both **oral and documentary evidence**.
- Does **NOT** extend to:
  - Compulsory production of material objects
  - Giving thumb impressions, specimen signatures, blood specimens
  - Compulsory exhibition of the body
- Extends only to **criminal proceedings** — NOT to civil proceedings.

---

### 7.3 Article 21: Protection of Life and Personal Liberty

Article 21 declares: *"No person shall be deprived of his or her life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."*

Available to **both citizens and non-citizens**.

### Evolution: From Narrow to Wide Interpretation

**Gopalan Case (1950) — Narrow View:**
- The SC held that Article 21 protects only against **arbitrary executive action**, not against arbitrary legislative action.
- The State can deprive life/liberty of a person **based on a law**.
- The validity of a law prescribing a procedure cannot be questioned on the ground of being unreasonable or unjust.
- 'Personal liberty' = only liberty relating to the **person or body** of the individual.

**Maneka Gandhi Case (1978) — Wide View (Overruled Gopalan):**
- The SC held that life/liberty can be deprived by law only if the **procedure is reasonable, fair, and just** — NOT arbitrary, fanciful, or oppressive.
- The procedure must conform to **principles of natural justice** (i.e., the American expression **'due process of law'** was introduced).
- Protection under Article 21 is available against **both arbitrary executive AND arbitrary legislative action**.
- **'Right to life'** means NOT merely animal existence/survival but **the right to live with human dignity** and all aspects that make life meaningful, complete, and worth living.
- **'Personal Liberty'** is of the **widest amplitude** — covering a vast variety of rights.

### Rights Declared Under Article 21 by the Supreme Court:
1. Right to live with human dignity
2. Right to decent environment (pollution-free water and air; protection from hazardous industries)
3. Right to livelihood
4. **Right to privacy** *(K.S. Puttaswamy vs. Union of India, 2017)*
5. Right to shelter
6. Right to health
7. Right to free education up to 14 years of age
8. Right to free legal aid
9. Right against solitary confinement
10. **Right to speedy trial**
11. Right against handcuffing
12. Right against inhuman treatment
13. Right against delayed execution
14. Right to travel abroad
15. Right against bonded labour
16. Right against custodial harassment
17. Right to emergency medical aid (right to doctor's assistance)
18. Right to timely medical treatment in government hospital
19. Right not to be driven out of a state
20. **Right to fair trial**
21. Right of prisoner to have necessities of life
22. Right of women to be treated with decency and dignity
23. Right against public hanging
24. Right to road in hilly areas
25. **Right to information**
26. Right to reputation
27. Right of appeal from a judgement of conviction
28. Right to family pension
29. Right to social and economic justice and empowerment
30. Right against bar fetters
31. Right to appropriate life insurance policy
32. **Right to sleep**
33. Right to freedom from noise pollution
34. Right to sustainable development
35. Right to opportunity
36. Right to decent burial/cremation
37. **Right to marry a person of one's choice**
38. **Right to die with dignity (passive euthanasia)** *(Common Cause vs. Union of India, 2018)*
39. **Right against the adverse effects of climate change**

### Important Cases Expanding Article 21:

| # | Case (Year) | Right Declared |
|---|---|---|

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **K.S. Puttaswamy judgment (2017)** recognising **Right to Privacy** as a Fundamental Right under Article 21 has had massive downstream impact:
> - It influenced the drafting of the **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** — India's first comprehensive data protection law.
> - The **Personal Data Protection Bill** went through multiple revisions before the 2023 Act was passed.
> - Right to Privacy is also the basis for challenging **Aadhaar-linked mandates** and surveillance practices.
> - The **Pegasus spyware controversy (2021)** was widely discussed in the context of this right.

### 7.4 Article 21A: Right to Education

Article 21A declares: *"The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years."*

- Makes only **elementary education** a Fundamental Right (NOT higher or professional education).
- Added by the **86th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2002** — described as *"the dawn of the second revolution in citizens' rights."*

**Background:**
- Before this amendment, **Article 45** (a Directive Principle) provided for free and compulsory education for children. But being a Directive Principle, it was **not enforceable** by courts.
- After the amendment, **Article 45 was changed** to read: *"The State shall endeavour to provide early childhood care and education for all children until they complete the age of six years."*
- A **new fundamental duty under Article 51A** was added: Citizens must provide opportunities for education to their child/ward between **6 and 14 years of age**.

**Key Cases:**
- **Mohini Jain case (1992)**: SC partly declared a fundamental right to education up to any level — including professional education (medicine, engineering). *This was later overruled.*
- **Unni Krishnan case (1993)**: SC recognised a fundamental right to **primary education** under Article 21. Held that every child has the right to free education until age 14. After 14, the right to education is **subject to economic capacity and development of the state**. *This overruled the Mohini Jain judgment.*

**Right to Education Act:**
- In pursuance of Article 21A, Parliament enacted the **Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education (RTE) Act, 2009**.
- This Act provides that every child has a right to **full-time elementary education of satisfactory and equitable quality** in a formal school satisfying essential norms and standards.
- Both the 86th Amendment Act (2002) and the RTE Act (2009) came into force on **1 April 2010**.

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **RTE Act, 2009** requires private unaided schools to reserve **25% seats for economically weaker sections (EWS) children**. However, its implementation remains uneven. The **National Achievement Survey (NAS) 2021** revealed significant learning gaps — students in Grade 3 showed average proficiency of only 57% in language and 44% in mathematics. This led to the government launching the **National Initiative for Proficiency in Reading with Understanding and Numeracy (NIPUN Bharat, 2021)** — a mission to ensure foundational literacy and numeracy by Grade 3 by 2026-27, directly addressing the quality dimension of Article 21A.

### 7.5 Article 22: Protection Against Arrest and Detention

**Two Types of Detention:**
- **Punitive detention**: Punishment AFTER trial and conviction by a court.
- **Preventive detention**: Detention WITHOUT trial. Purpose is NOT to punish for a past offence but to **prevent** a future offence. It is a **precautionary measure based on suspicion**.

Article 22 has **two parts**:

**Part 1 — Rights Under Ordinary Law:**
A person arrested under ordinary law has:
1. Right to be **informed of the grounds of arrest**.
2. Right to **consult and be defended** by a legal practitioner.
3. Right to be **produced before a magistrate within 24 hours** (excluding journey time).
4. Right to be **released after 24 hours** unless magistrate authorises further detention.

These safeguards are **NOT available to**:
- **Enemy aliens**
- Persons arrested under **preventive detention law**

**Part 2 — Rights Under Preventive Detention Law:**
Available to **both citizens and aliens**:
1. Detention cannot exceed **3 months** unless an Advisory Board (consisting of High Court judges) reports sufficient cause for extended detention.
2. **Grounds of detention must be communicated** — but facts considered against public interest need **not** be disclosed.
3. The detained person (**detenu**) must be given an **opportunity to make a representation** against the detention order.

**Parliament's Power Under Article 22:**
Parliament can prescribe:
- Circumstances and classes of cases where detention can exceed 3 months without Advisory Board opinion.
- Maximum period of detention.
- Procedure to be followed by Advisory Board.

> ⚠️ The **44th Amendment Act (1978)** reduced the period for Advisory Board review from **3 to 2 months** — but this provision has **not yet been brought into force**, so the **original 3-month period** still continues.

**Division of Legislative Power on Preventive Detention:**
- **Parliament alone**: Defence, foreign affairs, security of India.
- **Parliament AND State Legislatures** (concurrently): Security of a state, maintenance of public order, maintenance of supplies and services essential to the community.

**Major Preventive Detention Laws Made by Parliament:**
1. Preventive Detention Act, 1950 — *Expired 1969*
2. Maintenance of Internal Security Act (MISA), 1971 — *Repealed 1978*
3. Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act (COFEPOSA), 1974
4. National Security Act (NSA), 1980
5. Prevention of Blackmarketing and Maintenance of Supplies of Essential Commodities Act (PBMSECA), 1980
6. Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act (TADA), 1985 — *Repealed 1995*
7. Prevention of Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act (PITNDPSA), 1988
8. Prevention of Terrorism Act (POTA), 2002 — *Repealed 2004*
9. **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), 1967** — as amended in **2004, 2008, 2012, and 2019**

> ⚠️ **Unique Feature**: India is the only democratic country in the world that has made **preventive detention an integral part of its Constitution**. It was unknown in the USA. Britain resorted to it only during World War I and II. In India, it existed even during British rule (e.g., Bengal State Prisoners Regulation of 1818; Defence of India Act, 1939).

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **UAPA (Amendment) Act, 2019** is highly significant — it allows the **designation of individuals as terrorists** (not just organisations), which critics argue creates a risk of misuse. The Supreme Court in **Union of India vs. K.A. Najeeb (2021)** held that courts CAN grant bail in UAPA cases if Fundamental Rights (especially Article 21) are violated. This marked an important limit on preventive detention. The **National Human Rights Commission (NHRC)** regularly receives complaints about misuse of detention laws.

## 8. Right Against Exploitation (Articles 23–24)

### 8.1 Article 23: Prohibition of Traffic in Human Beings and Forced Labour

- **Prohibits**: Traffic in human beings, **begar** (forced labour without payment), and other similar forms of forced labour.
- Any contravention is a **punishable offence**.
- Available to **both citizens and non-citizens**.
- Protects against **both the State and private persons**.

**'Traffic in Human Beings' includes:**
- Selling and buying of men, women, and children like goods
- Immoral traffic in women and children, including prostitution
- Devadasi system
- Slavery

Parliament enacted the **Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956** (originally called the Suppression of Immoral Traffic in Women and Girls Act, 1956).

**Begar**: Compulsory work without remuneration — a peculiar Indian system where zamindars forced tenants to work without payment.

**'Forced Labour'**: Compelling a person to work against their will. Includes not just physical/legal force but also **force arising from economic compulsion** (e.g., working for less than minimum wage).

Related laws enacted:
- **Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act, 1976**
- **Minimum Wages Act, 1948**
- **Contract Labour Act, 1970**
- **Equal Remuneration Act, 1976**

**Exception**: The State can impose **compulsory service for public purposes** (e.g., military service, social service) without payment — but **without discrimination** on grounds of religion, race, caste, or class.

---

### 8.2 Article 24: Prohibition of Employment of Children in Factories, etc.

- Prohibits employment of **children below 14 years** in any **factory, mine, or hazardous activities** (like construction or railway).
- Does **NOT** prohibit employment in **harmless or innocent work**.

**Key Legislation:**
- **Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act, 1986** — most important law in this direction.
- Other laws: Employment of Children Act (1938), Factories Act (1948), Mines Act (1952), Merchant Shipping Act (1958), Plantation Labour Act (1951), Motor Transport Workers Act (1951), Apprentices Act (1961), Bidi and Cigar Workers Act (1966), etc.

**Supreme Court Directions (1996):**
- Establishment of **Child Labour Rehabilitation Welfare Fund**.
- Offending employer must deposit a fine of **₹20,000 per child** employed illegally.
- Directions for improvement of education, health, and nutrition of children.

**Other Developments:**
- **Commissions for Protection of Child Rights Act, 2005**: Established the **National Commission** and **State Commissions for Protection of Child Rights** and Children's Courts for speedy trial.
- **2006**: Government banned employment of children as **domestic servants or workers** in hotels, dhabas, restaurants, shops, factories, resorts, spas, tea-shops, etc.
- **Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act, 2016**: Renamed the principal Act as the **Child and Adolescent Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act, 1986**.

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` During the **COVID-19 pandemic (2020-21)**, there were widespread reports of an increase in child labour across India as economic hardship forced families to pull children out of school. The **UNICEF India report (2021)** flagged this reversal of progress. The government's **PM POSHAN (formerly Mid-Day Meal) scheme** and **PM eVIDYA (2020)** initiative aimed at keeping children in school even during the pandemic — both directly support Article 24 and Article 21A goals.

## 9. Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28)

### 9.1 Article 25: Freedom of Conscience and Free Profession, Practice, and Propagation of Religion

All persons are equally entitled to:

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Freedom of conscience** | Inner freedom to shape one's relationship with God in any way desired |
| **Right to profess** | Openly and freely declaring one's religious beliefs and faith |
| **Right to practice** | Performing religious worship, rituals, ceremonies; exhibiting beliefs and ideas |
| **Right to propagate** | Sharing/transmitting one's religious beliefs to others; explaining religion's tenets |

> ⚠️ **Propagation does NOT include the right to convert another person by force.** Forcible conversions violate the 'freedom of conscience' guaranteed to all persons.

- Covers both **religious beliefs (doctrines)** and **religious practices (rituals)**.
- Available to **all persons — citizens and non-citizens**.
- Subject to: public order, morality, health, and other Fundamental Rights provisions.

**State Permitted to:**
1. Regulate or restrict any **economic, financial, political, or other secular activity** associated with religious practice.
2. Provide for **social welfare and reform** or throw open **Hindu religious institutions** of a public character to **all classes and sections of Hindus**.

**Special Explanations in Article 25:**
- Wearing and carrying of **kirpans** is included in the profession of the **Sikh religion**.
- For this clause, **Hindus include Sikhs, Jains, and Buddhists** — and Hindu religious institutions are construed accordingly.

---

### 9.2 Article 26: Freedom to Manage Religious Affairs

Every **religious denomination or any section thereof** has the right to:
1. **Establish and maintain** institutions for religious and charitable purposes.
2. **Manage its own affairs** in matters of religion.
3. **Own and acquire** movable and immovable property.
4. **Administer** such property in accordance with law.

- Article 25 = rights of **individuals** | Article 26 = rights of **religious denominations** (collective freedom).
- Subject to: public order, morality, and health (but NOT subject to other Fundamental Rights provisions — unlike Article 25).

**Three conditions for a 'religious denomination' (SC ruling):**
1. A collection of individuals with a **system of beliefs** (doctrines) they regard as conducive to spiritual well-being.
2. A **common organisation**.
3. Designated by a **distinctive name**.

**SC Rulings:**
- **'Ramakrishna Mission'** and **'Ananda Marga'** = religious denominations within the Hindu religion. ✅
- **'Aurobindo Society'** = NOT a religious denomination. ❌

---

### 9.3 Article 27: Freedom from Taxation for Promotion of a Religion

- **No person shall be compelled to pay taxes** for the promotion or maintenance of any particular religion or denomination.
- The State cannot spend public money collected as tax for **promoting any one religion**.
- The State can, however, use taxes for the promotion of **ALL religions** (no favouritism).

**Important Distinction:**
- This provision prohibits **levy of a tax** but NOT a **fee**.
- A fee is charged for **regulating secular administration** of religious institutions — NOT for promoting religion.
- Example: A fee can be levied on pilgrims for special services or safety measures; a fee can be levied on religious endowments for regulation expenditure.

---

### 9.4 Article 28: Freedom from Attending Religious Instruction

**Four types of educational institutions and religious instruction rules:**

| Type of Institution | Rule on Religious Instruction |
|---|---|
| Wholly maintained by the State | **Completely prohibited** |
| Administered by the State but established under endowment/trust requiring religious instruction | **Permitted** |
| Recognised by the State | **Permitted, but on a voluntary basis (with consent)** |
| Receiving aid from the State | **Permitted, but on a voluntary basis (with consent)** |

- For minors, the **consent of their guardian** is needed.

---

## 10. Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30)

### 10.1 Article 29: Protection of Interests of Minorities

- Any **section of citizens** residing in India with a **distinct language, script, or culture** has the right to **conserve** the same.
- No citizen shall be denied admission to any educational institution **maintained by the State or receiving State aid** on grounds **only** of religion, race, caste, or language.

**Important Points:**
- **First provision** = protects the right of a **group**.
- **Second provision** = guarantees the right of a **citizen as an individual**.
- Grants protection to **both religious AND linguistic minorities**.
- The SC held the scope of Article 29 is **NOT limited to minorities only** — 'section of citizens' includes **majority** as well.
- The SC also held that the **right to conserve language includes the right to agitate** for its protection. Political speeches or promises for conservation of language do not amount to a **corrupt practice** under the Representation of the People Act, 1951.

---

### 10.2 Article 30: Right of Minorities to Establish and Administer Educational Institutions

Grants the following rights to **minorities (religious OR linguistic)**:
1. All minorities have the right to **establish and administer** educational institutions of their choice.
2. The **compensation** fixed by the State for compulsory acquisition of minority educational institution property shall not restrict or abrogate this right. *(Added by the 44th Amendment Act, 1978 — the same amendment that deleted the Right to Property.)*
3. In granting aid, the State shall **not discriminate** against any minority-managed educational institution.

> ⚠️ **Note**: The term **'minority' is NOT defined** anywhere in the Constitution.

**Three Types of Minority Educational Institutions:**

| Type | State's Power |
|---|---|
| Seeks both **recognition AND aid** | Subject to State regulation (syllabus, standards, discipline, sanitation, teaching staff, etc.) |
| Seeks **only recognition** (not aid) | Subject to State regulation |
| Seeks **neither recognition nor aid** | Free to administer affairs, but subject to general laws (contract law, labour law, tax law, etc.) |

**Key Principles (Secretary of Malankara Syrian Catholic College case, 2006):**

1. Rights under Article 30 include:
   - Choosing its **governing body** in whom founders have faith
   - Appointing **teaching and non-teaching staff**; taking action for dereliction of duty
   - Admitting **eligible students of their choice** and setting a **reasonable fee structure**
   - Using **properties and assets** for the benefit of the institution

2. Article 30 is meant only to ensure **equality with the majority** — NOT to place minorities in an advantageous position. There is **no reverse discrimination** in favour of minorities.

3. The right to establish and administer is **NOT absolute** and does **NOT include the right to maladminister**. Regulatory measures for educational character, standards, and academic excellence are permissible.

4. **Unaided** minority institutions have the **freedom to appoint teachers** by any rational selection procedure, subject to the State's eligibility conditions being met.

5. **State aid** does not alter the nature and character of the minority institution — but conditions can be imposed to ensure proper utilisation of aid, without abridging Article 30(1) rights.

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020** raised concerns among minority educational institutions about regulatory changes. The **Aligarh Muslim University (AMU) minority status case** is currently before a **7-judge Constitution Bench** of the Supreme Court — one of the most significant Article 30 disputes in recent years. The SC is reconsidering whether a university established by a pre-constitutional statute can claim minority status. This case will likely redefine the boundaries of Article 30.

## 11. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)

A declaration of Fundamental Rights is meaningless without an **effective enforcement mechanism**. Article 32 provides the remedy.

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar called Article 32 the **most important article** of the Constitution:
> *"An article without which this Constitution would be a nullity. It is the very soul of the Constitution and the very heart of it."*

The SC ruled that **Article 32 is a basic feature** of the Constitution — it cannot be abridged or taken away, even by constitutional amendment.

**Four Key Provisions of Article 32:**
1. The right to **move the Supreme Court** for enforcement of Fundamental Rights is **guaranteed**.
2. The SC has power to issue **directions, orders, or writs** for enforcement of Fundamental Rights.
3. **Parliament** can empower any other court to issue writs (but this has not been done so far — hence only SC and HCs can issue writs).
4. The right to move the SC **shall not be suspended** except as provided by the Constitution (i.e., the President can suspend this right during a National Emergency under Article 359).

**Article 32 vs. Article 226:**

| Feature | Article 32 (Supreme Court) | Article 226 (High Court) |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | ONLY for enforcement of Fundamental Rights | For Fundamental Rights AND any other purpose (ordinary legal rights) |
| Territorial jurisdiction | Throughout India | Within its territorial jurisdiction (or where cause of action arises) |
| Nature of remedy | In itself a **Fundamental Right** — SC cannot refuse | **Discretionary** — HC may refuse |
| Scope | Narrower (only FRs) | **Wider** (FRs + other rights) |

> 💡 **Important**: The SC's territorial jurisdiction for writs is **wider** than an HC's, but its subject-matter jurisdiction for writs is **narrower** than an HC's.

> ⚠️ Only **Fundamental Rights** can be enforced under Article 32 — NOT non-fundamental constitutional rights, statutory rights, or customary rights.

The jurisdiction of the SC under Article 32 is **original but not exclusive** — it is **concurrent** with HC jurisdiction under Article 226. An aggrieved party can approach either directly. However, the SC has ruled that **where HC relief is available under Article 226, the aggrieved party should first approach the HC**.

## 12. Writs — Types and Scope

The SC (under Article 32) and HCs (under Article 226) can issue **five types of writs**:

> 💡 **Historical Note**: Before 1950, only the HCs of Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras could issue writs. Article 226 now empowers **all High Courts**. These writs are borrowed from **English law** where they are called **'prerogative writs'** — issued in exercise of the King's prerogative (who was described as the 'fountain of justice').

---

### Writ 1: Habeas Corpus
- Latin: **"To have the body of"**
- An order to a person who has **detained** another person, to **produce the body** of the detained person before the court.
- Court examines the **cause and legality** of detention and sets the person free if detention is **illegal**.
- A **bulwark of individual liberty** against arbitrary detention.
- Can be issued against **both public authorities AND private individuals**.

**Habeas Corpus is NOT issued when:**
- Detention is **lawful**
- Proceeding is for **contempt** of a legislature or court
- Detention is by a **competent court**
- Detention is **outside the court's jurisdiction**

---

### Writ 2: Mandamus
- Latin: **"We command"**
- A command to a **public official** to perform their **official duties** that they have failed or refused to perform.
- Can also be issued against **public bodies, corporations, inferior courts, tribunals, or governments**.

**Mandamus CANNOT be issued:**
- Against a **private individual or body**
- To enforce **departmental instructions** without statutory force
- When the duty is **discretionary** (not mandatory)
- To enforce a **contractual obligation**
- Against the **President of India or State Governors**

---

### Writ 3: Prohibition
- Literally: **"To forbid"**
- Issued by a **higher court to a lower court/tribunal** to prevent it from **exceeding its jurisdiction** or usurping jurisdiction it doesn't have.
- Unlike Mandamus (directs activity), Prohibition **directs inactivity**.
- Can be issued **ONLY against judicial and quasi-judicial authorities** — NOT against administrative authorities.
- Issued **BEFORE** the final order is passed.

---

### Writ 4: Certiorari
- Latin: **"To be certified / to be informed"**
- Issued by a **higher court to a lower court/tribunal** to **quash** an order already passed.
- Grounds: **excess of jurisdiction or lack of jurisdiction**.
- Issued **AFTER** the final order is passed.
- Initially: only against judicial and quasi-judicial authorities.
- **Later SC ruling**: Certiorari can be issued even against **administrative authorities** affecting rights of individuals.

> 💡 Both Prohibition and Certiorari are **jurisdictional writs** — but issued at different stages: Prohibition = before final order; Certiorari = after final order.

---

### Writ 5: Quo-Warranto
- Latin: **"By what authority or warrant"**
- Issued to enquire into the **legality of a person's claim to a public office**.
- Prevents **illegal usurpation of public office**.
- Can be issued only for **substantive public office** of a permanent character created by a **statute or the Constitution**.
- Cannot be issued for **ministerial office or private office**.
- Can be sought by **any public-minded person** — not necessarily the aggrieved person.

---

## 13. Armed Forces and Fundamental Rights (Article 33)

- **Parliament** (not state legislatures) can **restrict or abrogate** Fundamental Rights of members of:
  - Armed forces
  - Para-military forces
  - Police forces
  - Intelligence agencies
  - Analogous forces
- Objective: Ensure **proper discharge of duties** and **maintenance of discipline**.
- Such laws **cannot be challenged** in any court on the ground of violating Fundamental Rights.

**Laws enacted under Article 33:**
- Army Act (1950), Navy Act (1950), Air Force Act (1950)
- **Police Forces (Restriction of Rights) Act, 1966**
- Border Security Force Act, etc.

**Restrictions imposed on these forces:**
- Freedom of speech
- Right to form associations
- Membership of trade unions or political associations
- Right to communicate with the press
- Right to attend public meetings or demonstrations

**Who counts as 'members of armed forces'?** Even non-combatant employees like barbers, carpenters, mechanics, cooks, chowkidars, bootmakers, tailors.

**Note**: A parliamentary law under Article 33 can also **exclude court martial** from the writ jurisdiction of the SC and HCs regarding Fundamental Rights.

---

## 14. Martial Law and Fundamental Rights (Article 34)

- Article 34 provides for **restrictions on Fundamental Rights** while **martial law** is in force.
- Parliament can **indemnify** any government servant or person for acts done in connection with maintenance/restoration of order in an area under martial law.
- Parliament can also **validate** sentences, punishments, forfeitures, or other acts done under martial law.
- Such Acts of Indemnity **cannot be challenged** in courts on Fundamental Rights grounds.

**What is Martial Law?**
- Borrowed from **English common law**.
- The term is **not defined** in the Constitution.
- Literally means **'military rule'** — civil administration is run by **military authorities** under their own rules (outside ordinary law).
- Implies **suspension of ordinary law** and **government by military tribunals**.
- **Different from military law** (which applies to armed forces).
- **No express provision** in the Constitution authorises the executive to declare martial law — it is **implicit** in Article 34.
- Imposed under extraordinary circumstances: **war, invasion, insurrection, rebellion, riot, or violent resistance to law**.

**Martial Law vs. National Emergency:**

| # | Martial Law | National Emergency |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Affects only **Fundamental Rights** | Affects FRs, Centre-State relations, revenue distribution, legislative powers, and may extend Parliament's tenure |
| 2 | **Suspends** the government and ordinary law courts | **Continues** the government and ordinary law courts |
| 3 | Imposed to restore **breakdown of law and order** (any reason) | Can be imposed only on 3 grounds: war, external aggression, or armed rebellion |
| 4 | Imposed in a **specific area** | Imposed in the **whole country or any part** of it |
| 5 | **No specific provision** in the Constitution (implicit) | **Specific and detailed** provision in the Constitution (explicit) |

> 💡 **SC Ruling**: Declaration of martial law does **not ipso facto** (automatically) result in the suspension of the writ of habeas corpus.

## 15. Legislation to Give Effect to Fundamental Rights (Article 35)

Article 35 ensures that certain matters related to Fundamental Rights are **legislated only by Parliament** (not by state legislatures) — to maintain **uniformity throughout India**.

**Parliament alone (not state legislatures) can legislate on:**
1. Prescribing **residence** as a condition for certain employments (Article 16)
2. Empowering courts other than SC/HCs to issue **directions, orders, and writs** (Article 32)
3. **Restricting/abrogating** Fundamental Rights of armed forces, police forces, etc. (Article 33)
4. **Indemnifying** government servants for acts done during martial law (Article 34)

**Parliament alone can prescribe punishment for:**
1. **Untouchability** (Article 17)
2. **Traffic in human beings and forced labour** (Article 23)

Parliament is **obligated** (made mandatory) to enact such laws after the commencement of the Constitution.

Any **existing law** on these matters continues in force until **Parliament alters, repeals, or amends** it.

> ⚠️ **Key Note**: Article 35 extends Parliament's competence to legislate even on matters that may normally fall within the **State List** — in order to ensure national uniformity on Fundamental Rights.

## 16. Present Position of the Right to Property

### Historical Background
Originally, the Right to Property was a Fundamental Right under **Part III**:
- **Article 19(1)(f)**: Every citizen had the right to acquire, hold, and dispose of property.
- **Article 31**: Every person (citizen or non-citizen) had the right against deprivation of property. State could acquire property only for **public purpose** and with **payment of compensation**.

### Why Was It Controversial?
The Fundamental Right to Property was the **most controversial** of all rights. It caused:
- **Confrontations between the SC and Parliament**
- Multiple Constitutional Amendments: **1st, 4th, 7th, 25th, 39th, 40th, and 42nd Amendments**
- Additions of **Articles 31A, 31B, and 31C** to protect certain laws from judicial challenge

### The Change — 44th Amendment Act, 1978
- The **44th Amendment Act, 1978** abolished the Right to Property as a **Fundamental Right** by repealing **Article 19(1)(f)** and **Article 31** from Part III.
- A new **Article 300A** was inserted in **Part XII** (under the heading 'Right to Property').
- It now reads: *"No person shall be deprived of his property except by authority of law."*

### Right to Property Now: Legal/Constitutional Right (NOT Fundamental Right)
The right to property is now a **legal right/constitutional right** but NOT a Fundamental Right. It is also **NOT** a part of the basic structure.

**Implications:**
1. Can be curtailed/abridged/modified by an **ordinary law** of Parliament (no constitutional amendment needed).
2. Protects private property against **executive action** but **NOT against legislative action**.
3. In case of violation, the aggrieved person **cannot directly approach SC under Article 32**. They can approach the **HC under Article 226**.
4. **No guaranteed right to compensation** for acquisition/requisition of private property by the State.

### Two Cases Where Compensation IS Still Guaranteed (Even After Abolition):
1. When the State acquires property of a **minority educational institution** (Article 30) — *added by the 44th Amendment Act, 1978.*
2. When the State acquires land held by a person under **personal cultivation** within **statutory ceiling limits** (Article 31A) — *added by the 17th Amendment Act, 1964.*

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The **Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013** (Land Acquisition Act) is the key legislation governing property acquisition. It requires **social impact assessment, consent of landowners, and compensation at 2-4x the market rate** — directly addressing the concern that Article 300A provides no guaranteed compensation. Controversies over this Act's dilution via **Ordinances in 2014-15** highlighted the ongoing tension between the State's power of acquisition and individual property rights — a tension rooted in the 44th Amendment.

## 17. Exceptions to Fundamental Rights (Articles 31A, 31B, 31C)

### 17.1 Article 31A: Saving Laws for Acquisition of Estates, etc.

Article 31A saves **five categories of laws** from being challenged on the grounds of violating **Articles 14** (equality) and **19** (six freedoms). These five categories are:

1. **Acquisition of estates** and related rights by the State
2. **Taking over the management** of properties by the State
3. **Amalgamation** (merging) of corporations
4. **Extinguishment or modification of rights** of directors or shareholders of corporations
5. **Extinguishment or modification of mining leases**

> 💡 A **state law** under Article 31A is immune from challenge only if it has been **reserved for Presidential consideration** and has **received Presidential assent**.

Article 31A also mandates **compensation at market value** when the State acquires land held under **personal cultivation within statutory ceiling limits**.

---

### 17.2 Article 31B: Validation of Certain Acts and Regulations (The Ninth Schedule)

- Saves acts and regulations **included in the Ninth Schedule** from being challenged for violating **any** Fundamental Right.
- **Wider scope than Article 31A** — provides immunity regardless of which category the law falls under.

**Key SC Rulings:**
- **Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)**: SC ruled that Ninth Schedule laws **can be challenged** if they violate the **basic structure** of the Constitution.
- **Waman Rao case (1980)**: SC held that acts included in the Ninth Schedule **after 24 April 1973** (date of Kesavananda Bharati judgment) are valid only if they do **not damage the basic structure**.
- **I.R. Coelho case (2007)**: SC reaffirmed — **no blanket immunity** from judicial review for Ninth Schedule laws. Judicial review is a **basic feature** of the Constitution. Laws placed in the Ninth Schedule **after 24 April 1973** can be challenged if they violate rights under **Articles 14, 15, 19, 21** or the **basic structure**.

**Size of the Ninth Schedule:**
- Originally (1951): **13 acts and regulations**.
- Currently: **282 acts and regulations** (last entry is numbered 284, but three entries — 87, 92, 130 — have been deleted, and one is numbered 257A).
- State legislature acts: deal with **land reforms and abolition of zamindari**.
- Parliamentary acts: deal with **other matters**.

---

### 17.3 Article 31C: Saving Laws Giving Effect to Certain Directive Principles

Inserted by the **25th Amendment Act of 1971**. Contains two provisions:

1. No law implementing socialist Directive Principles under **Article 39(b)** or **39(c)** shall be void for violating **Articles 14 or 19**.
   - Article 39(b): Distribution of ownership and control of material resources for common good.
   - Article 39(c): Prevention of concentration of wealth and production to common detriment.

2. No law containing a declaration that it gives effect to such policy shall be questioned in any court.

**SC Rulings:**
- **Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)**: Provision (2) — the **judicial exclusion clause** — was struck down as unconstitutional (judicial review is a basic feature). Provision (1) was upheld.
- **42nd Amendment Act (1976)**: Extended the protection to laws implementing **any** Directive Principle (not just Article 39(b) or (c)).
- **Minerva Mills case (1980)**: The SC struck down the 42nd Amendment's extension as unconstitutional. Only laws implementing **Article 39(b) and (c)** retain protection under Article 31C.

> 💡 Like Article 31A, a state law under Article 31C is immune only if **reserved for Presidential consideration** and **given Presidential assent**.

## 18. Criticism of Fundamental Rights

Despite their importance, Fundamental Rights have faced significant criticism:

1. **Excessive Limitations**: Subjected to innumerable exceptions, restrictions, qualifications, and explanations. **Jaspat Roy Kapoor** suggested the chapter should be renamed *"Limitations on Fundamental Rights"*.

2. **No Social and Economic Rights**: The list mainly consists of political rights. It does not include important rights like:
   - Right to social security
   - Right to work / right to employment
   - Right to rest and leisure
   (Rights provided in advanced democracies and in the erstwhile USSR/China under socialist constitutions.)

3. **No Clarity**: Vague, indefinite, and ambiguous language. Terms like 'public order', 'minorities', 'reasonable restriction', and 'public interest' are **not clearly defined**. **Sir Ivor Jennings** called the Indian Constitution a **'paradise for lawyers'**.

4. **No Permanency**: Not sacrosanct or immutable — Parliament can curtail or abolish them (e.g., abolition of Right to Property in 1978). Only protection is the judicially innovated **'doctrine of basic structure'**.

5. **Suspension During Emergency**: Suspension of most Fundamental Rights during National Emergency (except Articles 20 and 21) *"cuts at the roots of the democratic system"* by placing millions of innocent people's rights in jeopardy. Critics say rights should be enjoyable in **all situations — Emergency or no Emergency**.

6. **Expensive Remedy**: Judicial enforcement is **too expensive** for ordinary citizens. Rights therefore benefit mainly the **rich sections of society**.

7. **Preventive Detention**: Article 22 takes away the spirit of the Fundamental Rights chapter. It **confers arbitrary powers on the State** and negates individual liberty. India is unique — **no other democratic country** has made preventive detention an integral part of its Constitution.

8. **No Consistent Philosophy**: **Sir Ivor Jennings** noted that Fundamental Rights are based on no consistent philosophical principle — making them difficult for courts to interpret uniformly.

---

## 19. Significance of Fundamental Rights

Despite criticism, Fundamental Rights are significant because they:

1. Form the **bedrock of the democratic system** in India.
2. Provide necessary conditions for the **material and moral protection** of individuals.
3. Serve as a formidable **bulwark of individual liberty** against State power.
4. Facilitate the establishment of the **Rule of Law** in the country.
5. **Protect the interests of minorities** and weaker sections of society.
6. **Strengthen the secular fabric** of the Indian State.
7. **Check the absoluteness** of the authority of the government.
8. Lay down the foundation stone of **social equality and social justice**.
9. **Ensure the dignity and respect** of individuals.
10. Facilitate the **participation of people** in the political and administrative process.

## 20. Rights Outside Part III (Non-Fundamental Constitutional Rights)

Besides Fundamental Rights, certain other rights exist in **other parts of the Constitution** — called **constitutional rights, legal rights, or non-fundamental rights**:

1. **No tax shall be levied** except by authority of law (Article 265 in Part XII).
2. **No person shall be deprived of property** except by authority of law (Article 300-A in Part XII).
3. **Trade, commerce, and intercourse** throughout India shall be free (Article 301 in Part XIII).
4. **Elections to Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assembly** shall be on the basis of adult suffrage (Article 326 in Part XV).

**Key Difference from Fundamental Rights:**
- These rights are equally **justiciable** (enforceable in court).
- But if violated, the aggrieved person **CANNOT** directly approach the **SC under Article 32** (which is itself a Fundamental Right).
- They can only approach the **High Court** through an ordinary suit or **under Article 226** (writ jurisdiction of HC).
