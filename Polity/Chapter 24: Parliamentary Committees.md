# Chapter 24: Parliamentary Committees

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. Parliament is too large and busy to examine everything in detail — so it uses **Parliamentary Committees** as its eyes and ears.
> 2. There are **two broad types**: **Standing Committees** (permanent) and **Ad Hoc Committees** (temporary).
> 3. The **three Financial Committees** — PAC, Estimates Committee, and Committee on Public Undertakings — are the most important for controlling public money.
> 4. **24 Department-Related Standing Committees (DRSCs)** were set up to make the Executive (government) more accountable to Parliament.
> 5. **Consultative Committees** are NOT Parliamentary Committees — they don't meet the four defining criteria.

---

## 🧭 Introduction: Why Does Parliament Need Committees?

Parliament is a massive body with hundreds of members. It deals with:
- Complex legislation (law-making)
- Scrutiny of government spending
- Oversight of government policies
- Day-to-day functioning of the House

It simply does **not have the time or expertise** to examine every issue in full detail on the floor of the House. This is where **Parliamentary Committees** come in — they are smaller, specialized groups of MPs that do the detailed work.

> 💡 **Think of it like this:** Parliament is the Board of Directors of India Inc. The committees are the specialized sub-committees (Finance Sub-committee, Audit Sub-committee, HR Sub-committee) that do the deep-dive work and report back.

The **Constitution of India** mentions these committees at various places but does **not specify** their composition, tenure, or functions. All these details are governed by the **rules of the two Houses** (Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha).

---

## PART 1: WHAT MAKES A COMMITTEE A "PARLIAMENTARY COMMITTEE"?

A committee qualifies as a **Parliamentary Committee** only if it meets **all four** of the following conditions:

1. ✅ It is **appointed or elected by the House** OR **nominated by the Speaker/Chairman**
2. ✅ It **works under the direction** of the Speaker/Chairman
3. ✅ It **presents its report** to the House or to the Speaker/Chairman
4. ✅ It has a **secretariat provided** by the Lok Sabha/Rajya Sabha

> ⚠️ **Important Note:** **Consultative Committees** — even though they consist of Members of Parliament — are **NOT Parliamentary Committees** because they do not fulfil the above four conditions.

---

## PART 2: CLASSIFICATION OF PARLIAMENTARY COMMITTEES

Parliamentary committees are broadly of **two kinds**:

| Type | Nature | Duration |
|------|--------|----------|
| **Standing Committees** | Permanent | Constituted every year or periodically; work on a continuous basis |
| **Ad Hoc Committees** | Temporary | Cease to exist once their assigned task is completed |

---

### 🅰️ STANDING COMMITTEES

Standing committees are classified into **six categories** based on the nature of their functions:

#### Category 1: Financial Committees
- (a) **Public Accounts Committee (PAC)**
- (b) **Estimates Committee**
- (c) **Committee on Public Undertakings**

#### Category 2: Department-Related Standing Committees
- There are **24** such committees

#### Category 3: Committees to Inquire
- (a) **Committee on Petitions**
- (b) **Committee of Privileges**
- (c) **Ethics Committee**

#### Category 4: Committees to Scrutinise and Control
- (a) **Committee on Government Assurances**
- (b) **Committee on Subordinate Legislation**
- (c) **Committee on Papers Laid on the Table**
- (d) **Committee on Welfare of SCs and STs**
- (e) **Committee on Empowerment of Women**
- (f) **Joint Committee on Offices of Profit**

#### Category 5: Committees Relating to the Day-to-Day Business of the House
- (a) **Business Advisory Committee**
- (b) **Committee on Private Members' Bills and Resolutions**
- (c) **Rules Committee**
- (d) **Committee on Absence of Members from the Sittings of the House**

#### Category 6: House-Keeping Committees (Service Committees)
*(Committees concerned with the provision of facilities and services to members)*
- (a) **General Purposes Committee**
- (b) **House Committee**
- (c) **Library Committee**
- (d) **Joint Committee on Salaries and Allowances of Members of Parliament**

---

### 🅱️ AD HOC COMMITTEES

Ad hoc committees are divided into **two sub-categories**:

#### 1. Inquiry Committees
- Constituted from time to time, either by the two Houses on a motion adopted, or by the Speaker/Chairman.
- They inquire into and report on **specific subjects**.

**Examples of Inquiry Committees:**
- (a) Railway Convention Committee
- (b) Committee on Members of Parliament Local Area Development Scheme (MPLADS)
- (c) Joint Committee on Security in Parliament Complex
- (d) Committee on Provision of Computers to Members of Parliament, Offices of Political Parties and Officers of the Lok Sabha Secretariat
- (e) Committee on Food Management in Parliament House Complex
- (f) Committee on Installation of Portraits/Statues of National Leaders and Parliamentarians in Parliament House Complex
- (g) Joint Committee on Maintenance of Heritage Character and Development of Parliament House Complex
- (h) Committee on Violation of Protocol Norms and Contemptuous Behaviour of Government Officers with Members of Lok Sabha
- (i) Committee on Welfare of Other Backward Classes
- (j) Committee to Inquire into the Improper Conduct of a Member

> 📌 *Note:* The **Railway Convention Committee, 1949** was the **first committee** set up after independence. Initially it only determined the rate of dividend payable by Railways to General Revenues. Since **1971**, these committees have also taken up matters related to the working of Railways and Railway Finances.

#### 2. Advisory Committees
- These are **Select or Joint Committees on Bills.**
- They are appointed to **consider and report on particular bills.**
- The procedure for these committees is laid down in the **Rules of Procedure** and **Directions by the Speaker/Chairman.**

**How they work:**
- When a Bill comes before a House for general discussion, the House can refer it to:
  - A **Select Committee** of that House (members from one House only), OR
  - A **Joint Committee** of both Houses (members from both Houses)

> 💡 **Note:** A **joint committee** consists of members of **both** the Houses of Parliament.

- A motion to refer the bill must be **moved and adopted** in the House where the bill is being considered.
- If the motion is for a Joint Committee, the decision is conveyed to the **other House**, requesting it to nominate its members to the committee.
- The Select or Joint Committee examines the Bill **clause by clause**, just as the Houses do.
- **Amendments** can be moved by committee members.
- The committee can also **take evidence** from associations, public bodies, or experts interested in the Bill.
- After examination, the committee **submits its report** to the House.
- Members who **disagree** with the majority report can append their **minutes of dissent** to the report.

## PART 3: THE THREE FINANCIAL COMMITTEES (Most Important for UPSC)

### 💰 A. Public Accounts Committee (PAC)

#### Origin and Composition:
- **First set up in 1921** under the provisions of the **Government of India Act of 1919.**
- Currently consists of **22 members** — **15 from Lok Sabha** and **7 from Rajya Sabha.**
- Members are **elected by Parliament every year** according to the principle of **proportional representation** (all parties get due representation) by means of the **single transferable vote.**
- **Term of office: One year.**
- A **minister cannot** be elected as a member.

#### Chairman:
- Appointed by the **Speaker** from amongst its members.
- Until **1966-67**, the chairman belonged to the **ruling party.**
- Since **1967**, a convention has developed: the chairman is **selected from the Opposition** — a significant democratic practice.

#### Functions of PAC:
The PAC examines the **annual audit reports of the Comptroller and Auditor General of India (CAG)**, which are laid before Parliament by the President.

The CAG submits **three audit reports** to the President:
1. Audit report on **appropriation accounts**
2. Audit report on **finance accounts**
3. Audit report on **public undertakings**

The PAC examines public expenditure not just from a legal and formal point of view (to find technical irregularities) but also to uncover:
- **Waste, loss, corruption**
- **Extravagance, inefficiency**
- **Nugatory expenses** (pointless/useless expenditure)

**Detailed Functions:**

1. To examine the **appropriation accounts** and the **finance accounts** of the Union government and any other accounts laid before the Lok Sabha.
   - **Appropriation Accounts** — compare actual expenditure with expenditure sanctioned by Parliament through the Appropriation Act
   - **Finance Accounts** — show the annual receipts and disbursements of the Union Government

2. In scrutinising the appropriation accounts and the CAG's audit report, the committee must satisfy itself that:
   - The money disbursed was **legally available** for the applied service or purpose
   - The expenditure **conforms to the authority** that governs it
   - Every **re-appropriation** (diversion of funds from one head to another) was made in accordance with related rules

3. To examine accounts of **state corporations, trading concerns and manufacturing projects** and the CAG's audit report on them (except those allotted to the Committee on Public Undertakings)

4. To examine accounts of **autonomous and semi-autonomous bodies** whose audit is conducted by the CAG

5. To consider the **CAG's report** relating to the audit of any receipt or to examine the **accounts of stores and stocks**

6. To examine **money spent in excess** of the amount granted by Lok Sabha for a service during a financial year

> 💡 **Role of CAG:** The CAG acts as the **"guide, friend and philosopher"** of the PAC — a phrase from Ashok Chanda (himself a former CAG), who observed that the PAC has developed into *"a powerful force in the control of public expenditure"* conforming to *"the highest traditions of a parliamentary democracy."*

#### Limitations of PAC:
- **(a)** It is **not concerned with questions of policy** in the broader sense.
- **(b)** It conducts a **post-mortem examination** — it looks at expenditure that has already been incurred.
- **(c)** It **cannot intervene** in matters of day-to-day administration.
- **(d)** Its recommendations are **advisory and not binding** on the ministries.
- **(e)** It is **not vested with the power of disallowance** (it cannot reject/cancel expenditures by departments).
- **(f)** It is **not an executive body** and hence cannot issue an order. Only Parliament can take a final decision on its findings.

---

### 💰 B. Estimates Committee

#### Origin and Composition:
- Origin can be traced to the **Standing Financial Committee set up in 1921.**
- The **first Estimates Committee in post-independence India** was constituted in **1950** on the recommendation of **John Mathai**, the then Finance Minister.
- Originally had **25 members**; in **1956**, membership was raised to **30.**
- **All 30 members are from Lok Sabha only** — the Rajya Sabha has **no representation** in this committee.
- Members are elected by Lok Sabha **every year** from amongst its own members, according to the **principle of proportional representation** by means of a **single transferable vote.**
- **Term of office: One year.**
- A **minister cannot** be elected as a member.
- The chairman is appointed by the **Speaker** and is **generally from the ruling party.**

#### Functions of the Estimates Committee:
This committee examines the **estimates (budget projections)** and suggests **'economies'** in public expenditure. Hence, it is described as a **"continuous economy committee."**

**Detailed Functions:**

1. To report what **economies, improvements in organisation, efficiency and administrative reform** — consistent with the policy underlying the estimates — can be effected

2. To suggest **alternative policies** in order to bring about efficiency and economy in administration

3. To examine whether the **money is well laid out** within the limits of the policy implied in the estimates

4. To suggest the **form in which the estimates** are to be presented to Parliament

**Additional Rules:**
- The committee shall **not exercise its functions** in relation to public undertakings allotted to the Committee on Public Undertakings.
- It may examine estimates **throughout the financial year** and report as its examination proceeds.
- It is **not obligatory** for the committee to examine the entire estimates of any one year.
- The **demands for grants may be finally voted** even if the committee has made no report.

#### Limitations of the Estimates Committee:
- **(a)** It examines budget estimates **only after** they have been voted by Parliament, not before.
- **(b)** It **cannot question the policy** laid down by Parliament.
- **(c)** Its recommendations are **advisory and not binding** on the ministries.
- **(d)** It examines **only certain selected ministries and departments** every year — it covers all of them by rotation over a number of years.
- **(e)** It **lacks the expert assistance of the CAG**, which is available to the PAC.
- **(f)** Its work is in the **nature of a post-mortem.**

---

### 💰 C. Committee on Public Undertakings

#### Origin and Composition:
- Created in **1964** on the recommendation of the **Krishna Menon Committee.**
- Originally had **15 members** (10 from Lok Sabha, 5 from Rajya Sabha).
- In **1974**, membership was raised to **22** (15 from Lok Sabha and 7 from Rajya Sabha).
- Members elected by Parliament **every year** according to the principle of **proportional representation** by means of the **single transferable vote.**
- **Term of office: One year.**
- A **minister cannot** be elected as a member.
- Chairman is appointed by the **Speaker** from amongst its members.

#### Functions:
1. To examine the **reports and accounts** of public undertakings
2. To examine the **CAG's reports** on public undertakings
3. To examine (in the context of **autonomy and efficiency** of public undertakings) whether the affairs are managed in accordance with **sound business principles and prudent commercial practices**
4. To exercise such other functions of the PAC and Estimates Committee relating to public undertakings as the **Speaker allots to it** from time to time

#### What the Committee Cannot Do:
- **(i)** Examine matters of **major government policy** as distinct from business/commercial functions of public undertakings
- **(ii)** Examine matters of **day-to-day administration**
- **(iii)** Examine matters for which a **special statutory machinery** has been established under the act creating that particular public undertaking

#### Limitations:
- **(a)** It can take up examination of **no more than ten to twelve public undertakings** in a year.
- **(b)** Its work is in the **nature of a post-mortem.**
- **(c)** It does **not look into technical matters** as its members are not technical experts.
- **(d)** Its recommendations are **advisory and not binding** on the ministries.

## PART 4: DEPARTMENT-RELATED STANDING COMMITTEES (DRSCs)

### Background and Evolution:

- In **1989**, three DRSCs were constituted dealing with Agriculture, Science & Technology, and Environment & Forests.
- On the recommendation of the **Rules Committee of the Lok Sabha**, **17 DRSCs** were set up in **1993** — the earlier three were superseded.
- In **2004**, **seven more** DRSCs were added, increasing the total from 17 to **24.**

> 💡 **Why were they created?** While inaugurating the DRSC system on **31st March 1993**, the then Vice-President and Chairman of Rajya Sabha, **K.R. Narayanan**, stated: *"The main purpose of these Committees is to ensure the accountability of Government to Parliament through more detailed consideration of measures in these Committees. The intention is not to weaken or criticise the administration but to strengthen it by investing it with more meaningful Parliamentary support."*

### Main Objectives:
- To secure **more accountability of the Executive** (i.e., the Council of Ministers) to Parliament, particularly **financial accountability.**
- To assist Parliament in **debating the budget more effectively.**

### Composition:
- The 24 DRSCs **cover all ministries/departments** of the Central Government.
- Each DRSC consists of **31 members** — **21 from Lok Sabha** and **10 from Rajya Sabha.**
- Members of Lok Sabha are **nominated by the Speaker**; members of Rajya Sabha are **nominated by the Chairman.**
- A **minister is not eligible** to be nominated as a member.
- If a sitting member is **appointed as a minister after nomination**, they **cease to be a member** of the DRSC.
- **Term of office: One year** from the date of constitution.

> 📌 *Note:* Until the **13th Lok Sabha**, each DRSC had up to **45 members** (30 from LS, 15 from RS). After restructuring in **July 2004**, each DRSC was reduced to **31 members** (21 from LS, 10 from RS).

### Distribution:
- **8 DRSCs** work under the **Rajya Sabha**
- **16 DRSCs** work under the **Lok Sabha**

### Functions of Each DRSC:

1. To **consider the demands for grants** of the concerned ministries/departments *before* they are discussed and voted in the Lok Sabha.
   - ⚠️ The report should **not suggest anything in the nature of cut motions** (motions to reduce government grants).

2. To **examine bills** pertaining to the concerned ministries/departments

3. To **consider annual reports** of ministries/departments

4. To **consider national basic long-term policy documents** presented to the Houses

### Limitations on DRSCs:
- **(i)** They **should not consider matters of day-to-day administration** of the concerned ministries/departments.
- **(ii)** They **should not generally consider matters** which are already being considered by other parliamentary committees.
- ⚠️ Recommendations of DRSCs are **advisory in nature and not binding** on Parliament.

### Procedure for Considering Demands for Grants:
- **(a)** After **general discussion on the budget** in the Houses is over, the Houses are **adjourned for a fixed period.**
- **(b)** The DRSCs **consider the demands for grants** of concerned ministries during this period.
- **(c)** Committees **must make their report within the period** — they cannot ask for more time.
- **(d)** The demands for grants shall be considered by the House **in the light of the reports** of the committees.
- **(e)** There shall be a **separate report** on the demands for grants of each ministry.

### Procedure for Examining Bills:
- **(a)** The committee shall consider the **general principles and clauses** of bills referred to it.
- **(b)** The committee shall consider **only bills introduced in either House and referred to it.**
- **(c)** The committee shall **make its report on bills within the given time.**

### Merits of the DRSC System:
1. Their proceedings are **devoid of any party bias.**
2. The procedure adopted by them is **more flexible** than in the Lok Sabha.
3. The system makes parliamentary control over the executive **more detailed, close, continuous, in-depth and comprehensive.**
4. The system ensures **economy and efficiency** in public expenditure as ministries/departments are more careful in formulating their demands.
5. They **facilitate opportunities for all MPs** to participate in and understand the functioning of the government.
6. They can **avail of expert opinion or public opinion** — they are authorised to invite experts and eminent persons to testify and incorporate their opinions.
7. **Opposition parties and the Rajya Sabha** can play a greater role in exercising financial control over the executive.

### Overall Impact of DRSCs:
- The recommendations of DRSCs have been of **immense importance.**
- Ministries/departments have been **quite receptive** to their recommendations and have largely accepted and implemented them.
- DRSCs have made **parliamentary debates better informed** by nourishing the information supply system.
- Their activities have created a **"corpus of knowledge"** (a large body of information) for MPs and the public.
- Effectiveness can be judged on **four yardsticks:**
  - (i) Their success in **holding officials to account**
  - (ii) **Publication of information**
  - (iii) Their **effect on the House and the wider public**
  - (iv) Their **influence on government's decisions**

## PART 5: COMMITTEES TO INQUIRE

### 🔍 Committee on Petitions

- Examines **petitions on bills** and on matters of **general public importance.**
- Also entertains **representations from individuals and associations** on matters pertaining to Union subjects.
- **Lok Sabha committee**: 15 members
- **Rajya Sabha committee**: 10 members

---

### 🔍 Committee of Privileges

- Functions are **semi-judicial** (partly judicial, partly parliamentary) in nature.
- Examines **cases of breach of privileges** of the House and its members.
- **Recommends appropriate action.**
- **Lok Sabha committee**: 15 members
- **Rajya Sabha committee**: 10 members

---

### 🔍 Ethics Committee

- **Constituted in Rajya Sabha in 1997** and in **Lok Sabha in 2000.**
- **Enforces the code of conduct** of Members of Parliament.
- Examines **cases of misconduct** and recommends appropriate action.
- Engaged in **maintaining discipline and decorum** in Parliament.
- **Lok Sabha committee**: 15 members
- **Rajya Sabha committee**: 10 members

---

## PART 6: COMMITTEES TO SCRUTINISE AND CONTROL

### 🔎 Committee on Government Assurances

- **Constituted in 1953.**
- Examines **assurances, promises and undertakings** given by ministers on the floor of the House.
- Reports on the **extent to which they have been carried through** (implemented).
- **Lok Sabha**: 15 members | **Rajya Sabha**: 10 members

---

### 🔎 Committee on Subordinate Legislation

- **Constituted in 1953.**
- Examines and reports whether the powers to make **regulations, rules, sub-rules and bye-laws** — delegated by Parliament or conferred by the Constitution to the Executive — are being **properly exercised.**
- **Both Houses**: 15 members each

---

### 🔎 Committee on Papers Laid on the Table

- **Constituted in 1975.**
- Examines all **papers laid on the table of the House by ministers** to see whether they comply with provisions of the Constitution, or the related Act or Rule.
- Does **not examine** statutory notifications and orders that fall under the jurisdiction of the **Committee on Subordinate Legislation.**
- **Lok Sabha**: 15 members | **Rajya Sabha**: 10 members

---

### 🔎 Committee on Welfare of SCs and STs

- Consists of **30 members** (20 from Lok Sabha and 10 from Rajya Sabha).

**Functions:**
- **(i)** To consider the reports of the **National Commission for the SCs** and the **National Commission for the STs.**
- **(ii)** To examine all matters relating to the **welfare of SCs and STs**, including implementation of constitutional and statutory safeguards, working of welfare programmes, etc.

---

### 🔎 Committee on Empowerment of Women

- **Constituted in 1997.**
- Consists of **30 members** (20 from Lok Sabha and 10 from Rajya Sabha).
- Considers the reports of the **National Commission for Women.**
- Examines the measures taken by the Union Government to **secure status, dignity and equality for women** in all fields.

---

### 🔎 Joint Committee on Offices of Profit

- Examines the **composition and character of committees and other bodies** appointed by the Central, state and union territory governments.
- **Recommends** whether persons holding these offices should be **disqualified from being elected** as Members of Parliament.
- Consists of **15 members** (10 from Lok Sabha and 5 from Rajya Sabha).

---

## PART 7: COMMITTEES RELATING TO DAY-TO-DAY BUSINESS

### 📅 Business Advisory Committee

- **Regulates the programme and timetable** of the House.
- **Allocates time** for the transaction of legislative and other business brought before the House by the government.
- **Lok Sabha**: 15 members including the **Speaker as its chairman.**
- **Rajya Sabha**: 11 members including the **Chairman as its ex-officio chairman.**

---

### 📅 Committee on Private Members' Bills and Resolutions

- **Classifies bills** and **allocates time** for discussion on bills and resolutions introduced by **private members** (members other than ministers).
- A **special committee of the Lok Sabha only.**
- Consists of **15 members** including the **Deputy Speaker as its chairman.**
- **Rajya Sabha does NOT have** any such committee — this function in Rajya Sabha is performed by the **Business Advisory Committee** of that House.

---

### 📅 Rules Committee

- Considers **matters of procedure and conduct of business** in the House.
- **Recommends necessary amendments or additions** to the rules of the House.
- **Lok Sabha**: 15 members including the **Speaker as its ex-officio chairman.**
- **Rajya Sabha**: 16 members including the **Chairman as its ex-officio chairman.**

---

### 📅 Committee on Absence of Members from the Sittings of the House

- Considers all **applications from members for leave of absence** from the sittings of the House.
- Examines cases of members who have been **absent for 60 days or more without permission.**
- A **special committee of the Lok Sabha only** — consists of **15 members.**
- **Rajya Sabha does NOT have** any such committee — all such matters are dealt by the **House itself.**

---

## PART 8: HOUSE-KEEPING COMMITTEES

### 🏠 General Purposes Committee

- Considers and advises on **matters concerning affairs of the House** that do not fall within the jurisdiction of any other parliamentary committee.

**Composition (in each House):**
- **Presiding Officer** (Speaker/Chairman) as **ex-officio chairman**
- **Deputy Speaker** (Deputy Chairman in case of Rajya Sabha)
- Members of **panel of chairpersons** (panel of vice-chairpersons in Rajya Sabha)
- **Chairpersons of all departmental standing committees** of the House
- **Leaders of recognised parties and groups** in the House
- Such **other members as nominated** by the presiding officer

---

### 🏠 House Committee

- Deals with **residential accommodation** of members.
- Also handles **other amenities** like food, medical aid, etc., in their houses and hostels in Delhi.
- **Both Houses have their respective House Committees.**
- **Lok Sabha**: 12 members | **Rajya Sabha**: 10 members

---

### 🏠 Library Committee

- Considers all **matters relating to the Parliament's library.**
- Assists members in **utilising the library's services.**
- Consists of **9 members** (6 from Lok Sabha and 3 from Rajya Sabha).

---

### 🏠 Joint Committee on Salaries and Allowances of Members of Parliament

- Constituted under the **Salary, Allowances and Pension of Members of Parliament Act, 1954.**
- Consists of **15 members** (10 from Lok Sabha and 5 from Rajya Sabha).
- **Frames rules** for regulating payment of salary, allowances, and pension to Members of Parliament.

---

## PART 9: CONSULTATIVE COMMITTEES

### What Are They?

Consultative Committees are attached to **various ministries/departments** of the Central Government. They consist of members of **both Houses of Parliament.**

### Key Features:

- The **Minister/Minister of State** in charge of the concerned ministry acts as the **chairman** of the Consultative Committee of that ministry.
- They provide a **forum for informal discussions** between ministers and MPs on policies, programmes, and their implementation.
- They are **constituted by the Ministry of Parliamentary Affairs**, which also formulates guidelines and arranges meetings.
- **Membership is voluntary** — left to the choice of members and their party leaders.
- **Maximum membership**: 30 | **Minimum membership**: 10
- These committees are constituted after a **new Lok Sabha is constituted** (after General Elections).
- They **stand dissolved upon dissolution of every Lok Sabha** and are **reconstituted upon constitution of each new Lok Sabha.**

> ⚠️ **Remember:** Consultative Committees are **NOT Parliamentary Committees** — they do not meet the four defining criteria mentioned at the beginning.

### Informal Consultative Committees for Railway Zones:
- Separate **Informal Consultative Committees** are also constituted for **all Railway Zones.**
- MPs belonging to the area falling under a particular Railway Zone are **nominated on the Informal Consultative Committee** of that Railway Zone.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *Added to connect the chapter's core ideas to present-day developments.*

---

### 1. 🏛️ DRSCs and the Farm Laws Controversy (2020-21)

`[Modern Example]`

The **three Farm Laws of 2020** — passed by Parliament — were criticized precisely because they were **not referred to any DRSC** for examination before being passed. Critics, including Opposition leaders and agricultural experts, argued that the laws were rushed through without proper committee scrutiny.

This highlights the **importance of the DRSC system**: when laws bypass committee examination, their quality and acceptability suffer. The laws were eventually **repealed in November 2021** following massive protests. This episode strengthened the argument for **mandatory committee referral of all major bills.**

---

### 2. 🏛️ PAC and Financial Accountability — CAG Reports (Recent)

`[Modern Example]`

The CAG has published several significant audit reports in recent years that have come before the **Public Accounts Committee:**

- **CAG Report on PM-KISAN Scheme (2021):** Found that payments were made to **ineligible beneficiaries**, including income taxpayers and government employees.
- **CAG Report on MGNREGS (2022):** Flagged irregularities in job card maintenance and wage payments.

These reports demonstrate the PAC's critical role: the CAG finds the problems, and the PAC holds the government accountable for them.

---

### 3. 🏛️ Ethics Committee — Cash-for-Query Case and Expulsion of MPs

`[Modern Example]`

In **2023**, the **Ethics Committee of Lok Sabha** recommended the **expulsion of Mahua Moitra, MP** in the **'cash-for-query' case** — where she was accused of asking questions in Parliament in exchange for monetary benefits.

This is a **live, real-world example** of the Ethics Committee in action — exactly as the chapter describes its role of *"examining cases of misconduct and recommending appropriate action."*

---

### 4. 🗳️ Joint Parliamentary Committee (JPC) — A Recurring Demand

`[Modern Example]`

The demand for a **Joint Parliamentary Committee (JPC)** — a type of Ad Hoc Advisory Committee — has been raised repeatedly in recent years:

- **JPC on the Adani-Hindenburg Issue (2023):** The Opposition demanded a JPC to investigate the allegations against the Adani Group and possible regulatory failures.
- **JPC on the Data Protection Bill:** Before the **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** was passed, there were demands for its referral to a JPC for wider scrutiny.

These cases show that **JPCs remain relevant** as a tool for Parliament to investigate matters of public importance.

---

### 5. 🏛️ Committee on Empowerment of Women — Relevance in Modern Context

`[Modern Example]`

The **Committee on Empowerment of Women** has taken up examination of critical issues in recent years:

- It examined the **status of women in the judiciary** and recommended more appointments of women judges.
- The committee has also examined issues related to **women's safety, the POCSO Act implementation**, and **maternity benefits.**

This connects directly to major government schemes like the **Beti Bachao Beti Padhao** scheme and the **One Stop Centre Scheme** for women in distress.

---

### 6. 📊 Declining Rate of Bill Referrals to Committees — A Concern

`[Modern Example]`

**PRS Legislative Research** data shows a worrying trend:

- In the **14th Lok Sabha (2004-2009)**: About **60% of bills** were referred to Parliamentary Committees.
- In the **15th Lok Sabha (2009-2014)**: About **71% of bills** were referred.
- In the **16th Lok Sabha (2014-2019)**: Only about **27% of bills** were referred.
- In the **17th Lok Sabha (2019-2024)**: The rate of referral dropped even further, with several important bills like the **Citizenship Amendment Bill** and **Farm Laws** passed without committee scrutiny.

This data point directly illustrates the **limitations of the committee system** in practice and is a frequently asked UPSC topic.
