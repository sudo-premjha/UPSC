## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. India follows a **parliamentary system modelled on Britain** — the **Council of Ministers headed by the PM** is the *real* executive authority, not the President.
> 2. **Articles 74 and 75** are the two core articles governing the Council of Ministers — Article 74 deals with *status*, Article 75 deals with *appointment, tenure, responsibility, oath, and salary*.
> 3. The **42nd and 44th Amendment Acts** made ministerial advice **binding on the President** — the President must act on the advice of the Council of Ministers.
> 4. There are **three types of ministers**: Cabinet Ministers, Ministers of State, and Deputy Ministers. The **Cabinet** is a smaller, more powerful subset of the Council of Ministers.
> 5. The **principle of collective responsibility** (Article 75) is the cornerstone of the parliamentary system — all ministers "swim or sink together."

## ⚙️ Constitutional Provisions — Explained Simply

---

### 📜 Article 74 — Council of Ministers to Aid and Advise the President

**Provision 1:**
- There shall be a **Council of Ministers with the PM at the head** to **aid and advise the President**.
- The President **shall act in accordance** with such advice.
- However, the President **may send the advice back** for reconsideration once.
- After reconsideration, the President **must act on** the revised advice — no further option to refuse.

**Provision 2:**
- The advice given by ministers to the President **cannot be questioned or examined by any court**.
- This protects the **confidential relationship** between the President and the ministers.

> 🔑 **Key Amendment History:**
> - **42nd Amendment Act (1976):** Made ministerial advice **binding** on the President for the first time.
> - **44th Amendment Act (1978):** Added the **reconsideration proviso** — the President can send advice back once, but must accept it after reconsideration.

---

### 📜 Article 75 — Other Provisions as to Ministers

| # | Provision | Key Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Appointment** | PM appointed by President; other ministers appointed by President **on PM's advice** |
| 2 | **Size Limit** | Total ministers (including PM) **shall not exceed 15% of total Lok Sabha strength** — added by **91st Amendment Act, 2003** |
| 3 | **Disqualification on Defection** | A member disqualified under the **anti-defection law** is also **disqualified from being a minister** — added by **91st Amendment Act, 2003** |
| 4 | **Tenure** | Ministers hold office **during the pleasure of the President** |
| 5 | **Collective Responsibility** | Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha** |
| 6 | **Oath** | President administers **oaths of office and secrecy** |
| 7 | **Membership Requirement** | A minister who is **not an MP for 6 consecutive months** ceases to be a minister |
| 8 | **Salary** | Salaries and allowances determined by **Parliament** |

> 💡 **The 91st Amendment (2003) is very important for Prelims!** It capped the size of the Council of Ministers at **15% of Lok Sabha strength** and also linked **anti-defection disqualification** to ministerial eligibility.

---

### 📜 Article 77 — Conduct of Business of the Government of India

- **All executive actions** of the Government of India shall be expressed to be taken **in the name of the President**.
- Orders and instruments made in the President's name shall be **authenticated** as per rules made by the President.
- The validity of an authenticated order **cannot be challenged** on the ground that it was not personally made by the President.
- The President shall make rules for the **convenient transaction of government business** and for **allocation of business among ministers**.

---

### 📜 Article 78 — Duties of the Prime Minister

The PM has three constitutional duties:

1. To **communicate to the President** all decisions of the Council of Ministers relating to the administration of the Union and proposals for legislation.
2. To **furnish information** relating to administration and legislation as the President may call for.
3. If the President requires, to **submit for consideration of the full Council** any matter on which a decision was taken by a single minister but not yet discussed by the entire Council.

---

### 📜 Article 88 — Rights of Ministers in Parliament

- Every minister has the **right to speak and take part** in the proceedings of:
  - Either House of Parliament
  - Any joint sitting of the Houses
  - Any Committee of Parliament of which they are named a member
- **BUT** they are **not entitled to vote** in a House of which they are not a member.

> 💡 **Simple Example:** If a minister is a Rajya Sabha member, they can speak in the Lok Sabha but **cannot vote** there.

---

## 🔍 Nature of Advice by Ministers — Deep Dive

- Article 74 makes the Council of Ministers the **real executive** and the President a **constitutional head**.
- The advice given by ministers to the President is **binding** (post 42nd and 44th Amendments).
- The **nature of the advice cannot be inquired into by any court** — this protects the confidentiality of Cabinet deliberations.

---

## 🏛️ Landmark Court Cases — Explained in Detail

---

### ⚖️ CASE 1: U.N.R. Rao vs. Indira Gandhi (1971)

**Background:**
After the 1970 mid-term elections were announced, the Lok Sabha was dissolved. The question arose: Can the President govern **without a Council of Ministers** after the dissolution of the Lok Sabha? Is a caretaker government constitutionally valid?

**What Happened:**
U.N.R. Rao challenged the continuation of Indira Gandhi's government as Prime Minister after the dissolution of the Lok Sabha, arguing that the Council of Ministers should also cease to exist once the Lok Sabha is dissolved.

**What the Supreme Court Ruled:**
- The Supreme Court ruled that **even after the dissolution of the Lok Sabha, the Council of Ministers does NOT cease to hold office**.
- **Article 74 is mandatory** — the President **cannot exercise executive power** without the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers.
- Any exercise of executive power **without ministerial advice is unconstitutional** as it violates Article 74.
- Therefore, a **caretaker government continues** in office until a new government is formed after elections.

**Significance for UPSC:**
This case firmly establishes that:
- The President is **never without a Council of Ministers**.
- Even a dissolved Lok Sabha does not create a "ministerless" presidency.
- The Council of Ministers continues as a **caretaker** until a new government is sworn in.

---

### ⚖️ CASE 2: Shamsher Singh vs. State of Punjab (1974)

**Background:**
This case dealt with two probationary civil servants who were dismissed by the Governor of Punjab without consulting the Council of Ministers. The question was: Does the Governor (or President) act on their **personal satisfaction** or on the **satisfaction of the Council of Ministers**?

**What Happened:**
Shamsher Singh, a civil servant, was dismissed. He challenged the dismissal, arguing that the Governor had acted on personal discretion rather than on the advice of the Council of Ministers.

**What the Supreme Court Ruled:**
- The Supreme Court held that **wherever the Constitution requires the satisfaction of the President or Governor**, it is **not the personal satisfaction** of the President/Governor.
- It is the **satisfaction of the Council of Ministers** with whose aid and on whose advice the President/Governor exercises powers and functions.
- The President and Governor are **constitutional heads** — they act as per the advice of the Council of Ministers in virtually all matters.
- The court confirmed that India has a **parliamentary form of government**, not a presidential one, and the real executive power is with the **Cabinet**.

**Significance for UPSC:**
This case is crucial because it:
- Confirms the **parliamentary character** of the Indian Constitution.
- Establishes that "Presidential/Governor's satisfaction" = "Council of Ministers' satisfaction."
- Limits the scope of **discretionary powers** of the President and Governor.
- Is often cited alongside the **U.N.R. Rao case** to explain the supremacy of the Council of Ministers in the executive structure.

---

### ⚖️ CASE 3: Devi Lal Oath Case (1990)

**Background:**
Devi Lal was sworn in as **Deputy Prime Minister** in 1990. However, the Constitution **does not mention** the post of Deputy Prime Minister — it only mentions the Prime Minister and Ministers. The oath was challenged as unconstitutional.

**What the Supreme Court Ruled:**
- The Supreme Court **upheld the oath as valid**.
- It stated that describing a person as "Deputy Prime Minister" is **merely descriptive** — it does not confer on them the powers of the Prime Minister.
- The description of a minister as "Deputy Prime Minister," "Minister of State," or "Deputy Minister" — none of which are mentioned in the Constitution — **does not invalidate the oath**, as long as the **substantive part of the oath is correct**.
- In other words, the **title is just a label**; what matters is that the person takes the proper oath of a minister.

**Significance for UPSC:**
- Clarifies that **Deputy PM is not a constitutional post** — it is a **political arrangement**.
- The Court confirmed that ministerial titles not mentioned in the Constitution do not make the appointment unconstitutional.
- This also applies to "Minister of State" and "Deputy Minister" — neither is mentioned in the Constitution, but both are valid.

## 👥 Appointment of Ministers

- The **Prime Minister is appointed by the President**.
- All **other ministers are appointed by the President on the advice of the Prime Minister**.
- The President can only appoint those persons whom the PM recommends — **no independent discretion**.

### Who Can Be a Minister?
- Usually, members of either **Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha** are appointed as ministers.
- A **non-MP can also be appointed** as a minister, BUT:
  - Within **6 months**, they must become a member of either House (by election or nomination).
  - If they fail to do so within 6 months, they **cease to be a minister**.

### Rights of a Minister in Parliament:
- A minister who is a member of **one House** has the right to:
  - **Speak** in the other House
  - **Take part in proceedings** of the other House
- But they can **vote only in the House** of which they are a member.

## 🤝 Oath and Salary of Ministers

### Oath of Office — The Minister Swears:
1. To bear **true faith and allegiance** to the Constitution of India.
2. To **uphold the sovereignty and integrity** of India.
3. To **faithfully and conscientiously discharge** the duties of office.
4. To **do right to all manner of people** in accordance with the Constitution and the law, without **fear or favour, affection or ill will**.

### Oath of Secrecy — The Minister Swears:
- Not to **directly or indirectly communicate or reveal** to any person any matter brought under consideration or known to them as a Union minister — **except as required for the due discharge of ministerial duties**.

### Salary and Allowances:
- Determined by **Parliament** from time to time (under the **Salaries and Allowances of Ministers Act, 1952**).
- A minister gets:
  - The **salary and allowances payable to an MP**
  - A **sumptuary allowance** *(an additional rank-based monetary allowance)* according to their rank
  - **Free accommodation**
  - **Travelling allowance**
  - **Medical facilities**

**Revised Sumptuary Allowances (2001)** under the Salaries and Allowances of Ministers (Amendment) Act, 2001:

| Rank | Old Allowance | New Allowance |
|---|---|---|
| Prime Minister | ₹1,500/month | ₹3,000/month |
| Cabinet Minister | ₹1,000/month | ₹2,000/month |
| Minister of State | ₹500/month | ₹1,000/month |
| Deputy Minister | ₹300/month | ₹600/month |

## ⚖️ Responsibility of Ministers

There are **three types of ministerial responsibility** — this is a crucial topic.

---

### 🔴 1. Collective Responsibility (Article 75)

This is the **fundamental principle** of the parliamentary system.

**What It Means:**
- All ministers own **joint responsibility** to the Lok Sabha for **all their acts of omission and commission**.
- They work as a team — **"swim or sink together."**
- **Cabinet decisions bind ALL ministers**, even if some disagreed during the Cabinet meeting.
- Every minister must **stand by and defend** Cabinet decisions — inside and outside Parliament.
- If a minister **disagrees and refuses to defend** a Cabinet decision, they **must resign**.

**No-Confidence Motion:**
- When the Lok Sabha passes a **no-confidence motion**, **ALL ministers must resign** — including those who are Rajya Sabha members.
- Note: **Each minister need not resign separately** — the resignation of the PM amounts to the resignation of the entire Council of Ministers.

**Alternatively:**
- The Council of Ministers can advise the President to **dissolve the Lok Sabha** and call for fresh elections.
- BUT the President **may not oblige** a Council of Ministers that has already **lost the confidence** of the Lok Sabha.

**Historical Examples of Ministerial Resignations Due to Collective Responsibility:**

| Minister | Year | Reason for Resignation |
|---|---|---|
| **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** | 1953 | Differences with colleagues on the **Hindu Code Bill** |
| **C.D. Deshmukh** | — | Differences on the **policy of reorganisation of states** |
| **Arif Mohammed** | — | Opposition to the **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986** |

---

### 🟡 2. Individual Responsibility (Article 75)

- Article 75 states that ministers hold office **during the pleasure of the President**.
- This means the President **can remove a minister** even when the Council of Ministers enjoys the Lok Sabha's confidence.
- However, in practice, the President removes a minister **only on the advice of the Prime Minister**.
- If the PM is dissatisfied with a minister, the PM can:
  - Ask the minister to **resign**, OR
  - Advise the President to **dismiss** them.

**Why This Matters:**
This power of the PM is what **enforces collective responsibility** in practice. As **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** observed:

> *"Collective responsibility can be achieved only through the instrumentality of the Prime Minister. Therefore, unless and until we create that office and endow that office with statutory authority to nominate and dismiss ministers, there can be no collective responsibility."* *(Constituent Assembly Debates, Volume VIII, p. 1160)*

---

### 🔵 3. No Legal Responsibility (Contrast with Britain)

**In Britain:**
- Every order of the **King** for any public act is **countersigned by a minister**.
- If the order violates any law, the **minister is held legally responsible** and is liable in court.
- The accepted phrase is: **"The King can do no wrong"** — so the King cannot be sued; the minister is accountable.

**In India:**
- There is **no provision** in the Constitution for **legal responsibility** of a minister.
- It is **not required** that an order of the President for a public act be countersigned by a minister.
- Furthermore, **courts are barred** from enquiring into the **nature of advice** rendered by ministers to the President (Article 74(2)).

> 💡 **In Summary:** In India, ministers have **collective and individual political responsibility**, but **no legal responsibility** in the British sense.

## 🏗️ Composition of the Council of Ministers

The Council of Ministers has **three tiers** (plus the PM and sometimes a Deputy PM):

---

### 🔺 Tier 1: Cabinet Ministers (Highest Rank)
- Head **important ministries** (Home, Defence, Finance, External Affairs, etc.).
- Are **members of the Cabinet** and attend Cabinet meetings.
- Play a **major role in policy decisions**.
- Their responsibilities extend over the **entire gamut** of Central government.

---

### 🔶 Tier 2: Ministers of State

**Two Types:**

**A. With Independent Charge:**
- Given **independent charge** of a ministry/department.
- Perform the **same functions and exercise the same powers** as Cabinet Ministers in relation to their ministry.
- Are **NOT members of the Cabinet** and do not attend Cabinet meetings.
- May be **specially invited** to Cabinet meetings when matters related to their ministry are discussed.

**B. Attached to a Cabinet Minister:**
- Either given charge of **departments** within a Cabinet Minister's ministry, OR
- Allotted **specific items of work** related to the Cabinet Minister's ministry.
- Work **under the supervision, guidance, and overall charge** of the Cabinet Minister.

---

### 🔷 Tier 3: Deputy Ministers (Lowest Rank)
- **NOT given independent charge** of any ministry or department.
- Attached to Cabinet Ministers or Ministers of State.
- Assist senior ministers in **administrative, political, and parliamentary duties**.
- Are **NOT members of the Cabinet** and do not attend Cabinet meetings.

---

### 🔹 Deputy Prime Minister
- Not a constitutional post (confirmed by the Devi Lal case, 1990).
- Appointed **mostly for political reasons** (coalition management, etc.).
- At times included in the Council of Ministers.

---

### 🔹 Parliamentary Secretaries (Special Category)
- **NOT members** of the Council of Ministers.
- Appointed by the **Prime Minister** (not the President).
- Have **no department** under their control.
- Attached to senior ministers and assist them in **parliamentary duties** only.

---

## 📊 Council of Ministers vs. Cabinet — Key Differences

| # | Council of Ministers | Cabinet |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Wider body** | **Smaller body** |
| 2 | Includes **all three tiers**: Cabinet Ministers, Ministers of State, Deputy Ministers | Includes **Cabinet Ministers only** — it is a *part* of the Council of Ministers |
| 3 | Does **NOT meet as a body** to transact business; has **no collective functions** | Meets **frequently (usually once a week)** to deliberate and decide government business |
| 4 | Vested with all powers — but **only in theory** | **Exercises powers in practice** on behalf of the Council of Ministers |
| 5 | Its functions are **determined by the Cabinet** | **Directs** the Council of Ministers by taking policy decisions binding on all ministers |
| 6 | **Implements** decisions taken by the Cabinet | **Supervises** the implementation of its decisions by the Council of Ministers |
| 7 | Is a **constitutional body** under Articles 74 & 75. Size capped at **15% of Lok Sabha** by 91st Amendment, 2003. Classification into three tiers is based on **British parliamentary conventions**, but has **legislative sanction** under the Salaries and Allowances of Ministers Act, 1952 (which defines 'minister' as 'a member of the council of ministers, by whatever name called, and includes a deputy minister') | Formally inserted in **Article 352** by the **44th Amendment Act, 1978** — not in the original Constitution. Article 352 only *defines* it as 'the council consisting of the PM and other ministers of Cabinet rank appointed under Article 75' without describing its powers. Its role is based on **British parliamentary conventions** |
| 8 | **Collectively responsible** to the Lok Sabha | **Enforces** the collective responsibility of the Council of Ministers to the Lok Sabha |

## 🌟 Role of the Cabinet — 9 Key Functions

1. **Highest decision-making authority** in India's politico-administrative system.
2. **Chief policy formulating body** of the Central government.
3. **Supreme executive authority** of the Central government.
4. **Chief coordinator** of Central administration.
5. **Advisory body to the President** — its advice is binding on the President.
6. **Chief crisis manager** — deals with all emergency situations.
7. Deals with all **major legislative and financial matters**.
8. Exercises control over **higher appointments** (constitutional authorities, senior secretariat administrators).
9. Deals with all **foreign policies and foreign affairs**.

## 🍳 Kitchen Cabinet (Inner Cabinet)

### What Is It?
- The formal Cabinet is already a small body.
- But an **even smaller, informal body** called the **'Inner Cabinet'** or **'Kitchen Cabinet'** has become the **real centre of power**.
- It is an **extra-constitutional body** (not mentioned anywhere in the Constitution or law).

### Who Is in It?
- The **Prime Minister**
- A **few trusted, influential colleagues**
- Even **outsiders** — friends and family members of the PM

### What Does It Do?
- Advises the PM on **important political and administrative issues**.
- Assists in **making crucial decisions**.
- Decisions are **"cooked"** here and placed before the formal Cabinet for **rubber-stamp approval**.

### Historical Context:
- **Every Prime Minister** in India has had their own Inner Cabinet.
- During the era of **Indira Gandhi**, the Kitchen Cabinet was **particularly powerful**.
- This phenomenon is **not unique to India** — it also exists in the **USA and Britain**.

### ✅ Merits of the Kitchen Cabinet:
1. Being a **small unit**, it is a **more efficient** decision-making body than the large Cabinet.
2. It can **meet more often** and deal with business **more expeditiously**.
3. It helps the PM in **maintaining secrecy** on important political issues.

### ❌ Demerits of the Kitchen Cabinet:
1. It **reduces the authority and status** of the Cabinet as the highest decision-making body.
2. It **circumvents the legal process** by allowing **outside persons** (who are not ministers or elected officials) to play an influential role in government functioning.

## 🌍 Modern Context

---

### 🔹 On the 91st Amendment (15% Cap on Council Size)

`[Modern Example]` After the **2024 General Elections**, when PM Narendra Modi formed his third government (NDA-3), the **Council of Ministers had 71 members** (including the PM). With the Lok Sabha having **543 seats**, the **15% cap = ~81 ministers maximum**. The 2024 Cabinet formation reflects the constitutional constraint of the 91st Amendment. Coalition politics, particularly managing NDA partners like TDP and JD(U), also shaped the Cabinet's composition — a practical illustration of **political reasons for Cabinet size decisions**.

---

### 🔹 On Collective Responsibility — Recent Example

`[Modern Example]` The principle of collective responsibility was tested during the formation of the **INDIA Alliance** discussions (2023-24), where parties with differing ideologies debated portfolio sharing. The concept of "swim or sink together" is also visible in the **Nitish Kumar and Chandrababu Naidu** role in the NDA coalition — their support is critical for the government's majority in the Lok Sabha, making collective responsibility a live political reality.

---

### 🔹 On the Kitchen Cabinet — Modern Relevance

`[Modern Example]` The **Prime Minister's Office (PMO)** under PM Modi has been widely discussed as a highly centralised decision-making body. Senior bureaucrats and trusted officials in the PMO have been described as functioning like an informal "Kitchen Cabinet." The **Economic Advisory Council to the PM (EAC-PM)**, reconstituted in 2017 with economists like Bibek Debroy, serves a similar advisory function — though it is a formal body, its influence mirrors the Kitchen Cabinet concept.

---

### 🔹 On the 44th Amendment and Binding Advice

`[Modern Example]` The binding nature of ministerial advice (post-44th Amendment) became relevant during the **West Bengal gubernatorial controversy (2021-2023)**, where Governor Jagdeep Dhankhar frequently acted without or against the advice of the state Cabinet under CM Mamata Banerjee. While this is a state-level parallel (Governor + State Cabinet), it illustrates the constitutional principle that constitutional heads must act on Cabinet advice — the same principle that applies to the President under Article 74. The **Supreme Court in State of Telangana vs. Governor of Telangana (2023)** reiterated that Governors must act on the aid and advice of the elected state Cabinet, reinforcing the Shamsher Singh principle.

---

### 🔹 On Anti-Defection and Ministerial Eligibility (91st Amendment)

`[Modern Example]` The 91st Amendment's provision disqualifying defectors from ministerial posts was tested in the **Maharashtra Political Crisis (2022)**. When rebel Shiv Sena MLAs led by Eknath Shinde split from the party, disqualification petitions were filed. The **Supreme Court in Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** held that the Governor's invitation to Eknath Shinde to form government was improper. While the disqualification proceedings are still pending before the Speaker, this case directly connects to the 91st Amendment's anti-defection provisions for ministers.

---

### 🔹 On Right to Information and Ministerial Advice Secrecy (Article 74(2))

`[Modern Example]` Article 74(2) states that the advice given by ministers to the President **cannot be enquired into by any court**. The **Right to Information Act, 2005** under **Section 8(1)(i)** also exempts from disclosure "cabinet papers including records of deliberations of the Council of Ministers." In **Chief Information Commissioner vs. State of Manipur (2011)** and subsequent RTI cases, courts have consistently upheld this principle of cabinet secrecy, balancing transparency with the constitutional protection of ministerial confidentiality.
