### *Indian Polity by M. Laxmikanth — Simplified & Enhanced Study Notes*

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Top 5 Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. The Indian Constitution has **4 key mechanisms** for managing inter-state relations: water dispute adjudication, inter-state councils, mutual recognition of acts/records, and freedom of trade & commerce.
> 2. **Article 262** deals with inter-state water disputes — and crucially, it can **exclude even the Supreme Court** from jurisdiction over such disputes.
> 3. **Article 263** provides for an **Inter-State Council** — a body for Centre-State and State-State coordination, established in **1990** on the Sarkaria Commission's recommendation.
> 4. **Zonal Councils** are **statutory bodies** (not constitutional) created by the **States Reorganisation Act, 1956** — they are purely advisory and deliberative.
> 5. **Article 301** guarantees freedom of inter-state trade and commerce, but Parliament and State Legislatures can impose **reasonable restrictions** in public interest.

---

## 🔍 Why Inter-State Relations Matter

India's federal system *(a system dividing power between Centre and States)* works well only when:
- The **Centre and States** cooperate with each other, AND
- **States cooperate with each other** (called *inter se* relations — meaning "among themselves")

To ensure this harmony, the Constitution makes **four key provisions:**

1. ⚖️ **Adjudication** *(settlement by a neutral authority)* of inter-state water disputes
2. 🤝 **Coordination** through Inter-State Councils
3. 📜 **Mutual recognition** of public acts, records, and judicial proceedings
4. 🚚 **Freedom** of inter-state trade, commerce, and intercourse

Additionally, **Parliament** has created **Zonal Councils** to further promote cooperation.

## 💧 PART 1: Inter-State Water Disputes

### What Does Article 262 Say?

**Article 262** has two important provisions:

- **(i)** Parliament **may enact a law** to adjudicate *(officially settle)* any dispute over the **use, distribution, and control** of waters of any **inter-state river or river valley**.
- **(ii)** Parliament **may also provide** that **neither the Supreme Court nor any other court** shall have jurisdiction *(legal authority to hear a case)* over such disputes.

> 📌 This is a **remarkable exception** — the Constitution allows Parliament to **bar even the Supreme Court** from hearing water disputes. This is unique because the SC normally has wide jurisdiction.

---

### Laws Enacted by Parliament Under Article 262

Parliament has enacted **two laws** under this provision:

#### 1. 🏞️ River Boards Act (1956)
- Provides for the establishment of **River Boards** for the **regulation and development** of inter-state rivers and river valleys.
- A River Board is set up by the **Central Government** on the **request of the concerned state governments**.
- Its role is **advisory** — it advises the states.

#### 2. ⚖️ Inter-State River Water Disputes Act (1956)
- Empowers the Central Government to set up an **ad hoc tribunal** *(a temporary body set up for a specific purpose)* for adjudicating water disputes between **two or more states**.
- The tribunal's decision is **final and binding** on all parties.
- **Neither the Supreme Court nor any other court** has jurisdiction over disputes referred to such a tribunal.

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The **Cauvery Water dispute** between Karnataka and Tamil Nadu has been one of India's most prolonged water conflicts. The **Supreme Court in February 2018** modified the Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal award, reducing Karnataka's allocation and fixing Tamil Nadu's share at **404.25 TMC** (thousand million cubic feet). It also directed the Centre to set up the **Cauvery Water Management Authority (CWMA)** and the **Cauvery Water Regulation Committee (CWRC)**, both set up in **2018**.
> - The **Mahanadi dispute** between Odisha and Chhattisgarh (tribunal set up in 2018) remains active, with Odisha alleging that Chhattisgarh's upstream barrages are reducing water flow.
> - The **Inter-State River Water Disputes (Amendment) Bill, 2019** was passed by the Lok Sabha to set up a **permanent tribunal** with fixed timelines (3 years + 2 years extension) instead of multiple ad hoc tribunals — though it is yet to be passed by the Rajya Sabha as of the latest update.

## 🤝 PART 2: Inter-State Councils

### What Does Article 263 Say?

**Article 263** empowers the **President** to establish an **Inter-State Council** if it appears that it would serve the **public interest**.

The President can:
- Define the **nature of duties** to be performed by such a council
- Define its **organisation and procedure**

The duties that **can be assigned** to such a council under Article 263 are:

- **(a)** Enquiring into and **advising upon disputes** which may arise between states
- **(b)** **Investigating and discussing subjects** of common interest to states, or between Centre and states
- **(c)** Making **recommendations** for the better **co-ordination of policy and action**

> 📌 **Key distinction:** The Council's function of advising on disputes is *complementary* to the Supreme Court's jurisdiction under **Article 131** (which decides *legal* controversies between governments). The Council can deal with **both legal and non-legal** controversies — but its function is **advisory**, unlike the Court which gives **binding decisions**.

---

### Councils Already Established Under Article 263

The President has established the following councils:
- **Central Council of Health and Family Welfare**
- **Central Council of Local Government** *(originally called Central Council of Local Self-Government, 1954)*
- **Four Regional Councils for Sales Tax** for the Northern, Eastern, Western, and Southern Zones

---

### 🏛️ The Establishment of the Inter-State Council (1990)

#### Sarkaria Commission's Recommendation
The **Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1983–88)** strongly recommended the establishment of a **permanent Inter-State Council** under Article 263.

Key recommendations:
- It should be called **"Inter-Governmental Council"** to distinguish it from other bodies under Article 263.
- It should be charged with duties under **clauses (b) and (c)** of Article 263.

#### Formation
- The **Janata Dal Government headed by V.P. Singh** established the **Inter-State Council in 1990** (by the Inter-State Council Order dated **May 28, 1990**).

---

### Members of the Inter-State Council

**(i)** **Prime Minister** — as Chairman
**(ii)** **Chief Ministers of all States**
**(iii)** **Chief Ministers of Union Territories** having legislative assemblies
**(iv)** **Administrators of Union Territories** not having legislative assemblies
**(v)** **Governors of States** under President's Rule
**(vi)** **Six Central Cabinet Ministers** (including the Home Minister) nominated by the Prime Minister

Additionally, **five Ministers of Cabinet rank / Ministers of State (independent charge)** nominated by the Chairman (PM) are **permanent invitees** to the Council.

---

### Functions of the Inter-State Council

The Council is a **recommendatory body** on issues relating to:
- Inter-state relations
- Centre–State relations
- Centre–Union Territory relations

Its specific duties:
- **Investigating and discussing** subjects of common interest to states or Centre
- **Making recommendations** for better coordination of policy and action
- **Deliberating** on matters of general interest referred to it by the Chairman

**Working procedure:**
- Meetings to be held **at least thrice a year**
- Meetings are held **in camera** *(in private — not open to public)*
- All questions decided by **consensus** *(mutual agreement, not voting)*

---

### Standing Committee of the Inter-State Council

- Set up in **1996** for **continuous consultation** and **processing of matters** for the Council's consideration.
- Members:
  - **(i)** **Union Home Minister** — as Chairman
  - **(ii)** **Five Union Cabinet Ministers**
  - **(iii)** **Nine Chief Ministers**

---

### Inter-State Council Secretariat

- Set up in **1991**; headed by a **Secretary to the Government of India**
- Since **2011**, it also functions as the **secretariat of the Zonal Councils**

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The **Inter-State Council met in 2016** (its 11th meeting) after a gap of nearly a decade (last met in 2006), indicating a revival of this important institution.
> - The **Punchhi Commission on Centre-State Relations (2010)** also recommended strengthening the Inter-State Council and making it meet more regularly.
> - As of recent years, there have been renewed calls — especially from opposition-governed states — to make the ISC more active as a genuine forum for federal dialogue, especially on issues like **GST compensation, COVID resource allocation, and NEET centralization**.

## 📜 PART 3: Public Acts, Records and Judicial Proceedings

### The "Full Faith and Credit" Clause

Since each state's jurisdiction *(legal authority)* is **limited to its own territory**, acts and records of one state might not be automatically recognised in another state. To prevent this problem, the Constitution contains the **"Full Faith and Credit" clause**.

This clause (under **Article 261**) provides:

#### (i) Universal Recognition Across India
- **Full faith and credit** must be given throughout India to:
  - **Public acts** (both legislative *and* executive acts of the government)
  - **Public records** (any official book, register, or record made by a public servant in discharge of official duties)
  - **Judicial proceedings** of the Centre and every State

#### (ii) Parliament's Power to Lay Down Mode of Proof
- The **manner** in which and **conditions** under which such acts, records, and proceedings are to be **proved** — and their **effect determined** — shall be as provided by **Parliamentary laws**.
- This means Parliament controls *how* you prove a public act from another state and *what effect* it has.

#### (iii) Execution of Civil Court Judgements
- **Final judgements and orders of civil courts** anywhere in India are capable of **execution anywhere within India** — without the need for a fresh suit (fresh legal case) upon the judgement.
- ⚠️ This rule applies **only to civil judgements**, NOT to criminal judgements.
- In other words, courts of one state are **NOT required to enforce the penal (criminal) laws of another state**.

## 🚚 PART 4: Inter-State Trade and Commerce

### Articles 301–307 (Part XIII of the Constitution)

**Article 301** is the foundational provision: it declares that **trade, commerce, and intercourse throughout the territory of India shall be free.**

**Purpose:** To break down border barriers between states and create **one unified economic unit** that encourages free flow of trade and commerce across India.

**Scope:** This freedom covers **both:**
- **Inter-state** trade, commerce and intercourse (between states)
- **Intra-state** trade, commerce and intercourse (within a state)

> 📌 Article 301 is violated whether restrictions are imposed **at the frontier of a state** OR at any **prior or subsequent stage**.

---

### Restrictions on the Freedom (Articles 302–305)

The freedom under Article 301 is not absolute. It is subject to restrictions provided in Articles 302 to 305:

#### (i) Parliament's Power to Restrict (Article 302)
- Parliament **can impose restrictions** on freedom of trade, commerce, and intercourse — both between states and within a state — **in public interest**.
- **BUT:** Parliament **cannot** give preference to one state over another **OR** discriminate between states, **except** in the case of **scarcity of goods** in any part of India.

> 📌 *Example:* Parliament enacted the **Essential Commodities Act (1955)** — this allows the Central Government to control the production, supply, and distribution of essential commodities like **petroleum, coal, iron and steel**, etc.

#### (ii) State Legislature's Power to Restrict (Article 304)
- A State Legislature **can impose reasonable restrictions** on freedom of trade, commerce, and intercourse — within its state or with other states — **in public interest**.
- **BUT:** A bill for this purpose can be introduced in the legislature **only with the previous sanction of the President**.
- Further, the state legislature **cannot** give preference to one state over another or discriminate between states.

#### (iii) Non-Discriminatory Taxes on Imported Goods (Article 304)
- A State Legislature **can impose taxes** on goods imported from other states or Union Territories — **but only if similar goods manufactured within that state are subject to the same tax**.
- This **prohibits discriminatory taxation** by states.

#### (iv) Nationalisation Laws (Article 305)
- The freedom under Article 301 is subject to **nationalisation laws** *(laws that give the government a monopoly over certain industries)*.
- Parliament **or** State Legislatures can make laws for the carrying on by the respective government of any **trade, business, industry or service** — whether to the **complete or partial exclusion of citizens**.

---

### Authority for Trade and Commerce (Article 307)
- Parliament **can appoint an appropriate authority** for carrying out the purposes of Articles 301–304.
- Parliament can also confer on that authority the necessary powers and duties.
- ⚠️ **However, no such authority has been appointed so far.**
- 📌 In the USA, a similar authority is known as the **Inter-State Commerce Commission**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The **Goods and Services Tax (GST)**, implemented in **July 2017**, is the most transformative reform related to inter-state trade and commerce. It replaced a patchwork of state taxes (VAT, entry tax, octroi) with a unified national tax — directly fulfilling the vision of Article 301 (one India, one market).
> - The **GST Council (Article 279A)** functions as a federal body coordinating tax policy between Centre and States — a real-world embodiment of inter-state cooperation.
> - The **One Nation One Market** vision was further strengthened by the **e-NAM (National Agriculture Market)** platform launched in 2016, connecting agricultural mandis across state borders.
> - However, states like **Kerala and Punjab have challenged central interventions** in trade matters, raising fresh debates about the balance between Article 301 (free trade) and states' rights to regulate.

## 🗺️ PART 5: Zonal Councils

### What Are Zonal Councils?

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Nature** | **Statutory bodies** *(created by law, not by the Constitution)* |
| **Created by** | **States Reorganisation Act of 1956** |
| **Number of Zones** | **Five** (Northern, Central, Eastern, Western, Southern) |
| **Constitutional Status** | NOT a constitutional body — no mention in the Constitution |

> 📌 This is a very important distinction: **Zonal Councils are statutory, NOT constitutional.** They were created by an Act of Parliament, unlike the Inter-State Council which flows from Article 263.

---

### How Were the Zones Formed?

Several factors were considered:
- Natural divisions of the country
- River systems and means of communication
- Cultural and linguistic affinity
- Requirements of economic development
- Security and law and order

---

### Members of Each Zonal Council

**Regular Members:**
- **(a)** **Home Minister of Central Government**
- **(b)** **Chief Ministers of all States** in the zone
- **(c)** **Two other ministers** from each state in the zone
- **(d)** **Administrator of each Union Territory** in the zone

**Advisors (without voting rights):**
- **(i)** A person nominated by the **NITI Aayog**
- **(ii)** **Chief Secretary** of the government of each state in the zone
- **(iii)** **Development Commissioner** of each state in the zone

---

### Leadership

- The **Home Minister of Central Government** is the **common chairman** of all **five Zonal Councils**.
- Each **Chief Minister** acts as **vice-chairman** by **rotation**, holding office for **one year at a time**.

---

### Functions and Objectives of Zonal Councils

Zonal Councils discuss and make recommendations on:
- Economic and social planning
- Linguistic minorities
- Border disputes
- Inter-state transport
- And other matters of common concern

They are **only deliberative and advisory bodies** *(they discuss and suggest, but cannot enforce anything)*.

**Detailed Objectives:**

- ✅ Achieve **emotional integration** of the country
- ✅ Help **arrest the growth** of acute state-consciousness, regionalism, linguism, and particularistic trends
- ✅ Help **remove after-effects of separation** so that reorganisation, integration, and economic advancement may proceed together
- ✅ Enable Centre and states to **cooperate on social and economic matters** and exchange ideas to evolve **uniform policies**
- ✅ Cooperate in the **speedy execution of major development projects**
- ✅ Secure **political equilibrium** between different regions of the country

---

---

### 🌐 North-Eastern Council — A Special Addition

- Created by a **separate Act of Parliament — the North-Eastern Council Act of 1971**
- Came into existence on **August 8, 1972**
- **Members:** Assam, Manipur, Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh, Nagaland, Meghalaya, Tripura, and **Sikkim** (added in **2002** as the **8th member**)

**Functions:** Similar to Zonal Councils, **plus** two additional functions:
1. **Formulate a unified and coordinated regional plan** covering matters of common importance
2. **Review measures** taken by member states for the **maintenance of security and public order** in the region

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - Zonal Councils have been revived and made more active in recent years. The **Northern Zonal Council** meeting held in **2023** (chaired by Union Home Minister Amit Shah) discussed issues like border disputes, illegal immigration, drugs, and development projects.
> - Since **2011**, the **Inter-State Council Secretariat also serves as the Secretariat for Zonal Councils** — improving coordination.
> - The **North-Eastern Council** has played an important role in monitoring infrastructure development in the region under the **Act East Policy** and the **PM-DevINE (Prime Minister's Development Initiative for North East Region)** scheme launched in 2022 with an outlay of ₹6,600 crore.
> - The **Look East / Act East Policy** and projects like the **Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Project** and **India-Myanmar-Thailand Highway** fall within the NEC's domain of regional cooperation.

## ⚖️ Important Court Cases — Explained in Detail

### 🏛️ Case 1: Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal & Supreme Court Intervention

**Background:**
The Cauvery river flows through Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and the Union Territory of Puducherry. The dispute is over how much water each gets — especially during low-rainfall years.

**Timeline:**
- A tribunal was first set up in **1990** under the Inter-State River Water Disputes Act, 1956.
- The tribunal gave its award in **2007**, allocating water shares to each party.
- Karnataka and Tamil Nadu both challenged parts of the award.

**Supreme Court Judgment (February 2018):**
- The SC modified the tribunal's award.
- It **reduced Karnataka's share** and **fixed Tamil Nadu's allocation** at **404.25 TMC**.
- Crucially, the SC directed the Centre to set up:
  - **Cauvery Water Management Authority (CWMA)**
  - **Cauvery Water Regulation Committee (CWRC)**
- Both were constituted in **June 2018**.

**Constitutional Significance:**
- This case shows the **tension between Article 262** (which allows Parliament to bar SC jurisdiction) and the **SC's powers under Article 32 and 136**.
- The SC exercised jurisdiction here because the parties challenged the tribunal's award — showing that while the SC cannot be the *first forum*, it can still act as an *appellate authority* in some circumstances.

---

### 🏛️ Case 2: S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994) — Indirectly Related to Inter-State Relations

*(Fully detailed in Chapter 15, but relevant here for understanding Centre-State balance in federal relations)*

**Background:**
S.R. Bommai was the Chief Minister of Karnataka. His government was dismissed using **Article 356** (President's Rule) in 1989. This case challenged the arbitrary use of President's Rule.

**Supreme Court Judgment (1994) — 9-Judge Constitution Bench:**
- President's Rule **cannot be imposed arbitrarily** — it is subject to judicial review.
- The floor of the **state legislature** is the proper place to test a government's majority, not the Governor's discretion.
- The **floor test** must be held before recommending President's Rule.
- Secularism was declared part of the **basic structure** of the Constitution.

**Relevance to Inter-State Relations:**
- This judgment strengthened federalism and ensured that Centre cannot easily use Article 356 to destabilise a state government — directly improving Centre-State harmony which is the foundation of inter-state relations.

---

### 🏛️ Case 3: Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973) — The Basic Structure Doctrine

*(Mentioned in Chapter 4 but relevant as background for inter-state constitutional law)*

**Background:**
Kesavananda Bharati, the head of a religious institution in Kerala, challenged the Kerala Land Reform Act which restricted his right to manage religious property.

**Supreme Court Judgment (1973) — 13-Judge Constitution Bench:**
- Parliament **can amend any part** of the Constitution under Article 368.
- BUT it **cannot alter or destroy the "basic structure"** of the Constitution.
- Basic structure includes: supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, secularism, separation of powers, federal character, etc.

**Relevance to Inter-State Relations:**
- The **federal character** of the Constitution is part of the basic structure — meaning Parliament cannot pass an amendment that destroys federalism or takes away states' autonomy entirely.
- This protects the framework within which inter-state relations operate.

---

### 🏛️ Case 4: State of Rajasthan vs. Union of India (1977) — President's Rule and Federalism

**Background:**
After the 1977 general elections brought the Janata Party to power at the Centre, the new government (through the Home Minister) asked several Congress-governed states to dissolve their assemblies and call fresh elections, threatening to use Article 356 otherwise.

**Supreme Court Judgment (1977):**
- The SC held that the **dissolution of state assemblies was valid** in this case.
- However, it also clarified that **Article 356 is not beyond judicial review** — the SC can examine whether the President's satisfaction was based on relevant material or was mala fide *(in bad faith)*.
- This was a **precursor to the more powerful Bommai judgment (1994)**.

**Relevance to Inter-State Relations:**
- It established early principles about the limits of Central power over states — a key element of the inter-state balance of power.

## 🔑 Key Comparisons & Distinctions

### Inter-State Council vs. Zonal Councils

| Feature | Inter-State Council | Zonal Councils |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | Article 263 (Constitutional) | States Reorganisation Act, 1956 (Statutory) |
| **Scope** | **National** — all states and UTs | **Regional** — specific zone |
| **Chairman** | Prime Minister | Union Home Minister |
| **Nature** | Advisory and recommendatory | Advisory and deliberative |
| **Meetings** | At least 3 times a year | As needed |
| **Secretariat** | Inter-State Council Secretariat (est. 1991) | Same secretariat (since 2011) |

---

### Article 262 vs. Article 131 — Water Disputes

| Feature | Article 262 | Article 131 |
|---|---|---|
| **Deals with** | Inter-state water disputes | Disputes between Centre and States / between States |
| **Forum** | Ad hoc Tribunal (under the 1956 Act) | Supreme Court |
| **SC Jurisdiction** | Can be **excluded by Parliament** | **Cannot be excluded** |
| **Nature of decision** | Final and binding | Final and binding |
| **Type of dispute** | Only water disputes | Any legal dispute |
