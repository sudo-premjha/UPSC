## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 things you MUST remember from this chapter.**

1. India follows a **federal system** but the word "federation" is **never used** in the Constitution — Article 1 calls India a **"Union of States"**.
2. The Indian Constitution has **7 Federal Features** AND **14 Unitary Features** — making it a unique hybrid system.
3. India follows the **Canadian model** (strong Centre) — NOT the American model.
4. The landmark **S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)** case declared **federalism as a basic feature** of the Indian Constitution.
5. Scholars describe India's federalism differently — **KC Wheare** says "quasi-federal"; **Granville Austin** says "cooperative federalism"; **Ivor Jennings** says "federation with strong centralising tendency."

---

## 📌 Section 1: What is a Federal vs. Unitary Government?

### The Basic Idea

Political scientists classify governments into two types based on how power is shared between the national government and regional governments:

- **Unitary Government** (one central authority runs everything): All powers rest with the national (central) government. Regional governments, if they exist, get their authority *from* the national government — not from the Constitution directly.

- **Federal Government** (power divided by the Constitution itself): Powers are divided between the national and regional governments *by the Constitution itself*. Both levels operate **independently** within their own areas.

### Real-World Examples

| Type | Countries |
|------|-----------|
| **Unitary** | Britain, France, Japan, China, Italy, Norway, Sweden |
| **Federal** | USA, Switzerland, Australia, Canada, Russia, Brazil, Argentina |

> 💡 In federal systems, the national government is called the **Federal/Central/Union government** and regional governments are called **state/provincial governments**.

---

### What Does "Federation" Even Mean?

The word **'federation'** comes from the Latin word **foedus** meaning **'treaty' or 'agreement'**. So, a federation is a new political system formed through a treaty/agreement between various units.

These units are called by different names:
- **States** → USA
- **Cantons** → Switzerland
- **Provinces** → Canada
- **Republics** → Russia

---

### Two Ways a Federation Can Be Formed

**Method 1 — Integration** (coming together):
- Weak or economically backward independent states unite to form a strong union.
- Example: **USA** — 13 small states united for collective strength.

**Method 2 — Disintegration** (holding together):
- A large unitary state converts itself into a federation by giving **autonomy** (self-governing power) to provinces to promote regional interests.
- Example: **Canada**, and also **India**.

> 🏆 **The USA** is the **first and oldest federation** in the world, formed in **1787** after the American Revolution (1775–83). It started with 13 states and now has **50 states**.
> 🍁 **Canada** was formed in **1867**, starting with 4 provinces and now has **10 provinces**.

---

### Comparing Federal and Unitary Governments

| Feature | **Federal Government** | **Unitary Government** |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Structure** | **Dual Government** (national + regional) | **Single Government** (national only; may create regional bodies) |
| **Constitution** | Always **Written** | May be written (France) or unwritten (Britain) |
| **Powers** | **Divided** between national and regional by Constitution | **Not divided** — all powers with national government |
| **Supremacy** | **Constitution is supreme** | Constitution may be supreme (Japan) or not supreme (Britain) |
| **Rigidity** | **Rigid Constitution** | May be rigid (France) or flexible (Britain) |
| **Judiciary** | **Independent Judiciary** | May or may not be independent |
| **Legislature** | **Bicameral** (two houses) | May be bicameral (Britain) or unicameral/single house (China) |

## 📌 Section 2: Why Did India Choose a Federal System?

The **framers of the Constitution** adopted a federal system for **two main reasons**:

1. **Large size** of the country
2. **Socio-cultural diversity** of the population

They realised the federal system would:
- ✅ Ensure **efficient governance** of a large, diverse nation
- ✅ **Reconcile national unity** (keeping India together) with **regional autonomy** (letting states govern themselves)

---

### India: "Union of States" — Not "Federation of States"

The word **'federation' appears nowhere** in the Indian Constitution. Instead, **Article 1** describes India as a **'Union of States'**.

**Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** explained that 'Union of States' was preferred over 'Federation of States' for two specific reasons:

1. The Indian federation was **NOT formed by an agreement** between states (unlike the American federation where states voluntarily came together).
2. The states have **NO right to secede** (break away) from the federation. The federation is **indestructible**.

---

### India Follows the Canadian Model — Not the American Model

| Feature | **Canadian Model** (India follows this) | **American Model** |
|---------|----------------------------------------|-------------------|
| **How formed** | By **disintegration** (large unitary state → federation) | By **integration** (small states came together) |
| **Name used** | Called a **'Union'** | Called a 'Federation' |
| **Centre's power** | **Very strong Centre** | States are more powerful |
| **Governor** | **Appointed** by Centre | **Elected** by people |
| **Citizenship** | **Single citizenship** | Dual citizenship |

## 📌 Section 3: The 7 Federal Features of the Indian Constitution

### 1. 🏛️ Dual Polity (two separate levels of government)
- The Constitution creates **two separate governments**:
  - **Union Government** at the Centre
  - **State Governments** at the regional level
- Each has **sovereign powers** (supreme authority) in its own assigned field.
- **Union Government** handles: defence, foreign affairs, currency, communication, etc.
- **State Governments** handle: public order, agriculture, health, local government, etc.

---

### 2. 📝 Written Constitution
- India has a written Constitution — and not just written, but the **lengthiest Constitution in the world**.
- **Originally (1950):** Preamble + **395 Articles** (in 22 Parts) + **8 Schedules**
- **Currently:** Preamble + about **470 Articles** (in 25 Parts) + **12 Schedules**

> 📊 For comparison: USA had only **7 Articles**, Australia had **128**, Canada had **147**.

- It specifies the **structure, organisation, powers, and functions** of both Central and state governments.
- By having everything written down, it **avoids misunderstandings** between the two levels of government.

---

### 3. ⚖️ Division of Powers
- The Constitution divides powers through **three lists** in the **Seventh Schedule**:

| List | Current Subjects | Original Subjects | Who Legislates? |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Union List** | **98 subjects** | Originally 97 | Only **Parliament** (Centre) |
| **State List** | **59 subjects** | Originally 66 | Only **State Legislatures** |
| **Concurrent List** | **52 subjects** | Originally 47 | **Both** Centre and States |

- In case of **conflict** between Centre and state laws on the Concurrent List → **Central law prevails**.
- **Residuary subjects** (not mentioned in any list) → given to the **Centre** (unlike USA where they go to states).

---

### 4. 👑 Supremacy of the Constitution
- The Constitution is the **supreme (highest) law** of the land.
- All laws made by Centre or states must **conform to** the Constitution.
- If they don't, the **Supreme Court or High Courts** can declare them **invalid** through their power of **judicial review** (courts' power to strike down unconstitutional laws).
- All organs of government (legislative, executive, judicial) at both levels must operate within the **jurisdiction (boundary of authority)** prescribed by the Constitution.

---

### 5. 🔒 Rigid Constitution
- The Constitution is **rigid** (difficult to change) for federal provisions.
- Provisions related to **federal structure** (Centre-state relations, judicial organisation) can be amended **only by joint action** of Central and state governments.
- This requires:
  - A **special majority** in Parliament (two-thirds of members present and voting + majority of total membership of each House)
  - **Approval of at least half** of the state legislatures

> This rigidity protects the **division of powers** and **supremacy of the Constitution**.

---

### 6. ⚖️ Independent Judiciary
- The Constitution establishes an **independent judiciary** headed by the **Supreme Court** for two purposes:
  1. **Protecting** the supremacy of the Constitution through judicial review
  2. **Settling disputes** between Centre and states, or between states
- Measures to ensure judicial independence include:
  - Security of **tenure** (judges cannot be easily removed)
  - **Fixed service conditions** (government cannot change terms to pressure judges)

---

### 7. 🏛️ Bicameralism (two-house legislature)
- The Constitution provides for a **bicameral legislature** (a parliament with two houses):
  - **Rajya Sabha** (Upper House) → represents the **states** of the Indian Federation
  - **Lok Sabha** (Lower House) → represents the **people** of India as a whole
- The **Rajya Sabha**, even though the less powerful house, maintains **federal equilibrium** (balance) by protecting states' interests against undue Centre interference.

---

## 🌍 Modern Context — Federal Features

`[Modern Example]`
- **GST (Goods and Services Tax), 2017** is a prime example of Division of Powers in practice. The **GST Council** — with both Centre and states — represents cooperative federalism where both levels share taxation powers. However, debates about Centre's dominance in GST decisions continue to reflect the tension between federal and unitary tendencies.
- **Article 246A** (added by the 101st Constitutional Amendment Act, 2016) gave **concurrent power** to both Parliament and State Legislatures to make laws on GST — a new addition to the Concurrent List in spirit.

## 📌 Section 4: The 14 Unitary Features of the Indian Constitution

Despite the federal features, the Constitution has **many unitary (centralising) features** that tilt the balance towards the Centre.

### 1. 💪 Strong Centre
The division of powers heavily favours the Centre:
- **Union List has MORE subjects** than the State List
- **More important subjects** (defence, foreign affairs, atomic energy) are in the Union List
- Centre has **overriding authority** over the Concurrent List
- **Residuary powers** (leftover powers not in any list) go to the **Centre** (in USA, they go to states)

---

### 2. 🗺️ States Are Not Indestructible
- Unlike real federations, Indian states have **NO right to territorial integrity** (right to keep their own borders).
- Parliament can **unilaterally** (on its own, without states' consent) change the:
  - **Area** of a state
  - **Boundaries** of a state
  - **Name** of a state
- This requires only a **simple majority** (more than 50%), not a special majority!

> 🔴 **Key Comparison:**
> - India = **"Indestructible Union of Destructible States"**
> - USA = **"Indestructible Union of Indestructible States"**

---

### 3. 📜 Single Constitution
- In most federations, states can frame their **own separate constitutions**.
- In India, there is only **ONE Constitution** for both the Centre and all states.
- **Exception:** The erstwhile state of **Jammu & Kashmir** had its own separate state Constitution (until **Article 370 was abrogated in 2019**).

---

### 4. 🔓 Flexibility of the Constitution
- Despite being called rigid, the **bulk of the Constitution** can be amended by **Parliament alone** — without states' involvement.
- Parliament can amend by:
  - **Simple majority** (ordinary bills)
  - **Special majority** (important provisions)
- The **power to initiate** a constitutional amendment rests **only with the Centre**.
- In USA, **states can also propose** amendments to the Constitution.

---

### 5. 📊 No Equality of State Representation in Rajya Sabha
- In India, states get **representation in Rajya Sabha based on population**.
- Membership ranges from **1 to 31** per state.
- In **USA**, every state — big or small — gets **exactly 2 senators** (100 senators total for 50 states).
- The American principle of **equal representation** is a safeguard for smaller states — India does NOT follow this.

---

### 6. 🚨 Emergency Provisions
- The Constitution provides for **three types of emergencies**:
  1. **National Emergency** (Article 352) — war, external aggression, armed rebellion
  2. **State Emergency / President's Rule** (Article 356) — failure of constitutional machinery in a state
  3. **Financial Emergency** (Article 360) — threat to financial stability

- During an emergency, the **Central government becomes all-powerful** and states come under **total Centre control**.
- This effectively **converts the federal structure into a unitary one** — without formally amending the Constitution.
- **This kind of transformation is not found in any other federation.**

---

### 7. 🪪 Single Citizenship
- Despite having dual polity (two levels of government), India like Canada adopted **single citizenship**.
- There is only **Indian citizenship** — no separate state citizenship.
- All citizens, regardless of which state they're from, enjoy **the same rights** across the country.
- In contrast, **USA and Australia** have **dual citizenship** (national + state).

---

### 8. 🏛️ Integrated Judiciary (one unified court system)
- India has a **single, integrated judicial system**:
  - **Supreme Court** at the top
  - **State High Courts** below it
- This **one court system** enforces **both** Central and state laws.
- In **USA**, there is a **double court system** — federal courts for federal laws and state courts for state laws.

---

### 9. 👔 All-India Services
- Centre and states have their own separate public services.
- But additionally, India has **All-India Services** — **IAS** (Indian Administrative Service), **IPS** (Indian Police Service), and **IFoS** (Indian Forest Service) — which serve **both levels**.
- These officers are **recruited and trained by the Centre**, which also has **ultimate control** over them.
- These services are deployed in states but ultimately answer to the Centre — this **violates the principle of federalism**.

---

### 10. 🔍 Integrated Audit Machinery
- The **Comptroller and Auditor-General of India (CAG)** audits accounts of **both** the Central government and state governments.
- But the CAG is **appointed and removed by the President** — **without consulting the states**.
- This **restricts the financial autonomy** (independent financial decision-making power) of states.
- In **USA**, the Comptroller-General has **no role** in auditing state accounts.

---

### 11. 📋 Parliament's Authority Over State List
- Even within their limited sphere, states do **NOT have exclusive control**.
- Parliament can **legislate on any subject of the State List** if the **Rajya Sabha passes a resolution** to that effect in the **national interest**.
- This means Parliament's **law-making power can be extended** without amending the Constitution.
- Crucially, this can happen even when **no emergency is in force**.

---

### 12. 👤 Appointment of Governor
- The **Governor** (head of the state) is **appointed by the President** (i.e., by the Centre).
- The Governor holds office **during the pleasure of the President** (can be removed anytime).
- The Governor also acts as an **agent of the Centre** — allowing Centre to exercise control over states.
- In USA, state heads (Governors) are **elected by the people** — India adopted the **Canadian system** of appointment here.

---

### 13. 🗳️ Integrated Election Machinery
- The **Election Commission of India** conducts elections for **both** Parliament and state legislatures.
- But the Election Commission is **constituted by the President** — states have **no say** in its appointment or removal.
- In **USA**, there are **separate election machineries** for federal and state elections.

---

### 14. 🚫 Veto Over State Bills
- The **Governor** can **reserve** certain types of bills passed by the state legislature for the **President's consideration**.
- The **President** can **withhold assent** to such bills:
  - Not just the **first time**, but also the **second time** (absolute veto — not just a temporary suspensive veto)
- This means the President has **absolute veto** (power to permanently reject) over certain state bills.
- In **USA and Australia**, states are autonomous within their fields — no such reservation/veto provision exists.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Unitary Features

`[Modern Example]`

### Emergency Provisions in Recent Times
- **COVID-19 Pandemic (2020):** The Central Government invoked the **Disaster Management Act, 2005** to effectively centralise decision-making during the pandemic — declaring national lockdowns without consulting state assemblies. This was widely seen as an example of the unitary tendency in practice.
- Several states (Kerala, Maharashtra, West Bengal) publicly disagreed with Central directives — showing the ongoing federal-unitary tension.

### Abrogation of Article 370 (2019)
`[Modern Example]`
- The **removal of J&K's special status** (the only state with its own Constitution) via the **Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order, 2019**, and the reorganisation of J&K into two Union Territories, is the most dramatic recent example of the **"States are Destructible"** unitary feature. Parliament changed the status of a state by a **simple majority**.
- The Supreme Court upheld this in **Dr. Justice S. Manohar vs. Union of India** related matters (2023), reaffirming Parliament's power under Article 3.

## 📌 Section 5: Critical Evaluation — What Kind of Federal System Does India Have?

This is the most debated question in Indian polity. Here are the views of various experts:

---

### 🔴 Critics Who Say India is NOT Truly Federal

**KC Wheare** (British political scientist):
- Described India's Constitution as **"quasi-federal"** (partly federal, partly unitary — not fully either).
- Remarked: *"Indian Union is a unitary state with subsidiary federal features rather than a federal state with subsidiary unitary features."*

**K. Santhanam** (Indian expert):
- Identified two factors responsible for India's **unitary bias** (tendency toward centralization):
  1. **Centre's dominance in finances** — states depend heavily on Central grants.
  2. **Erstwhile Planning Commission** — it controlled developmental processes in states (Note: the Planning Commission was replaced by **NITI Aayog in 2015**).
- Observed: *"India has practically functioned as a unitary state though the Union and the states have tried to function formally and legally as a federation."*

---

### 🟢 Those Who Say India IS Federal (in its Own Way)

**Paul Appleby** (American administrative expert):
- Characterised India as **"extremely federal"**.

**Morris Jones** (political scientist):
- Termed it **"bargaining federalism"** (a system where Centre and states negotiate and bargain over powers).

**Ivor Jennings** (British constitutional expert):
- Described it as a **"federation with a strong centralising tendency"**.
- Observed: *"The Indian Constitution is mainly federal with unique safeguards for enforcing national unity and growth."*

**Alexandrowicz** (constitutional scholar):
- Stated that India is a case of **"sui generis"** (a Latin phrase meaning *unique in character* — India doesn't fit any standard category).

**Granville Austin** (American constitutional expert):
- Called Indian federalism **"cooperative federalism"** (Centre and states cooperate rather than compete).
- Said: Though the Constitution created a strong Centre, it has **NOT made state governments weak** and has **not reduced them to mere administrative agencies** of the Centre.

---

### 🔵 Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's Own View

In the Constituent Assembly, Dr. Ambedkar made these important observations:

**On the federal nature:**
> *"The Constitution is a Federal Constitution in as much as it establishes a dual polity. The Union is not a league of states, united in a loose relationship, nor are the states the agencies of the Union, deriving powers from it. Both the Union and the states are created by the Constitution, both derive their respective authority from the Constitution."*

**On flexibility:**
> *"Yet the Constitution avoids the tight mould of federalism and could be both unitary as well as federal according to the requirements of time and circumstances."*

**On the charge of over-centralisation:**
> *"It is clear that this view is not only an exaggeration but is also founded on a misunderstanding of what exactly the Constitution contrives to do... The states are in no way dependent upon the Centre for their legislative or executive authority. The states and the Centre are co-equal in this matter."*

## 📌 Section 6: The S.R. Bommai Case (1994) — The Landmark Judgment 🏛️

### Full Case Name
**S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)**

---

### Background — What Was the Dispute?

**S.R. Bommai** was the Chief Minister of Karnataka (Janata Dal government). In **1989**, his government was dismissed by the Governor, who claimed that Bommai had lost his majority in the Assembly. The President's Rule (Article 356) was imposed in Karnataka without giving Bommai a chance to prove his majority on the floor of the Assembly.

Similar dismissals of state governments happened in several other states (Madhya Pradesh, Himachal Pradesh, Rajasthan) in **1992**, following the demolition of the Babri Masjid. The Central Government used Article 356 to dismiss BJP governments in these states.

Bommai and others challenged these dismissals in various High Courts, and the cases eventually came to the **Supreme Court** as a consolidated batch.

---

### What the Supreme Court Decided — Key Rulings

The Supreme Court gave a **9-judge Constitutional Bench** verdict in 1994. This is one of the most significant constitutional judgments in Indian history. Here are the key rulings:

**1. Federalism is a Basic Feature:**
The Court held that **federalism is a part of the basic structure** of the Constitution (the Basic Structure Doctrine — first established in the Kesavananda Bharati case, 1973 — means there are certain fundamental features of the Constitution that even Parliament cannot destroy through amendments). Federalism cannot be destroyed even by a constitutional amendment.

**2. States Are NOT Mere Appendages:**
The Court clearly stated:
> *"The fact that under the scheme of our Constitution, greater power is conferred upon the Centre vis-à-vis the states does not mean that the states are mere appendages of the Centre. The states have an independent constitutional existence. They are not satellites or agents of the Centre. Within the sphere allotted to them, the states are supreme."*

**3. Emergency Powers Are Exceptions, Not the Rule:**
The Court said that the fact that Centre can override states during emergencies does **not destroy the essential federal character** of the Constitution. These are **exceptions**, not the rule.

**4. Federalism is a Principle, Not Just Convenience:**
> *"Let it be said that the federalism in the Indian Constitution is not a matter of administrative convenience, but one of principle — the outcome of our own process and a recognition of the ground realities."*

**5. Judicial Review of President's Rule:**
The Court held that the imposition of **President's Rule (Article 356)** is **subject to judicial review** (courts can examine whether it was justified). The floor of the House (state assembly) is the **proper place to test majority** — not the Governor's opinion alone.

**6. Restrictions on Misuse of Article 356:**
The Supreme Court imposed several **procedural limitations** on the use of Article 356 to prevent its misuse:
- The President cannot dismiss a state government **without giving it a chance** to prove its majority on the floor of the Assembly.
- The **Proclamation of President's Rule** can be challenged in courts.
- If the Proclamation is found invalid, courts can **restore the dismissed government**.
- The **Central Cabinet's advice** to the President for imposing Article 356 must be based on **relevant material** — not political motives.

---

### Why is This Case So Important?

| Before Bommai (1994) | After Bommai (1994) |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| Centre could easily dismiss state governments using Article 356 for political reasons | Article 356 subject to **judicial review** |
| No obligation to test majority on Assembly floor | Must be given chance to prove majority |
| Courts had limited role | Courts can **restore** dismissed governments |
| Federalism not clearly defined as basic structure | **Federalism = Basic Structure** (cannot be destroyed) |

> 🔴 **UPSC Tip:** The Bommai case is asked very frequently. Remember: It linked **Article 356** with **judicial review** and declared **federalism as a basic feature**.

## 🌍 Modern Context — S.R. Bommai Case

`[Modern Example]`
- The Bommai judgment continues to be invoked regularly. In **2021**, when political crises emerged in states like **Rajasthan** (Sachin Pilot vs. Ashok Gehlot) and **Maharashtra** (Uddhav Thackeray government crisis, 2022), the **floor test** principle from Bommai was directly applied.
- The **Maharashtra political crisis (2022)** — involving the split in Shiv Sena and the subsequent formation of the Eknath Shinde government — was extensively litigated in the Supreme Court. The Court's observations reaffirmed the Bommai principle that the **Governor cannot act arbitrarily** in calling/denying floor tests.
- **Subramanian Swamy vs. Election Commission** and related cases in 2022-23 also referenced Bommai's federalism principles.

## 📌 Section 7: India's Federalism — A Compromise

The federalism in India represents a **compromise** between two conflicting needs:

1. **Normal division of powers** — states enjoy autonomy within their own spheres (federal principle)
2. **National integrity and a strong Union** — needed under exceptional circumstances (unitary principle)

---

### Evidence of Federal Spirit in India's Political Reality

The following real-world trends show that India **does** function with a federal spirit:

- 🗺️ **Territorial disputes between states** — e.g., between **Maharashtra and Karnataka over Belgaum** (now Belagavi)
- 💧 **River water disputes** — e.g., between **Karnataka and Tamil Nadu over Cauvery water**
- 🗳️ **Rise of regional parties** — coming to power in states like **Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu**, etc.
- 🏙️ **Creation of new states** — to fulfil regional aspirations (e.g., **Mizoram, Jharkhand, Telangana**)
- 💰 **States demanding more financial grants** from the Centre
- 🛡️ **States asserting autonomy** and resisting Centre's interference
- ⚖️ **Supreme Court imposing procedural limitations** on use of Article 356 (S.R. Bommai case, 1994)

---

## 🌍 Modern Context — India's Federal Spirit Today

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. Finance Commission and States' Demands
- The **15th Finance Commission** (Chairman: N.K. Singh, Report 2021–26) recommended a **41% share** of Central taxes for states (down from 42% after J&K became a UT). Several states protested, reflecting the ongoing Centre-state fiscal tension.
- Southern states (Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka) have consistently argued that the formula used **penalises better-performing states** — a live federal debate.

### 2. NITI Aayog vs. Planning Commission `[Modern Example]`
- In 2015, the **Planning Commission** (which K. Santhanam had cited as increasing unitary bias) was replaced by **NITI Aayog** (National Institution for Transforming India).
- Unlike the Planning Commission, NITI Aayog is a **think-tank** without the power to allocate funds. States now have more say in their development plans — a shift toward cooperative federalism.

### 3. GST Council — Cooperative Federalism in Action `[Modern Example]`
- The **GST Council** (Centre + all state Finance Ministers) is often cited as India's best example of **cooperative federalism** in recent times, where both Centre and states jointly decide tax rates.
- However, states' concerns about compensation cess delays (especially post-COVID) showed the limits of this cooperation.

### 4. Telangana — Federal Spirit in State Formation `[Modern Example]`
- The formation of **Telangana** in 2014 (the most recent new state) exemplifies the federal spirit of "creation of new states to fulfil regional aspirations" — a point directly mentioned in the textbook.
