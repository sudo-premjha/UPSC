## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways You Must Know:**
> 1. India follows **Single Citizenship** (unlike the USA's double citizenship system), meaning every Indian owes allegiance only to the Union of India.
> 2. The **Constitution (Articles 5–11)** only defines who were citizens at commencement (Jan 26, 1950). All further rules come from the **Citizenship Act, 1955**.
> 3. Citizenship can be acquired in **5 ways**: Birth, Descent, Registration, Naturalisation, and Incorporation of Territory.
> 4. Citizenship can be lost in **3 ways**: Renunciation, Termination, and Deprivation.
> 5. The **Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 (CAA)** is a landmark amendment that fast-tracks citizenship for persecuted minorities from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan.

## PART 1: Meaning and Significance of Citizenship

### Who Lives in India? Citizens vs. Aliens

Like every modern nation, India has two broad categories of people living within its territory:

**1. Citizens**
- Full members of the Indian State.
- Owe **allegiance** (loyalty and obedience) to India.
- Enjoy **all** civil and political rights.

**2. Aliens**
- Citizens of *another* country, currently in India.
- Do **not** enjoy all civil and political rights.
- Further divided into two types:

| Type | Who Are They? | Rights Available? |
|---|---|---|
| **Friendly Aliens** | Subjects of countries with cordial (friendly) relations with India | Standard limited rights |
| **Enemy Aliens** | Subjects of a country **at war** with India | Even *fewer* rights — e.g., they do **not** get protection against arrest & detention *(Article 22)* |

---

### What Exclusive Rights Do Citizens Enjoy?

The Constitution grants the following rights **only to citizens** — aliens are excluded:

- **Article 15** — Right against discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth.
- **Article 16** — Right to equality of opportunity in public employment (government jobs).
- **Article 19** — Right to freedom of speech & expression, assembly, association, movement, residence, and profession.
- **Articles 29 & 30** — Cultural and Educational Rights (e.g., minorities can run their own institutions).
- **Right to Vote** in elections to the **Lok Sabha** and **State Legislative Assemblies**.
- **Right to Contest** for membership of Parliament and State Legislatures.
- **Eligibility for key public offices**, including:
  - President of India
  - Vice-President of India
  - Judges of Supreme Court and High Courts
  - Governor of States
  - Attorney General of India
  - Advocate General of States

> 💡 **Important Comparison — India vs. USA:**
> In **India**, *both* citizens by birth *and* naturalised citizens can become **President**.
> In the **USA**, *only* a citizen by birth can become President — a naturalised citizen cannot.

---

### Duties of Citizens

Along with rights, citizens also owe duties to the Indian State, such as:
- Paying taxes
- Respecting the National Flag and National Anthem
- Defending the country

## PART 2: Single Citizenship — India's Unique Choice

### What is Single Citizenship?

Even though the Indian Constitution is **federal** (power shared between Centre and States) and creates a **dual polity** (Centre + States), it provides for only **one citizenship — Indian citizenship**.

- Citizens owe allegiance **only to the Union of India**.
- There is **no separate state citizenship**.

### India vs. USA/Australia on Citizenship

| Feature | India | USA / Australia |
|---|---|---|
| Type | **Single Citizenship** | **Double Citizenship** |
| Allegiance | Only to the Union | To both the nation AND the state |
| Sets of Rights | One uniform set | Two sets (national + state) |
| Risk of Discrimination | Minimised | States may favour their own residents |

> **The Core Problem with Double Citizenship:** In the USA, a state can discriminate *in favour of its own residents* in matters like voting, holding public offices, and practicing professions. India's single citizenship system **avoids this problem**.

---

### Is India's Single Citizenship Perfectly Uniform?

**No.** There are **4 important exceptions** where some differentiation is permitted:

**Exception 1 — Residence for Certain Jobs (Article 16)**
- Parliament *can* prescribe residence within a state as a condition for certain (non-Gazetted) government jobs.
- The **Public Employment (Requirement as to Residence) Act, 1957** allowed this for Andhra Pradesh, Himachal Pradesh, Manipur, and Tripura.
- This Act expired in 1974. Today, only **Andhra Pradesh** (under Article 371-D, inserted by the 32nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1973) and **Telangana** (under the Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2014) retain this provision.

**Exception 2 — State Benefits for Residents (Article 15)**
- Article 15 bans discrimination based on religion, race, caste, sex, or place of *birth* — but **NOT on place of residence**.
- So, a state *can* give benefits (like **fee concessions in education**) to its own residents. This is allowed.

**Exception 3 — Protection of Tribal Areas (Article 19)**
- The freedom of movement and residence can be **restricted for outsiders** entering tribal areas.
- This is done to protect the distinctive **culture, language, customs, manners, traditional vocations, and property** of Scheduled Tribes from exploitation.

**Exception 4 — Jammu & Kashmir (Article 35-A) [Till 2019]**
- *Until 2019*, the J&K legislature could define "permanent residents" of the state and give them special rights related to:
  - Employment under the state government
  - Acquisition of immovable property in the state
  - Settlement in the state
  - Right to scholarships and state-provided aid
- This was based on **Article 35-A**, inserted by "**The Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order, 1954**" under **Article 370**.
- **In 2019**, "**The Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order, 2019**" was issued, which **superseded** the 1954 order and **abolished** this special status entirely.
- Additionally, the **Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019** bifurcated the former state into two **Union Territories**: *(i) UT of Jammu & Kashmir* and *(ii) UT of Ladakh*.

---

### Why Single Citizenship? And Has it Worked?

- India (like **Canada**) chose single citizenship to promote **fraternity** (a sense of brotherhood) and **unity**, and to build an **integrated Indian nation**.
- However, India has still witnessed **communal riots, class conflicts, caste wars, linguistic clashes, and ethnic disputes**.
- The goal of the founding fathers — a truly united and integrated India — has **not been fully realised** yet.

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> The scrapping of **Article 370** and **Article 35-A** in **August 2019** was a massive step toward enforcing true single citizenship across India. The Supreme Court of India upheld the constitutional validity of this abrogation in **December 2023** (*Dr. Shah Faesal vs. Union of India & connected cases*), ruling that Article 370 was a temporary provision and its revocation was valid.

## PART 3: Constitutional Provisions on Citizenship (Articles 5–11)

### What Does the Constitution Actually Say?

The Constitution deals with citizenship in **Part II, Articles 5 to 11**. However:
- It contains **no permanent or elaborate provisions**.
- It only identifies who became citizens at **commencement (January 26, 1950)**.
- It does **NOT** deal with acquisition or loss of citizenship *after* that date.
- It empowers **Parliament** to make laws on citizenship.
- Parliament responded by enacting the **Citizenship Act, 1955**.

---

### Who Became Citizens on January 26, 1950? (4 Categories)

**Category 1 — Persons Domiciled in India:**
A person with **domicile** (permanent home) in India who also met *any one* of these:
- Was born in India; OR
- Either parent was born in India; OR
- Had been ordinarily resident in India for **five years** before January 26, 1950.

**Category 2 — Migrants FROM Pakistan:**
A person who migrated *from* Pakistan to India who was born in *undivided India* (or whose parents/grandparents were), AND:
- Migrated **before July 19, 1948** → must have been ordinarily resident in India since the date of migration; OR
- Migrated **on or after July 19, 1948** → must have registered as an Indian citizen (after 6 months of residence).
> *Note: July 19, 1948 is the date when the permit system for migration was introduced.*

**Category 3 — Persons Who Migrated TO Pakistan but Returned:**
- Left India for Pakistan after **March 1, 1947**, but later came back for resettlement.
- Had to be resident in India for **six months** before applying for registration.
- *(This covers migration after March 1, 1947 but before January 26, 1950. Post-1950 cases are handled by the Citizenship Act, 1955.)*

**Category 4 — Overseas Indians:**
- A person (or their parents/grandparents) born in undivided India but **ordinarily residing outside India**.
- Could become a citizen by registering at an **Indian diplomatic or consular mission** in their country of residence.

---

### Other Key Constitutional Rules

- A person who **voluntarily acquires** the citizenship of a foreign state shall **NOT** be an Indian citizen (Article 9).
- Every existing citizen shall **continue** to be a citizen, subject to any law Parliament may make (Article 10).
- **Parliament** has the power to make laws on acquisition, termination, and all other matters related to citizenship (Article 11).

---

### Table: Articles 5–11 at a Glance

| Article No. | Subject Matter |
|---|---|
| **Art. 5** | Citizenship at the commencement of the Constitution |
| **Art. 6** | Rights of citizenship of persons who migrated from Pakistan |
| **Art. 7** | Rights of citizenship of certain migrants to Pakistan |
| **Art. 8** | Rights of citizenship of persons of Indian origin residing outside India |
| **Art. 9** | Persons voluntarily acquiring foreign citizenship cease to be Indian citizens |
| **Art. 10** | Continuance of the rights of citizenship |
| **Art. 11** | Parliament to regulate the right of citizenship by law |

## PART 4: The Citizenship Act, 1955

This Act, amended multiple times, governs everything about citizenship *after* January 26, 1950.

> **Note:** The Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2003 **repealed** all provisions relating to **Commonwealth Citizenship** from the Principal Act.

---

## A. ACQUISITION OF CITIZENSHIP (5 Ways + 2 Special Provisions)

### 1. 🎂 By Birth

| Date of Birth | Condition for Citizenship |
|---|---|
| **Jan 26, 1950 – Jun 30, 1987** | Citizen by birth **irrespective** of parents' nationality |
| **Jul 1, 1987 – Dec 2, 2004** | At least **one parent** must be a citizen of India at the time of birth |
| **Dec 3, 2004 onwards** | **Both parents** must be citizens, OR one parent is a citizen and the other is **not an illegal migrant** |

> ⚠️ **Exceptions:** Children of **foreign diplomats** posted in India and **enemy aliens** cannot acquire citizenship by birth.

---

### 2. 🧬 By Descent

| Date of Birth | Condition |
|---|---|
| **Jan 26, 1950 – Dec 9, 1992** | **Father** must be a citizen of India at time of birth |
| **Dec 10, 1992 onwards** | **Either parent** must be a citizen of India at time of birth |
| **From Dec 3, 2004 onwards** | Birth must be **registered at an Indian consulate within one year** (or with Central Government permission after that) |

**Additional Rules (from Dec 3, 2004):**
- Parents must submit an **undertaking in writing** that the child does not hold a passport of another country.
- A **minor** who is a citizen by descent but *also* a citizen of another country must **renounce** the other citizenship within **6 months of attaining full age (18)**, or they lose Indian citizenship.

---

### 3. 📝 By Registration

The Central Government can register any person (**not being an illegal migrant**) as a citizen if they fall in one of these categories:

1. **Person of Indian Origin** (PIO — *person whose parents/grandparents were from India*) ordinarily resident in India for **7 years** before applying.
2. **PIO** ordinarily resident in any country outside undivided India.
3. Person **married to an Indian citizen** and ordinarily resident in India for **7 years** before applying.
4. **Minor children** of Indian citizens.
5. Person of full age and capacity whose **parents are registered** as Indian citizens.
6. Person of full age and capacity who (or either of whose parents) was earlier a citizen of **independent India**, and is ordinarily resident in India for **12 months** before applying.
7. Person of full age and capacity registered as an **OCI Cardholder for 5 years** and ordinarily resident in India for **12 months** before applying.

> ✅ **All such persons must take an Oath of Allegiance** before registration.
> *Oath: "I, A/B… do solemnly affirm (or swear) that I will bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India as by law established, and that I will faithfully observe the laws of India and fulfil my duties as a citizen of India."*

> A person is deemed to be of **Indian origin** if they, or either of their parents, was born in **undivided India** or in a territory that became part of India after **August 15, 1947**.

---

### 4. 🌐 By Naturalisation

**Naturalisation** (process of granting citizenship to a long-term resident foreigner) is granted by the Central Government if the person meets **all** of the following qualifications:

1. Their home country does **not** prevent Indian citizens from being naturalised there.
2. If they are a citizen of another country, they undertake to **renounce** that citizenship upon acceptance.
3. Has **resided in India or served in a Government in India** for the **12 months immediately preceding** the application.
4. In the **14 years before** those 12 months, has resided in India or served an Indian Government for a total of at least **11 years**.

> 🚨 **CAA Exception:** The **Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019** reduced this aggregate requirement to **5 years** for persons belonging to **Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, or Christian communities** from **Afghanistan, Bangladesh, or Pakistan**.

5. Is of **good character**.
6. Has adequate knowledge of a **language in the 8th Schedule** of the Constitution *(the 8th Schedule currently recognises 22 languages, originally 14)*.
7. Intends to **reside in India** or work for an Indian Government/international organisation/company after naturalisation.

> 🏆 **Special Waiver:** The Government of India **may waive ALL conditions** for a person who has rendered **distinguished service** to science, philosophy, art, literature, world peace, or human progress.

> ✅ Every naturalised citizen must also take the **Oath of Allegiance**.

---

### 5. 🗺️ By Incorporation of Territory

- If a **foreign territory becomes part of India**, the Government of India specifies which people of that territory become Indian citizens, from a notified date.
- **Example:** When **Pondicherry** became part of India, the Government issued the **Citizenship (Pondicherry) Order, 1962** under the Citizenship Act, 1955.

---

### 6. 🤝 Special Provisions — Assam Accord (Citizenship Amendment Act, 1985)

These provisions relate to foreigners who came to Assam from Bangladesh:

- **Before January 1, 1966:** Persons of Indian origin who came from Bangladesh to Assam *before* this date and have been ordinarily resident since are deemed **citizens of India from January 1, 1966**.

- **January 1, 1966 – March 24, 1971:** Persons of Indian origin who came from Bangladesh in this period, have been ordinarily resident in Assam, and were detected as foreigners must **register themselves**. They become full citizens **after 10 years** from the date of detection as a foreigner.
  - During the 10-year period: They have all rights of Indian citizens *except* the **right to vote**.

---

### 7. ⭐ Special Provisions — CAA 2019 (Afghanistan, Bangladesh, Pakistan)

The **Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019** added special provisions for migrants belonging to **Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, or Christian** communities from **Afghanistan, Bangladesh, or Pakistan** who entered India **on or before December 31, 2014**.

**Key Provisions:**

1. The Central Government may grant a **certificate of registration or naturalisation** to such a person. They shall be deemed a citizen of India **from the date of their entry into India**.
2. Any **pending legal proceedings** against such a person for illegal migration or citizenship shall stand **abated** (cancelled) upon conferment of citizenship.
3. These provisions do **NOT apply** to:
   - Tribal areas of **Assam, Meghalaya, Mizoram, or Tripura** (covered under the **Sixth Schedule**).
   - Areas covered under "**The Inner Line**" notified under the **Bengal Eastern Frontier Regulation, 1873**.

**Background Timeline:**
- **Before CAA 2019:** These migrants (who entered without valid documents) were considered **illegal migrants** and were *ineligible* for citizenship.
- **2015:** Central Government **exempted** them from penal consequences under the Passport (Entry into India) Act, 1920 and the Foreigners Act, 1946.
- **2016:** Central Government made them eligible for a **long-term visa**.
- **January 10, 2020:** CAA, 2019 came into **force**.

> 🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> The CAA, 2019 triggered widespread national debate and protests (the "Anti-CAA movement"). The **Supreme Court of India** has admitted multiple petitions challenging its constitutional validity. As of 2024, the case is still pending before a Constitution Bench. Meanwhile, the government notified the **Citizenship (Amendment) Rules, 2024** in March 2024, finally operationalising the CAA after a 4-year delay. The rules specify the online application process for eligible migrants.

## B. LOSS OF CITIZENSHIP (3 Ways)

### 1. ✋ By Renunciation (Voluntary Act)

- Any citizen of India of **full age and capacity** can declare renunciation of Indian citizenship.
- Upon **registration of that declaration**, they cease to be an Indian citizen.
- **Exception during war:** If India is engaged in a war, registration of such a declaration shall be **withheld** by the Central Government.
- **Effect on minor children:** When a person renounces citizenship, every **minor child** of that person also **loses Indian citizenship**.
  - However, when such a child turns **18**, they may resume Indian citizenship **within one year**.

---

### 2. 🔄 By Termination (Automatic)

- When an Indian citizen **voluntarily** (consciously, knowingly, without duress, undue influence, or compulsion) acquires citizenship of **another country**, their Indian citizenship **automatically terminates**.
- This provision does **NOT apply during a war** in which India is engaged.

---

### 3. ❌ By Deprivation (Compulsory — Government Initiated)

The Central Government can forcibly terminate citizenship if any of these 5 conditions are met:

1. Citizenship was obtained by **fraud**.
2. The citizen has shown **disloyalty to the Constitution of India**.
3. The citizen has **unlawfully traded or communicated with the enemy** during a war.
4. The citizen has been **imprisoned for 2 years** in any country within **5 years** after registration or naturalisation.
5. The citizen has been **ordinarily resident outside India for 7 years continuously**.

> ⚠️ **Exception to #5:** This will NOT apply if the person is: (a) a student abroad, (b) in service of an Indian Government or an international organisation of which India is a member, or (c) has **registered annually at an Indian consulate** their intention to retain Indian citizenship.

## PART 5: Overseas Citizenship of India (OCI)

### Background — How Did OCI Come About?

- **September 2000:** The Government of India set up a **High Level Committee on the Indian Diaspora** *(diaspora = communities living outside their homeland)* under the chairmanship of **L.M. Singhvi**.
- **Mandate:** Conduct a comprehensive study of the global Indian Diaspora and recommend measures for a constructive relationship with them.
- **January 2002:** Committee submitted its report, recommending amendment of the Citizenship Act, 1955, to provide **dual citizenship** to Persons of Indian Origin (PIOs) of certain specified countries.

### Legislative Journey of OCI

| Year | Amendment | Key Change |
|---|---|---|
| **2003** | Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2003 | Introduced OCI for PIOs of **16 specified countries** (excluding Pakistan & Bangladesh). Also removed Commonwealth Citizenship provisions. |
| **2005** | Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2005 | Expanded OCI to PIOs of **all countries** (except Pakistan & Bangladesh) whose home countries allow dual citizenship. |
| **2015** | Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2015 | **Merged** the PIO Card Scheme and OCI Card Scheme into one: "**Overseas Citizen of India Cardholder**". PIO scheme rescinded from **January 9, 2015**. All existing PIO cardholders deemed OCI cardholders from that date. |
| **2019** | Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 | Added a 5th ground for cancellation of OCI card (violation of the Citizenship Act or any other specified law) and introduced the right to be heard before cancellation. |

> ⚠️ **Important Clarification:** OCI is **NOT** dual citizenship. The Indian Constitution **forbids dual citizenship** (Article 9). OCI is essentially a **long-term multi-entry visa** with some added privileges.

---

### I. Who Can Register as an OCI Cardholder?

The Central Government may register:

**Category A — Person of full age and capacity who:**
1. Was a citizen of India at the time of, or at any time after, the commencement of the Constitution; OR
2. Was eligible to become a citizen of India at the commencement of the Constitution; OR
3. Belonged to a territory that became part of India after **August 15, 1947**; OR
4. Is a **child, grandchild, or great-grandchild** of such a citizen.

**Category B:** A minor child of any person in Category A.

**Category C:** A minor child whose **both parents are Indian citizens** OR one parent is an Indian citizen.

**Category D:** A **spouse of foreign origin** of an Indian citizen OR spouse of foreign origin of an existing OCI Cardholder, whose marriage has been **registered** and has **subsisted for at least 2 continuous years** before the application.

> ❌ **Who is INELIGIBLE?** Any person who (or either of whose parents/grandparents/great-grandparents) **is or has been a citizen of Pakistan, Bangladesh**, or any other country specified by the Central Government.

---

### II. Rights & Privileges of OCI Cardholders

**What OCI Cardholders ARE entitled to:**

1. **Multiple entry, lifelong visa** for visiting India for any purpose.
   - *Special permission/permit required for:* Research, Missionary/Tabligh/Mountaineering/Journalistic activities, internship in foreign Diplomatic Missions in India, or visiting Protected/Restricted/Prohibited areas.
2. **Exemption from registration** with FRRO/FRO for any length of stay in India.
3. **Parity with Indian nationals** in:
   - Domestic air fare tariffs.
   - Entry fees for national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, national monuments, historical sites, and museums.
4. **Parity with Non-Resident Indians (NRIs)** in:
   - Inter-country adoption of Indian children.
   - Appearing for all-India entrance tests like **NEET, JEE (Mains/Advanced)** — but only against **NRI seats or supernumerary seats** (NOT seats reserved for Indian citizens).
   - Purchase or sale of immovable property (other than agricultural land, farmhouse, or plantation property).
   - Pursuing professions in India: **doctors, dentists, nurses, pharmacists, advocates, architects, and chartered accountants**.
5. In all other economic, financial, and educational fields not covered above, OCI Cardholders have the **same rights as a foreigner**.
6. Eligible for **teaching faculty appointments** in **IITs, NITs, IIMs, IISERs, IISc, Central Universities**, and new AIIMS set up under **Pradhan Mantri Swasthya Suraksha Yojana**.

---

**What OCI Cardholders are NOT entitled to:**

1. Right to equality of opportunity in **public employment** (Article 16).
2. Eligibility for election as **President** (Article 58).
3. Eligibility for election as **Vice-President** (Article 66).
4. Eligibility for appointment as **Judge of the Supreme Court** (Article 124).
5. Eligibility for appointment as **Judge of a High Court** (Article 217).
6. Right to be **registered as a voter**.
7. Eligibility to be a member of **Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha**.
8. Eligibility to be a member of a **State Legislative Assembly or State Legislative Council**.
9. Appointment to **public services and posts** (Union or State) — except those specifically permitted by the Central Government.

---

### III. Renunciation of OCI Card

- An OCI Cardholder can declare renunciation of their OCI status.
- Upon registration of the declaration by the Central Government, they cease to be an OCI Cardholder.
- **Effect:** The foreign-origin spouse and every minor child of that person (who had OCI cards) also **cease to be OCI Cardholders**.

---

### IV. Cancellation of OCI Registration

The Central Government can cancel OCI registration if:

1. It was obtained by **fraud, false representation, or concealment** of material facts.
2. The OCI Cardholder has shown **disaffection towards the Constitution of India**.
3. They unlawfully **traded or communicated with an enemy** during a war.
4. They were **sentenced to imprisonment for 2+ years** within five years after registration.
5. They **violated provisions of the Citizenship Act, 1955** or any other specified law. *(Added by CAA 2019)*
6. It is necessary in the interests of **sovereignty and integrity of India, security of India, friendly relations with foreign countries, or interests of the general public**.
7. Their **marriage has been dissolved** by a court or they have solemnised **another marriage** while the first marriage subsists. *(Added by CAA 2019)*

> ✅ **Right to be Heard:** Before cancellation, the OCI Cardholder must be given a **reasonable opportunity of being heard**. *(Added by CAA 2019)*

## PART 6: NRI vs. PIO vs. OCI — Quick Comparison Table

| # | Feature | NRI | PIO | OCI Cardholder |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Who?** | Indian citizen residing outside India, holding Indian passport | Person (or whose ancestors were) Indian national, holding *foreign* passport | Person registered as OCI Cardholder under Citizenship Act, 1955 |
| 2 | **Visa needed for India?** | ❌ No | ✅ Yes | ❌ No (OCI card = lifelong multi-entry visa) |
| 3 | **Register with police?** | ❌ No | ✅ Yes, if stay > 180 days | ❌ No |
| 4 | **Path to Indian Citizenship** | Already an Indian citizen | Resident in India for **7 years**, then apply for registration | OCI Cardholder for **5 years** + resident in India for **12 months**, then apply |
| 5 | **Specific benefits?** | All benefits as Indian citizens | No specific benefits | Multiple defined benefits (see above) |
