## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 things you MUST remember from this chapter.**

1. **M.N. Roy** first proposed a Constituent Assembly in **1934**; the British formally accepted the demand via the **'August Offer' of 1940**.
2. The Constituent Assembly was set up under the **Cabinet Mission Plan (1946)** with an original strength of **389 members**.
3. The **Drafting Committee**, chaired by **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar**, was the most critical committee — it prepared the actual text of the Constitution.
4. The Constitution was **adopted on November 26, 1949** (now celebrated as **Constitution Day**) and **enforced on January 26, 1950** (Republic Day).
5. The final Constitution had a **Preamble, 395 Articles, and 8 Schedules**. A total of **284 members** actually signed it.

## 📌 Section 1: The Demand for a Constituent Assembly

### How the Idea Was Born — A Timeline

The journey from a simple idea to a fully functioning Constituent Assembly took over a decade. Here is how it unfolded step by step:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1934** | **M.N. Roy** (pioneer of the communist movement in India) first proposed the idea of a Constituent Assembly for India. |
| **1935** | The **Indian National Congress (INC)** officially demanded a Constituent Assembly for the first time. |
| **1938** | **Jawaharlal Nehru**, on behalf of the INC, declared that the Constitution of free India must be framed *without outside interference*, by an assembly elected on the basis of **adult franchise** (the right of every adult citizen to vote). |
| **1939** | The INC passed a formal resolution reiterating and endorsing Nehru's declaration. |
| **1940** | The British Government finally accepted the demand **in principle** through the **'August Offer' of 1940**. |
| **1942** | **Sir Stafford Cripps** (a Cabinet Member) came to India with a British draft proposal for framing an independent Constitution after World War II. The **Cripps Proposals** were rejected by the **Muslim League**, which wanted India divided into two states with two separate Constituent Assemblies. |
| **1946** | A **Cabinet Mission** was sent to India. It rejected the idea of two assemblies but put forward a scheme that largely satisfied the Muslim League. |

> 💡 **Quick Recall:** The sequence is Roy → INC → Nehru → August Offer → Cripps → Cabinet Mission.

## 📌 Section 2: Composition of the Constituent Assembly

The Constituent Assembly was **formally constituted in November 1946** under the **Cabinet Mission Plan**.

### Key Features of the Scheme

- **📊 Total Strength: 389 members**
  - **296 seats** → British Indian Provinces
    - 292 from **eleven Governors' Provinces** *(Madras, Bombay, UP, Bihar, Central Provinces, Orissa, Punjab, NWFP, Sindh, Bengal and Assam)*
    - 4 from **four Chief Commissioners' Provinces** *(Delhi, Ajmer–Merwara, Coorg and British Baluchistan)*, one each
  - **93 seats** → Princely States (Indian States)

- **📐 Proportional Representation:** Each province and princely state was allotted seats based on population — roughly **one seat per million people**.

- **🏘️ Community-wise Division:** Seats for each British province were divided among **three communities**:
  1. **Muslims**
  2. **Sikhs**
  3. **General** (everyone except Muslims and Sikhs)

- **🗳️ Election Method:** Representatives of each community were elected by members of the **provincial legislative assemblies**, using the method of **proportional representation by single transferable vote** (a voting system where voters rank candidates in order of preference to ensure fair representation).

- **👑 Princely States:** Representatives of princely states were **nominated** (not elected) by the heads of those states.

> ⚠️ The Assembly was thus **partly elected and partly nominated**. Furthermore, election was **indirect** — members were chosen by provincial assemblies, which themselves were elected on a **limited franchise** (the Government of India Act of 1935 granted the right to vote only based on tax, property, and other qualifications — not to everyone).

## 📌 Section 3: Working of the Constituent Assembly

### Key Office-Bearers

- **December 9, 1946** → First meeting of the Constituent Assembly (only **207 members** attended; the Muslim League **boycotted** it, demanding a separate Pakistan).
- **Temporary Chairman:** **Dr. Sachchidananda Sinha** (the oldest member), following the **French practice** of having the senior-most member chair the first session.
- **December 11, 1946** → **Dr. Rajendra Prasad** elected as the permanent **President (Chairman)** of the Assembly.
- **January 25, 1947** → **H.C. Mukherjee** elected as the **Vice-President**.
- **July 16, 1947** → **V.T. Krishnamachari** elected as the **second Vice-President** (after the Assembly resolved to have two Vice-Presidents).

---

### The Objectives Resolution — The Soul of the Constitution

On **December 13, 1946**, **Jawaharlal Nehru** moved the historic **'Objectives Resolution'** in the Assembly. Think of this as the *mission statement* of the Constitution-making process. It laid out the fundamental philosophy of the constitutional structure.

The Resolution declared that India shall:

1. Be an **Independent Sovereign Republic**.
2. Be a **Union** of all territories — British India, Indian States, and other willing territories.
3. Have all units with **autonomous powers** (self-governing powers) plus residuary powers (leftover powers not assigned to anyone), except for powers assigned to the Union.
4. Derive all power from the **people**.
5. Guarantee to all people: **Justice** (social, economic, political), **Equality** of status and opportunity, and **Freedom** of thought, expression, belief, faith, worship, vocation, association, and action.
6. Provide **adequate safeguards** for minorities, backward and tribal areas, and depressed/backward classes.
7. Maintain the **integrity of the territory** of the Republic and its sovereign rights on land, sea and air.
8. Enable India to take its rightful place in the world and contribute to **world peace and the welfare of mankind**.

> ✅ The Resolution was **unanimously adopted on January 22, 1947**.
> Its **modified version forms the Preamble** of the present Constitution of India.

## 📌 Section 4: Changes Brought by the Indian Independence Act, 1947

### Princely States Join In

- On **April 28, 1947**, representatives of **six states** — Baroda, Bikaner, Jaipur, Patiala, Rewa, and Udaipur — joined the Assembly.
- After the **Mountbatten Plan (June 3, 1947)** was accepted for the partition of the country, most other princely states also sent their representatives.
- Muslim League members from the Indian Dominion also joined the Assembly.

### Three Major Changes Made by the Indian Independence Act, 1947

**Change 1 — Fully Sovereign Body:**
The Assembly became a **fully sovereign body** (completely independent, supreme power). It could frame any Constitution it wished, and was empowered to change or cancel any law made by the British Parliament concerning India.

**Change 2 — Also a Legislative Body:**
The Assembly was now given **two functions**:
  - Drafting the Constitution
  - Passing ordinary laws for the country

These two roles were performed on separate days:
  - As **Constituent body** → chaired by **Dr. Rajendra Prasad**
  - As **Legislative body (Dominion Legislature)** → chaired by **G.V. Mavalankar** *(The Assembly first met as Dominion Legislature on November 17, 1947, and elected G.V. Mavalankar as its Speaker)*

This dual function continued until **November 26, 1949**, when the Constitution-making work was completed.

**Change 3 — Reduced Membership:**
Muslim League members from areas that went to Pakistan — West Punjab, East Bengal, NWFP, Sindh, Baluchistan, and Sylhet District of Assam — **withdrew** from the Indian Constituent Assembly. A separate Constituent Assembly was set up for Pakistan.

As a result:
- Total strength **fell from 389 to 299**
- Indian Provinces: from 296 → **229**
- Princely States: from 93 → **70**

## 📌 Section 8: Enactment of the Constitution

### The Three Readings

| Stage | Date | What Happened |
|-------|------|---------------|
| **First Reading** | November 4, 1948 | Dr. B.R. Ambedkar introduced the final draft. General discussion for 5 days (till November 9, 1948). |
| **Second Reading** | November 15, 1948 – October 17, 1949 | Clause-by-clause consideration. **7,653 amendments proposed**; **2,473 actually discussed**. |
| **Third Reading** | November 14, 1949 | Dr. Ambedkar moved the motion — *"the Constitution as settled by the Assembly be passed."* |

- **November 26, 1949** → Constitution **declared as passed**. Members and the President **signed** it.
- Out of 299 members, only **284 were present** and signed on that day.
- This date is mentioned in the **Preamble** as the day the people of India adopted, enacted, and gave themselves this Constitution.

### What the Constitution Looked Like on Adoption

- **Preamble** (enacted *after* the entire Constitution was already enacted)
- **395 Articles**
- **8 Schedules**

> 🔴 **Key Fact:** The **Preamble was enacted last**, even though it appears first in the document.

---

### Dr. B.R. Ambedkar — Father of the Constitution

- He was the **Law Minister** at the time and piloted (guided) the Draft Constitution through the Assembly.
- Known for his **logical, forceful, and persuasive** arguments on the floor.
- Officially recognised as the **'Father of the Constitution of India'** and the **'Chief Architect of the Constitution of India'**.
- A brilliant writer, constitutional expert, and undisputed leader of the Scheduled Castes.

### Constitution Day

> On **November 19, 2015**, the Government of India decided to celebrate **November 26** every year as **"Constitution Day" ("Samvidhan Divas")** to promote constitutional values among citizens.

## 📌 Section 9: Enforcement of the Constitution

### Two Dates to Remember

**Date 1 — November 26, 1949** *(Adoption Date)*
Some specific provisions came into force immediately on this date. These were provisions relating to:
- Citizenship
- Elections
- Provisional Parliament
- Temporary and Transitional provisions
- Short title

Specifically, **Articles 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 60, 324, 366, 367, 379, 380, 388, 391, 392, and 393** came into force on this date.

**Date 2 — January 26, 1950** *(Commencement Date / Republic Day)*
The **remaining and major part** of the Constitution came into force on this date. This is what the Constitution calls its **'date of commencement'**.

### Why January 26?

January 26 was **deliberately chosen** because of its historical importance.

- It was on **January 26, 1930**, that **Purna Swaraj Day** (Complete Independence Day) was first celebrated.
- This was a direct follow-up to the **Lahore Session (December 1929)** of the INC, which passed the famous Purna Swaraj resolution.

### What Was Repealed?

When the Constitution came into force, the following were **repealed (cancelled)**:
- The **Indian Independence Act of 1947**
- The **Government of India Act of 1935** (along with all laws amending or supplementing it)

> ✅ **Exception:** The **Abolition of Privy Council Jurisdiction Act (1949)** was **continued** (not repealed).

## 📌 Section 11: Hindi Text of the Constitution

Originally, the Constitution **had no provision** for an authoritative (officially recognised) Hindi text.

This gap was filled by the **58th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1987**, which inserted a **new Article 394-A** in **Part XXII** (the last part) of the Constitution.

### What Article 394-A Provides:

1. **The President shall cause to be published:**
   - A Hindi translation of the full Constitution (with modifications only to match language, style, and terminology of central Acts in Hindi, and including all amendments made before publication).
   - A Hindi translation of every future amendment to the Constitution.

2. **Legal standing:** If the Hindi text and the English text conflict in meaning, the **Hindi text shall be construed to have the same meaning** as the English original. If difficulty persists, the President shall cause the Hindi text to be **revised suitably**.

3. **Authoritative status:** The published Hindi translation shall be treated as the **authoritative text of the Constitution in Hindi** for all purposes.

## 📌 Section 12: Criticisms of the Constituent Assembly

The Constituent Assembly has been criticised on several grounds. Let's look at each criticism honestly:

### 1. 🔴 Not a Representative Body
**Criticism:** Members were NOT directly elected by the people through universal adult franchise (right of every citizen to vote regardless of wealth or status). They were elected indirectly, by provincial assemblies that themselves had limited voter bases.

### 2. 🔴 Not a Sovereign Body
**Criticism:** The Assembly was created by proposals of the British Government and held sessions with British permission — it was not fully independent from the start.

### 3. 🔴 Time Consuming
**Criticism:** It took about **3 years** to draft the Constitution, while the framers of other constitutions worked much faster.

| Country | Articles | Time Taken |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **U.S.A.** | 7 | Less than **4 months** (May 25 – September 17, 1787) |
| **Canada** | 147 | About **2.5 years** (October 1864 – March 1867) |
| **Australia** | 128 | About **9 years** (March 1891 – July 1900) |
| **South Africa** | 153 | **1 year** (October 1908 – September 1909) |

> **Naziruddin Ahmed** (a member of the Constituent Assembly himself) humorously renamed the Drafting Committee the **"Drifting Committee"** to show his frustration with the delays.

### 4. 🔴 Dominated by Congress
**Criticism:** Congress won 208 out of 296 seats, making it effectively a **Congress-dominated** body.

> **Granville Austin** observed: *"The Constituent Assembly was a one-party body in an essentially one-party country. The Assembly was the Congress and the Congress was India."*

### 5. 🔴 Lawyer–Politician Domination
**Criticism:** Lawyers and politicians dominated the Assembly, with other sections of society (farmers, workers, industrialists) under-represented. Critics argue this explains why the Constitution is **bulky and uses complicated language**.

### 6. 🔴 Dominated by Hindus
**Criticism:** Some critics felt the Assembly was Hindu-dominated.
- **Lord Viscount Simon** called it *"a body of Hindus."*
- **Winston Churchill** commented it represented *"only one major community in India."*

## 🌍 Modern Context — Constitution and Its Living Legacy

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The 75th Anniversary of the Constitution (2024–25)
- India is marking **75 years of the Constitution** with large-scale awareness programs. The government's **"Hamara Samvidhan, Hamara Samman"** initiative encourages citizens to engage with constitutional values.

### 2. Recent Supreme Court Judgments on Constitutional Basics
- In the landmark **Basic Structure Doctrine** reaffirmation, the Supreme Court (in various recent judgments) has continued to uphold that Parliament cannot amend the Constitution in a way that destroys its **basic structure** — a concept rooted in the very philosophy of the Objectives Resolution that Nehru moved in 1946.

### 3. Preamble Reading in Schools `[Modern Example]`
- Following the 2015 Constitution Day declaration, the **Ministry of Education (formerly HRD)** has made it a regular practice for schools to read the **Preamble** on Constitution Day every year — directly linking back to the Objectives Resolution adopted by the Constituent Assembly.

### 4. Constitutional Values in Governance `[Modern Example]`
- Several recent government schemes like **PM Jan Dhan Yojana** (financial inclusion), **Right to Education Act**, and **National Food Security Act** are direct legislative expressions of the **social, economic, and political justice** promised in the Objectives Resolution of 1946 — showing how the Assembly's vision is still being implemented 75 years later.

### 5. Ambedkar Jayanti — April 14 `[Modern Example]`
- The Government of India observes **April 14** as **Ambedkar Jayanti** (also called **Equality Day**), formally recognising the 'Father of the Constitution.' In 2022, the government launched the **Dr. Ambedkar National Memorial** in New Delhi as a permanent tribute to his role in Constitution-making.
