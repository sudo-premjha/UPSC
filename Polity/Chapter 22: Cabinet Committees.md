## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways before you begin reading:**
>
> 1. **Cabinet Committees are extra-constitutional** — they are NOT mentioned in the Constitution but are established through the **Rules of Business**.
> 2. They are of **two types: Standing (permanent) and Ad Hoc (temporary)** — and their number, names, and composition change based on the Prime Minister's discretion.
> 3. Currently, **8 Cabinet Committees** are functional — the most powerful being the **Cabinet Committee on Political Affairs**, often called the **"Super-Cabinet"**.
> 4. **Groups of Ministers (GoMs)** are a separate but related mechanism — ad hoc bodies that either recommend or decide on specific issues on behalf of the Cabinet.
> 5. The **Second Administrative Reforms Commission (2005–2009)**, headed by **Veerappa Moily**, recommended **selective but time-bound use of GoMs** to prevent delays.

---

## 🔹 Section 1: What Are Cabinet Committees?

The **Cabinet** — the supreme executive body of India — has an enormous workload. To manage this effectively, it works through smaller, focused groups called **Cabinet Committees**.

Think of it this way: Just like a large company divides work among departments, the Cabinet divides its policy work among committees — each specialising in a specific area.

---

## 🔹 Section 2: Features of Cabinet Committees

### 1. 📜 Extra-Constitutional in Origin
- **Extra-constitutional** (not mentioned in the Constitution; created by rules/conventions) — Cabinet Committees are **NOT mentioned anywhere in the Constitution of India**.
- However, the **Rules of Business** (rules that govern how the Government of India functions) provide for their establishment.
- This means their existence is legally valid but constitutionally informal.

---

### 2. 🔄 Two Types: Standing and Ad Hoc

| Type | Nature | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Standing Committees** | Permanent | They exist continuously and deal with recurring subjects |
| **Ad Hoc Committees** | Temporary | Created for a specific purpose; dissolved once the task is complete |

> 📌 **Example:** The **Emergency Committee** was set up in **1962** after the **Chinese invasion** — a classic example of an ad hoc committee created for a special problem and then disbanded.

---

### 3. 🛠️ Set Up by the Prime Minister

- Cabinet Committees are set up by the **Prime Minister** based on:
  - The **exigencies of the time** (urgent needs of the moment)
  - The **requirements of the situation**
- Because of this, their:
  - **Number** (how many exist)
  - **Nomenclature** (what they are called)
  - **Composition** (who is in them)
  ...all **vary from time to time**.

---

### 4. 👥 Membership: Primarily Cabinet Ministers

- Membership varies from one committee to another.
- They **usually include only Cabinet Ministers** (senior ministers who attend Cabinet meetings).
- However:
  - **Non-cabinet ministers** *can* also be appointed as members.
  - Non-cabinet ministers can also **attend meetings as special invitees**.

---

### 5. 🎯 Includes Both Subject Ministers and Senior Ministers

- A Cabinet Committee does **not only include** the ministers in charge of the specific subject it covers.
- It **also includes other senior ministers** — bringing broader wisdom and cross-departmental coordination to the table.

---

### 6. 👑 Headed Mostly by the Prime Minister

- Most Cabinet Committees are **headed by the Prime Minister**.
- Sometimes, other **senior Cabinet Ministers** act as Chairman.
- **Crucial rule:** If the **Prime Minister is a member** of any committee, he/she **invariably presides over it** (always chairs it, no exceptions).

---

### 7. ⚖️ Decision-Making Powers + Cabinet Oversight

Cabinet Committees perform two functions:
- **Sort out issues and formulate proposals** for the Cabinet's consideration.
- **Take decisions directly** on their own.

> **Important check:** The **Cabinet can review the decisions** of Cabinet Committees — so they are not completely autonomous.

---

### 8. 📉 Reducing Workload + Enabling Depth

Cabinet Committees serve three organisational purposes:
- **Reduce the enormous workload** of the full Cabinet.
- **Facilitate in-depth examination** of specific policy issues.
- **Enable effective coordination** across ministries.

They are based on two management principles:
- **Division of Labour** (each committee handles a specific domain)
- **Effective Delegation** (power is delegated to smaller groups)

---

> Under the **Modi Government (2014–present)**, the number of Cabinet Committees has been **rationalised** (reduced and streamlined). The **Cabinet Committee on Investment and Growth** and the **Cabinet Committee on Employment and Skill Development** are relatively newer additions reflecting current economic priorities like the **Make in India** initiative and **Skill India Mission**. `[Modern Example]`

## 🔹 Section 4: Functions of Each Cabinet Committee

Let's understand what each committee actually does:

---

### 1. 🏛️ Cabinet Committee on Political Affairs
- Deals with **all policy matters** pertaining to:
  - **Domestic affairs** (internal political issues)
  - **Foreign affairs** (India's international relations and policies)
- Called the **"Super-Cabinet"** because of its wide scope and enormous influence over national policy direction.

---

### 2. 💰 Cabinet Committee on Economic Affairs
- **Directs and coordinates** all governmental activities in the **economic sphere**.
- Essentially steers India's economic policy at the highest executive level.

---

### 3. 📋 Appointments Committee of the Cabinet
- Decides all **higher-level appointments** in:
  - **Central Secretariat** (top bureaucratic positions)
  - **Public Enterprises** (government-owned companies)
  - **Banks**
  - **Financial Institutions**
- This is one of the most consequential committees — it controls who gets top government jobs.

---

### 4. 🏟️ Cabinet Committee on Parliamentary Affairs
- Looks after the **progress of government business in Parliament**.
- Manages the legislative agenda — what bills are introduced, when, and how they are scheduled.

---

### 5. 🔒 Cabinet Committee on Security
- Deals with all issues relating to:
  - **Defence**
  - **Law and Order**
  - **Internal Security**
  - **Atomic Energy**
- One of the most sensitive and secretive committees given its subject matter.

---

### 6. 🏠 Cabinet Committee on Accommodation
- Deals with all issues regarding the **allotment of government accommodation** to:
  - **Members of Parliament**
  - **Central Government Employees**
  - **Other Persons**
  - **Various Organisations**

---

### 7. 📈 Cabinet Committee on Investment and Growth
- Deals with all policy matters aimed at:
  - **Accelerating capital inflows** (attracting investment into India)
  - **Export promotion** (boosting India's exports)
  - **Import substitution** (replacing imports with domestic production)
  - **Improving Ease of Doing Business** (making it simpler to run businesses in India)

---

### 8. 👩‍💼 Cabinet Committee on Employment and Skill Development
- Deals with all issues related to:
  - **Developing skills** in the workforce
  - **Enhancing employability** of workforce
  - **Increasing women workforce participation**

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> The **Cabinet Committee on Security (CCS)** has been particularly active in recent years:
> - It approved the **Agnipath Scheme (2022)** — the short-term military recruitment scheme — which was a major defence policy decision.
> - All decisions related to **nuclear weapons, strategic assets, and cross-border military operations** go through the CCS.
> - The **Cabinet Committee on Investment and Growth** aligns directly with India's goal of becoming a **$5 trillion economy**, and its work connects to schemes like **PM Gati Shakti National Master Plan (2021)** and **Production Linked Incentive (PLI) Schemes**.
> - The **Appointments Committee of the Cabinet** became newsworthy when the Supreme Court, in **DK Nagpal vs. Union of India**, examined the boundaries of executive appointments — highlighting the committee's critical role. `[Modern Example]`

## 🔹 Section 5: Groups of Ministers (GoMs)

### What Are GoMs?

In addition to Cabinet Committees, the government also constitutes several **Groups of Ministers (GoMs)** to look into different issues or subjects.

**GoMs are:**
- **Ad hoc bodies** (temporary; formed as needed)
- Formed to give **recommendations to the Cabinet** on:
  - Emergent (urgent, sudden) issues
  - Critical problem areas

### Two Types of GoMs:

| Type | Power |
|---|---|
| **Empowered GoMs** | Can take **decisions on behalf of the Cabinet** directly |
| **Regular GoMs** | Can only **make recommendations** to the Cabinet for its approval |

### How Do GoMs Work?

1. A specific problem or issue arises.
2. **Ministers heading the concerned ministries** are inducted into the relevant GoM.
3. The GoM deliberates and **crystallises** (finalises) its advice or decision.
4. Once the work is done, the GoM is **disbanded** (dissolved).

### Why Are GoMs Important?

> *"The institution of GoMs has become a viable and effective instrument of coordination among the ministries."*

They bring together ministers from multiple departments who all have a stake in a particular issue — ensuring cross-ministry coordination before a final decision reaches the full Cabinet.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> GoMs have been used extensively across governments:
> - The **UPA Government (2004–2014)** constituted a large number of GoMs — sometimes leading to the very delays the Second ARC warned about.
> - In the **GST (Goods and Services Tax) implementation process**, multiple GoMs were formed to resolve disputes between states and the Centre on tax rates — a process that continued even after GST launched in **July 2017**.
> - As recently as **2022–23**, GoMs under the **GST Council** have been constituted to review tax rates on specific goods like online gaming, casinos, and horse racing — demonstrating that GoMs remain a live and active governance tool. `[Modern Example]`

## 🔹 Section 6: Second Administrative Reforms Commission (2005–2009) on GoMs

### Background

The **Second Administrative Reforms Commission of India (2005–2009)** — headed by **Veerappa Moily** (senior Congress leader and former Chief Minister of Karnataka) — studied the working of GoMs in detail and made critical observations.

*(Source: Report on Organisational Structure of Government of India, 2009, pp. 136–137 and 140)*

---

### Observations and Recommendations:

#### 🔴 Observation 1: Too Many GoMs = Delays
> The Commission observed that the **constitution of a large number of GoMs** has resulted in many GoMs **not being able to meet regularly** to complete their work — thus leading to **significant delays on many major issues**.

**Simple meaning:** When you create too many GoMs, they don't function effectively. They don't meet on time, work piles up, and important decisions get delayed.

---

#### 🟡 Observation 2: Selective Use = More Effective
> The Commission felt that **more selective use of the institution of GoMs** would perhaps lead to **more effective coordination** — particularly if they are **empowered to arrive at a decision on behalf of the Cabinet** with **time limits** prescribed for completing the work entrusted to them.

**Simple meaning:** Don't create GoMs for everything. Create fewer GoMs, give them real decision-making power, and set **strict deadlines** for them to finish their work.

---

#### 🟢 Recommendation 3: Clear Mandate + Time Limits
> The Commission recommended that there is need to ensure that the **existing coordination mechanism of GoMs functions effectively** and helps in **early resolution of issues**. **Selective, but effective use of GoMs** with **clear mandate** and **prescribed time limits** would be helpful.

**Simple meaning:** GoMs should have:
- A **clear mandate** (defined scope of what they must deal with)
- **Time limits** (deadlines for completing work)
- **Empowerment** to decide, not just recommend

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> The Second ARC's recommendations remain relevant today:
> - The **NITI Aayog**, established in **2015** (replacing the Planning Commission), has itself adopted a committee/sub-group approach with time-bound mandates — reflecting the spirit of ARC recommendations.
> - India's **Poor ranking on Ease of Doing Business** (before improvements) was partly attributed to delayed inter-ministerial coordination — exactly the problem the ARC identified with non-functional GoMs.
> - The **15th Finance Commission (2020–21)** worked with a clear mandate and fixed timeline — an example of the ARC's principles being followed in spirit. `[Modern Example]`

## 📊 Quick Comparison: Cabinet Committees vs. Groups of Ministers (GoMs)

| Feature | Cabinet Committees | Groups of Ministers (GoMs) |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Standing + Ad Hoc | Only Ad Hoc |
| **Constitutional Status** | Extra-constitutional | Extra-constitutional |
| **Created by** | Prime Minister | Cabinet/PM |
| **Membership** | Cabinet + Non-Cabinet Ministers | Ministers of concerned ministries |
| **Decision Power** | Yes (subject to Cabinet review) | Some empowered GoMs can decide; others only recommend |
| **Permanence** | Standing ones are permanent | Always temporary; disbanded after task |
| **Example** | Cabinet Committee on Security | GST-related GoMs |

## ⚠️ Note on Court Cases

> 📌 **This chapter does not cite any Supreme Court judgments or court cases directly.** The chapter is purely about the organisational and functional structure of Cabinet Committees and GoMs. Therefore, no case law analysis is applicable here.
>
> However, for broader context: `[Modern Example]`
> - The **Supreme Court in Subramanian Swamy vs. Union of India** and related cases has examined the **Appointments Committee of the Cabinet's** role in judicial appointments and the collegium system — though that is a separate constitutional debate.
