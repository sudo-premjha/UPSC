# 🏛️ CHAPTER 25: INDIAN PARLIAMENTARY GROUP (IPG)

---

## 📖 Why Does the IPG Exist? (The Rationale)

*As explained by M.N. Kaul and S.L. Shakdher in "Practice and Procedure of Parliament" (Lok Sabha Secretariat, 6th Edition, 2009, p. 1160):*

The world today is deeply interconnected. Problems that one country's parliament faces today may become another country's problem tomorrow. This **inter-dependence of nations** makes it essential for parliamentarians to:

- **Connect** with each other across borders
- **Safeguard democracy** jointly
- **Convert global challenges** into opportunities for peace and prosperity

To maintain this link, India exchanges delegations, goodwill missions, correspondence, and documents with foreign parliaments. The **machinery** that manages all this is the **IPG**.

The IPG serves a dual role:
1. As the **National Group of the Inter-Parliamentary Union (IPU)**
2. As the **India Branch of the Commonwealth Parliamentary Association (CPA)**

---

## 🏗️ Composition of the IPG

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Nature** | Autonomous body |
| **Year of Formation** | **1949** |
| **Founded via** | A motion adopted by the Constituent Assembly (Legislative) on **August 16, 1948** |
| **Membership** | Open to **all Members of Parliament** |
| **Associate Members** | **Former MPs** can become associate members |
| **President (Ex Officio)** | **Speaker of Lok Sabha** |
| **Vice-Presidents (Ex Officio)** | **Deputy Speaker of Lok Sabha** + **Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha** |
| **Secretary-General (Ex Officio)** | **Secretary-General of Lok Sabha** |

### ⚠️ Limitations on Associate Members
Associate members have **limited rights**. They are NOT entitled to:
- Representation at meetings and conferences of the **IPU** and **CPA**
- Travel concessions provided by certain branches of the CPA

> 📝 **Note:** A member or ex-member of Parliament can become a **life member** of the IPG by paying a fixed life subscription.

---

## 🎯 Objectives of the IPG

The IPG has four core aims and objects:

1. **Promote personal contact** between members of the Parliament of India.
2. **Study important public questions** likely to come before Parliament; arrange seminars, discussions, and orientation courses; and publish materials for the information of IPG members.
3. **Arrange lectures** on political, defence, economic, social, and educational problems by MPs and distinguished persons.
4. **Arrange visits to foreign countries** to develop contacts with members of other parliaments.

---

## ⚙️ Functions of the IPG

The IPG performs the following key functions:

1. **Bridge Role:** Acts as the link between India's Parliament and parliaments worldwide through exchange of delegations, goodwill missions, correspondence, and documents.

2. **Dual Institutional Role:** Functions as:
   - **(a)** The **National Group of the IPU**
   - **(b)** The **main branch of the CPA** in India

3. **Hosting Distinguished Visitors:** Arranges addresses to MPs by visiting **Heads of State and Government** of foreign countries, and talks by eminent persons.

4. **Knowledge Events:** Organises **seminars and symposia** on parliamentary subjects of topical interest at both national and international levels.

5. **Facilitating Travel for Members:** When IPG members visit abroad, they are given:
   - **Letters of introduction** to Secretaries of the National Groups of the IPU and CPA branches
   - Indian Missions in the countries being visited are also informed to provide assistance and courtesies.

6. **Delegation Eligibility Rule:** Only those MPs who are **members of the IPG for at least six months** at the time of composition of a delegation can be included in Indian Parliamentary delegations to foreign countries.

7. **IPG Newsletter:** An uninterrupted flow of information is maintained through the **IPG Newsletter**, published **every quarter**. It is sent to all IPG members, including associate members.

8. **Outstanding Parliamentarian Award:** This award was instituted in **1995**, to be given **annually**.
   - A **committee of five persons**, constituted by the **Speaker of Lok Sabha**, invites and finalises nominations.

---

## 🤝 Parliamentary Friendship Groups (PFGs)

To encourage **bilateral relations**, the IPG creates **Parliamentary Friendship Groups (PFGs)** with other countries within the Indian Parliament.

### Structure of PFGs
- **Members:** Sitting MPs from **both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha**
- **President:** Appointed by the **Speaker of Lok Sabha**
- **Management & Control:** Vests in the **IPG**
- **Meetings:** Held on the **sidelines of visiting Foreign Parliamentary Friendship Groups/Committees/Delegations**, within the **Parliament House complex**

### Aims & Objectives of PFGs

1. Maintain **political, economic, social and cultural contact** between two countries.
2. Create favourable conditions for **continuous development of inter-parliamentary contacts** — including talks, mutual exchanges, and cooperation between parliaments.
3. **Exchange information and experiences** on issues related to parliamentary activities.
4. **Promote cooperation between delegations** of the two countries in international organisations and on issues of mutual interest.
5. **Increase ties** between member countries.

---

## 🌐 The IPG and the IPU

### What is the IPU?
The **Inter-Parliamentary Union (IPU)** is an **international organisation of the parliaments of sovereign states**.

| IPU Fact | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Current Membership** | **179 parliaments** of sovereign nations |
| **Aim** | Work for **peace and cooperation** among peoples and for the firm establishment of **representative institutions** |

The IPU also:
- Fosters contacts, coordination, and exchange of experience among parliaments of all member countries
- Expresses views on burning international issues
- Suggests ways to improve the working standard of international institutions

### Benefits of IPG Membership (via IPU)

1. Helps Indian parliamentary delegations **develop contacts** with parliamentarians of IPU member countries.
2. Provides opportunity to **study contemporary changes and reforms** in various countries.
3. Allows members to **meet parliamentarians in different countries** during foreign tours.
4. Members are **eligible to visit foreign countries** as part of Indian delegations to Inter-Parliamentary Conferences.
5. IPG members have **held various positions** in IPU bodies — as office bearers in IPU committees, Rapporteurs, Chairman of Drafting Committees, etc. — helping India put forward its viewpoint effectively.

---

## 🌍 The IPG and the CPA

### What is the CPA?
The **Commonwealth Parliamentary Association (CPA)** is an association of approximately:
- **17,000 parliamentarians and parliamentary staff**
- Spread across **180 National, State, Provincial, and Territorial Parliaments and Legislatures**
- In **53 Commonwealth countries**

**CPA's Aim:** To promote knowledge and understanding of constitutional, legislative, economic, social, and cultural systems within a **parliamentary democratic framework**, with particular reference to Commonwealth countries and countries with close historical and parliamentary associations.

**CPA's Mission:** To promote the **advancement of parliamentary democracy** by enhancing knowledge of democratic governance and building an informed parliamentary community to deepen the Commonwealth's democratic commitment.

### Benefits of IPG Membership (via CPA)

1. **Conferences and Seminars:** Participation in plenary and regional conferences, seminars, visits, and exchanges of delegations.

2. **Publications:** All IPG members receive, **free of charge**:
   - *'The Parliamentarian'* — published **quarterly**
   - *'First Reading'* (newsletter) — published **every second month**
   *(Both published by the CPA Secretariat, London)*

3. **Information:** The **Parliamentary Information and Reference Centre** of the CPA Secretariat provides information on parliamentary, constitutional, and Commonwealth matters.

4. **Introductions:** CPA branches assist in arranging introductions for members visiting other jurisdictions.

5. **Parliamentary Facilities:** Members visiting other Commonwealth countries are normally accorded **parliamentary courtesies**, especially access to debates and local members.

6. **Travel Facilities:** Some branches provide a designated number of their members annually to undertake **study tours** of Commonwealth and other countries. Other branches arrange ad hoc visits.

# ⚖️ CHAPTER 26: SUPREME COURT OF INDIA

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance — Supreme Court

> **Key Takeaways:**
> 1. India has a **unified/integrated judicial system** — one system for both central and state laws.
> 2. The Supreme Court was inaugurated on **January 28, 1950**, succeeding the Federal Court of India.
> 3. The **Collegium System** governs judicial appointments after the NJAC was struck down in 2015.
> 4. The Supreme Court has **extensive jurisdiction** — wider than almost any other apex court in the world.
> 5. **Articles 124 to 147** in **Part V** of the Constitution deal with the Supreme Court.

---

## 🏗️ India's Judicial Structure: The Big Picture

### India vs. USA: Judicial Systems

| Feature | India | USA |
|---|---|---|
| **Type of System** | **Unified/Integrated** judicial system | **Double/Separated** judicial system |
| **Who enforces laws** | One court system enforces both Central and State laws | Federal judiciary for federal laws; State judiciary for state laws |
| **Origin** | Adopted from the **Government of India Act, 1935** | Embedded in the US Constitution |

**Key Point:** Even though India is a **federal country** like the USA, it has a **unified judiciary** and **one system of fundamental law and justice**.

---

## 📅 Establishment of the Supreme Court

- **Inaugurated on:** January 28, 1950
- **Succeeded:** The **Federal Court of India** (established under the Government of India Act of 1935)
- **Key Upgrade:** The Supreme Court replaced the **British Privy Council** as the highest court of appeal.
  - *(Before 1950, the British Privy Council had jurisdiction to hear appeals from India)*
- **Governing Articles:** **Articles 124 to 147** in **Part V** of the Constitution

---

## 👨‍⚖️ Composition and Appointment of Judges

### Current Strength of the Supreme Court

> **Present Strength: 34 judges** (1 Chief Justice + 33 other judges)

### Historical Progression of Judge Strength

| Year / Act | Max. Judges (Excluding CJI) | Total (Including CJI) |
|---|---|---|
| Article 124 of Constitution (Original) | 7 | **8** |
| Supreme Court (Number of Judges) Act, **1956** | 10 | 11 |
| Amendment Act, **1960** | 13 | 14 |
| Amendment Act, **1977** | 17 | 18 |
| Amendment Act, **1986** | 25 | 26 |
| Amendment Act, **2008** | 30 | 31 |
| Amendment Act, **2019** | 33 | **34** |

---

## 🔑 How Are Judges Appointed?

### The Basic Rule (Constitutional Provision)
- All judges of the Supreme Court are **appointed by the President**.
- **Chief Justice:** Appointed after consultation with such judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts as the President *deems necessary*.
- **Other Judges:** Appointed after consultation with the Chief Justice AND such other judges as the President *deems necessary*.
- **Consultation with CJI is OBLIGATORY** when appointing any judge other than the Chief Justice.

---

## ⚔️ THE JUDGES CASES: A Detailed Explanation

This is one of the most important and evolving areas of constitutional law. The word "consultation" in the appointment provision has been interpreted very differently by the Supreme Court over time.

---

### 📜 Case 1: First Judges Case (1981)
**Full Name:** *S.P. Gupta vs. Union of India (1981)*

**What Happened:**
This was a landmark case that questioned the executive's power over judicial appointments. Lawyers and judges challenged the government's practice of transferring High Court judges and not confirming additional judges.

**What the Court Decided:**
- The Court held that **"consultation" does NOT mean "concurrence"** (agreement).
- It only means an **exchange of views**.
- This meant the **President (i.e., the government/executive) had the final say** in appointing judges. The CJI's opinion was important, but not binding.

**Impact:** This gave the executive enormous power over judicial appointments, which was seen as a threat to judicial independence.

---

### 📜 Case 2: Second Judges Case (1993)
**Full Name:** *Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India (1993)*

**What Happened:**
This case was brought after controversial judicial appointments and transfers that were seen as the executive pressuring the judiciary. The case directly challenged the First Judges Case ruling.

**What the Court Decided:**
- The Court **reversed** its earlier ruling in the First Judges Case.
- **"Consultation" was reinterpreted to mean "concurrence"** (binding agreement).
- The **advice of the Chief Justice of India (CJI) is BINDING on the President** in matters of appointment.
- **BUT:** The CJI cannot act alone. He/She must consult the **two most senior judges** of the Supreme Court before giving advice.
- Additionally, the Court ruled: **The seniormost judge of the Supreme Court should ALONE be appointed as Chief Justice of India** — ending the government's discretion to supersede judges.

**Impact:** This case **established the Collegium System** (a group of senior judges deciding appointments). Power shifted significantly from the executive to the judiciary.

---

### 📜 Case 3: Third Judges Case (1998)
**Full Name:** *In Re-Presidential Reference (1998)*

**Background:** The President, under **Article 143**, sent a reference to the Supreme Court seeking clarification on the "consultation process" that the CJI must follow, as doubts arose from the 1993 judgment.

**What the Court Decided:**
- The consultation process requires **"consultation of plurality of judges"** — meaning a collegium, not just one person.
- The **sole opinion of the CJI alone does NOT constitute** valid consultation.
- The CJI must consult a **collegium of the four seniormost judges** of the Supreme Court.
- **If even two judges in the collegium give an adverse opinion**, the CJI should **NOT send the recommendation** to the government.
- Recommendations made **without following this process are NOT binding** on the government.

**Impact:** This case **refined and strengthened the Collegium System**, making it a body of 5 judges (CJI + 4 senior judges) for Supreme Court appointments.

---

### 📜 Case 4: Fourth Judges Case (2015)
**Full Name:** *Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India (2015)*

**Background:**
The **99th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2014** and the **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) Act of 2014** were passed by Parliament to **replace the Collegium System** with a new body called the **NJAC**.

**What was the NJAC?**
The NJAC was a 6-member body that would have included:
- The Chief Justice of India (Chairperson)
- Two senior-most Supreme Court judges
- The Union Law Minister
- Two eminent persons (nominated jointly by CJI, PM, and Leader of Opposition)

The presence of the **Law Minister and two eminent persons** was seen as bringing executive influence into judicial appointments.

**What the Court Decided:**
- The Supreme Court declared **BOTH** the **99th Constitutional Amendment** AND the **NJAC Act** as **unconstitutional and void**.
- The Court held that the NJAC would **affect the independence of the judiciary** — a basic feature of the Constitution (per the Basic Structure Doctrine from Kesavananda Bharati case).
- Consequently, the **earlier Collegium System became operative again**.

**Impact:** This case confirmed that the Collegium System is the law of the land. The ongoing tension between the executive and judiciary over judicial appointments continues to this day.

---

## 🌍 Modern Context — Judicial Appointments

> **[Modern Example]** The debate over the Collegium System remains very much alive. In **late 2022**, Union Law Minister **Kiren Rijiju** publicly criticized the Collegium System, calling it "opaque" and not accountable. The government and judiciary exchanged a series of statements, making it one of the most prominent constitutional debates of 2022-2023.
>
> **[Modern Example]** In **January 2023**, the Supreme Court took up a suo motu case on the implementation of the Collegium System's recommendations, after the government was seen as "sitting on" recommendations for months without acting. This highlighted the practical friction between the executive and the judiciary even after the 2015 judgment.

---

## 📅 Appointment of Chief Justice — The Seniority Controversy

- **Practice from 1950–1973:** The **seniormost judge of the Supreme Court** was conventionally appointed as CJI.
- **Violation in 1973:** **A.N. Ray** was appointed CJI, **superseding three senior judges**: **J.M. Shelat**, **K.S. Hegde**, and **A.N. Grover**.
  - All three superseded judges **resigned** from the Supreme Court.
  - Reason: They had delivered judgements in the **Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)** that were **not favourable to the government**.
- **Violation in 1977:** **M.U. Beg** was appointed CJI by superseding the then seniormost judge, **H.R. Khanna**.
  - **H.R. Khanna** also resigned.
  - Reason: His **dissenting judgement** in the **ADM Jabalpur vs. Shivkant Shukla case (1976)** had upheld the **right to life even during Emergency**, which was not appreciated by the Government.
- **Rule Established by Second Judges Case (1993):** The Court ruled that the **seniormost judge of the Supreme Court should ALONE be appointed CJI**, eliminating this executive discretion.

---

## ✅ Qualifications of a Supreme Court Judge

A person must fulfil **all** of the following:

1. **Must be a citizen of India.**
2. **Must satisfy ONE of the following:**
   - **(a)** Has been a **judge of a High Court** (or High Courts in succession) for **five years**; OR
   - **(b)** Has been an **advocate of a High Court** (or High Courts in succession) for **ten years**; OR
   - **(c)** Is a **distinguished jurist** in the **opinion of the President**.

> ⚠️ **Important:** The Constitution has **NOT prescribed a minimum age** for appointment as a Supreme Court judge.

---

## 🙏 Oath or Affirmation

Before entering office, a judge must take an oath before the **President** (or a person appointed by the President). In this oath, the judge swears to:

1. Bear true **faith and allegiance** to the Constitution of India.
2. Uphold the **sovereignty and integrity** of India.
3. Perform the **duties of the office faithfully and to the best of ability**, without fear or favour, affection or ill-will.
4. Uphold the **Constitution and the laws**.

## 📅 Tenure and Removal

### Tenure
The Constitution does **NOT fix a specific tenure**. It provides:

1. A judge holds office until he/she attains the age of **65 years**.
   - *(Any question regarding age is determined by Parliament-prescribed authority)*
2. Can **resign** by writing to the President.
3. Can be **removed** by the President on Parliament's recommendation.

---

### Removal — The Impeachment Process

> ⚠️ **Note:** The word **"impeachment"** is used in the Constitution only for the removal of the **President**. For judges, the term used is simply "removal." However, the process is colloquially called impeachment.

A judge can be removed by the **President** only after:
- An **address by Parliament** presented in the **same session**
- Supported by a **Special Majority** of each House:
  - Majority of **total membership** of that House; AND
  - Majority of **not less than two-thirds** of members **present and voting**

**Grounds for removal:**
1. **Proved misbehaviour**
2. **Incapacity**

### Step-by-Step Removal Process (Judges Enquiry Act, 1968)

**Step 1:** A removal motion is signed by:
- **100 members** (in the case of **Lok Sabha**), OR
- **50 members** (in the case of **Rajya Sabha**)
...and given to the **Speaker/Chairman**.

**Step 2:** The Speaker/Chairman may **admit or refuse** the motion.

**Step 3:** If admitted, the Speaker/Chairman constitutes a **3-member investigation committee** comprising:
- **(a)** The Chief Justice or a judge of the **Supreme Court**
- **(b)** A **Chief Justice of a High Court**
- **(c)** A **distinguished jurist**

**Step 4:** If the committee finds the judge **guilty**, the House takes up consideration of the motion.

**Step 5:** The motion is passed by **each House** by **special majority**.

**Step 6:** An **address is presented to the President**.

**Step 7:** The **President passes an order** removing the judge.

---

### 🔴 Historical Case: Justice V. Ramaswami (1991–1993)

- This is the **first and only case** where an impeachment motion was moved against a Supreme Court judge.
- The **Enquiry Committee found him GUILTY** of misbehaviour.
- However, he **could NOT be removed** because the **impeachment motion was DEFEATED in the Lok Sabha**.
- The **Congress Party abstained** from voting, which led to the motion failing to get the required special majority.

> **Result:** No judge of the Supreme Court has been successfully removed/impeached in India's history.

## 👥 Acting, Ad Hoc, and Retired Judges

### 1. Acting Chief Justice
The **President** can appoint a judge of the Supreme Court as **Acting Chief Justice** when:
- The office of CJI is **vacant**; OR
- The CJI is **temporarily absent**; OR
- The CJI is **unable to perform duties**.

### 2. Ad Hoc Judge
*(Ad Hoc = temporary for a specific purpose)*

When there is a **lack of quorum** (minimum number of judges needed) to hold/continue a session:

- The **Chief Justice of India** can appoint a **High Court judge** as an ad hoc judge of the Supreme Court.
- **Conditions:**
  - Prior **consultation with the Chief Justice of the High Court** concerned
  - **Previous consent of the President**
  - The judge must be **qualified** for SC appointment
- **Duties:** The ad hoc judge must **prioritise SC sittings** over other High Court duties.
- **While attending SC:** Enjoys all **jurisdiction, powers, and privileges** of a SC judge.

### 3. Retired Judge
- The **Chief Justice of India** can request a **retired SC or High Court judge** to act as a SC judge temporarily.
- **Conditions:**
  - **Previous consent of the President** AND of the **person to be appointed**
  - The retired judge must be **duly qualified** for SC appointment
- **Entitlements:** Allowances as determined by the President; all jurisdiction, powers, and privileges of a SC judge.
- **Important:** Such a judge is **NOT otherwise deemed to be a judge** of the Supreme Court.

## 🏢 Seat and Procedure of the Supreme Court

### Seat
- The Constitution declares **Delhi** as the seat of the Supreme Court.
- The **Chief Justice of India** can appoint **other places** as seat — but ONLY with the **approval of the President**.
- This provision is **optional, not compulsory**.
- **No court can direct** the President or CJI to appoint any other place as the seat.

### Procedure
- Rules for practice and procedure are made by the SC **with the approval of the President**.
- **Constitutional cases** or **Presidential References (Article 143):** Decided by a Bench of **at least 5 judges**.
- **All other cases:** Decided by single judges or division benches.
- Judgements are delivered in **open court**.
- **All judgements are by majority vote**; judges can give **dissenting judgements**.

---

## 🔒 Independence of the Supreme Court

The SC plays a crucial role as:
- A **Federal Court**
- The **Highest Court of Appeal**
- **Guarantor of Fundamental Rights**
- **Guardian of the Constitution**

For it to effectively perform these roles, it must be **free from executive and legislative interference**. The Constitution provides the following **9 safeguards**:

### 1. Mode of Appointment
Judges are appointed by the President *in consultation with* members of the judiciary itself. This **curtails absolute executive discretion** and prevents politically motivated appointments.

### 2. Security of Tenure
Judges can be removed **only** in the manner and on grounds specified in the Constitution. They do NOT hold office "at the pleasure of the President" (unlike some other officials).

### 3. Fixed Service Conditions
Salaries, allowances, and pensions are determined by Parliament and **cannot be changed to their disadvantage** after appointment (except during a Financial Emergency).

### 4. Expenses Charged on Consolidated Fund
Salaries, allowances, pensions of judges and staff, and all administrative expenses are charged on the **Consolidated Fund of India**. This means they are **non-votable by Parliament** (though they can be discussed).

### 5. Conduct Cannot Be Discussed
The Constitution **prohibits any discussion** in Parliament or State Legislature about a judge's conduct in the discharge of duties — **EXCEPT** when an impeachment motion is under consideration.

### 6. Ban on Practice After Retirement
Retired SC judges are **prohibited from pleading or acting** in any court or before any authority within India. This prevents them from favouring anyone in the hope of post-retirement benefits.

### 7. Power to Punish for Contempt
The SC can punish any person for contempt of itself (and of lower courts). This protects its **authority, dignity, and honour**.

### 8. Freedom to Appoint its Own Staff
The **CJI can appoint officers and servants** of the SC without executive interference and can prescribe their service conditions.

### 9. Jurisdiction Cannot Be Curtailed
Parliament **cannot curtail** the SC's jurisdiction. It can only **extend** it. The Constitution guarantees the SC's various jurisdictions.

---

## ⚖️ Jurisdiction and Powers of the Supreme Court

> **Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar**, a member of the Drafting Committee of the Constituent Assembly, stated:
> *"The Supreme Court of India has more powers than any other Supreme Court in any part of the world."*

The SC's jurisdiction covers **8 major areas**:

---

### 1. 🏛️ Original Jurisdiction (Federal Cases)

As a **Federal Court**, the SC decides disputes between units of the Indian Federation. These include any dispute:

**(a)** Between the **Centre and one or more states**
**(b)** Between the **Centre and any state(s)** on one side and **one or more other states** on the other
**(c)** Between **two or more states**

**Key Features:**
- **Exclusive** (no other court can decide such disputes) and **Original** (heard in the first instance, not by way of appeal).
- Dispute must involve a **question of law or fact** on which a **legal right depends**. *Political questions are excluded.*
- Suits by **private citizens** against the Centre or a state **cannot be entertained** here.

**Exceptions — The following are EXCLUDED from this jurisdiction:**

- Disputes from any **pre-Constitution treaty/agreement/covenant** that continues in operation
- **Inter-state river water disputes** *(The Inter-State River Water Disputes Act, 1956 has excluded this)*
- Matters referred to the **Finance Commission**
- **Adjustment of certain expenses and pensions** between Centre and states

> **🔎 Historical Example:** In **1961**, the first suit under this jurisdiction was filed by **West Bengal against the Centre**, challenging the constitutional validity of the **Coal Bearing Areas (Acquisition and Development) Act, 1957**. The Supreme Court **dismissed the suit** and upheld the validity of the Act.

---

### 2. ✍️ Writ Jurisdiction (Fundamental Rights Enforcement)

The SC is the **guarantor and defender of fundamental rights**. It can issue **5 types of writs**:

| Writ | What it means (simply) |
|---|---|
| **Habeas Corpus** | "Produce the body" — Release an illegally detained person |
| **Mandamus** | "We command" — Order a public body to do its duty |
| **Prohibition** | Stop a lower court from exceeding its jurisdiction |
| **Quo Warranto** | "By what authority" — Challenge an unlawful claim to a public office |
| **Certiorari** | Transfer a case from lower court to higher court / quash an order |

**Key Distinctions:**

| Feature | Supreme Court | High Court |
|---|---|---|
| **Can issue writs for** | ONLY enforcement of **Fundamental Rights** | Fundamental Rights + **Other purposes** (wider scope) |
| **Nature** | **Concurrent** with High Courts | **Concurrent** with SC |
| **Can Parliament expand SC's writ power?** | **Yes**, Parliament can confer power to issue writs for other purposes | — |

### 3. 📋 Appellate Jurisdiction (Court of Appeal)

The SC hears appeals against judgements of High Courts under **4 categories**:

#### (a) Constitutional Matters
An appeal can be made if the High Court **certifies** that the case involves a **substantial question of law requiring constitutional interpretation**.

#### (b) Civil Matters
An appeal lies if the High Court certifies:
- **(i)** The case involves a **substantial question of law of general importance**; AND
- **(ii)** The question needs to be decided by the Supreme Court.

> **Historical Note:** Originally, only civil cases involving a sum of **not less than ₹20,000** could be appealed. This monetary limit was **removed by the 30th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1972**.

#### (c) Criminal Matters
An appeal lies to the SC from a criminal proceeding of a High Court if the High Court:
- **(i)** Has **reversed an acquittal** and sentenced the accused to **death**; OR
- **(ii)** Has taken a case from a subordinate court, convicted the accused, and sentenced to **death**; OR
- **(iii)** **Certifies** the case as fit for appeal.

> In the first two cases — appeal is a **matter of right** (no High Court certificate needed).

**Parliament enlarged Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction in 1970:**
Appeals also lie if the High Court has sentenced the accused to:
- **(i)** **Imprisonment for life** or **ten years** (after reversing an acquittal); OR
- **(ii)** **Imprisonment for life** or **ten years** (after taking a case from a subordinate court)

#### (d) Appeal by Special Leave
The SC can grant, **in its discretion**, special leave to appeal from **ANY judgement** by **any court or tribunal** in India.

**EXCEPT:** **Military tribunals and court martial** are excluded.

**4 Key Features:**
1. It is a **discretionary power** — cannot be claimed as a matter of right.
2. Can be granted against **final or interlocutory** judgements.
3. May relate to **any matter** — constitutional, civil, criminal, income tax, labour, revenue, etc.
4. Can be against **any court or tribunal**, not necessarily a High Court.

> The SC itself said: *"Being an exceptional and overriding power, it has to be exercised sparingly and with caution and only in special extraordinary situations."*

### 4. 💡 Advisory Jurisdiction

**Article 143** authorises the **President to seek the SC's opinion** in two categories:

**(a)** On any **question of law or fact of public importance** that has arisen or is likely to arise.
**(b)** On any dispute arising out of **pre-Constitution treaties/agreements** excluded from original jurisdiction.

| Category | Is SC's Opinion Binding? | Must SC give opinion? |
|---|---|---|
| (a) Public importance | **NOT binding** on President | May or may **not** tender opinion |
| (b) Pre-Constitution disputes | **NOT binding** on President | SC **MUST** tender opinion |

> The opinion is **advisory, not a judicial pronouncement**. The President may or may not follow it. However, it provides an authoritative legal opinion.

### 5. 📚 Court of Record

As a Court of Record, the SC has **two powers:**

**(a)** The **judgements, proceedings, and acts** of the SC are **recorded for perpetual memory and testimony**:
- Admitted as **evidentiary value** — cannot be questioned when produced before any court.
- Recognised as **legal precedents and legal references**.

**(b)** Power to **punish for contempt** — not only of itself, but also of High Courts, subordinate courts, and tribunals.
*(SC ruled this in 1991)*

### The Contempt of Courts Act, 1971

**Background:**
- In **1961**, the government appointed a special committee under **H.N. Sanyal** (then Additional Solicitor General of India) to examine contempt laws.
- The committee submitted its report in **1963**.
- Based on recommendations, the **Contempt of Courts Act, 1971** was enacted.

### Types of Contempt:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Civil Contempt** | Wilful disobedience to any judgement/order/writ of a court OR wilful breach of an undertaking given to a court |
| **Criminal Contempt** | Publication or act that **(i)** scandalises/lowers authority of a court; OR **(ii)** prejudices/interferes with judicial proceedings; OR **(iii)** interferes with administration of justice |

### What is NOT Contempt:
- **Innocent publication** and distribution of matter
- **Fair and accurate report** of judicial proceedings
- **Fair criticism** of judicial acts

### Punishment:
- Simple imprisonment up to **6 months**, OR
- Fine up to **₹2,000**, OR both.

### Other Important Provisions:
- No proceedings can be initiated after **one year** from the alleged contempt.
- Act is **NOT applicable** to **Nyaya Panchayats or village courts** established for administration of justice.

### 6. 🔍 Power of Judicial Review

**Judicial review** (power to examine constitutionality — *is it valid under the Constitution?*) is the power of the SC to examine:
- **Legislative enactments** (laws made by Parliament or State Legislatures)
- **Executive orders** of both Central and State governments

If found **violative of the Constitution (ultra-vires — beyond legal power)**, they can be declared:
- **Illegal, unconstitutional, and invalid (null and void)**
- And consequently **cannot be enforced** by the government.

---

### 7. 🔄 Review Jurisdiction

The SC has the power to **review any judgement or order** it has made.

**Rules for Review Petition:**
- Must be filed within **30 days** from the date of judgement/order.
- Must be submitted to the **same judge or bench** that delivered it.

**Grounds for Review:**
1. Discovery of **new and important matter or evidence**
2. **Mistake or error apparent** on the face of the record
3. **Any other sufficient reason**

The court may either **dismiss** the petition or direct **notice to the opposite party**.

**Even after dismissal of review petition**, the court can reconsider via a **Curative Petition** on limited grounds:
1. Violation of **principles of natural justice**
2. To cure a **gross miscarriage of justice**
3. To prevent **abuse of the process** of the court
4. **Bias of the judge**

### 8. 📖 Constitutional Interpretation

The SC is the **ultimate interpreter of the Constitution**. It gives the **final version** to the spirit and content of constitutional provisions.

The SC applies various **doctrines** while interpreting the Constitution:

| # | Doctrine | Simple Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Doctrine of Severability** | Invalid part of law removed; rest survives |
| 2 | **Doctrine of Waiver** | Citizens can waive their fundamental rights |
| 3 | **Doctrine of Eclipse** | Pre-Constitution law dormant but can revive |
| 4 | **Doctrine of Territorial Nexus** | State law applies if sufficient connection exists |
| 5 | **Doctrine of Pith and Substance** | Core subject of law determines validity |
| 6 | **Doctrine of Colourable Legislation** | Cannot do indirectly what you cannot do directly |
| 7 | **Doctrine of Implied Powers** | Powers not explicit but necessarily implied |
| 8 | **Doctrine of Incidental and Ancillary Powers** | Powers necessary to exercise main power |
| 9 | **Doctrine of Precedent** | Earlier judgements bind later courts |
| 10 | **Doctrine of Occupied Field** | Centre occupies the field; states cannot legislate |
| 11 | **Doctrine of Prospective Overruling** | New ruling applies only to future cases |
| 12 | **Doctrine of Harmonious Construction** | Reconcile conflicting provisions |
| 13 | **Doctrine of Liberal Interpretation** | Broad, purposive reading of the Constitution |

---

### 9. ➕ Other Powers

1. Decides disputes regarding **election of the President and Vice-President** — has **original, exclusive, and final** authority.

2. **Enquires into conduct** of Chairman and Members of UPSC/SPSC/JSPSC on a **Presidential reference**. If found guilty, it can **recommend removal to the President**. This advice is **binding on the President**.

3. Can **withdraw cases pending before High Courts** and dispose of them itself. Can also **transfer cases** from one High Court to another.

4. **SC's law is binding on ALL courts** in India. Its **decree or order is enforceable throughout the country**. All civil and judicial authorities must **act in aid of the SC**.

5. Has power of **judicial superintendence and control** over all courts and tribunals in the entire territory of India.

**Parliament can enlarge SC's jurisdiction** for matters in the Union List. For other matters, jurisdiction can be enlarged by a **special agreement** of the Centre and states.

## 🇮🇳 vs 🇺🇸 India and America's Supreme Courts Compared

| Feature | Indian Supreme Court | American Supreme Court |
|---|---|---|
| **Original Jurisdiction** | Confined to **federal cases** | Covers federal cases + naval forces, maritime activities, ambassadors, etc. |
| **Appellate Jurisdiction** | Covers **constitutional, civil, and criminal** cases | Confined to **constitutional cases only** |
| **Special Leave** | Very **wide discretionary power** to grant special leave against any court/tribunal | **No such plenary power** |
| **Advisory Jurisdiction** | **Has advisory jurisdiction** | **No advisory jurisdiction** |
| **Scope of Judicial Review** | **Limited** | Very **wide** |
| **Rights Standard** | Defends rights per **"procedure established by law"** | Defends rights per **"due process of law"** |
| **Jurisdiction Expansion** | Can be **enlarged by Parliament** | Limited to what the **Constitution confers** |
| **Superintendence over State Courts** | **Has this power** due to integrated judicial system | **No such power** due to double judicial system |

## 🌍 Modern Context — Supreme Court

> **[Modern Example]** The **Contempt of Courts Act** was in the news in **2020** when the Supreme Court initiated contempt proceedings against lawyer-activist **Prashant Bhushan** for tweets about the judiciary. The Court convicted him but imposed a symbolic fine of **Re. 1**. This case reignited debate about the boundaries between contempt law and free speech.
>
> **[Modern Example]** Judicial review was powerfully demonstrated in the **Pegasus Spyware Case (2021-2022)**, where the SC appointed a Technical Expert Committee to investigate the alleged surveillance of Indian citizens, including journalists and activists. The Court held that the Right to Privacy (a Fundamental Right per the **Puttaswamy judgment, 2017**) could not be violated by the State.
>
> **[Modern Example]** The **Collegium vs. Government** standoff reached a new peak in **2023**, with the SC publicly noting that many of its collegium recommendations were pending with the government for months. This prompted discussions about introducing a more transparent system, possibly a reformed NJAC, though no formal legislative change has occurred.
>
> **[Modern Example]** On **Inter-State River Water Disputes**: The **Mahadayi Water Disputes Tribunal Final Award (2024)** between Goa, Karnataka, and Maharashtra is a recent illustration of how such disputes are handled outside the SC's original jurisdiction as per the Inter-State River Water Disputes Act, 1956.
