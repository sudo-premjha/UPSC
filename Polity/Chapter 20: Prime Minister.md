## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. The **President** is the **nominal/de jure executive** (head of State) while the **Prime Minister** is the **real/de facto executive** (head of government) — the PM holds actual power.
> 2. The Constitution (**Article 75**) simply says the PM is appointed by the President — but by **convention**, the President MUST appoint the **leader of the majority party** in the Lok Sabha.
> 3. The PM is the **pivot of the entire government machinery** — their resignation or death automatically dissolves the entire Council of Ministers.
> 4. The PM need **not** be a member of the Lok Sabha — they can be from the **Rajya Sabha** (e.g., Manmohan Singh, Deve Gowda, Indira Gandhi in 1966).
> 5. A **Caretaker Government** has no constitutional basis — it is purely a **situational arrangement** and cannot make major policy decisions.

## 1. The PM in the Parliamentary System

India follows the **parliamentary system of government**. In this system, there are **two executives**:

| Type | Who | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Nominal Executive** (de jure — legal on paper) | **President** | Head of State; acts on PM's advice |
| **Real Executive** (de facto — actual power) | **Prime Minister** | Head of Government; runs the country |

> 💡 **De jure** = *legally recognised but no real power*. **De facto** = *actually holds real power*.

Think of it this way: The President is like the **owner of a company on paper**, while the Prime Minister is like the **CEO who actually runs it**.

## 2. Appointment of the Prime Minister

### What Does the Constitution Say?
- **Article 75** simply states: *"The Prime Minister shall be appointed by the President."*
- The Constitution provides **no specific procedure** for selecting the PM.

### The Convention (Normal Situation)
By the **conventions of parliamentary government**, the President **must** appoint the **leader of the majority party** in the Lok Sabha as PM. The President has no personal discretion here — it is automatic.

### When Does the President Have Discretion?
The President can exercise **personal discretion** in two situations:

**Situation 1: No Clear Majority in Lok Sabha (Hung Parliament)**
- If no single party wins a clear majority, the President usually appoints the **leader of the largest party or coalition**.
- The appointed PM is asked to **prove a vote of confidence** in the House within a month.
- **First time this discretion was exercised**: In **1979**, President **Neelam Sanjiva Reddy** appointed **Charan Singh** (coalition leader) as PM after the fall of the Janata Party government headed by **Morarji Desai**.

**Situation 2: Sudden Death of the Incumbent PM with No Obvious Successor**
- When the sitting PM dies suddenly and the ruling party hasn't elected a new leader yet.
- **Example**: When **Indira Gandhi was assassinated in 1984**, President **Zail Singh** appointed **Rajiv Gandhi** as PM — bypassing the usual precedent of appointing a **caretaker PM**.
  - Earlier precedent (on the deaths of Nehru and Shastri when leadership was contested): The President made temporary arrangements by appointing the **seniormost minister** as PM until the party formally elected a new leader. Both times, it was **Gulzari Lal Nanda** who acted as PM.
- Importantly: If the ruling party **elects a new leader** after the PM's death, the President has **no choice** but to appoint that person as PM.

### Does the PM Need to Prove Majority Before Appointment?
- **No.** In **1980**, the **Delhi High Court** held that the Constitution does **not require** a person to prove their majority in the Lok Sabha **before** being appointed as PM.
- The President can first **appoint** them as PM and then ask them to **prove their majority within a reasonable period**.

**Examples of PMs appointed this way (without prior majority proof):**
- Charan Singh (1979)
- V.P. Singh (1989)
- Chandrasekhar (1990)
- P.V. Narasimha Rao (1991)
- A.B. Vajpayee (1996)
- Deve Gowda (1996)
- I.K. Gujral (1997)
- A.B. Vajpayee (1998)

### Can a Non-MP Become PM?
- **Yes, temporarily.** In **1996**, the **Supreme Court** held that a person who is **not a member of either House of Parliament** can be appointed as PM for **six months**.
- Within those six months, they **must** become a member of either House.
- If they fail to do so, they **cease to be PM**.

### Which House Must the PM Belong To?
- Constitutionally, the PM can be a member of **either House** (Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha).
- **Three PMs who were Rajya Sabha members:**
  - **Indira Gandhi** (1966)
  - **Deve Gowda** (1996)
  - **Manmohan Singh** (2004)
- **Contrast with Britain**: In the UK, the PM **must** be a member of the **House of Commons** (the lower house). India's system is more flexible.

## 3. Oath, Term, and Salary

### Oath
Before taking office, the President administers **two oaths** to the PM:

**Oath of Office** — The PM swears to:
1. Bear true faith and allegiance to the **Constitution of India**
2. Uphold the **sovereignty and integrity of India**
3. Faithfully and conscientiously **discharge the duties** of office
4. Do right to all people in accordance with the **Constitution and law**, without fear or favour, affection or ill will

**Oath of Secrecy** — The PM swears not to:
- Directly or indirectly **communicate or reveal** to any person any matter brought under their consideration or known to them as a Union Minister
- **Exception**: Disclosure is permitted only as required for the **due discharge of ministerial duties**

> 📝 *The form of oath for the PM is the same as for any Union Minister.*

### Term of Office
- The PM's term is **not fixed**.
- The PM holds office **during the pleasure of the President**.
- **BUT** — this does NOT mean the President can dismiss the PM at any time.
- As long as the PM enjoys **majority support in the Lok Sabha**, they **cannot** be dismissed by the President.
- If the PM **loses the confidence of the Lok Sabha**, they must **resign** or the President can **dismiss** them.

> 📝 *Examples of PMs who resigned after defeat in Lok Sabha: V.P. Singh (1990) and Deve Gowda (1997).*

### Salary and Allowances
- Determined by **Parliament** from time to time (under the **Salaries and Allowances of Ministers Act, 1952**).
- The PM gets the **salary and allowances payable to an MP**.
- Additionally, the PM receives:
  - **Sumptuary allowance** (In **2001**, increased from ₹1,500 to ₹3,000 per month — under the **Salaries and Allowances of Ministers (Amendment) Act, 2001**)
  - Free accommodation
  - Travelling allowance
  - Medical facilities

## 4. Powers and Functions of the Prime Minister

The PM's powers can be grouped into **four major areas**:

---

### 4.1 Powers in Relation to the Council of Ministers

The PM is the **head of the Council of Ministers** and wields enormous power over it:

1. **Recommends ministers**: The President can only appoint ministers who are **recommended by the PM**. The PM's word is final here.
2. **Allocates and reshuffles portfolios**: The PM decides which minister handles which ministry — and can change these assignments at will.
3. **Can ask a minister to resign** or **advise the President to dismiss** a minister in case of differences.
4. **Presides over meetings** of the Council of Ministers and **influences its decisions**.
5. **Guides, directs, controls, and coordinates** the activities of all ministers.
6. **Can dissolve the Council** by simply **resigning from office**.

> ⚠️ **Critical Point**: The PM's resignation or death **automatically dissolves the entire Council of Ministers** — creating a vacuum. The resignation or death of any OTHER minister only creates a vacancy, which the PM may or may not fill.

This is why the PM is called the **"keystone of the cabinet arch"** — if the keystone falls, the entire arch collapses.

---

### 4.2 Powers in Relation to the President

The PM is the **principal channel of communication** between the President and the Council of Ministers (specifically under **Article 78**).

**Under Article 78, it is the PM's duty to:**
1. Communicate to the President **all decisions of the Council of Ministers** relating to Union administration and proposed legislation.
2. Furnish **any information** relating to Union administration and legislation that the **President may call for**.
3. If the President requires it, **submit to the Council of Ministers** any matter on which a minister has taken a decision individually (without Council consideration) — for the Council's review.

**Advisory Role:**
The PM **advises the President** on appointments of key constitutional and statutory officials, including:
- Attorney General of India
- Comptroller and Auditor General of India
- Chairman and Members of the UPSC
- Election Commissioners
- Chairman and Members of the Finance Commission
- And other important officials

---

### 4.3 Powers in Relation to Parliament

The PM is the **leader of the Lok Sabha** (Lower House). In this capacity:

1. **Advises the President** on summoning and proroguing (ending) sessions of Parliament.
2. Can **recommend dissolution of the Lok Sabha** to the President at any time.
3. **Announces government policies** on the floor of the House.

---

### 4.4 Other Powers and Functions

1. **Chairperson of key bodies**:
   - **NITI Aayog** (which succeeded the Planning Commission)
   - **National Integration Council**
   - **Inter-State Council**
   - **National Water Resources Council**
   - Several other bodies

2. Plays a significant role in **shaping India's foreign policy**.
3. Is the **chief spokesman** of the Union government.
4. Is the **crisis manager-in-chief** at the political level during emergencies.
5. As leader of the nation, **meets various sections of people** in different states and receives memoranda regarding their problems.
6. Is the **leader of the ruling party**.
7. Is the **political head of the services** (the bureaucracy).

> 💡 **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** stated: *"If any functionary under our Constitution is to be compared with the US President, he is the Prime Minister and not the President of the Union."*

The PM's role has grown so dominant that observers now describe it as a **'Prime Ministerial government'** rather than a cabinet government:

- **R.H. Crossman**: *"The post-war epoch has been the final transformation of cabinet government into Prime Ministerial government."*
- **Humphrey Berkeley**: *"Parliament is not, in practice, sovereign... The basic defect in the British system of governing is the superministerial powers of the Prime Minister."*

> 💡 These observations apply equally to the **Indian context**.

## 6. Constitutional Relationship: President and Prime Minister

Three key Articles govern this relationship:

### Article 74
- There shall be a **Council of Ministers with the PM at the head** to **aid and advise the President**.
- The President **shall act in accordance** with such advice.
- However, the President **may ask** the Council to **reconsider** the advice.
- After reconsideration, the President **must act** on the revised advice.

### Article 75
- The PM is **appointed by the President**.
- Other ministers are appointed by the President **on the advice of the PM**.
- Ministers hold office **during the pleasure of the President**.
- The Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible** to the Lok Sabha (House of the People).

### Article 78
- It is the **duty of the PM** to:
  1. Communicate all Council of Ministers' decisions to the President.
  2. Furnish information as called for by the President.
  3. Submit any individual ministerial decision (not yet considered by the full Council) to the Council if the President requires.

## 8. Caretaker Government

### What Is a Caretaker Government?
> ⚠️ **The Constitution of India contains NO provision for a caretaker government.** It is purely a **situational arrangement** and a **functional necessity**.

A caretaker government is a temporary arrangement that arises in two scenarios:

**Scenario 1**: After the **dissolution of the Lok Sabha** (popular chamber), a caretaker government manages affairs until a new government is formed after elections. It is essential to ensuring **free and fair polls**.

**Scenario 2**: When a Council of Ministers has **resigned** (after losing confidence or otherwise) but is asked by the President to **continue till alternative arrangements are made**. If no alternative government can be formed immediately and general elections must be held, the outgoing Council holds charge till elections conclude and a new government is formed.

---

### The Limited Role of a Caretaker Government

A caretaker government is meant **only for managing routine day-to-day activities**. It is **NOT expected to**:
- Make significant policy decisions
- Initiate new measures or schemes

**Exception**: Matters relating to **national security or national interest** can be acted upon.

### Key Observations on the Limited Role:

**1. The Tarkunde Committee (1974–75):**
- This committee on electoral reforms was appointed in **1974** by **Jayaprakash Narayan (JP)** during his **"Total Revolution" movement**.
- It was headed by **Justice Vithal Mahadeo Tarkunde**.
- Also known as the **JP Committee**.
- It was an **unofficial committee** that submitted its report in **1975**.

**Recommendations**: A caretaker government should NOT:
- Initiate and announce new policies
- Promise or start new projects
- Grant allowances or loans, salary increases
- Hold official functions attended by ministers

**2. President N. Sanjeeva Reddy's Communique (August 1979):**
When dissolving the Lok Sabha, the President issued a communique after consultations with the PM and cabinet colleagues, assuring that:
- Elections will be **peaceful, free, and fair**
- Electoral roll revision would begin immediately; elections to be completed by December 1979
- The Government would **not take decisions** that set new policies, involve new significant spending, or constitute major administrative/executive decisions
- **Exception**: Work of an urgent nature involving **national interest** would not be held up

**3. Calcutta High Court Judgement (December 1979):**
The court observed:
- There is **no mention of a caretaker government** in the Constitution
- But such extraordinary situations call for one
- A caretaker PM and Council of Ministers can only carry on **day-to-day administration** necessary for making alternative arrangements

**4. Two Key Constraints on Caretaker PMs:**
1. The normal channel of **accountability through Parliament does not exist** (Parliament is dissolved)
2. The government must **not abuse its position** to gain **electoral advantage**

## 10. Key Court Cases Explained

### Case 1: Delhi High Court Judgement (1980)
**Context**: The question was whether a person must prove their majority in the Lok Sabha **before** being appointed as PM.

**What the Court Said**: The Constitution does **not require** a person to prove their majority before appointment. The President may **first appoint** them as PM and then ask them to prove their majority within a **reasonable period**.

**Significance**: This enabled coalition governments to function — leaders of coalitions could be appointed as PM and then build majority support. Leaders like Charan Singh (1979), V.P. Singh (1989), Chandrasekhar (1990), Narasimha Rao (1991), Vajpayee (1996, 1998), Deve Gowda (1996), and Gujral (1997) were all appointed in this manner.

---

### Case 2: Supreme Court Judgement (1996)
**Context**: Can a person who is NOT a member of either House of Parliament be appointed as PM?

**What the Court Said**: **Yes**, but only for a maximum of **six months**. Within this period, they must become a member of **either House of Parliament**. If they fail to do so, they **cease to be PM**.

**Significance**: This clarified a constitutional ambiguity — Article 75(5) already states that a minister (including PM) who is not a member of either House for six consecutive months ceases to be a minister. The SC confirmed this applies to the PM as well.

---

### Case 3: Calcutta High Court Judgement (December 1979) — Caretaker Government
**Context**: What is the legal status and scope of powers of a caretaker government?

**What the Court Said**: The Constitution has **no mention** of a caretaker government. However, such extraordinary situations demand one. A caretaker PM and Council of Ministers can **only carry on day-to-day administration** in office — work necessary for making alternative arrangements.

**Significance**: This judgement established the legal basis and limitations of caretaker governments in India, even though the Constitution is silent on the matter.

## 11. Summary: Why is the PM So Powerful?

The PM's position is the **most powerful in the Indian constitutional system** for the following reasons:

- **Controls the Cabinet**: Recommends who gets appointed, allocated, reshuffled, or dismissed.
- **Controls Parliament**: Advises on summoning, proroguing, and dissolution of Lok Sabha.
- **Controls the President**: Acts as the principal communicator; the President must act on PM's advice (after possible reconsideration).
- **Controls Key Appointments**: Advises on all major constitutional posts.
- **Heads Key Bodies**: NITI Aayog, Inter-State Council, National Integration Council, etc.
- **Single Point of Failure**: Resignation or death collapses the entire government.

As **Jennings** put it most vividly: *"All roads in the constitution lead to the Prime Minister."*
