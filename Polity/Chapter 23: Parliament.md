## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Main Takeaways before you begin reading:**
>
> 1. **Parliament = President + Rajya Sabha + Lok Sabha** — the President is an integral part despite not being a member of either House.
> 2. The **Rajya Sabha** (max 250) is a **permanent body** (never dissolved); the **Lok Sabha** (max 550) has a **5-year term** and can be dissolved.
> 3. There are **three types of bills** — Ordinary, Money, and Financial — each with a distinct procedure; and **four types of majority** — Simple, Effective, Absolute, and Special.
> 4. Parliamentary control over the Executive works through **Question Hour, Zero Hour, Motions, and Financial Committees** — but is often more theoretical than practical.
> 5. The **Indian Parliament is NOT sovereign** like the British Parliament — it is limited by the written Constitution, federalism, judicial review, and Fundamental Rights.

## 🧭 Introduction: What is Parliament?

Parliament is the **supreme legislative organ** (law-making body) of the Union (Central) Government.

It occupies a **central and pre-eminent position** in India's democratic system because India follows the **Parliamentary form of government** — also called the **'Westminster' model** (named after Westminster, the place in London where the British Parliament is located).

**Constitutional Basis:** Articles **79 to 122** in **Part V** of the Constitution deal with everything about Parliament — its organisation, composition, duration, officers, procedures, privileges, and powers.

---

## 🏛️ Organisation of Parliament

The **Parliament of India** consists of **three parts**:

1. **The President of India**
2. **The Council of States** → Known as **Rajya Sabha** (Hindi name adopted in 1954)
3. **The House of the People** → Known as **Lok Sabha** (Hindi name adopted in 1954)

### Understanding the Two Houses

| Feature | Rajya Sabha | Lok Sabha |
|---------|------------|----------|
| **Also called** | Upper House / Second Chamber / House of Elders | Lower House / First Chamber / Popular House |
| **Represents** | States and Union Territories | People of India as a whole |

### Why is the President Part of Parliament?

The President is **not a member** of either House and does not attend Parliament's meetings. Yet, the President is an **integral part of Parliament** because:

- A bill passed by both Houses **cannot become law** without the President's assent.
- The President **summons and prorogues** both Houses.
- The President **dissolves the Lok Sabha**.
- The President **addresses both Houses**.
- The President **issues Ordinances** when Parliament is not in session.

### India vs. Britain vs. USA

| Feature | India | Britain | USA |
|---------|-------|---------|-----|
| **Legislature consists of** | President + Rajya Sabha + Lok Sabha | Crown + House of Lords + House of Commons | Senate + House of Representatives (Congress) |
| **Is the Head of State part of Legislature?** | ✅ Yes (President-in-Parliament) | ✅ Yes (Crown-in-Parliament) | ❌ No (President is separate from Congress) |
| **Form of Government** | Parliamentary | Parliamentary | Presidential |

> **Key principle:** The **Parliamentary form** emphasises **interdependence** of Legislature and Executive. The **Presidential form** emphasises **separation** of Legislature and Executive.

## 👥 Composition of the Two Houses

### 🔵 Composition of Rajya Sabha

- **Maximum strength:** **250** members
  - Up to **238** representatives of States and Union Territories (elected indirectly)
  - **12** nominated by the President
- **Present strength:** **245** members
  - **225** represent States
  - **8** represent Union Territories
  - **12** nominated by President

The **Fourth Schedule** of the Constitution deals with the allocation of seats in the Rajya Sabha.

#### 1. Representation of States
- Elected by the **elected members of state legislative assemblies**.
- Election system: **Proportional Representation** (a system where seats are distributed in proportion to votes received) **by means of Single Transferable Vote**.
- Seats are allocated **based on population** — so states with more people get more seats.
  - Example: **Uttar Pradesh** has **31 members**; **Tripura** has only **1 member**.
- **Compare with USA:** In the American Senate, all 50 states get **equal representation** — 2 seats each (total 100 senators), regardless of population.

#### 2. Representation of Union Territories
- Representatives are **indirectly elected** by members of a specially constituted **electoral college**.
- Election method: Same as states — Proportional Representation by Single Transferable Vote.
- Out of 8 Union Territories, only **three** have Rajya Sabha representation:
  - **Delhi** (National Capital Territory)
  - **Puducherry**
  - **Jammu & Kashmir**
- The other five UTs have populations too small to have representation.

#### 3. Nominated Members
- The President nominates **12 members** from persons with **special knowledge or practical experience** in:
  - Art
  - Literature
  - Science
  - Social Service
- **Purpose:** To give eminent persons a place in Parliament **without going through elections**.
- **Note:** The American Senate has **no nominated members**.

---

### 🟢 Composition of Lok Sabha

- **Maximum strength:** **550** members
  - Up to **530** representatives of States
  - Up to **20** representatives of Union Territories
- **Present strength:** **543** members
  - **524** represent States
  - **19** represent Union Territories

#### 1. Representation of States
- Members are **directly elected** by the people from **territorial constituencies**.
- Based on **Universal Adult Franchise** (every citizen's right to vote, regardless of caste, gender, or religion).
- Eligibility to vote: Every Indian citizen **above 18 years** of age who is not disqualified.
- The voting age was **reduced from 21 to 18 years** by the **61st Constitutional Amendment Act, 1988**.

#### 2. Representation of Union Territories
- Parliament has the power to prescribe how UT representatives are chosen.
- Parliament enacted the **Union Territories (Direct Election to the House of the People) Act, 1965** — under which UT members are also chosen by **direct election**.

#### 3. Nominated Members (Anglo-Indian Community) — Now Discontinued
- **Before 2020:** The President could nominate **2 members** from the **Anglo-Indian community** if the community was not adequately represented.
  - **Anglo-Indian:** A person whose father or any male progenitor in the male line is or was of European descent, but who is domiciled within India.
- This provision was **originally for 10 years (up to 1960)**, but was repeatedly extended by 10 years each time.
- The **last extension (up to 2020)** was made by the **95th Amendment Act, 2009**.
- The **104th Amendment Act, 2019** did **NOT** extend it further.
- This provision **ceased to have effect on 25th January, 2020**.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` The **104th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2019** also extended the reservation of seats for **Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes** in the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies until **2030** (previously up to 2020). This is a live example of Parliament using its amendment power to extend affirmative action provisions. Additionally, the **106th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2023** (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam) provided **33% reservation for women** in the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies — one of the most significant changes to Lok Sabha composition in recent decades.

## 🗳️ System of Elections to Lok Sabha

### Territorial Constituencies

For direct elections to the Lok Sabha, each state is divided into **territorial constituencies**. The Constitution ensures two things:

1. The **ratio of Lok Sabha seats to population** should be the **same for all states** (except states with population less than 6 million).
2. Within each state, each constituency should have the **same ratio of population to seats** — ensuring **uniform representation** throughout the state.

> "Population" here means the population as recorded in the **last published census**.

### Readjustment after Each Census

After every census, the following are readjusted:
- Allocation of seats in the Lok Sabha to states
- Division of each state into territorial constituencies

Parliament enacts **Delimitation Commission Acts** for this purpose — done in **1952, 1962, 1972, and 2002**.

**Key Amendments on Delimitation:**

| Amendment | Year | What it did |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **42nd Amendment Act** | 1976 | Froze seat allocation and constituency division at **1971 census levels** until **year 2000** |
| **84th Amendment Act** | 2001 | Extended the freeze for another **25 years (until 2026)**; also empowered readjustment based on **1991 census** |
| **87th Amendment Act** | 2003 | Provided for delimitation based on **2001 census** (not 1991) — but **without changing** the number of seats per state |

> **Why freeze?** The objective was to **encourage population-limiting measures** — states that controlled population growth should not be penalised by losing Lok Sabha seats.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` The **Delimitation Commission (2020)** has been working on readjusting constituencies for **Jammu & Kashmir** (now a UT) and **Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, Manipur, Nagaland**. The **Jammu & Kashmir Delimitation Order, 2022** increased assembly seats from 83 to 90 and added 6 Lok Sabha seats, restructuring representation significantly. The **next major national delimitation** is expected after 2026, based on the yet-to-be-conducted census (originally due in 2021 but delayed due to COVID-19).

### Reservation of Seats for SCs and STs

- The Constitution provides for **reservation of seats** for **Scheduled Castes (SCs)** and **Scheduled Tribes (STs)** in the Lok Sabha — based on **population ratios**.
- Originally to operate for **10 years (up to 1960)**, extended every 10 years since.
- Under the **104th Amendment Act, 2019**, this reservation continues until **2030**.

**Key points about reserved seats:**
- Reserved seats are **elected by ALL voters** in the constituency — there is **no separate electorate**.
- An SC/ST member is **NOT barred** from contesting a **general (non-reserved) seat**.
- The **84th Amendment Act (2001)** provided for refixing reserved seats based on **1991 census**.
- The **87th Amendment Act (2003)** changed this to **2001 census**.

### First-Past-The-Post System (FPTP)

The Lok Sabha uses **territorial representation** — also called the **First-Past-The-Post (FPTP) system**:

- Each constituency elects **one representative** (single-member constituency).
- The candidate with the **highest number of votes** wins — even without an absolute majority.

**Why not Proportional Representation (PR) for Lok Sabha?**

The Constituent Assembly rejected PR for the Lok Sabha for two reasons:
1. **Low literacy levels** — voters would find the complex PR system difficult to understand.
2. **Instability** — PR tends to multiply political parties, leading to unstable coalition governments.

**Additional demerits of Proportional Representation:**
- It is **highly expensive**.
- It gives **no scope for by-elections**.
- It **eliminates close contact** between voters and representatives.
- It **promotes minority thinking** and group interests.
- It **increases the significance of party system** and decreases that of the individual voter.

> **Note:** India uses **Proportional Representation by Single Transferable Vote** for elections to the Rajya Sabha, State Legislative Councils, President, and Vice-President.

## ⏳ Duration of the Two Houses

### Duration of Rajya Sabha

- The Rajya Sabha was **first constituted in 1952**.
- It is a **permanent body** — it is **never dissolved**.
- **One-third of its members retire every two years** — their seats are filled by fresh elections/nominations at the beginning of every third year.
- Retiring members are **eligible for re-election/renomination any number of times**.
- The **term of office of a Rajya Sabha member is 6 years** — fixed by the Parliament in the **Representation of the People Act (1951)**, not in the Constitution itself.
- The same Act also empowered the President to curtail the terms of members in the **first Rajya Sabha** (decided by lottery).
- The President made the **Rajya Sabha (Term of Office of Members) Order, 1952** to govern retirement orders.

### Duration of Lok Sabha

- The Lok Sabha is **NOT a permanent body** — it has a **normal term of 5 years** from the date of its first meeting after general elections.
- It **automatically dissolves** after 5 years.
- The **President can dissolve** the Lok Sabha **at any time** even before 5 years — and this **cannot be challenged in court**.
- The term can be **extended during a National Emergency** by Parliament for **one year at a time**, for any length of time. However, this extension **cannot continue beyond 6 months after the Emergency ceases**.

> **Historical Example:** The **Fifth Lok Sabha's** term was extended **twice by one year each** under the House of the People (Extension of Duration) Acts of 1976 — during the Emergency. However, it was dissolved on 18 January 1977, after existing for 5 years, 10 months and 6 days.

## 📋 Membership of Parliament

### Qualifications for Parliament Membership

**Constitutional Qualifications:**
1. Must be a **citizen of India**.
2. Must make and subscribe to an **oath or affirmation** before the person authorised by the Election Commission, swearing to:
   - Bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India
   - Uphold the sovereignty and integrity of India
3. **Age requirement:**
   - Rajya Sabha: Not less than **30 years**
   - Lok Sabha: Not less than **25 years**
4. Must possess **other qualifications prescribed by Parliament**.

**Additional Qualifications (under Representation of People Act, 1951):**
1. Must be **registered as an elector** for a parliamentary constituency (same for both Houses).
   - The requirement that a Rajya Sabha candidate must be an elector of that **specific state** was **dispensed with in 2003**.
   - In **2006**, the Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of this change.
2. Must be a member of a **SC or ST** to contest a reserved seat — but can also contest a general seat.

---

### Disqualifications for Parliament Membership

**Constitutional Disqualifications:**
A person is disqualified if:
1. They hold any **office of profit** under the Union or State government (except minister or any exempted office).
2. They are of **unsound mind** and so declared by a court.
3. They are an **undischarged insolvent** (a person who owes debts and has not been cleared by court).
4. They are **not a citizen of India**, have voluntarily acquired foreign citizenship, or acknowledge allegiance to a foreign state.
5. They are **disqualified under any law** made by Parliament.

**Additional Disqualifications (Representation of People Act, 1951):**
1. Must not have been found guilty of certain **electoral offences or corrupt practices**.
2. Must not have been convicted for any offence resulting in **imprisonment for 2 or more years** (but preventive detention is NOT a disqualification).
3. Must not have **failed to lodge election expense accounts** within the time.
4. Must not have any **interest in government contracts, works or services**.
5. Must not be a **director or managing agent** or hold an office of profit in a corporation where the government has **at least 25% share**.
6. Must not have been **dismissed from government service** for corruption or disloyalty.
7. Must not have been convicted for **promoting enmity between groups** or for bribery.
8. Must not have been punished for **preaching and practising social crimes** such as untouchability, dowry, and sati.

> On whether a member is subject to any disqualification — the **President's decision is final**, but the President must obtain the **opinion of the Election Commission** and act accordingly.

---

### Disqualification on Grounds of Defection (Tenth Schedule)

A member is disqualified under the **anti-defection law** if:
1. They **voluntarily give up membership** of the political party on whose ticket they were elected.
2. They **vote or abstain from voting** contrary to the direction of their party.
3. Any **independently elected member joins any political party**.
4. Any **nominated member joins any political party** after the expiry of **6 months**.

**Who decides?**
- **Rajya Sabha:** The **Chairman**
- **Lok Sabha:** The **Speaker**
- (NOT the President of India)

### 📜 Kihoto Hollohan vs. Zachillhu (1992) — Explained

**Full Case Name:** *Kihoto Hollohan vs. Zachillhu (1992)*

**Background:**
Kihoto Hollohan, a member of the Congress party in a state legislature, challenged the **Tenth Schedule** (anti-defection law, added by the **52nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1985**). He argued that the Tenth Schedule was unconstitutional because:
- It gave **final and binding powers** to the Speaker/Chairman to decide disqualification cases.
- This violated the principles of natural justice and judicial review.
- He also argued it should have been ratified by states (as it affected the powers of state legislatures).

**Supreme Court Ruling (3:2 majority):**
- The Tenth Schedule is **constitutionally valid**.
- However, the decision of the Speaker/Chairman on disqualification **IS subject to judicial review** — specifically by the High Court or Supreme Court — but **ONLY after the final order** is made (not during the proceedings).
- The Court held that the Speaker/Chairman acts as a **"tribunal"** when deciding defection cases, and tribunals are subject to judicial review under Articles 136, 226, and 227.
- The part of the Tenth Schedule that said the Speaker's decision was "final" was read down to mean it is final subject to judicial review.

**Why is this important?**
This case confirmed that even the Speaker's decisions on anti-defection matters are not completely immune from judicial scrutiny — a critical check on potential political misuse of the anti-defection law.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` The anti-defection law and the role of the Speaker in deciding defection petitions has been hugely controversial. In **2023**, the Supreme Court in *Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra* (the Maharashtra political crisis case involving Shiv Sena factions) ruled that the **Speaker cannot decide disqualification petitions when their own position is disputed**, and referred several pending disqualification matters to a newly-elected Speaker. The Court reaffirmed the *Kihoto Hollohan* principle that Speaker's decisions are subject to judicial review and set **3-month timelines** for deciding defection petitions.

### Vacating of Seats

A member of Parliament vacates their seat in the following cases:

#### 1. Double Membership
A person **cannot** be a member of both Houses of Parliament simultaneously.

Under the **Representation of People Act (1951)**:
- If elected to **both Houses**: Must intimate within **10 days** which House they choose. If no intimation — seat in **Rajya Sabha becomes vacant**.
- If a sitting member of one House is **elected to the other House**: Seat in the **first House becomes vacant**.
- If elected to **two seats in a House**: Must exercise option for one. Otherwise, **both seats become vacant**.

Also, a person **cannot** be a member of both Parliament and a state legislature simultaneously. If so elected, the **parliamentary seat becomes vacant** if they do not resign from the state legislature within **14 days** (under the Prohibition of Simultaneous Membership Rules, 1950).

#### 2. Disqualification
If a member becomes subject to any disqualification (including under the Tenth Schedule), their seat becomes vacant.

#### 3. Resignation
A member may resign by writing to:
- **Chairman of Rajya Sabha** (for Rajya Sabha members)
- **Speaker of Lok Sabha** (for Lok Sabha members)

The seat falls vacant **when the resignation is accepted**. The Chairman/Speaker **may refuse** to accept a resignation if they believe it is **not voluntary or genuine**.

#### 4. Absence
If a member is absent from all meetings of the House for **60 days** without permission, the House can declare the seat vacant.
- In counting the 60 days, periods of prorogation or adjournment of **more than 4 consecutive days** are excluded.

#### 5. Other Cases
A member must vacate their seat if:
- Their **election is declared void by court**
- They are **expelled by the House**
- They are **elected President or Vice-President**
- They are **appointed Governor of a state**

> If a **disqualified person is elected** to Parliament, the **Representation of the People Act (1951)** enables the **High Court** to declare the election void. The aggrieved party can appeal to the Supreme Court against the High Court's order.

### Oath or Affirmation

Every member must make and subscribe to an oath or affirmation before the President (or their appointee) before taking their seat. The oath includes:
1. Bearing true faith and allegiance to the **Constitution of India**
2. Upholding the **sovereignty and integrity of India**
3. Faithfully discharging their **duties**

**Consequences of not taking the oath:**
- Cannot vote or participate in proceedings
- Not entitled to parliamentary privileges and immunities
- Subject to a **penalty of ₹500 for each day** they sit or vote without taking the oath, or when they know they are disqualified.

- Basic pension: Increased from ₹20,000 to **₹25,000/month** (2018)

## 🎙️ Presiding Officers of Parliament

Each House has its own presiding officer. There is also a panel system for substitutes.

### Speaker of Lok Sabha

#### Election and Tenure
- Elected by the **Lok Sabha from amongst its members** (as soon as possible after the first sitting).
- Date of election fixed by the **President**.
- Normally remains in office for the **life of the Lok Sabha** (5 years).

**The Speaker vacates office early if:**
1. They cease to be a member of Lok Sabha
2. They resign by writing to the **Deputy Speaker**
3. They are removed by a resolution passed by an **effective majority** (majority of all *then* members of Lok Sabha)

**On removal resolution:**
- **14 days' advance notice** is required before moving such a resolution.
- The Speaker **cannot preside** while the removal resolution is being considered — but can be present, can speak, can vote in **the first instance** (unlike during normal sessions), but **not in case of equality of votes**.

> ⚠️ **Important:** When the Lok Sabha is dissolved, the **Speaker does NOT vacate office** — they continue until the newly elected Lok Sabha meets.

#### Powers and Functions of the Speaker

The Speaker derives powers from three sources:
1. The **Constitution of India**
2. The **Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business of Lok Sabha**
3. **Parliamentary Conventions** (unwritten residuary powers)

**Key Powers and Functions:**

1. **Maintains order and decorum** in the House — this is their primary responsibility with final power.
2. **Final interpreter** of the Constitution, Rules of Procedure, and parliamentary precedents *within the House*.
3. **Adjourns the House** or suspends the meeting in the absence of a quorum (quorum = one-tenth of total House strength).
4. **Does not vote in the first instance** — but casts a **casting vote** (deciding vote in case of a tie).
5. **Presides over joint sittings** of both Houses (summoned by the President to resolve deadlocks on bills).
6. Can allow a **'secret' sitting** of the House at the request of the Leader of the House.
7. **Decides if a bill is a Money Bill** — this decision is **final** and cannot be questioned in any court, either House, or even by the President.
8. **Decides disqualification** of members of the Lok Sabha under the **Tenth Schedule** (anti-defection law) — subject to judicial review (*Kihoto Hollohan case, 1992*).
9. **Ex-officio Chairman** of the **Indian Parliamentary Group** (link between Indian Parliament and world parliaments) and of the **Conference of Presiding Officers** of legislative bodies in the country.
10. **Appoints chairman** of all parliamentary committees of Lok Sabha and supervises their functioning. Is the chairman of the **Business Advisory Committee**, the **Rules Committee**, and the **General Purposes Committee**.

#### Independence and Impartiality of the Speaker

The following provisions ensure the Speaker's independence:

1. **Security of tenure** — can only be removed by **effective majority** (not simple majority); the removal motion needs support of **at least 50 members** to be considered.
2. **Salary and allowances charged on Consolidated Fund of India** — not subject to annual vote of Parliament.
3. Their **work and conduct cannot be discussed** in Lok Sabha except on a substantive motion.
4. Their powers of regulating procedure or maintaining order are **not subject to jurisdiction of any court**.
5. They **cannot vote in the first instance** — can only cast a vote in case of a tie, ensuring impartiality.
6. Given **seventh rank** in the order of precedence — same as the **Chief Justice of India**. This means higher rank than all Cabinet Ministers except the Prime Minister and Deputy Prime Minister.

> **India vs. Britain:** In Britain, the Speaker is a **strictly non-party person** — there is a convention that the Speaker resigns from their party. This convention is **NOT fully established in India** — the Speaker does not resign from party membership.

### Deputy Speaker of Lok Sabha

- Also **elected by the Lok Sabha** from amongst its members — elected **after** the Speaker's election.
- Date of election fixed by the **Speaker**.
- Vacates office in the same three ways as the Speaker (ceasing to be a member, resignation by writing to the Speaker, or removal by effective majority after 14 days' notice).

**Key features:**
- Performs the Speaker's duties when the Speaker's office is **vacant** or the Speaker is **absent**.
- Presides over **joint sittings** when the Speaker is absent.
- The Deputy Speaker is **NOT subordinate to the Speaker** — directly responsible to the House.
- **Special privilege:** Whenever appointed as a member of a parliamentary committee, the Deputy Speaker **automatically becomes its Chairman**.
- Like the Speaker, cannot vote in the first instance — only a casting vote in case of a tie.
- When the Speaker presides, the Deputy Speaker is **like any ordinary member** — can speak, participate, and vote.
- Entitled to regular salary and allowance fixed by Parliament and **charged on the Consolidated Fund of India**.

**Convention:** Usually, the Speaker comes from the **ruling party/alliance**, while the Deputy Speaker post goes to the **opposition party/alliance** (though there are exceptions).

**Both the Speaker and Deputy Speaker do NOT make any separate oath or affirmation** when assuming office.

**Historical Origins:**
- The institutions of Speaker and Deputy Speaker in India originated in **1921** under the **Government of India Act of 1919** (Montague-Chelmsford Reforms).
- They were then called **President and Deputy President** (this continued till 1947).
- Before 1921, the **Governor-General** presided over the Central Legislative Council.
- In **1921**, **Frederick Whyte** was appointed the first Speaker and **Sachidanand Sinha** the first Deputy Speaker of the Central Legislative Assembly (both appointed by the Governor-General).
- In **1925**, **Vithalbhai J. Patel** became the **first Indian and first elected Speaker** of the Central Legislative Assembly.
- The **Government of India Act of 1935** renamed "President" and "Deputy President" to **"Speaker"** and **"Deputy Speaker"** — but the old names continued till 1947 as the federal part of the Act was not implemented.
- **G.V. Mavalankar** = First Speaker of Lok Sabha (held the post from 1946 to 1956 — continuously for one decade, also including Constituent Assembly and provisional Parliament).
- **Ananthasayanam Ayyangar** = First Deputy Speaker of Lok Sabha.

---

### Panel of Chairpersons of Lok Sabha

- The Speaker nominates a panel of **not more than ten chairpersons** from amongst members.
- Any of them can preside in the absence of both the Speaker and Deputy Speaker.
- They have the **same powers as the Speaker** while presiding.
- They hold office till a new panel is nominated.
- If no panel member is present, **any other member** as determined by the House presides.

> ⚠️ A panel member **CANNOT preside** when the office of Speaker **or** Deputy Speaker is **vacant** (not just absent). During vacancy, the President appoints a member to perform Speaker's duties, and elections are held as soon as possible.

---

### Speaker Pro Tem

- As per the Constitution, the Speaker of the last Lok Sabha **vacates office immediately before the first meeting** of the newly-elected Lok Sabha.
- Therefore, the **President appoints a Speaker Pro Tem** (usually the **seniormost member** of the new Lok Sabha).
- The **President administers oath** to the Speaker Pro Tem.
- Has **all powers of the Speaker**.
- **Main duties:**
  - Presides over the first sitting of the newly-elected Lok Sabha
  - Administers oath to new members
  - Enables the House to elect the new Speaker
- Once the new Speaker is elected, the **office of Speaker Pro Tem ceases to exist** (temporary office).

> **Example:** In the 13th Lok Sabha, **Mr. Indrajit Gupta** was appointed Speaker Pro Tem on **20 October 1999** and served until **22 October 1999** when **G.M.C. Balayogi** was elected as the new Speaker.

---

### Chairman of Rajya Sabha

- The presiding officer of Rajya Sabha is the **Chairman**.
- The **Vice-President of India** is the **ex-officio Chairman** of Rajya Sabha.
- When the Vice-President acts as or discharges functions of the President, they do **NOT** perform duties as Chairman of Rajya Sabha.
- The Chairman can be removed from office **only if** removed from the office of Vice-President.

**Powers similar to Speaker:** The Chairman's powers in Rajya Sabha are similar to those of the Speaker in Lok Sabha.

**Two special powers of the Speaker that the Chairman does NOT have:**
1. The Speaker decides whether a bill is a **money bill** — Chairman cannot.
2. The Speaker **presides over joint sittings** — Chairman does not (as the Chairman is not a member of either House).

**Key differences from Speaker:**
- The Chairman is **NOT a member** of Rajya Sabha (unlike the Speaker who is a member of Lok Sabha).
- Like the Speaker, the Chairman **cannot vote in the first instance** — only a casting vote in case of tie.
- When a resolution for the **Chairman's removal** is under consideration, the Vice-President **cannot preside** over Rajya Sabha but **can be present, speak, and participate** — but **cannot vote** (whereas the Speaker CAN vote in the first instance when their removal resolution is being discussed).

**Salary:** Same as Speaker — fixed by Parliament and charged on the Consolidated Fund of India. When the Vice-President acts as President, they receive the **President's salary** instead.

---

### Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha

- **Elected by Rajya Sabha** from amongst its members.
- Vacates office in the same three ways (ceasing to be member, resignation to Chairman, or removal by effective majority with 14 days' notice).
- Performs Chairman's duties when the Chairman's office is vacant or the Vice-President is acting as President.
- Is **NOT subordinate to the Chairman** — directly responsible to Rajya Sabha.
- Like the Chairman — only casting vote (no first-instance vote).
- Cannot preside when a resolution for their own removal is under consideration.
- When Chairman presides, the Deputy Chairman is like any **ordinary member**.
- Entitled to salary and allowance — **charged on Consolidated Fund of India**.

---

### Panel of Vice-Chairpersons of Rajya Sabha

- The Chairman nominates a panel of **vice-chairpersons** from amongst members.
- Any of them can preside in the absence of both Chairman and Deputy Chairman.
- Same powers as Chairman while presiding.
- Same restriction: Cannot preside when Chairman's **or** Deputy Chairman's office is **vacant** — President appoints a member in such cases.

---

### Secretariat of Parliament

- Each House has its own **separate secretarial staff** (though some posts can be common to both).
- Recruitment and service conditions are **regulated by Parliament**.
- Each secretariat is headed by a **Secretary-General** — a permanent officer appointed by the presiding officer of the House.

---

## 👤 Leaders in Parliament

### Leader of the House

**In Lok Sabha:**
- The **Prime Minister**, if a member of Lok Sabha, **OR**
- A minister who is a Lok Sabha member and is **nominated by the Prime Minister** to function as Leader of the House.

**In Rajya Sabha:**
- A minister who is a Rajya Sabha member, nominated by the Prime Minister.

- The Leader of the House exercises **direct influence** on the conduct of business.
- Can nominate a **Deputy Leader of the House**.
- **American equivalent:** Called the **"majority leader"**.

---

### Leader of the Opposition

- Present in **each House of Parliament**.
- Key functions:
  - Provide **constructive criticism** of government policies
  - Provide an **alternative government**
- Accorded **statutory recognition in 1977** under the **Salary and Allowances of Leaders of Opposition in Parliament Act, 1977**.
- Entitled to salary, allowances, and facilities **equivalent to a Cabinet Minister**.
- The office was first recognised in **1969**.
- **American equivalent:** Called the **"minority leader"**.

**Legal definition under the Act:**
The Leader of Opposition is the leader in that House of the **party in opposition having the greatest numerical strength**, recognised by the Chairman/Speaker.

- If two or more opposition parties have the **same numerical strength**, the Chairman/Speaker recognises one of their leaders (having regard to the status of the parties) — this recognition is **final and conclusive**.
- **Minimum numerical strength** for recognition as a political party: **one-tenth of total membership** of that House (per directions of both Chairman and Speaker).

**Britain's Shadow Cabinet:**
- An interesting concept — the Opposition forms a **"Shadow Cabinet"** where every Cabinet Minister is "shadowed" by a corresponding Opposition member.
- The Shadow Cabinet serves as the **"alternate Cabinet"** — ready to take power if the government changes.
- **Ivor Jennings** described the Leader of Opposition as the **"alternative Prime Minister"**.

> **Note:** The offices of Leader of the House and Leader of the Opposition are **NOT mentioned in the Constitution** — they are mentioned in Rules of the House and Parliamentary Statute respectively.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` In the **18th Lok Sabha (2024)**, the Leader of the Opposition post was taken up by **Rahul Gandhi** — the first time in 10 years that the post was officially recognised (in the 16th and 17th Lok Sabhas, no party had the requisite 55-seat strength individually to claim the post). This was a significant development for parliamentary democracy as the Opposition leader's role is crucial for accountability.

---

### Whips

- The office of "whip" is **neither in the Constitution nor in the Rules of the House** — it is based on **parliamentary conventions**.
- Every political party (ruling and opposition) has its own **Chief Whip and Whips** in Parliament.
- The whip serves as an **assistant floor leader**.

**Functions of a whip:**
- Ensure **attendance** of party members in large numbers
- Secure their **support** for or against particular issues
- **Regulate and monitor** members' behaviour in Parliament
- Members must follow directives given by the whip — otherwise **disciplinary action** can be taken

**Chief Whips:**
- Government party in Lok Sabha: **Minister of Parliamentary Affairs**
- Government party in Rajya Sabha: **Minister of State for Parliamentary Affairs**
- The Chief Whip is directly responsible to the **Leader of the House**.

**Statutory recognition:**
- The **Leaders and Chief Whips of Recognised Parties and Groups in Parliament (Facilities) Act, 1998** provides telephone and secretarial facilities to Chief Whips.
- **Recognised party:** Strength of **not less than 55 members** (Lok Sabha) or **25 members** (Rajya Sabha).
- **Recognised group:** Strength of **not less than 30 members** (Lok Sabha) or **15 members** (Rajya Sabha).

---

## 📅 Sessions of Parliament

### Summoning

- The **President summons** each House of Parliament to meet at such time and place as they think fit.
- **Maximum gap between two sessions: 6 months** — Parliament must meet **at least twice a year**.

**Three usual sessions:**
1. **Budget Session** (February to May)
2. **Monsoon Session** (July to September)
3. **Winter Session** (November to December)

**Key terms:**
- **Session:** Period from the **first sitting** to the **prorogation** (or dissolution, for Lok Sabha).
- **Recess:** Period between the prorogation and reassembly in a new session.

---

### Adjournment

- A session consists of many **meetings**. Each meeting of a day has two sittings:
  - Morning sitting: **11 AM to 1 PM**
  - Post-lunch sitting: **2 PM to 6 PM**

- A sitting can be terminated by:
  - **Adjournment** (suspended for a specified time — hours, days, or weeks)
  - **Adjournment sine die** (terminated for an indefinite period)
  - **Prorogation**
  - **Dissolution** (only Lok Sabha)

### Adjournment Sine Die
- **"Sine die"** means "without a day" — when the House is adjourned without naming a date for reassembly.
- Power lies with the **presiding officer**.
- The presiding officer can **call a sitting before the adjourned date** or at any time after adjournment sine die.

### Prorogation
- After the presiding officer declares adjournment sine die (business of session completed), the **President issues a notification for prorogation** within a few days.
- The **President can also prorogue** the House while it is in session.

### Key Differences: Adjournment vs. Prorogation

| Feature | Adjournment | Prorogation |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **What it terminates** | Only a **sitting**, not the session | Both a **sitting AND the session** |
| **Done by** | **Presiding Officer** of the House | **President of India** |
| **Effect on pending bills** | No effect — bills resume when House meets | No effect on pending bills *(Article 107(3))* |
| **Effect on pending notices** | Notices don't lapse | All pending notices (except bill introduction notices) **lapse** — fresh notices needed |
| **British practice** | Same | In Britain, prorogation **ends all pending bills** |

### Dissolution

- **Rajya Sabha** is a permanent body — **never dissolved**.
- Only **Lok Sabha** can be dissolved.
- Unlike prorogation, **dissolution ends the very life** of the existing House — a new House is constituted after fresh elections.

**Two ways dissolution can happen:**
1. **Automatic dissolution:** On expiry of the 5-year term (or extended term during emergency).
2. **Presidential dissolution:** The President decides to dissolve — authorised to do so at any time. **Once dissolved before normal tenure, the dissolution is irrevocable** (cannot be undone).

### Lapsing of Bills on Dissolution

When the Lok Sabha is dissolved:
- Most pending business (bills, motions, resolutions, notices, petitions) **lapse**.
- They must be **reintroduced** in the newly constituted Lok Sabha.

**Specific rules:**

| Situation | Outcome |
|-----------|---------|
| Bill **pending in Lok Sabha** (whether originating in LS or transmitted from RS) | **Lapses** |
| Bill **passed by Lok Sabha but pending in Rajya Sabha** | **Lapses** |
| Bill on which President has **notified a joint sitting** before dissolution | **Does NOT lapse** |
| Bill **pending in Rajya Sabha** but not passed by Lok Sabha | **Does NOT lapse** |
| Bill **passed by both Houses** but pending President's assent | **Does NOT lapse** |
| Bill **passed by both Houses** but returned by President for reconsideration | **Does NOT lapse** |

---

### Quorum

- **Quorum** (minimum members required to conduct business) = **one-tenth of total members** of each House, including the presiding officer.
- **Lok Sabha:** Minimum **55 members** (out of 545 including Speaker).
- **Rajya Sabha:** Minimum **25 members** (out of 245).
- If no quorum, the presiding officer must **adjourn the House** or **suspend the meeting** until quorum is achieved.

---

### Voting in the House

- All matters are decided by a **majority of votes of members present and voting**, excluding the presiding officer.
- Only specific matters require **effective majority** or **special majority**.
- The presiding officer **cannot vote in the first instance** — only **casting vote** in case of a tie.
- Proceedings are **valid** despite any unauthorised voting, participation, or vacancies in membership.

**Voting Procedure in Lok Sabha:**
1. After debate, the Speaker puts the question — invites "**Aye**" and "**No**" responses.
2. Speaker declares whether "Ayes" or "Noes" have it. If unchallenged, the question is decided accordingly.
3. If challenged: The Lobby is cleared; 3 minutes 30 seconds later, Speaker puts the question again. If challenged again, votes are formally recorded.
4. If Speaker thinks a Division (formal vote count) is unnecessary, they may ask Ayes and Noes to rise in their places for a physical count — names not recorded.

**Six Methods of Voting in Lok Sabha:**
1. **Voice Vote** — decided by "Ayes" or "Noes"
2. **Division** — three methods:
   - By **Automatic Vote Recording Equipment**
   - By distributing **'Ayes' and 'Noes' slips**
   - By members going into the **Lobbies** (now obsolete since Automatic Vote Recorder was installed)
3. **Secret Ballot** — LED flashes white light to show vote recorded (no colour shown)
4. **Recording of Votes by Slips** — used when Automatic Equipment fails or at start of new Lok Sabha before seat numbers are allotted
5. **Physical Count of Members** — if Speaker thinks Division is unnecessarily claimed; names not recorded
6. **Casting Vote** — used only when Ayes and Noes are equal

---

### Language in Parliament

- **Hindi and English** are the official languages for transacting business.
- The **presiding officer can permit** a member to address in their **mother tongue**.
- Facilities for **simultaneous interpretation** into Hindi and English are available for all languages in the **Eighth Schedule**.
- If no simultaneous interpretation facility is available for a language, the member must provide a **translation copy** in Hindi or English.
- English was to be discontinued after **15 years** from commencement (i.e., in 1965), but the **Official Languages Act (1963)** allowed English to **continue along with Hindi**.

---

### Rights of Ministers and Attorney General

Every **minister** and the **Attorney General of India** have the right to:
- Speak and take part in proceedings of **either House**
- Participate in any **joint sitting** of both Houses
- Take part in proceedings of any **committee of Parliament** of which they are a member

**But they CANNOT vote** (unless they are members of that House).

**Two reasons for this provision:**
1. A minister from one House can participate in proceedings of the **other House**.
2. A minister who is **not a member of either House** can still participate in both Houses (as a person can remain a minister for **6 months** without being a member of either House).

---

### Lame-Duck Session

- Refers to the **last session of the preceding Lok Sabha** after a new Lok Sabha has been elected.
- Members who could **not get re-elected** to the new Lok Sabha are called **"lame-ducks"**.
- Historical examples: In **1957 and 1962**, one session each of the preceding Lok Sabha was held after elections but before the new Lok Sabha was constituted.
- **Purpose:** To pass the **Vote on Account** to enable the government to carry on until the new Lok Sabha was constituted.
- There was **no lame-duck session of the Third Lok Sabha** as the Fourth Lok Sabha was constituted soon after election results were announced — the Vote on Account was passed in the first session of the new Lok Sabha.

## 🔢 Types of Majority

There are **four types of majority** used in Parliament for different purposes:

### 1. Simple Majority
- **Definition:** Majority of members **present and voting** (excluding absent members and vacancies).
- Also called: **Ordinary majority, Functional majority, or Working majority**.
- **Constitutional basis:** Article 100 — this is the **general rule** for determining questions in Parliament.

**Required for:**
- (a) Passing **ordinary bills, money bills, and financial bills**
- (b) Passing **Adjournment Motion, No-Confidence Motion, Confidence Motion, Censure Motion, and Motion of Thanks**
- (c) **Removal of Vice-President** in Lok Sabha (Article 67)
- (d) **Approval of imposition of President's Rule** (Article 356)
- (e) **Approval of proclamation of Financial Emergency** (Article 360)
- (f) **Election of Speaker and Deputy Speaker** of Lok Sabha (Article 93)
- (g) **Election of Deputy Chairman** of Rajya Sabha (Article 89)
- (h) Passing a **resolution by Lok Sabha disapproving continuance of National Emergency** (Article 352)

---

### 2. Effective Majority
- **Definition:** Majority of the **total membership of the House excluding vacant seats** — i.e., majority of the actual (effective) strength of the House.
- Referred to in the Constitution as **"a majority of all the then members of the House"**.

**Example:**
- If Rajya Sabha has **25 vacant seats**: Effective strength = 220; Effective majority = **111**
- If Lok Sabha has **15 vacant seats**: Effective strength = 528; Effective majority = **265**

**Required for:**
- (a) **Removal of Vice-President** in Rajya Sabha (Article 67)
- (b) **Removal of Deputy Chairman** of Rajya Sabha (Article 90)
- (c) **Removal of Speaker and Deputy Speaker** of Lok Sabha (Article 94)

---

### 3. Absolute Majority
- **Definition:** Majority of the **total membership of the House**, irrespective of vacancies or absentees.
- **Example:**
  - Rajya Sabha: **123** (out of 245)
  - Lok Sabha: **272** (out of 543)

- **Note:** Absolute majority is **NOT prescribed as a standalone requirement** by the Constitution for any specific purpose — but it forms a **component of Special Majority-I**.

---

### 4. Special Majority

This is further divided into three types:

#### Special Majority-I
**Requirement:** Majority of **total membership** of each House (Absolute Majority component) **AND** **two-thirds majority** of members **present and voting**.

**Required for:**
- (a) **Amendment of the Constitution** (Article 368)
- (b) **Removal of Judges of Supreme Court** (Article 124)
- (c) **Removal of Judges of High Courts** (Article 217)
- (d) **Removal of Comptroller and Auditor General** (Article 148)
- (e) **Removal of Chief Election Commissioner** (Article 324)
- (f) **Removal of State Election Commissioner** (Article 243K)
- (g) **Parliamentary approval of proclamation of National Emergency** (Article 352)

#### Special Majority-II
**Requirement:** **Two-thirds majority of total membership** of each House.

**Required for:**
- **Impeachment of the President** (Article 61)

#### Special Majority-III
**Requirement:** **Two-thirds majority of members present and voting** (in Rajya Sabha only).

**Required for:**
- (a) **Recommendation for creation of new All-India Services** (Article 312)
- (b) **Parliamentary legislation on matters in the State List** (Article 249)

## 🏛️ Devices of Parliamentary Proceedings

### Question Hour
- The **first hour of every parliamentary sitting** is for Question Hour.
- Ministers answer questions from members.

**Three kinds of questions:**

| Type | Description | Supplementary Questions? | Colour of List |
|------|-------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Starred Question** *(marked with asterisk)* | Requires **oral answer** | ✅ Yes | **Green** |
| **Unstarred Question** | Requires **written answer** | ❌ No | **White** |
| **Short Notice Question** | Notice of **less than 10 days**; answered **orally** | ✅ Yes | **Light Pink** |
| **Questions to Private Members** | On matters relating to a bill/resolution for which that member is responsible | Same as minister questions | **Yellow** |

---

### Zero Hour
- **Not mentioned** in the Rules of Procedure — it is an **informal device**.
- Members raise matters of **urgent public importance**.
- Starts **immediately after Question Hour** and lasts until the regular business (agenda) begins.
- The time gap between Question Hour and the agenda is the **Zero Hour**.
- **Indian innovation** in parliamentary procedures — has been in existence **since 1962**.

---

### Motions

No discussion on any matter of general public importance can take place **except on a motion** (with the consent of the presiding officer).

**Three principal categories:**

#### 1. Substantive Motion
- A **self-contained, independent proposal** dealing with a very important matter.
- Examples: Impeachment of the President, removal of the Chief Election Commissioner.

#### 2. Substitute Motion
- Moved in **substitution of an original motion** — proposes an alternative.
- If adopted, it **supersedes** the original motion.

#### 3. Subsidiary Motion
- Has no independent meaning — depends on the original motion or proceedings.
- Three sub-categories:
  - **(a) Ancillary Motion:** Used as the regular way of proceeding with various business types.
  - **(b) Superseding Motion:** Moved during debate on another issue — seeks to supersede that issue.
  - **(c) Amendment:** Seeks to modify or substitute only a **part** of the original motion.

---

### Closure Motion
A motion to **cut short the debate** on a matter before the House.

**Four kinds:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Simple Closure** | Member moves that "the matter having been sufficiently discussed be now put to vote" |
| **Closure by Compartments** | Clauses of a bill are grouped into parts before debate; each part debated and voted as a whole |
| **Kangaroo Closure** | Only **important clauses** are debated and voted; intervening clauses are **skipped** and taken as passed |
| **Guillotine Closure** | **Undiscussed clauses** are also put to vote along with discussed ones due to time constraints |

---

### Privilege Motion
- Moved by a member when they feel a **minister has committed a breach of parliamentary privilege** by withholding facts or giving wrong/distorted facts.
- Purpose: To **censure the concerned minister**.

---

### Calling Attention Motion
- Introduced by a member to **call the attention of a minister** to a matter of **urgent public importance** and seek an authoritative statement.
- **Indian innovation** — has been in existence **since 1954**.
- **Unlike Zero Hour:** It is **mentioned in the Rules of Procedure**.

---

### Adjournment Motion
- Introduced to draw the House's attention to a **definite matter of urgent public importance**.
- Needs the support of **50 members** to be admitted.
- Regarded as an **extraordinary device** — interrupts normal business.
- Involves an element of **censure against the government**.
- **Rajya Sabha is NOT permitted** to use this device.
- Discussion must last for **not less than 2 hours and 30 minutes**.

**Restrictions on Adjournment Motion:**
1. Must raise a matter that is **definite, factual, urgent, and of public importance**
2. Should **not cover more than one matter**
3. Should relate to a **specific matter of recent occurrence** — not general terms
4. Should **not raise a question of privilege**
5. Should **not revive discussion** on a matter discussed in the same session
6. Should **not deal with matters under adjudication** by a court
7. Should **not raise a question that can be raised on a distinct motion**

---

### No-Confidence Motion
- Based on **Article 75** — the Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible** to the Lok Sabha.
- The Lok Sabha can **remove the ministry** by passing a no-confidence motion.
- Needs support of **50 members** to be admitted.

---

### Confidence Motion
- A newer procedural device to deal with situations of **hung Parliament, minority governments, and coalition governments**.
- The President may call upon the government to **prove its majority on the floor of the House**.
- Sometimes the government itself seeks to **prove its majority** by moving a confidence motion.
- If the confidence motion is **negated**, the government **falls**.

---

### Censure Motion vs. No-Confidence Motion

| Feature | Censure Motion | No-Confidence Motion |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Reasons required?** | Must **state reasons** for adoption | Need **not state reasons** |
| **Against whom?** | Can be against an **individual minister, group of ministers, or entire Council** | Only against the **entire Council of Ministers** |
| **Purpose** | To censure for **specific policies and actions** | To ascertain **overall confidence** in the Council |
| **Consequence if passed?** | Council of Ministers **need NOT resign** | Council of Ministers **must resign** |

---

### Motion of Thanks
- The President addresses **the first session after each general election** and the **first session of every fiscal year**.
- The address outlines the government's **policies and programmes** for the preceding and ensuing years.
- This corresponds to the **"Speech from the Throne"** in Britain.
- Discussed in both Houses on a motion called the **"Motion of Thanks"**.
- The motion must be **passed** — if it fails, it amounts to the **defeat of the government**.
- Members can use this occasion to raise discussions and criticise the government.

---

### Other Motions (Quick Reference)

| Motion | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **No-Day-Yet-Named Motion** | Admitted by Speaker but **no date fixed** for discussion yet |
| **Dilatory Motion** | Motion to **adjourn the debate** on a bill/motion/resolution or to retard/delay business under consideration; can be moved at any time after a motion has been made |
| **Point of Order** | Raised when proceedings **do not follow normal rules of procedure**; must relate to interpretation/enforcement of Rules or Constitution; **no debate allowed**; usually raised by opposition to control government; **suspends proceedings** before the House |
| **Half-an-Hour Discussion** | For matters of sufficient public importance that have been extensively debated and need elucidation on fact; Speaker can allot **3 days/week**; no formal motion or voting |
| **Short Duration Discussion** | Also called **"two-hour discussion"** — for urgent public importance matters; Speaker allots **2 days/week**; no formal motion or voting; in existence **since 1953** |
| **Special Mention** | For matters that cannot be raised through other devices in **Rajya Sabha**; Lok Sabha equivalent is **"Notice (Mention) Under Rule 377"** |

---

### Resolutions

- A resolution is a **self-contained independent proposal** for the approval of the House — capable of expressing a decision of the House.
- All resolutions are **substantive motions**.
- Discussion must be **strictly relevant** to and within the scope of the resolution.
- A member who has moved a resolution or amendment **cannot withdraw it except with leave of the House**.

**Objectives of a resolution:**
- Declaration of opinion or recommendation
- Recording approval or disapproval of a government act or policy
- Conveying a message, commending, urging, or requesting action
- Calling attention to a matter for government consideration

**Three categories of resolutions:**

1. **Private Member's Resolution:** Moved by a private member (not a minister); discussed only on **alternate Fridays in the afternoon sitting**.

2. **Government Resolution:** Moved by a minister; of three kinds:
   - Approving **international treaties, conventions, or agreements**
   - Declaring or approving **certain government policies**
   - Approving **recommendations of certain committees**

3. **Statutory Resolution:** Can be moved by either a private member or a minister; so called because it is in pursuance of a provision in the **Constitution or an Act of Parliament**.

> **Resolutions vs. Motions:** All resolutions are substantive motions, but not all motions are substantive. The difference is more procedural than content-based — "Quite often both motion and resolution, on the same subject, are admitted with some slight change of form."

---

### Youth Parliament

Started on the recommendation of the **Fourth All India Whips Conference**.

**Objectives:**
1. To acquaint younger generations with **practices and procedures of Parliament**
2. To imbibe the spirit of **discipline and tolerance**, cultivating character in the minds of youth
3. To inculcate **basic values of democracy** and enable students to understand democratic institutions

The **Ministry of Parliamentary Affairs** provides training and encouragement to states in introducing this scheme.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` The **National Youth Parliament Festival** has been held annually since 2019 under the **National Youth Parliament Scheme** by the Ministry of Parliamentary Affairs, with thousands of young participants from schools and colleges across India. This is a direct continuation of the Youth Parliament concept, now significantly expanded in scale under the **MyGov platform**.

## 📜 Legislative Procedure in Parliament

The legislative procedure is **identical in both Houses**. Every bill passes through the **same stages** in each House.

### Types of Bills

**Based on who introduces them:**

| Public Bill (Government Bill) | Private Bill (Private Member's Bill) |
|-------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Introduced by a **minister** | Introduced by any **non-minister MP** |
| Reflects **government's policies** | Does not reflect government's stand |
| **Greater chance** of approval | **Lesser chance** of approval |
| Rejection = **vote of no-confidence** (may lead to resignation) | Rejection has **no implication** on government confidence |
| **7 days' notice** required | **1 month's notice** required |
| Drafted by **concerned department + law department** | Drafted by the **member concerned** |

**Based on content:**
- **(a)** Original bills (new proposals/ideas/policies)
- **(b)** Amending bills (modify/amend/revise existing Acts)
- **(c)** Consolidating bills (consolidate existing laws on a subject)
- **(d)** Expiring Laws (Continuance) bills (continue an expiring Act)
- **(e)** Repealing bills (repeal existing Acts)
- **(f)** Validating bills (give validity to certain actions)
- **(g)** Bills to replace Ordinances
- **(h)** Constitution (Amendment) bills
- **(i)** Money and Financial bills

**Based on procedure (4 broad types):**
1. **Ordinary Bills** — concerned with any non-financial matter
2. **Money Bills** — concerned with financial matters like taxation, public expenditure
3. **Financial Bills** — concerned with financial matters but different from money bills
4. **Constitution Amendment Bills** — concerned with amending the Constitution

---

### Ordinary Bills — Five Stages

#### Stage 1: First Reading
- The bill can be **introduced in either House** by a minister or any member.
- The member **asks for leave** of the House to introduce the bill.
- When granted leave, the member **reads the title and objectives** — no discussion at this stage.
- The bill is **published in the Gazette of India**.
- If published in the Gazette **before introduction**, leave of the House is not necessary.
- Introduction + Gazette publication = **First Reading**.

#### Stage 2: Second Reading (Most Important Stage)
The bill receives **general and detailed scrutiny** — it assumes its final shape here. Contains three sub-stages:

**(a) Stage of General Discussion:**
- Printed copies distributed to all members.
- **Principles and provisions discussed generally** — not the details.
- The House can take one of four actions:
  - (i) Take the bill into consideration **immediately or on a fixed date**
  - (ii) Refer to a **select committee** of the House
  - (iii) Refer to a **joint committee** of both Houses
  - (iv) **Circulate to elicit public opinion**

> **Select Committee:** Members of the House where the bill originated.
> **Joint Committee:** Members of both Houses of Parliament.

**(b) Committee Stage:**
- Usual practice: Refer to a **select committee**.
- Committee examines the bill **clause by clause**.
- Can **amend provisions** but **cannot alter the principles**.
- Reports the bill back to the House.

**(c) Consideration Stage:**
- House considers provisions **clause by clause**.
- Each clause is **discussed and voted upon separately**.
- Members can move **amendments** — if accepted, become part of the bill.

#### Stage 3: Third Reading
- Debate is confined to **acceptance or rejection of the bill as a whole** — no amendments allowed.
- If a **majority of members present and voting** accept the bill, it is passed.
- The bill is **authenticated by the presiding officer** and transmitted to the second House.

#### Stage 4: Bill in the Second House
The bill passes through the same three stages in the second House. The second House has four alternatives:
- **(a)** Pass the bill **without amendments**
- **(b)** Pass the bill **with amendments** — return to first House for reconsideration
- **(c)** **Reject** the bill altogether
- **(d)** Take **no action** (keep pending)

**If the bill is passed without amendments (or first House accepts amendments):** Bill is deemed passed by both Houses → sent to President for assent.

**Deadlock (three situations):**
- First House **rejects the amendments** of the second House, **OR**
- Second House **rejects the bill**, **OR**
- Second House takes **no action for 6 months**

In deadlock cases, the **President can summon a joint sitting** of both Houses. If the majority of members present and voting in the joint sitting approves the bill → deemed passed by both Houses.

#### Stage 5: Assent of the President
Three alternatives before the President:
- **(a) Give assent** → bill becomes an Act, placed on the Statute Book
- **(b) Withhold assent** → bill ends, does not become an Act
- **(c) Return for reconsideration** → If both Houses pass it again (with or without amendments) and present it again, the **President must give assent** (President has only a **"suspensive veto"** — meaning a delay, not an absolute veto)

---

### Money Bills

**Constitutional basis:** Article 110 defines money bills.

A bill is a money bill if it contains **'only'** provisions dealing with **any or all** of these matters:
1. **Imposition, abolition, remission, alteration, or regulation of any tax**
2. **Regulation of borrowing** of money by the Union government
3. **Custody of the Consolidated Fund of India or Contingency Fund** — payment into or withdrawal from these funds
4. **Appropriation of money** out of the Consolidated Fund of India
5. **Declaration of expenditure charged** on the Consolidated Fund or increasing such expenditure
6. **Receipt of money** on account of the Consolidated Fund or Public Account of India, or custody/issue of such money, or **audit of accounts** of the Union or a state
7. Any matter **incidental** to the above

**A bill is NOT a money bill merely because it provides for:**
1. Imposition of **fines or other pecuniary penalties**
2. Demand or payment of **fees for licenses or services**
3. Imposition/regulation of any tax by a **local authority or body** for local purposes

**Key procedural points for Money Bills:**
- **Speaker's certification is final** — cannot be questioned in any court, either House, or by the President.
- Can be **introduced only in Lok Sabha** and only on the **recommendation of the President**.
- Considered to be a **government bill** — introduced only by a minister.
- After Lok Sabha passes it, **transmitted to Rajya Sabha**.
- **Rajya Sabha's restricted powers:**
  - Cannot **reject or amend** a money bill
  - Can only make **recommendations**
  - Must return to Lok Sabha within **14 days** (with or without recommendations)
- **Lok Sabha can accept or reject** any/all recommendations of Rajya Sabha.
- If Lok Sabha **does not accept** any recommendations → bill deemed passed in the form originally passed by Lok Sabha.
- If Rajya Sabha **does not return** the bill within 14 days → bill deemed passed in the form originally passed by Lok Sabha.
- When presented to the President, they may:
  - **Give assent**, or
  - **Withhold assent**
  - But **CANNOT return** the bill for reconsideration (unlike ordinary bills).
  - Normally the President gives assent as the bill is introduced with prior Presidential recommendation.

---

### Financial Bills

**Three kinds of financial bills:**
1. **Money Bills** — Article 110
2. **Financial Bills (I)** — Article 117(1)
3. **Financial Bills (II)** — Article 117(3)

> All money bills are financial bills, but **not all financial bills are money bills**. Only financial bills that exclusively contain matters mentioned in Article 110 are money bills.

#### Financial Bills (I) — Article 117(1)
- Contains **not only** matters of Article 110 **but also** other matters of general legislation.
- Example: A bill with a borrowing clause that doesn't **exclusively** deal with borrowing.
- **Similar to money bill in two respects:**
  - (a) Can be **introduced only in Lok Sabha** (not Rajya Sabha)
  - (b) Can be introduced **only on President's recommendation**
- **Different from money bill in all other respects:** Governed by the **same procedure as ordinary bills**.
  - Rajya Sabha **can reject or amend** it (except that an amendment — other than for reduction/abolition of a tax — cannot be moved in either House without the President's recommendation).
  - A **joint sitting** can be convened in case of disagreement.
  - President can **give assent, withhold assent, or return** for reconsideration.

#### Financial Bills (II) — Article 117(3)
- Contains provisions involving **expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of India** — but **does NOT include** any matters of Article 110.
- Treated as an **ordinary bill** in all respects.
- **Special feature:** Cannot be passed by either House unless the **President has recommended** to that House the consideration of the bill — but this recommendation is required at the **consideration stage**, NOT the introduction stage.
- Can be introduced in **either House** — President's recommendation not needed for introduction.
- Can be **rejected or amended** by either House.
- A **joint sitting** can be convened in case of disagreement.
- President can **give assent, withhold assent, or return** for reconsideration.

---

## 🤝 Joint Sitting of Two Houses

A **joint sitting** is an extraordinary device to resolve a **deadlock** between the two Houses on a bill.

**Three situations constituting a deadlock:**
1. The bill is **rejected by the other House**
2. The Houses have **finally disagreed** on amendments
3. **More than 6 months have elapsed** from the date of receipt of the bill by the other House without it being passed

**Key rules about Joint Sittings:**
- Applicable **only to ordinary bills and financial bills** — NOT to money bills or constitutional amendment bills.
  - For money bills: Lok Sabha has overriding powers.
  - For constitutional amendment bills: Must be passed by each House **separately**.
- In calculating the 6-month period, periods of **prorogation or adjournment of more than 4 consecutive days** are excluded.
- If the Lok Sabha is **already dissolved** when the deadlock occurs and the bill has lapsed — **no joint sitting** can be called.
- But if the **President has already notified** their intention to hold a joint sitting before dissolution — the **joint sitting can proceed** (the bill doesn't lapse in this case).
- After the President notifies intention for a joint sitting — **neither House can proceed** with the bill independently.

**Who presides:**
1. **Speaker of Lok Sabha** (primary)
2. **Deputy Speaker of Lok Sabha** (if Speaker absent)
3. **Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha** (if Deputy Speaker also absent)
4. Any other member as determined by members present (if all above absent)
> Note: The Chairman of Rajya Sabha does NOT preside — as they are not a member of either House.

**Other rules:**
- Quorum = **one-tenth of total members of both Houses**
- Governed by **Rules of Procedure of Lok Sabha** (not Rajya Sabha)
- Bill is passed by a **majority of total members present and voting** — Lok Sabha with greater numbers normally wins.
- **New amendments** cannot be proposed at a joint sitting, **except:**
  1. Amendments that caused the **original disagreement** between Houses
  2. Amendments that became **necessary due to the delay** in passing the bill

**Three instances when Joint Sitting has been held (since 1950):**
1. **Dowry Prohibition Bill, 1960** — Joint sitting held on **6 May 1961** (Lok Sabha did not agree to Rajya Sabha's amendments)
2. **Banking Service Commission (Repeal) Bill, 1977** — Joint sitting held on **16 May 1978** (bill rejected by Rajya Sabha)
3. **Prevention of Terrorism Bill, 2002** — Joint sitting held on **26 March 2002** (bill rejected by Rajya Sabha); passed when **425 members voted for** and **296 against**

## 💰 Budget in Parliament

### What is a Budget?

The Constitution refers to the budget as the **"annual financial statement"** — the word "budget" is **nowhere used in the Constitution**. It has been dealt with in **Article 112**.

**Definition:** The budget is a statement of the **estimated receipts and expenditure** of the Government of India for a financial year (1 April to 31 March of the following year).

**Contents of the Budget:**
1. **Estimates of revenue and capital receipts**
2. **Ways and means to raise revenue**
3. **Estimates of expenditure**
4. **Details of actual receipts and expenditure** of the closing financial year and reasons for any deficit or surplus
5. **Economic and financial policy** of the coming year — taxation proposals, spending programmes, new schemes/projects

---

### Merger of Railway Budget with General Budget

- Till 2017, India had **two budgets:**
  - **Railway Budget:** Estimates of receipts and expenditures of only the Ministry of Railways
  - **General Budget:** Estimates of all other ministries

- The Railway Budget was separated from the General Budget in **1924** on the recommendations of the **Acworth Committee Report (1921)**.

**Reasons for the 1924 separation:**
1. To introduce **flexibility** in railway finance
2. To facilitate a **business approach** to railway policy
3. To **secure stability** of general revenues by providing an assured annual contribution from railway revenues
4. To enable railways to **keep their profits** for their own development (after paying a fixed annual contribution to general revenues)

- In **2017**, the Central Government **merged the Railway Budget into the General Budget** → Now there is only **one Union Budget** for the Government of India.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` Since the merger in 2017, the Union Budget now presents a comprehensive picture of government finances. The **Union Budget 2024-25** (presented on 23 July 2024 by Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman — her 7th consecutive budget, a record) had total expenditure of approximately **₹48.21 lakh crore**. The budget also introduced significant changes to capital gains tax and revised income tax slabs under the new tax regime.

---

### Constitutional Provisions on Budget

1. The President shall cause to be laid before both Houses a statement of **estimated receipts and expenditure** every financial year — Article 112
2. **No demand for a grant** shall be made except on the recommendation of the President — Article 113
3. **No money shall be withdrawn** from the Consolidated Fund of India except under appropriation made by law — Article 114
4. **No money bill imposing tax** shall be introduced except on the President's recommendation; shall not be introduced in Rajya Sabha — Article 117
5. **No tax shall be levied or collected** except by authority of law — Article 265
6. Parliament can **reduce or abolish a tax but cannot increase it** — Article 117
7. **Relative roles of Houses:**
   - (a) Money bill/finance bill on taxation must be introduced **only in Lok Sabha** — Article 109
   - (b) **Rajya Sabha has no power to vote** on demands for grants — Article 113
   - (c) Rajya Sabha must return the Money bill to Lok Sabha within **14 days** — Article 109
8. Budget shall separately show **expenditure charged on** the Consolidated Fund and expenditure **made from** it — Article 112
9. Budget shall **distinguish revenue expenditure from other expenditure** — Article 112
10. **Charged expenditure** shall not be submitted to vote of Parliament — but can be discussed — Article 113
11. Lok Sabha can **approve, refuse, or reduce** a demand — but **cannot increase it** — Article 113
12. **No amendment** to the Appropriation Bill that would vary the amount or alter the destination of any voted grant, or vary charged expenditure — Article 114
13. Lok Sabha can make a grant in advance (**Vote on Account**) for part of the financial year pending completion of budget procedure — Article 116

---

### Charged Expenditure (Non-Votable)

The budget has two types of expenditure:
1. **Charged on the Consolidated Fund of India** — **non-votable** (can only be discussed, not voted upon)
2. **Made from the Consolidated Fund of India** — **votable** (must be voted by Parliament)

**List of Charged Expenditure:**
1. Emoluments and allowances of the **President** and office expenditure
2. Salaries and allowances of the **Chairman and Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha and Speaker and Deputy Speaker of Lok Sabha**
3. Salaries, allowances and pensions of **judges of the Supreme Court**
4. **Pensions of judges of High Courts**
5. Salary, allowances and pension of the **Comptroller and Auditor General of India**
6. Salaries, allowances and pension of the **Chairman and members of the Union Public Service Commission**
7. **Administrative expenses** of the Supreme Court, CAG office, and UPSC (including salaries/pensions of their staff)
8. **Debt charges** of the Government of India — interest, sinking fund charges, redemption charges, and other loan-related expenditure
9. Any sum required to satisfy any **judgment, decree, or award** of any court or arbitral tribunal
10. Any other expenditure **declared by Parliament** to be so charged

---

### Six Stages in Enactment of Budget

#### Stage 1: Presentation of Budget
- Conventionally presented by the Finance Minister on the **last working day of February**.
- **Since 2017:** Advanced to **1st February**.
- Can be presented in **two or more parts** — each part dealt with as if it were the full budget.
- **No discussion** on the day of presentation.
- Finance Minister delivers the **"budget speech"**.
- At the end of the speech in Lok Sabha, budget is **laid before Rajya Sabha** (which can only discuss, not vote on demands for grants).

**Budget documents presented to Parliament include:**
- (i) Budget Speech
- (ii) Annual Financial Statement
- (iii) Demands for Grants
- (iv) Finance Bill
- (v) **FRBM Act** statements: Macro-Economic Framework Statement, Fiscal Policy Strategy Statement, Medium Term Fiscal Policy Statement
- (vi) Expenditure Budget
- (vii) Receipts Budget
- (viii) Expenditure Profile
- (ix) Memorandum Explaining Provisions in Finance Bill
- (x) Budget at a Glance
- (xi) Outcome Budget (Output-Outcome Monitoring Framework)
- (xii) Key Features of the Budget
- (xiii) Implementation of Previous Budget Announcements
- (xiv) Key to Budget Documents

> The **Economic Survey** (prepared by the Finance Ministry, indicating the state of the national economy) is now presented **one day or a few days before** the budget (earlier it was presented along with the budget).

#### Stage 2: General Discussion
- Begins a few days after presentation.
- Takes place in **both Houses** — usually lasts **3 to 4 days**.
- Lok Sabha can discuss the budget **as a whole** or any principle — but **no cut motion** and no vote at this stage.
- Finance Minister has a **general right of reply** at the end.

#### Stage 3: Scrutiny by Departmental Committees
- After general discussion, Houses are **adjourned for 3 to 4 weeks**.
- **24 departmental standing committees** of Parliament examine and discuss **demands for grants** of concerned ministries in detail.
- Reports submitted to **both Houses** for consideration.
- The standing committee system was **established in 1993** (expanded in 2004) — makes parliamentary financial control more **detailed, close, in-depth, and comprehensive**.

#### Stage 4: Voting on Demands for Grants
- In light of the standing committee reports, **Lok Sabha takes up voting of demands for grants**.
- Demands are presented **ministry-wise**.
- A demand becomes a **grant** after it is duly voted.
- **Voting on demands for grants is the exclusive privilege of Lok Sabha** — Rajya Sabha has no power.
- Voting is confined to the **votable part** of the budget (not the charged expenditure).
- Each demand is **voted separately** by the Lok Sabha.

**Cut Motions** — Three kinds:

| Type | What it says | Purpose |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Policy Cut Motion** | Amount of demand be reduced to **₹1** | Disapproval of the **policy** underlying the demand |
| **Economy Cut Motion** | Amount be reduced by a **specified amount** (lump sum or specific item) | Represent **economies** that can be made |
| **Token Cut Motion** | Amount be reduced by **₹100** | To ventilate a **specific grievance** within the government's responsibility |

**Admissibility conditions for cut motions:**
1. Should relate to **one demand only**
2. Must be **clearly expressed** — no arguments or defamatory statements
3. Must be confined to **one specific matter**
4. Must not suggest **amendment or repeal of existing laws**
5. Must not refer to a matter **not primarily the concern of Union government**
6. Must not relate to **charged expenditure**
7. Must not relate to a matter **under court adjudication**
8. Must not **raise a question of privilege**

**Significance of cut motions:**
- (a) Facilitates **concentrated discussion** on a specific demand for grant
- (b) Upholds **responsible government** by probing government activities

> In practice, cut motions are only moved and discussed — **rarely passed** because the government has majority support. If passed, it amounts to a vote of no-confidence and may lead to government resignation.

**Guillotine:** On the **last day** allotted for voting on demands, the Speaker **puts all remaining demands to vote** (whether discussed or not) — this is called the **"guillotine"**.

#### Stage 5: Passing of Appropriation Bill
- After demands for grants are voted, an **Appropriation Bill** is introduced to legalise the withdrawal of money from the Consolidated Fund of India to meet:
  - (a) The **grants voted** by Lok Sabha
  - (b) The **charged expenditure**
- No amendment can be proposed that would vary the amount or alter the destination of any voted grant or vary charged expenditure.
- After Presidential assent → becomes the **Appropriation Act** — authorises (legalises) all payments from the Consolidated Fund.

**Vote on Account:**
- The government cannot withdraw money from the Consolidated Fund until the Appropriation Bill is enacted (usually by end of April — after the financial year begins on 1 April).
- To bridge this gap, the Constitution authorises Lok Sabha to make a **Vote on Account** — a grant in advance for part of the financial year.
- Passed after the **general discussion on budget** is over.
- Generally granted for **2 months** for an amount equivalent to **one-sixth of total estimation**.
- In an **election year**: Vote on Account may be for **3 to 5 months**.

> **Since 2017:** Budget is presented on 1st February (advanced by one month). This allows Parliament to avoid a Vote on Account and **pass a single Appropriation Bill** before the close of the financial year. Vote on Account is now presented only in election years (when Interim Budget is presented).

**Interim Budget:**
- In a **general election year** for Lok Sabha, an **Interim Budget** (not a full budget) is presented.
- After elections and assumption of office by the new government, a **Regular Budget** is presented on a date decided by the new government.

#### Stage 6: Passing of Finance Bill
- The **Finance Bill** gives effect to the **financial (taxation) proposals** of the government for the next financial year.
- Subjected to all conditions applicable to a **Money Bill**.
- Unlike the Appropriation Bill, **amendments seeking to reject or reduce a tax** CAN be moved.
- During this stage, members can discuss general administration, local grievances, monetary/financial policy.
- Must be **enacted within 75 days** under the **Provisional Collection of Taxes Act of 1931** (passed by Parliament and assented to by President).
- The **Finance Act legalises the income side** of the budget — completes the budgetary process.

---

### Other Grants

| Grant | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Supplementary Grant** | When authorised amount for a service in the current year is **insufficient** |
| **Additional Grant** | When a need arises for **additional expenditure on a new service** not in the original budget |
| **Excess Grant** | When money has been **spent in excess** of the budgeted amount; voted by Lok Sabha **after the financial year**; must be approved by **Public Accounts Committee** before being submitted to Lok Sabha |
| **Vote of Credit** | For **unexpected demands** whose magnitude or indefinite character cannot be stated with usual budget detail — like a "blank cheque" to the Executive |
| **Exceptional Grant** | For a **special purpose** — not part of any financial year's current service |
| **Token Grant** | When funds can be made available by **reappropriation** (transfer of funds from one head to another — no additional expenditure) — a token sum is voted |

> Supplementary, additional, excess, and exceptional grants and vote of credit are governed by the **same procedure as the regular budget**.

---

### Three Funds of the Central Government

#### 1. Consolidated Fund of India (Article 266)
- **All receipts credited and all payments debited** to this fund.
- Includes: All revenues received by Government, all loans raised (treasury bills, etc.), all loan repayments received.
- All legally authorised payments on behalf of Government are made from this fund.
- **No money can be withdrawn** except in accordance with a **parliamentary law**.

#### 2. Public Account of India (Article 266)
- All **other public money** received by or on behalf of Government (other than Consolidated Fund).
- Includes: **Provident fund deposits, judicial deposits, savings bank deposits, departmental deposits, remittances**, etc.
- **Operated by executive action** — payments can be made **without parliamentary appropriation**.
- Such payments are mostly **banking transactions** in nature.

#### 3. Contingency Fund of India (Article 267)
- Parliament established this through the **Contingency Fund of India Act, 1950**.
- Placed at the disposal of the **President**.
- The President can make **advances out of it to meet unforeseen expenditure** — pending parliamentary authorisation.
- Held by the **Finance Secretary** on behalf of the President.
- Like the Public Account, it is also **operated by executive action**.

## 🌟 Multifunctional Role of Parliament

Parliament has **7 categories of powers and functions:**

### 1. Legislative Powers and Functions
- Primary function: **Making laws** for the governance of the country.
- **Exclusive power** over subjects in the **Union List** (currently **98 subjects**, originally 97) and **Residuary subjects**.
- **Concurrent List** (currently **52 subjects**, originally 47): Parliament has **overriding powers** — parliamentary law prevails over state law in conflict.
- Parliament can also make laws on **State List** (currently **59 subjects**, originally 66) in **5 abnormal circumstances:**
  - (a) When **Rajya Sabha passes a resolution** to that effect
  - (b) When a **National Emergency** is in operation
  - (c) When **two or more states make a joint request**
  - (d) When necessary to give effect to **international agreements, treaties, and conventions**
  - (e) When **President's Rule** is in operation in the state
- All **Ordinances** issued by the President (during Parliamentary recess) must be **approved by Parliament within 6 weeks** after reassembly — otherwise become inoperative.
- Parliament makes **skeleton laws** and authorises the Executive to make detailed rules — called **"delegated legislation"** (also executive legislation or subordinate legislation). Such rules are placed before Parliament for examination.

### 2. Executive Powers and Functions
- The Executive is **responsible to Parliament** for its policies and acts.
- Parliament exercises control through: **Question Hour, Zero Hour, Half-an-Hour Discussion, Short Duration Discussion, Calling Attention Motion, Adjournment Motion, No-Confidence Motion, Censure Motion**, and other discussions.
- Parliament also supervises through committees: **Committee on Government Assurances, Committee on Subordinate Legislation, Committee on Petitions**, etc.
- Ministers are **collectively responsible** to Parliament, specifically to Lok Sabha.
- **Individual responsibility** — each minister is responsible for the efficient administration of their ministry.
- Ministers continue in office as long as they enjoy **confidence of the majority** in Lok Sabha.
- Council of Ministers can be removed by Lok Sabha through a **no-confidence motion**.
- Lok Sabha can also express lack of confidence through:
  - (a) Not passing the **Motion of Thanks** on the President's address
  - (b) **Rejecting a money bill**
  - (c) Passing a **censure motion or adjournment motion**
  - (d) **Defeating the government** on a vital issue
  - (e) Passing a **cut motion**

> "The first function of Parliament can be said to be to select the group which is to form the government, support and sustain it in power so long as it enjoys its confidence, and to expel it when it ceases to do so." — N.N. Mallya, *Indian Parliament*, p. 39

### 3. Financial Powers and Functions
- **No tax can be levied or collected** without Parliamentary approval.
- **No expenditure** can be incurred by the Executive without Parliamentary authority.
- Parliament scrutinises spending through **financial committees:**
  - **Public Accounts Committee**
  - **Estimates Committee**
  - **Committee on Public Undertakings**
- These bring out cases of **illegal, irregular, unauthorised, improper usage, wastage, and extravagance** in public expenditure.
- **Two stages of financial control:**
  - (a) **Budgetary control:** Before appropriation of grants — through enactment of budget
  - (b) **Post-budgetary control:** After appropriation of grants — through three financial committees
- **Rule of Lapse:** Budget is based on the principle of **annuity** — if money is not spent by end of the financial year, the balance **expires** and returns to the Consolidated Fund. This prevents reserve funds from being built without parliamentary authorisation — but leads to **"March Rush"** (heavy expenditure rush at year-end).

### 4. Constituent Powers and Functions
- Parliament has the power to **amend the Constitution** by addition, variation, or repeal.
- Three methods:
  - (a) By **simple majority**
  - (b) By **special majority**
  - (c) By **special majority** + consent of **at least half of State Legislatures** (by simple majority)
- Only Parliament can **initiate** constitutional amendments (not state legislatures), with one exception: states can pass a resolution requesting Parliament to **create or abolish the Legislative Council** in the state.
- Constituent power is **not unlimited** — subject to the **"basic structure"** of the Constitution (*Kesavananda Bharati case, 1973*).

### 5. Judicial Powers and Functions
- (a) **Impeach the President** for violation of the Constitution
- (b) **Remove the Vice-President** from office
- (c) **Recommend removal** to the President of:
  - Judges (including Chief Justice) of Supreme Court and High Courts
  - Chief Election Commissioner
  - State Election Commissioner
  - Comptroller and Auditor General
- (d) **Punish its members or outsiders** for breach of its privileges or contempt of the House

### 6. Electoral Powers and Functions
- Participates in the **election of the President** (along with state legislative assemblies)
- **Elects the Vice-President**
- Lok Sabha elects its **Speaker and Deputy Speaker**
- Rajya Sabha elects its **Deputy Chairman**
- Authorised to make laws to **regulate elections** to President, Vice-President, Parliament, and State Legislatures.
- Key Acts enacted:
  - **Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act (1952)**
  - **Representation of the People Act (1950)**
  - **Representation of the People Act (1951)**

### 7. Other Powers and Functions
- (a) Serves as the **highest deliberative body** — discusses national and international issues
- (b) **Approves all three types of emergencies** (National, State, and Financial) proclaimed by the President
- (c) Can **create or abolish state legislative councils** on the recommendation of the concerned state legislative assemblies
- (d) Can **increase or decrease areas, alter boundaries, and change names of states**
- (e) Can **regulate the organisation and jurisdiction** of the Supreme Court and High Courts; can establish a **common High Court** for two or more states

## ⚠️ Ineffectiveness of Parliamentary Control

Parliamentary control over government and administration is **more theoretical than practical**. The following factors contribute to its ineffectiveness:

- **(a)** Parliament has **neither time nor expertise** to control an administration that has grown in volume and complexity.
- **(b)** Parliament's **financial control is hindered** by the technical nature of demands for grants — parliamentarians cannot fully understand them.
- **(c) Legislative leadership lies with the Executive** — it plays a significant role in formulating policies.
- **(d)** The **very size of Parliament is too large** and unmanageable to be effective.
- **(e)** The **majority support enjoyed by the Executive** in Parliament reduces the possibility of effective criticism.
- **(f)** Financial committees like the **Public Accounts Committee** examine expenditure **after it has been incurred** — they do **post-mortem work**.
- **(g)** Increased recourse to the **"guillotine"** has reduced the scope of financial control.
- **(h)** Growth of **"delegated legislation"** has reduced Parliament's role in making detailed laws and increased the powers of bureaucracy.
- **(i)** Frequent promulgation of **Ordinances by the President** dilutes Parliament's power of legislation.
- **(j)** Parliament's control is **sporadic, general, and mostly political** in nature.
- **(k)** **Lack of strong and steady opposition** and setbacks in parliamentary behaviour and ethics have also contributed to ineffectiveness.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` These concerns about ineffectiveness have been widely discussed in recent years. India's **Parliamentary productivity** has been declining — the **Winter Session 2023** saw one of the lowest productivities in recent times, with **143 Members of Parliament suspended** (the largest mass suspension in Indian parliamentary history). Critics argued this severely impaired parliamentary oversight. The **FRBM (Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management) Act, 2003** was enacted to address some financial ineffectiveness by mandating fiscal discipline targets and transparency.

## ⚖️ Position of Rajya Sabha

### Where Rajya Sabha is Equal to Lok Sabha

In the following matters, Rajya Sabha has **equal powers and status** as Lok Sabha:

1. Introduction and passage of **ordinary bills**
2. Introduction and passage of **Constitutional amendment bills**
3. Introduction and passage of **financial bills involving expenditure from the Consolidated Fund**
4. **Election and impeachment** of the President
5. **Election of Vice-President** (notably, Rajya Sabha **alone** can **initiate removal** of the Vice-President — by effective majority — agreed to by Lok Sabha by simple majority)
6. Making **recommendations to President for removal** of Chief Justice and judges of SC/HCs, Chief Election Commissioner, and CAG
7. **Approval of Ordinances** issued by the President
8. **Approval of all three types of emergencies** proclaimed by the President
9. **Selection of ministers** including the Prime Minister (ministers can be members of either House — though they are responsible only to Lok Sabha)
10. Consideration of reports of **constitutional bodies** like Finance Commission, UPSC, CAG, etc.
11. **Enlargement of jurisdiction** of the Supreme Court and UPSC

---

### Where Rajya Sabha is Unequal (Weaker) than Lok Sabha

1. A **Money Bill** can be **introduced only in Lok Sabha** — not in Rajya Sabha.
2. Rajya Sabha **cannot amend or reject a Money Bill** — must return within 14 days with/without recommendations.
3. Lok Sabha can **accept or reject all or any recommendations** — in both cases, the bill is deemed passed by both Houses.
4. A **Financial Bill (I)** (Article 117) can also be introduced **only in Lok Sabha** (though both Houses have equal powers in its passage).
5. The **final power to decide** if a bill is a Money Bill lies with the **Speaker of Lok Sabha**.
6. The **Speaker of Lok Sabha presides** over joint sittings.
7. In a joint sitting, Lok Sabha with **greater numbers usually wins** (except when the combined strength of the ruling party in both Houses is less than that of the opposition parties).
8. Rajya Sabha can only **discuss the budget** — it **cannot vote on demands for grants** (exclusive privilege of Lok Sabha).
9. A resolution for **discontinuance of National Emergency** can be passed **only by Lok Sabha**, not Rajya Sabha.
10. **Rajya Sabha cannot remove the Council of Ministers** by a no-confidence motion (Council is collectively responsible only to Lok Sabha). But Rajya Sabha can **discuss and criticise** government policies.

---

### Special Powers of Rajya Sabha (Exclusive Powers)

Rajya Sabha has **4 exclusive powers** not enjoyed by Lok Sabha:

1. Can **authorise Parliament to make a law on a State List subject** (Article 249) — by a two-thirds majority of members present and voting.
2. Can **authorise Parliament to create new All-India Services** common to Centre and states (Article 312) — by two-thirds majority of members present and voting.
3. **Alone can initiate the removal of the Vice-President** — a resolution for removal of the Vice-President can be introduced **only in Rajya Sabha**, not Lok Sabha (Article 67).
4. If a **proclamation of National Emergency, President's Rule, or Financial Emergency** is issued when the **Lok Sabha has been dissolved** (or dissolution takes place within the period allowed for its approval), the proclamation can remain effective if **approved by Rajya Sabha alone** (Articles 352, 356, and 360).

**Overall assessment of Rajya Sabha:**
- Not as weak as the **House of Lords** in the British system.
- Not as strong as the **Senate** in the American system.
- Except in financial matters and control over the Council of Ministers, Rajya Sabha's powers are **broadly equal and coordinate** with Lok Sabha.

**Why Rajya Sabha is useful:**
1. **Checks hasty, defective, or ill-considered legislation** made by Lok Sabha — provides revision and second thought.
2. Facilitates **representation of eminent professionals and experts** who cannot face direct elections (12 nominated members).
3. **Maintains federal equilibrium** by protecting state interests against undue interference of the Centre.

## 🛡️ Parliamentary Privileges

### Meaning

Parliamentary privileges are **special rights, immunities, and exemptions** enjoyed by:
- The two Houses of Parliament
- Their committees
- Their members

They are necessary to secure the **independence and effectiveness** of parliamentary actions. Without them, Houses cannot maintain their **authority, dignity, and honour**, nor can they protect members from obstruction in discharging their duties.

> **Extended to:** Persons entitled to speak and take part in proceedings — including the **Attorney General of India** and **Union Ministers**.
> **NOT extended to:** The **President** (even though an integral part of Parliament).

---

### Classification of Parliamentary Privileges

#### 🏛️ Collective Privileges (Privileges of each House collectively):

1. Right to **publish its reports, debates, and proceedings** — AND right to **prohibit others from publishing** the same.
   - The **44th Amendment Act of 1978** restored the **freedom of the press** to publish true reports of parliamentary proceedings **without prior permission**.
   - Exception: This does **not apply** in case of a **secret sitting** of the House.
2. Can **exclude strangers** from its proceedings and hold **secret sittings** for important matters.
3. Can **make rules** to regulate its own procedure, conduct of business, and adjudicate upon such matters.
4. Can **punish members and outsiders** for breach of privileges or contempt — by **reprimand, admonition, or imprisonment** (and **suspension or expulsion** in case of members).
   > Historical example: In 1977, the Sixth Lok Sabha expelled **Mrs. Indira Gandhi** from membership and sentenced her to **jail for a week** for contempt of House. But the Seventh Lok Sabha **rescinded** this resolution as politically motivated. In 1990, a former minister, **K.K. Tiwari**, was reprimanded by the Rajya Sabha.
5. Right to receive **immediate information** of the arrest, detention, conviction, imprisonment, and release of a member.
6. Can **institute inquiries, order attendance of witnesses**, and send for relevant papers and records.
7. **Courts are prohibited** from inquiring into the proceedings of a House or its committees.
8. **No person can be arrested** and **no legal process** (civil or criminal) can be served **within the precincts of the House** without the permission of the presiding officer.

#### 👤 Individual Privileges (Privileges of each member individually):

1. **Cannot be arrested** during the session of Parliament and **40 days before the beginning** and **40 days after the end** of a session.
   - Available **only in civil cases** — NOT in criminal cases or preventive detention cases.
2. **Freedom of speech in Parliament** — no member is liable to any court proceedings for anything said or any vote given in Parliament or its committees.
   - Subject to the provisions of the Constitution and the rules and standing orders of Parliament.
   - Note: Article 121 prohibits discussion in Parliament on the **conduct of any judge** of the Supreme Court or High Court in discharge of duties — except on a motion for removing the judge.
3. Exempted from **jury service**. Can **refuse to give evidence** and appear as a witness when Parliament is in session.

---

### Breach of Privilege and Contempt of the House

**Breach of Privilege:**
> "When any individual or authority disregards or attacks any of the privileges, rights and immunities, either of the member individually or of the House in its collective capacity, the offence is termed as breach of privilege and is punishable by the House." — M.N. Kaul and S.L. Shakdher

**Contempt of the House:**
> Any act or omission which obstructs a House of Parliament, its member or its officer in the performance of their functions, or which has a tendency — directly or indirectly — to produce results against the dignity, authority, and honour of the House.

**Key distinction:**
- **Contempt is NOT always a breach of privilege** (it's a broader concept)
- **A breach of privilege IS always contempt**
- Actions that are not breaches of any specific privilege but offend the **dignity and authority** of the House amount to **contempt**.
- Example: **Disobedience to a legitimate order** of the House is NOT a breach of privilege, but CAN be punished as contempt.

---

### Sources of Parliamentary Privileges

Originally, the Constitution (Article 105) expressly mentioned two privileges:
1. **Freedom of speech in Parliament**
2. **Right of publication of its proceedings**

For other privileges, the Constitution provided that they were to be the same as those of the **British House of Commons** on 26 January 1950 (commencement date).

The **44th Amendment Act of 1978** changed the reference date to **20 June 1979** (its own commencement date) — and **dropped the direct reference to the British House of Commons** (but didn't actually change the substance).

> The then Law Minister explained: "A proud country like India would like to avoid making any reference to a foreign institution in its own solemn constitutional document."

Parliament has **NOT yet made any special law** to exhaustively codify all privileges.

**Five sources of parliamentary privileges:**
1. **Constitutional provisions**
2. **Various laws made by Parliament**
3. **Rules of both Houses**
4. **Parliamentary conventions**
5. **Judicial interpretations**

## 🏆 Sovereignty of Parliament

### The British Concept
The doctrine of **"sovereignty of Parliament"** is associated with the **British Parliament**.

According to **A.V. Dicey** (British jurist), the principle has three implications:
1. Parliament can **make, amend, substitute, or repeal any law**. (De Lolme said: *"The British Parliament can do everything except make a woman a man and a man a woman."*)
2. Parliament can **make constitutional laws by the same procedure as ordinary laws** — no distinction between constituent and legislative authority.
3. **Parliamentary laws cannot be declared invalid** by the judiciary — there is **no judicial review** in Britain.

### Why Indian Parliament is NOT Sovereign

The Indian Parliament **cannot be regarded as sovereign** in the British sense — there are **legal restrictions** on its authority and jurisdiction.

**Four factors limiting Indian Parliamentary sovereignty:**

#### 1. Written Nature of the Constitution
- The Constitution is the **fundamental law of the land** — it defines the authority of all three organs.
- Parliament must **operate within constitutional limits**.
- There is a **legal distinction** between legislative authority and constituent authority.
- To amend certain provisions, **ratification of half the states** is also required.
- Britain has **no written constitution** and no concept of a fundamental law.

#### 2. Federal System of Government
- India has a **federal system** with **constitutional division of powers** between Union and states.
- Parliament's law-making authority is **confined to Union List and Concurrent List** — does not extend to State List (except in 5 abnormal circumstances).
- Britain has a **unitary system** — all powers vested in the Centre.

#### 3. System of Judicial Review
- India has an **independent judiciary** with the **power of judicial review**.
- The Supreme Court and High Courts can **declare parliamentary laws void** if they contravene the Constitution (**ultra vires** — beyond the powers conferred).
- In Britain, courts **cannot question the constitutionality** of Parliamentary laws — they must apply them.

#### 4. Fundamental Rights
- Part III of the Constitution incorporates a **code of justiciable Fundamental Rights**.
- **Article 13** prohibits the State from making any law that takes away or abridges Fundamental Rights.
- Any Parliamentary law that **contravenes Fundamental Rights** shall be **void**.
- Britain has **no codified fundamental rights** — but British citizens have maximum liberty through the **Rule of Law**.

### Overall Comparison

| Feature | India | Britain | USA |
|---------|-------|---------|-----|
| **Written Constitution?** | ✅ Yes | ❌ No | ✅ Yes |
| **Parliamentary Sovereignty?** | ❌ Limited | ✅ Yes | ❌ Limited |
| **Judicial Review?** | ✅ Yes | ❌ No | ✅ Yes |
| **Fundamental Rights in Constitution?** | ✅ Yes | ❌ No (but Rule of Law) | ✅ Yes (Bill of Rights) |
| **Federal System?** | ✅ Yes (quasi-federal) | ❌ No (Unitary) | ✅ Yes |

> **Conclusion:** "Unlike the British Parliament, the authority and jurisdiction of the Indian Parliament are **defined, limited, and restrained**. In this regard, the Indian Parliament is similar to the American Legislature (Congress)." — Sovereignty of both is restricted by written Constitution, federalism, judicial review, and fundamental rights.

## 📌 Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Quick Explanation |
|------|------------------|
| **Westminster Model** | Parliamentary system based on British Parliament |
| **Proportional Representation** | Seats distributed proportionally based on votes |
| **Single Transferable Vote** | Voters rank candidates; votes transfer if candidate is elected/eliminated |
| **Universal Adult Franchise** | Every adult citizen has the right to vote |
| **Prorogation** | Termination of a parliamentary session by the President |
| **Adjournment Sine Die** | Adjournment without naming a date for reassembly |
| **Quorum** | Minimum members (one-tenth) needed to transact business |
| **Zero Hour** | Informal session for urgent public matters; Indian innovation since 1962 |
| **Guillotine** | All remaining demands put to vote on the last allotted day |
| **Delegated Legislation** | Executive makes detailed rules within the framework of a parent law |
| **Rule of Lapse** | Unspent money at year-end returns to Consolidated Fund |
| **March Rush** | Heavy expenditure at the end of financial year due to Rule of Lapse |
| **Vote on Account** | Advance grant to run government while the full budget is being passed |
| **Casting Vote** | Presiding officer's vote used only to break a tie |
| **Lame-Duck Session** | Last session of Lok Sabha after new Lok Sabha is elected |
| **Censure Motion** | Motion against specific actions of minister(s) — resignation not mandatory |
| **No-Confidence Motion** | Motion against entire Council of Ministers — if passed, government must resign |
| **Shadow Cabinet** | British Opposition practice of "shadowing" every Cabinet Minister |
| **Ultra Vires** | Beyond the powers conferred — used for unconstitutional laws |
