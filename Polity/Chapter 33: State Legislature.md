# 🏛️ Chapter 33: State Legislature
### *From M. Laxmikanth's Indian Polity, 8th Edition — UPSC Study Notes*

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. **Articles 168–212 (Part VI)** govern the entire framework of State Legislatures.
> 2. Only **6 states** currently have a bicameral (two-House) legislature; the rest are unicameral.
> 3. The **Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)** is far more powerful than the **Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad)** — unlike at the Centre where Rajya Sabha has more bite.
> 4. The **Governor** plays a role similar to the President at the Centre — summoning, proroguing, dissolving, and assenting to bills.
> 5. The **Tenth Schedule (Anti-Defection Law)** applies to state legislatures too, and the Speaker/Chairman decides defection cases — subject to judicial review.

---

## 🗂️ SECTION 1: Organisation of State Legislatures

### What Does "Organisation" Mean Here?
It simply means: how many Houses does a state's legislature have?

- **Unicameral** (one House — just the Legislative Assembly): Followed by **22 states**.
- **Bicameral** (two Houses — Legislative Assembly + Legislative Council): Followed by **6 states**.

### ✅ States with TWO Houses (Bicameral):
| State | Legislative Council? |
|---|---|
| Andhra Pradesh | ✅ Yes |
| Telangana | ✅ Yes |
| Uttar Pradesh | ✅ Yes |
| Bihar | ✅ Yes |
| Maharashtra | ✅ Yes |
| Karnataka | ✅ Yes |

### ❌ Notable Abolitions & Non-Starters:
- **Jammu & Kashmir Legislative Council**: Abolished by the **Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019**.
- **Tamil Nadu Legislative Council Act, 2010**: Enacted by Parliament but **never brought into force** (the Tamil Nadu Assembly passed a fresh resolution in 2011 seeking abolition of the proposed council before it was even formed).
- **Andhra Pradesh**: Got its council **created in 1957**, **abolished in 1985**, and **revived again in 2007** (via the Andhra Pradesh Legislative Council Act, 2005).
- **Madhya Pradesh**: The **7th Amendment Act of 1956** allowed for a council, but the **President has never issued the required notification**. So MP remains unicameral.
- **Punjab & West Bengal**: Councils abolished in **1969**.
- **Tamil Nadu**: Council abolished in **1986**.

### 📝 Structure of a State Legislature:
- **Unicameral State** = Governor + Legislative Assembly
- **Bicameral State** = Governor + Legislative Council + Legislative Assembly

The **Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad)** = Upper House (House of Elders)
The **Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)** = Lower House (Popular House)

---

### How Can a Council Be Created or Abolished? (Article 169)

**Article 169** is the key provision here. The process works like this:

1. The **State Assembly** passes a **resolution** by a **special majority** (majority of total membership AND at least 2/3rd of members present and voting).
2. **Parliament** then passes a law to create or abolish the council.
3. **This law is NOT treated as a Constitutional Amendment** under Article 368 — it is passed by **simple majority**, like any ordinary law.

> 💡 *Why was this provision criticised in the Constituent Assembly?*
> Because the second chamber was seen as:
> - **Not representative** of the people
> - A cause of **delays** in legislation
> - An **expensive institution**
> Hence, states were given the **choice** to have a council or not, based on their own willingness and financial strength.

## 🗂️ SECTION 2: Composition of the Two Houses

### Part A: Composition of the Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)

#### Strength
- Members are **directly elected** by the people on the basis of **universal adult franchise** (every adult citizen gets a vote).
- **Maximum strength**: **500 members**
- **Minimum strength**: **60 members**
- Special cases:
  - **Arunachal Pradesh, Sikkim, Goa**: Minimum is **30**
  - **Mizoram**: Minimum is **40**
  - **Nagaland**: Minimum is **46**
- In **Sikkim** and **Nagaland**, some members are also **elected indirectly**.

#### Nominated Member (Anglo-Indian Community)
- **Before 2020**: The Governor could nominate **one member** from the **Anglo-Indian community** if it was not adequately represented.

> 📌 **Who is an Anglo-Indian?** A person whose father or any male ancestor in the male line is or was of European descent, but who is domiciled within India and born there to parents habitually resident in India (not temporarily).

- This provision was **originally meant to last only 10 years (until 1960)**. It was extended repeatedly, each time by 10 years.
- The **95th Amendment Act, 2009** extended it until **2020**.
- The **104th Amendment Act, 2019** did **NOT extend** it further.
- **Result**: This provision **ceased to operate on 25th January 2020**. The special representation of Anglo-Indians in the Assembly by nomination is now discontinued.

#### Territorial Constituencies
- For direct elections, each state is divided into **territorial constituencies**.
- The ratio of **population to seats allotted** must be **the same throughout the state** — ensuring uniform representation.
- "Population" = population as per the **last published census**.

#### Readjustment After Each Census (Delimitation)
After every census, two things need readjustment:
1. Total number of seats in the Assembly of each state.
2. Division of the state into territorial constituencies.

Parliament decides the authority and manner for this. Accordingly, Parliament enacted **Delimitation Commission Acts** in **1952, 1962, 1972, and 2002**.

**Key Amendments on Delimitation:**

| Amendment | What it Did |
|---|---|
| **42nd Amendment Act, 1976** | Froze seats and constituency divisions at **1971 levels until 2000** |
| **84th Amendment Act, 2001** | Extended the freeze until **2026**; also allowed rationalisation of constituencies based on **1991 census** |
| **87th Amendment Act, 2003** | Changed the basis from 1991 census to **2001 census** for delimitation, but without changing total seats |

> 💡 *Why freeze the seats?* To **encourage states to control population growth**. If seats were tied to population, states with higher populations would get more seats — which might discourage family planning.

#### Reservation of Seats for SCs and STs
- **Constitutional provision**: Seats reserved for SCs and STs in proportion to their population in the state.
- **Originally**: Meant to last **10 years (until 1960)**.
- Extended repeatedly by 10 years each time.
- Under the **104th Amendment Act of 2019**: Reservation is now extended until **2030**.

---

### Part B: Composition of the Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad)

#### Strength
- Members are **indirectly elected** (unlike Assembly members).
- **Maximum strength**: **1/3rd of the total strength of the Assembly**
- **Minimum strength**: **40 members**
- The actual strength within these limits is **fixed by Parliament**.
- The size is kept smaller than the Assembly to ensure the **predominance of the directly elected House**.

#### How Are Council Members Elected/Nominated?

Of the **total members** of the council:

| Fraction | Who Elects/Nominates Them? |
|---|---|
| **1/3** | Elected by members of **local bodies** (municipalities, district boards, etc.) |
| **1/12** | Elected by **graduates** of 3 years' standing residing in the state |
| **1/12** | Elected by **teachers** of 3 years' standing (not below secondary school level) |
| **1/3** | Elected by **members of the Legislative Assembly** from among non-Assembly members |
| **Remainder** | **Nominated by the Governor** from persons with special knowledge/experience in literature, science, art, cooperative movement, or social service |

- So: **5/6 are indirectly elected** and **1/6 are nominated** by the Governor.
- The system used is **Proportional Representation by Single Transferable Vote** (voters rank candidates in order of preference; votes transfer to next preference when a candidate is elected or eliminated).
- This composition scheme is **tentative** — Parliament can modify or replace it. However, it has not done so yet.

## 🗂️ SECTION 3: Duration of the Two Houses

### Legislative Assembly — Duration
- **NOT a continuing chamber** (like the Lok Sabha).
- **Normal term**: **5 years** from the date of its first meeting after general elections.
- At the end of 5 years, the Assembly is **automatically dissolved**.
- The **Governor can dissolve it earlier** (i.e., before 5 years) to pave the way for fresh elections.
- During a **National Emergency**: Parliament can extend the Assembly's term by **1 year at a time** for any length of time. But this extension **cannot go beyond 6 months** after the emergency ends.

### Legislative Council — Duration
- Is a **continuing/permanent chamber** (like the Rajya Sabha) — NOT subject to dissolution.
- **1/3rd of its members retire every 2 years**.
- Each member has a **6-year term**.
- Vacant seats are filled by fresh elections and nominations at the beginning of every third year.
- Retiring members can be **re-elected or re-nominated** any number of times.

## 🗂️ SECTION 4: Membership of State Legislature

### 1. Qualifications to Become a Member

A person must fulfil **all** of the following:

1. Must be a **citizen of India**.
2. Must make and subscribe an **oath or affirmation** before a person authorised by the **Election Commission**. In this oath, the person swears to:
   - Bear true faith and allegiance to the **Constitution of India**
   - Uphold the **sovereignty and integrity of India**
3. **Age requirement**:
   - **Legislative Council**: Not less than **30 years**
   - **Legislative Assembly**: Not less than **25 years**
4. Must possess **other qualifications** prescribed by Parliament.

**Additional Qualifications (under Representation of People Act, 1951):**
- For **Legislative Council**: Must be an elector for an Assembly constituency in the concerned state. For Governor's nomination, must be a **resident** of that state.
- For **Legislative Assembly**: Must be an elector for an Assembly constituency in the concerned state.
- Must be a **member of SC/ST** to contest a seat reserved for them. However, SC/ST members **can also contest** unreserved seats.

---

### 2. Disqualifications

#### Constitutional Disqualifications:
A person is disqualified from being a member if:

1. He/she holds any **office of profit** under Union or State Government (except Minister or any office exempted by the State Legislature).
   > 📌 Note: A Minister is **not** considered to hold an office of profit.
2. He/she is of **unsound mind** as declared by a court.
3. He/she is an **undischarged insolvent** (a person who owes unpaid debts and hasn't been legally discharged).
4. He/she is **not a citizen of India**, or has **voluntarily acquired foreign citizenship**, or has acknowledged allegiance to a foreign state.
5. He/she is disqualified under **any law made by Parliament**.

#### Statutory Disqualifications (under Representation of People Act, 1951):

1. Must not have been found guilty of **electoral offences or corrupt practices**.
2. Must not have been **convicted for an offence** with imprisonment of **2 or more years**. (Note: Preventive detention is NOT a disqualification.)
3. Must not have **failed to lodge election expense accounts** within time.
4. Must not have any **interest in government contracts, works or services**.
5. Must not be a **director or managing agent**, or hold an office of profit in a **corporation where the government has at least 25% share**.
6. Must not have been **dismissed from government service** for corruption or disloyalty.
7. Must not have been convicted for **promoting enmity between groups** or for **bribery**.
8. Must not have been punished for preaching or practising **social crimes** like untouchability, dowry, or sati.

> ⚖️ **Who Decides Disqualification?** On questions of disqualification (other than defection), the **Governor's decision is final**. However, the Governor **must obtain the opinion of the Election Commission** and act accordingly.

#### Disqualification on Ground of Defection (Tenth Schedule):
- A member is disqualified if he/she is disqualified under the **Tenth Schedule** (Anti-Defection Law).
- This question is decided by:
  - **Chairman** — in the case of the Legislative Council
  - **Speaker** — in the case of the Legislative Assembly
  - (NOT by the Governor)

---

### ⚖️ KEY COURT CASE: Kihoto Hollohan vs. Zachillhu (1992)

**Background:**
The Tenth Schedule (Anti-Defection Law) was added to the Constitution by the **52nd Amendment Act, 1985**. It gave the Speaker/Chairman of the House the power to decide whether a member had defected. The key question was: **Is this decision final? Can courts review it?**

**What Was Challenged?**
Several MLAs and MPs challenged the validity of the Tenth Schedule itself. They argued that:
- The Tenth Schedule was unconstitutional because it made the Speaker/Chairman the sole judge of defection.
- Since the Speaker is often a member of the ruling party, this was a **biased process** — the Speaker would be deciding cases involving political rivals of his/her own party.
- It violated the principles of **natural justice** (fair hearing by an impartial judge).
- It also abridged the **right of free speech** and the right to vote according to conscience.

**The Supreme Court's Ruling (5-Judge Constitutional Bench):**

The Supreme Court upheld the validity of the Tenth Schedule in most parts, but with a crucial modification:

1. ✅ **The Tenth Schedule is constitutionally valid**. Anti-defection law does not violate the basic structure of the Constitution.
2. ✅ **The Speaker/Chairman has the authority** to decide disqualification on grounds of defection.
3. ❗ **CRUCIAL RULING**: The decision of the Speaker/Chairman **IS subject to judicial review** by the High Courts and Supreme Court. It is NOT final and beyond review.
4. However, judicial review is only available **after** the Speaker/Chairman makes a decision — **not during** the pendency of proceedings before the Speaker/Chairman.
5. The provision that the decision of the Speaker shall be "final" (Paragraph 6 of the Tenth Schedule) was **read down** — meaning courts interpreted it narrowly to allow judicial review on grounds of being contrary to constitutional provisions or in violation of natural justice.

**Why Is This Case Important for UPSC?**
- It establishes the **limits of the Speaker's power** under the anti-defection law.
- It reaffirms the **principle of judicial review** — a part of the basic structure of the Constitution.
- It is frequently used in contemporary defection disputes (e.g., Maharashtra political crisis of 2022, discussed in Modern Context below).

🌍 **Modern Context:**

> `[Modern Example]` **Maharashtra Political Crisis (2022) — Shinde vs. Thackeray:**
> In 2022, a group of Shiv Sena MLAs led by Eknath Shinde broke away from the party led by Uddhav Thackeray. The Speaker's power to decide disqualification petitions became a central legal issue. The case went to the Supreme Court (*Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra & Ors., 2023*). The Supreme Court, relying on the **Kihoto Hollohan precedent**, reaffirmed that the Speaker's decision on disqualification is subject to judicial review and directed the new Speaker to decide disqualification petitions expeditiously. The Court also held that the Governor had acted improperly in calling for a floor test. This case is a live demonstration of Kihoto Hollohan's principles in action.

### 3. Oath or Affirmation

- Every member must take an oath/affirmation **before** taking their seat, administered by the **Governor** or a person appointed by him/her.

**In the oath, the member swears to:**
1. Bear true faith and allegiance to the **Constitution of India**.
2. Uphold the **sovereignty and integrity of India**.
3. Faithfully discharge the **duty of his/her office**.

**Consequences of not taking the oath:**
- Cannot **vote** or **participate** in proceedings.
- Cannot claim **privileges and immunities**.

**Penalty for sitting/voting without oath:**
A member is liable to a penalty of **₹500 per day** if he/she:
1. Sits or votes before taking the prescribed oath/affirmation.
2. Knows he/she is not qualified or is disqualified.
3. Knows he/she is prohibited from sitting/voting by any law.

Members also receive **salaries and allowances** as determined by the State Legislature from time to time.

---

### 4. Vacation of Seats

A member vacates his/her seat in the following cases:

1. **Double Membership**: Cannot be a member of both Houses simultaneously. If elected to both, one seat falls vacant as per state law.
2. **Disqualification**: Seat becomes vacant if a member becomes subject to any disqualification.
3. **Resignation**: By writing to the Chairman (for Council) or Speaker (for Assembly). The seat falls vacant **when the resignation is accepted**.
   > 📌 The Chairman/Speaker **need not accept** the resignation if satisfied it is not voluntary or genuine.
4. **Absence**: The House can declare the seat vacant if a member is absent for **all meetings for 60 days** without permission.
5. **Other Cases** — A member must vacate the seat if:
   - His/her election is **declared void** by a court.
   - He/she is **expelled** by the House.
   - He/she is **elected as President** or **Vice-President** of India.
   - He/she is **appointed as Governor** of a state.

## 🗂️ SECTION 5: Presiding Officers of State Legislature

Each House has its own presiding officer:

| House | Presiding Officer | Deputy Presiding Officer |
|---|---|---|
| Legislative Assembly | **Speaker** | **Deputy Speaker** |
| Legislative Council | **Chairman** | **Deputy Chairman** |

- A **Panel of Chairmen** (for Assembly) and a **Panel of Vice-Chairmen** (for Council) is also appointed.
- **Salaries and allowances** of all presiding officers are fixed by the State Legislature and are charged to the **Consolidated Fund of the State** (meaning they are NOT subject to annual vote — ensuring independence).

---

### Speaker of the Legislative Assembly

- **Elected by** the Assembly from amongst its own members.
- Normally remains in office **during the life of the Assembly**.

**The Speaker vacates office earlier if:**
1. He/she **ceases to be a member** of the Assembly.
2. He/she **resigns by writing** to the Deputy Speaker.
3. He/she is **removed by a resolution** passed by a **majority of all then-members** of the Assembly (Effective Majority). Such a resolution can only be moved after **14 days' advance notice**.

**Powers and Duties of the Speaker:**

1. **Maintains order and decorum** — his/her primary responsibility; final power in this regard.
2. **Final interpreter** of the Constitution, rules of procedure, and legislative precedents within the Assembly.
3. **Adjourns** the Assembly or **suspends the meeting** if there is no quorum.
4. **Does not vote** in the first instance but can exercise a **casting vote** in case of a tie.
5. Can allow a **'secret' sitting** of the House at the request of the Leader of the House.
6. **Decides whether a bill is a Money Bill** — his/her decision is **final**.
7. Decides questions of **disqualification** under the **Tenth Schedule** (Anti-Defection Law).
8. **Appoints Chairmen of all Assembly committees** and supervises their functioning. Is the ex-officio Chairman of the **Business Advisory Committee**, **Rules Committee**, and **General Purpose Committee**.

---

### Deputy Speaker of the Assembly

- **Elected by** the Assembly after the Speaker's election.
- Vacates office in the same three conditions as the Speaker (ceasing to be member, resignation to Speaker, or removal by effective majority with 14 days' notice).
- **Acts as Speaker** when the Speaker's office is vacant or when the Speaker is absent. Has **all the powers of the Speaker** in these situations.
- The Speaker nominates a **Panel of Chairmen** from among members — any one of them can preside in the absence of both Speaker and Deputy Speaker.

---

### Chairman of the Legislative Council

- **Elected by** the Council from amongst its members.
- Vacates office in the same three conditions (ceasing to be member, resignation to Deputy Chairman, or removal by effective majority with 14 days' notice).
- Powers and functions are **similar to the Speaker** in the Assembly.

> ⚠️ **One key difference**: The **Speaker** has the special power to **decide whether a bill is a Money Bill** — the **Chairman does NOT have this power**.

---

### Deputy Chairman of the Council

- **Elected by** the Council from amongst its members.
- Vacates office in same three conditions (ceasing to be member, resignation to Chairman, or removal by effective majority with 14 days' notice).
- Acts as Chairman when the Chairman's office is vacant or when the Chairman is absent. Has **all powers of the Chairman** in these situations.
- The Chairman nominates a **Panel of Vice-Chairmen** from among members.

---

## 🗂️ SECTION 6: Sessions of the State Legislature

### Summoning
- The **Governor** summons each House from time to time.
- Maximum gap between two sessions: **6 months** (i.e., the legislature must meet **at least twice a year**).

### Adjournment
- **Adjournment** = Suspending the work in a sitting for a specified time (hours, days, or weeks).
- **Adjournment sine die** (Latin: without a day) = Terminating a sitting **for an indefinite period**.
- Power to adjourn and to adjourn sine die lies with the **Presiding Officer** of the House.

### Prorogation
- The Presiding Officer declares the House **adjourned sine die** when business is complete.
- Within a few days, the **Governor issues a notification for prorogation** of the session.
- The Governor can also prorogue a House that is currently in session.
- **Prorogation terminates a session** of the House (unlike adjournment, which only terminates a sitting within a session).

### Dissolution
- The **Legislative Council** (being permanent) is **NOT subject to dissolution**.
- Only the **Legislative Assembly** is subject to dissolution.
- **Dissolution ends the life of the entire House**; a new House is constituted after fresh elections.

**Effect of Dissolution on Pending Bills:**

| Situation | What Happens? |
|---|---|
| Bill pending in the Assembly (whether originated in Assembly or sent from Council) | **LAPSES** |
| Bill passed by Assembly but pending in Council | **LAPSES** |
| Bill pending in Council but NOT passed by Assembly | **Does NOT lapse** |
| Bill passed by Assembly/both Houses but pending Governor's or President's assent | **Does NOT lapse** |
| Bill passed by Assembly/both Houses but returned by President for reconsideration | **Does NOT lapse** |

### Quorum
- **Quorum** = Minimum number of members needed for the House to conduct business.
- **Quorum = 10 members OR 1/10th of total membership (including presiding officer)**, whichever is **greater**.
- If no quorum, the Presiding Officer must **adjourn the House** or **suspend the meeting** until quorum is present.

### Voting in the House
- Matters decided by **simple majority** (majority of votes of members present and voting, **excluding** the presiding officer).
- Only specific matters mentioned in the Constitution require **effective majority** or **special majority**.
- The Presiding Officer **does not vote** in the first instance but exercises a **casting vote** in case of a tie.

### Language in the State Legislature
- The **official language(s) of the state, Hindi, or English** can be used for conducting business.
- Presiding Officer can permit a member to address the House in his/her **mother tongue**.
- After **15 years from the commencement of the Constitution (i.e., from 1965)**, the State Legislature decides whether to continue or discontinue English.
- Special cases:
  - **Himachal Pradesh, Manipur, Meghalaya, Tripura**: Time limit is **25 years**.
  - **Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, Mizoram**: Time limit is **40 years**.

### Rights of Ministers and Advocate General
- Every **Minister** and the **Advocate General** of the state can **speak and participate** in proceedings of either House or its committees (of which they are a named member) **without being entitled to vote**.
- Two reasons for this:
  1. A Minister can participate in the House **of which he/she is NOT a member**.
  2. A Minister who is **not a member of either House** can participate in **both Houses**.
  > 📌 A person can remain a minister for **6 months** without being a member of either House of the State Legislature.

## 🗂️ SECTION 7: Legislative Procedure in the State Legislature

### Ordinary Bills

#### In the Originating House
- An ordinary bill can originate in **either House** (in bicameral states).
- Can be introduced by a **minister** or **any member** (private member bill).
- Passes through **three stages**:
  1. **First Reading** — Introduction; title is read out.
  2. **Second Reading** — Detailed clause-by-clause examination.
  3. **Third Reading** — Final vote.
- After passage, the bill is **transmitted to the second House**.
- In **unicameral states**, the bill goes directly to the **Governor for assent** after Assembly passage.

#### In the Second House (The Council)
The bill passes through all three readings. The Council has **four alternatives**:

1. Pass the bill **as sent** (without amendments).
2. Pass the bill **with amendments** and return it to the Assembly.
3. **Reject the bill** altogether.
4. **Take no action** (keep it pending).

**What Happens Next:**
- If Council passes without amendments OR Assembly accepts the Council's amendments → Bill is **deemed passed by both Houses** → Sent to Governor.
- If Assembly rejects Council's amendments, OR Council rejects the bill, OR Council does nothing for **3 months** → Assembly may **pass the bill again** and send it back.
- If Council then rejects again, or passes with unacceptable amendments, or does nothing for **1 month** → Bill is **deemed passed by both Houses** in the form the Assembly passed it the second time.

> 🔑 **Key Point**: The ultimate power to pass an ordinary bill rests with the **Assembly**. The Council can **delay a bill for a maximum of 4 months** (3 months in first instance + 1 month in second instance).

> ⚠️ **Important Difference from Parliament**: The Constitution does **NOT** provide for a **joint sitting** of both Houses of a State Legislature to resolve a deadlock. (Parliament HAS this provision for Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha.) When a bill originating in the Council is rejected by the Assembly, **it is dead**.

---

### Assent of the Governor

Every bill passed (by Assembly alone in unicameral, or both Houses in bicameral) is presented to the Governor. The Governor has **four options**:

1. **Give assent** → Bill becomes an Act.
2. **Withhold assent** → Bill ends; does not become an Act.
3. **Return for reconsideration** → If the House(s) pass it again (with or without amendments) and present it again, the Governor **must give assent**. This means the Governor enjoys only a **suspensive veto** (power to delay, not permanently block).
4. **Reserve for President's consideration** → Goes to the President.

### Assent of the President (When Bill is Reserved)

When the Governor reserves a bill for the President:
- President may **give assent**, **withhold assent**, or **return for reconsideration** (for non-Money Bills).
- If returned, the House(s) must reconsider within **6 months** and present it again.
- The Constitution **does not specify** whether the President is obliged to give assent to such a reconsidered bill.

---

### Money Bills — Special Procedure

- **Cannot be introduced** in the Legislative Council. Only in the **Legislative Assembly**, and only on the **recommendation of the Governor**.
- Every Money Bill is a **government bill** — introduced only by a **Minister**.

**Flow of a Money Bill:**
1. Introduced in **Legislative Assembly** (with Governor's recommendation).
2. Passed by Legislative Assembly.
3. Transmitted to **Legislative Council**.
4. The Council **cannot reject or amend** a Money Bill — can only make **recommendations**.
5. Council must return it within **14 days**.
6. The Assembly can **accept or reject** any/all recommendations.
   - If accepts any recommendation → Bill deemed passed in modified form.
   - If rejects all recommendations → Bill deemed passed in the original form.
   - If Council does NOT return within 14 days → Bill deemed passed in the original form at the end of 14 days.
7. Presented to Governor:
   - Governor may **give assent**, **withhold assent**, or **reserve for Presidential assent**.
   - Governor **CANNOT return** a Money Bill for reconsideration.
8. If reserved for President:
   - President may **give assent** or **withhold assent**.
   - President **CANNOT return** a Money Bill for reconsideration.

> 🔑 **Maximum delay by Council on a Money Bill = 14 days only.**

---

### Bills Reserved for President's Consideration

The **Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1983–88)** classified state bills reserved for President's consideration into three categories:

**Category 1: Bills that MUST be reserved for President's consideration:**
- Bills that **derogate from the powers of the High Court** in a way that endangers the position it is designed to fill under the Constitution (Article 200).
- Bills relating to **imposition of taxes on water or electricity** in certain cases (Article 288).
- Bills that fall within **Article 360** (during a Financial Emergency).

**Category 2: Bills that MAY be reserved for specific purposes:**
- Bills seeking immunity under **Articles 14 and 19** (Fundamental Rights), specifically:
  - Bills for acquisition of estates (Article 31A).
  - Bills giving effect to certain Directive Principles (Article 31C).
- Bills on **Concurrent List subjects** that are repugnant to Union/existing laws — to secure operation despite repugnancy (Article 254).
- Bills imposing **restrictions on trade and commerce** that required prior Presidential sanction not obtained (Article 304).

**Category 3: Bills that MAY be reserved at Governor's discretion** under Article 200, even if they don't fall in the above categories.

## 🗂️ SECTION 8: Comparison of Legislative Procedure — Parliament vs. State Legislature

### On Ordinary Bills:

| Feature | Parliament | State Legislature |
|---|---|---|
| Can be introduced in | Either House | Either House |
| Introduced by | Minister or Private Member | Minister or Private Member |
| Stages | First, Second, Third Reading | First, Second, Third Reading |
| Deemed passed when | Both Houses agree | Both Houses agree |
| Deadlock triggered when | Second House rejects/amends/doesn't act within **6 months** | Council rejects/amends/doesn't act within **3 months** |
| Deadlock resolution | **Joint Sitting** of both Houses | NO joint sitting; Assembly passes again |
| Assembly override | Lok Sabha CANNOT override Rajya Sabha (only Joint Sitting) | **Assembly CAN override Council** by passing bill a second time |
| If bill from upper house rejected by lower house | Handled by joint sitting | **Bill ends (becomes dead)** |

### On Money Bills:

| Feature | Parliament | State Legislature |
|---|---|---|
| Can be introduced in | Lok Sabha only | Legislative Assembly only |
| Introduced on recommendation of | President | Governor |
| Introduced by | Minister only | Minister only |
| Upper House's power | Cannot reject/amend; return within 14 days | Cannot reject/amend; return within 14 days |
| Lower House's response | Lok Sabha can accept/reject Rajya Sabha's recommendations | Assembly can accept/reject Council's recommendations |
| If upper house doesn't return | Deemed passed in original form after 14 days | Deemed passed in original form after 14 days |

## 🗂️ SECTION 9: Position of the Legislative Council

### Where is the Council EQUAL to the Assembly?

1. **Introduction and passage of ordinary bills** (though Assembly's will prevails in case of disagreement).
2. **Approval of ordinances** issued by the Governor.
3. **Selection of ministers** including the Chief Minister. Ministers can be members of either House, but are **responsible only to the Assembly**.
4. **Consideration of reports** of constitutional bodies: State Finance Commission, State Public Service Commission, Comptroller and Auditor General of India.
5. **Enlargement of jurisdiction** of the State Public Service Commission.

### Where is the Council UNEQUAL (Weaker) than the Assembly?

1. A **Money Bill** can only be introduced in the Assembly — **not in the Council**.
2. The Council **cannot amend or reject** a Money Bill — can only make recommendations within 14 days.
3. The **Assembly has final say** on Money Bills — can accept/reject all recommendations.
4. The **Speaker of the Assembly** (not the Chairman) has the **final power to certify a bill as a Money Bill**.
5. The **final power to pass ordinary bills** also rests with the Assembly. The Council is **not even a revising body like the Rajya Sabha** — it is only a **dilatory chamber** (one that causes delays) or an **advisory body** (maximum delay: 4 months).
6. The Council can only **discuss the budget** — it **cannot vote on demands for grants** (which is the exclusive privilege of the Assembly).
7. The Council **cannot remove the Council of Ministers** by passing a no-confidence motion — because the Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible only to the Assembly**.
8. When a bill originating in the Council is **rejected by the Assembly**, the bill **dies**.
9. The Council does **not participate in the election of the President of India** or the election of **representatives of the state in the Rajya Sabha**.
10. The Council has **no effective say in ratifying Constitutional Amendment Bills** — the Assembly's will prevails.
11. The very **existence of the Council depends on the Assembly's will** — Parliament can abolish it on the Assembly's recommendation.

> 📌 **J.C. Johari's analysis** on constitutional amendments: "The Constitution is not clear on this point whether a bill of constitutional amendment referred to the states for ratification by their legislatures shall include the Vidhan Parishad or not. In practice, it may be understood that the will of the Vidhan Sabha has to prevail." *(Indian Government and Politics, Vishal, Thirteenth Edition, 2001, p. 441)*

---

### Why is the Council Weaker than the Rajya Sabha?

The Rajya Sabha is a far more powerful second chamber than the Legislative Council. Three key reasons explain why:

1. **Federal Significance**: The Rajya Sabha represents states in a **federal polity** (a system where power is shared between the Centre and states). It protects state interests against central interference. The Council has **no such federal role** — it operates entirely within one state.

2. **Composition**: The Rajya Sabha is **homogeneously constituted** — it primarily represents states and Union Territories, mostly through elected members (only 12 out of 250 are nominated). The Council is **heterogeneously constituted** — it has differently elected members (graduates, teachers, local bodies) plus nominated members. This mixed composition weakens its effectiveness as a revising body.

3. **Democratic Principle**: In a democracy, the **popular house** (directly elected) should have supremacy. The Assembly is directly elected by the people; the Council is not. This pattern is borrowed from the **British model**, where the **House of Lords** (Upper House) cannot oppose the **House of Commons** (Lower House) — the Lords can only delay an ordinary bill for **1 year** and a money bill for **1 month**.

---

### Critics' View vs. Supporters' View of the Council

**Critics call it:**
- A "secondary chamber"
- A "costly ornamental luxury"
- A "white elephant"
- A "refuge for those defeated in Assembly elections" — allowing unpopular or rejected politicians to become Chief Minister, Minister, or a member of the legislature without facing the electorate.

**Supporters argue it is useful because:**
1. It **checks hasty, defective, and ill-considered legislation** by providing an opportunity for revision and second thought.
2. It **facilitates representation of eminent professionals and experts** who cannot face direct elections. The Governor nominates 1/6th of the Council members specifically for this purpose.

## 🗂️ SECTION 10: Privileges of the State Legislature

**Legislative Privileges** = A set of special rights, immunities, and exemptions enjoyed by the Houses of the State Legislature, their committees, and their members.

**Why are they needed?**
- To secure the **independence and effectiveness** of legislative action.
- Without them, Houses cannot maintain their **authority, dignity, and honour**, nor protect members from **obstruction in discharging their legislative responsibilities**.

**Who gets these privileges?**
- Both Houses collectively, their committees, and individual members.
- Also: **Advocate General of the state** and **State Ministers** who speak and participate in proceedings.
- ⚠️ **The Governor does NOT get these privileges**, even though the Governor is an integral part of the State Legislature.

---

### Collective Privileges (Enjoyed by the House as a Whole)

1. Right to **publish its reports, debates, and proceedings** — and right to **prohibit others from publishing** the same.
   > 📌 The **44th Amendment Act of 1978** restored freedom of the press to **publish true reports** of the State Legislature **without prior permission**. However, this does NOT apply to **secret sittings**.

2. Can **exclude strangers** from its proceedings and hold **secret sittings** for important matters.

3. Can make **rules to regulate its own procedure** and adjudicate on such matters.

4. Can **punish members and outsiders** for breach of privilege or contempt — by **reprimand, admonition, or imprisonment**; members can also be **suspended or expelled**.

5. Right to receive **immediate information** of the **arrest, detention, conviction, imprisonment, and release** of a member.

6. Can **institute inquiries**, order attendance of witnesses, and send for **relevant papers and records**.

7. **Courts are prohibited** from inquiring into the proceedings of a House or its committees.

8. No person (member or outsider) can be **arrested**, and no **legal process** (civil or criminal) can be served **within the precincts of the House** without the presiding officer's permission.

---

### Individual Privileges (Enjoyed by Members Personally)

1. **Freedom from arrest** during the session and **40 days before** and **40 days after** the session.
   > ⚠️ This privilege is available only in **civil cases** — NOT in criminal cases or preventive detention cases.

2. **Freedom of speech** in the State Legislature. No member is liable to proceedings in **any court** for anything said or any vote given in the Legislature or its committees.
   > ⚠️ This is subject to:
   > - Constitutional provisions
   > - Rules and standing orders of the Legislature
   > 📌 **Article 211**: No discussion shall take place in any State Legislature regarding the conduct of **any judge of the Supreme Court or High Court** in the discharge of duties.
   > Use of **unparliamentary language** or **unparliamentary conduct** is prohibited under House rules.

3. **Exemption from jury service**. Can refuse to give evidence or appear as a witness in a court case when the Legislature is in session.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Connecting Chapter to Current Affairs

### `[Modern Example]` 1. Delimitation Exercise (Post-2026)
> The seats in State Assemblies are currently frozen at 1971 population levels until **2026** (by the 84th Amendment Act, 2001). After 2026, a fresh delimitation exercise is expected. The **Delimitation Commission** will then redraw constituencies based on the **2011 census** (and possibly 2021 census data). This is a major upcoming political event — Southern states like Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Karnataka, which have successfully controlled population growth, fear losing Assembly and Lok Sabha seats to northern states with higher population growth. This is the **North-South political tension over delimitation** — a live constitutional and political debate.

### `[Modern Example]` 2. 104th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2019 — Anglo-Indian Reservation Ends
> The ending of Anglo-Indian nomination to State Assemblies (effective 25 January 2020) marked the close of a 70-year-old provision. The Anglo-Indian community, once a visible presence in Indian polity, has now lost its guaranteed representation. According to the 2011 census data, the Anglo-Indian population in India is estimated at around 125,000 — too small to win seats in general elections.

### `[Modern Example]` 3. Jammu & Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019
> The bifurcation of the former state of J&K into two Union Territories — **J&K (with Legislature)** and **Ladakh (without Legislature)** — is a landmark event directly relevant to this chapter. The Legislative Council of J&K was abolished. The J&K Assembly now has 83 functional seats (out of 107 total, with 24 in PoK remaining vacant). The delimitation of J&K's Assembly constituencies was completed by the **Delimitation Commission in 2022** — the first delimitation exercise in J&K in over two decades.

### `[Modern Example]` 4. Maharashtra Political Crisis and Anti-Defection Law (2022–2023)
> The Shiv Sena split in Maharashtra (2022) created a constitutional crisis that directly tested provisions of this chapter — anti-defection law (Tenth Schedule), Speaker's powers, and the Governor's role. The Supreme Court in **Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** gave a landmark ruling:
> - The Governor's invitation to Eknath Shinde to form the government was held improper.
> - The Supreme Court reaffirmed that disqualification petitions under the Tenth Schedule must be decided by the Speaker.
> - It reiterated the **Kihoto Hollohan (1992)** principle — the Speaker's decision is subject to judicial review.
> - The Court also ruled that the **Speaker elected by the new government** could decide disqualification petitions against the rebel MLAs.

### `[Modern Example]` 5. SC/ST Reservation in Legislatures Extended to 2030
> The **104th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2019** extended the reservation of seats for SCs and STs in State Legislative Assemblies (and in the Lok Sabha) until **25 January 2030**. This is a recurring topic in UPSC — note that reservation of seats for SCs/STs (not OBCs) is a constitutional provision that needs periodic parliamentary renewal.

### `[Modern Example]` 6. Andhra Pradesh — Abolition of Legislative Council (2020)
> In January 2020, the Andhra Pradesh Legislative Assembly (under the Jagan Mohan Reddy government) passed a resolution seeking the **abolition of the Legislative Council**. This was done after the Council delayed the passage of several key bills (including the Three Capitals Bill and decentralisation bills). The matter was sent to Parliament under Article 169. This is a live example of the process under Article 169 and also illustrates the criticism that the Council can be a "dilatory chamber" used for political obstruction.

> `[Modern Example]` Update: As of the time of writing, the abolition process remains pending at the Parliamentary level. Subsequent to a change in government in Andhra Pradesh (2024 elections), the abolition resolution itself may be reconsidered.
