# 📘 Chapter 15: Centre–State Relations
### *Indian Polity by M. Laxmikanth – 8th Edition*

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. India's Constitution divides powers between Centre and States across **Legislative, Administrative, and Financial** dimensions — but judicial power remains **integrated** (unified under one system).
> 2. Legislative subjects are divided into **three lists**: Union List (98 subjects), State List (59 subjects), and Concurrent List (52 subjects) in the **Seventh Schedule**.
> 3. Parliament can legislate on State subjects in **5 extraordinary situations**: Rajya Sabha resolution, National Emergency, State request, International agreements, and President's Rule.
> 4. The **Sarkaria Commission (1983–88)** and **Punchhi Commission (2007–10)** are the two landmark commissions that examined Centre-State relations and made comprehensive reform recommendations.
> 5. The **S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** judgment is the most critical Supreme Court case limiting the misuse of **President's Rule (Article 356)**.

---

# PART I: LEGISLATIVE RELATIONS
### *(Articles 245–255, Part XI)*

---

## 1. 📍 Territorial Extent of Central and State Legislation

### What area can each level of government make laws for?

**(i) Parliament (Centre):**
- Can make laws for the **whole or any part of the territory of India**
- "Territory of India" includes:
  - All **States**
  - All **Union Territories**
  - Any **other area** temporarily included in India's territory

**(ii) State Legislature:**
- Can make laws for the **whole or any part of that State**
- State laws are **NOT applicable outside the state** — *except* when there is a **sufficient nexus** (reasonable connection) between the state and the object of the law

**(iii) Extra-Territorial Legislation:**
- **Only Parliament** can make **extra-territorial legislation** (laws applicable outside India's borders)
- So, Parliament's laws apply to **Indian citizens and their property anywhere in the world**

---

### ⚠️ Exceptions: Where Parliament's Laws Do NOT Apply

The Constitution places certain restrictions on Parliament's territorial jurisdiction:

| Situation | Who Has Special Power? |
|---|---|
| Union Territories of **Andaman & Nicobar Islands, Lakshadweep, Dadra & Nagar Haveli, Daman & Diu, and Ladakh** | **President** can make regulations — these have the same force as a Parliamentary Act |
| **Puducherry** | President can legislate *only when the Assembly is suspended or dissolved* |
| **Scheduled Areas** in any state | **Governor** can direct that a Parliamentary Act does not apply, or applies with modifications |
| **Tribal Areas (Autonomous Districts)** in **Assam** | **Governor of Assam** has the same power |
| **Tribal Areas in Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram** | **President** has the power to exclude or modify Parliamentary Acts |

---

## 2. 📚 Distribution of Legislative Subjects

The Constitution provides a **three-fold (threefold) distribution** of legislative subjects in the **Seventh Schedule**:

---

### 📋 The Three Lists at a Glance

| List | Who Legislates? | Present Subjects | Original Subjects | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **List I – Union List** | Parliament (exclusively) | **98** | 97 | Defence, Banking, Foreign Affairs, Currency, Atomic Energy, Insurance, Communication, Inter-state Trade, Census, Audit |
| **List II – State List** | State Legislature (in normal circumstances) | **59** | 66 | Public Order, Police, Public Health, Agriculture, Prisons, Local Government, Fisheries, Markets, Theatres, Gambling |
| **List III – Concurrent List** | Both Parliament AND State Legislatures | **52** | 47 | Criminal Law, Civil Procedure, Marriage & Divorce, Population Control, Electricity, Labour Welfare, Economic Planning, Drugs, Newspapers |

> 📝 **Important Note on Numbers:** Even now, the last entry in the Union List is numbered **97** but the total count is **98** (because entries 2A, 92A, and 92B were added, and entries 33, 92, and 92C were deleted). Similarly, the last entry in the State List is numbered **66** but the total is **59** (several entries omitted).

---

### 🔄 The 42nd Amendment Act, 1976 — A Major Shift

The **42nd Amendment Act of 1976** transferred **5 subjects** from the **State List** to the **Concurrent List**:

1. **Education**
2. **Forests**
3. **Weights and Measures**
4. **Protection of Wild Animals and Birds**
5. **Administration of Justice** (constitution and organisation of all courts except the Supreme Court and High Courts)

---

### 🧩 Other Key Provisions on Legislative Distribution

**(iv) Union Territories & Acquired Territories:**
Parliament can make laws on **State List subjects** for any part of India not included in a State (i.e., Union Territories or Acquired Territories, if any).

**(v) Goods and Services Tax (GST) — Special Provision:**
The **101st Amendment Act of 2016** inserted **Article 246-A**, giving:
- **Both Parliament and State Legislatures** concurrent power to make GST laws
- **Parliament alone** has exclusive power over GST on **inter-state** supply of goods/services

**(vi) Residuary Powers:**
- Subjects **NOT listed in any of the three lists** = **Residuary subjects**
- Power to legislate on these vests with **Parliament**
- This includes the power to levy **residuary taxes**

---

### 🌍 Comparative View: How India Differs from Other Countries

| Country | Method | Residuary Powers |
|---|---|---|
| **USA** | Single enumeration (only Federal powers listed) | With **States** |
| **Australia** | Single enumeration (followed American pattern) | With **States** |
| **Canada** | Double enumeration (Federal + Provincial) | With **Centre** |
| **Government of India Act, 1935** | Three-fold enumeration | With **Governor-General** |
| **India (Present Constitution)** | Three-fold enumeration (follows 1935 Act) | With **Parliament (Centre)** |

> 🇮🇳 **India follows the Canadian precedent** in keeping residuary powers with the Centre.

---

### ⚖️ Hierarchy of the Three Lists (Supremacy Order)

When there is a conflict, the order of precedence is:

```
Union List > Concurrent List > State List
```

**What happens when Central law and State law conflict on a Concurrent List subject?**
- **Central law prevails** over State law
- **Exception:** If the State law was *reserved for the President's consideration* AND received *Presidential assent*, **the State law prevails** in that State
- BUT Parliament can still **override** this State law later by making a new law on the same matter

---

## 3. 🚨 Parliamentary Legislation in the State Field

*(Parliament can enter the State's domain under 5 extraordinary situations)*

---

### A. When Rajya Sabha Passes a Resolution

- **What happens:** Rajya Sabha declares it is necessary in the **national interest** for Parliament to legislate on a State List matter (or GST matter)
- **Voting requirement:** Supported by **2/3 of members present and voting**
- **Duration:** Resolution remains in force for **1 year** (renewable any number of times, but *not exceeding 1 year at a time*)
- **After expiry:** Laws made under this provision cease to have effect **6 months after** the resolution ceases to be in force
- **State Legislature's power:** NOT restricted — it can still make laws on the same matter
- **Conflict rule:** If State law and Parliamentary law conflict, **Parliamentary law prevails**

---

### B. During a National Emergency

- **What happens:** Parliament gets power to legislate on **State List subjects** (or GST matters) while a National Emergency (under **Article 352**) is in operation
- **After expiry:** Laws become inoperative **6 months after** the emergency ceases
- **State Legislature's power:** NOT restricted
- **Conflict rule:** Parliamentary law prevails over State law

---

### C. When States Make a Request

- **Trigger:** Legislatures of **two or more states** pass resolutions *requesting* Parliament to legislate on a State subject
- **Scope:** Such a law applies **only to those states** that passed the resolutions
- **Adoption by other states:** Any other state can adopt this law later by passing a resolution in its own legislature
- **Amendment/Repeal:** Only by **Parliament** — the state legislatures lose this power
- **Effect on State Legislature:** The state legislature **ceases to have power** to make laws on that matter — it is treated as an **abdication/surrender** of state legislative power

**Examples of laws passed under this provision:**
- Prize Competition Act, 1955
- Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972
- Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974
- Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976
- Transplantation of Human Organs Act, 1994

---

### D. To Implement International Agreements

- Parliament can make laws on **any State List matter** to implement **international treaties, agreements, or conventions**
- Purpose: Enables the Central government to fulfil **international obligations and commitments**

**Examples of laws passed under this provision:**
- United Nations (Privileges and Immunities) Act, 1947
- Geneva Convention Act, 1960
- Anti-Hijacking Act, 1982
- Legislations relating to **environment and TRIPS** (Trade-Related Intellectual Property Rights)

---

### E. During President's Rule

- When **President's Rule is imposed** in a State (under **Article 356**), Parliament can make laws on **any State List subject** for that State
- **Duration after President's Rule ends:** The law **continues to be operative** even after President's Rule ends (it is NOT coterminous with President's Rule)
- **However:** The state legislature, once restored, **can repeal, alter, or re-enact** such a law

---

## 4. 🎛️ Centre's Control Over State Legislation

Besides directly legislating on state subjects, the Centre controls state legislation in these ways:

| Mechanism | Constitutional Article | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Governor's reservation of bills** | Articles 200 & 201 | Governor can reserve a State bill for Presidential consideration — President has **absolute veto** over such bills |
| **Prior Presidential sanction for trade bills** | Article 304 | A State bill restricting freedom of trade/commerce can be introduced **only with prior Presidential sanction** |
| **Financial Emergency directions** | Article 360 | Centre can direct states to reserve **money bills and financial bills** for President's consideration |
| **Governor's ordinances** | Article 213 | Governor **cannot make an ordinance** without Presidential instructions in certain cases |

> 📣 **Sarkaria Commission's Observation (1983–88)** on why Central supremacy in legislation is necessary:
> *"The rule of federal supremacy is a technique to avoid absurdity, resolve conflict and ensure harmony between the Union and state laws... Over-centralisation leads to blood pressure at the centre and anaemia at the periphery."*
> *(Report of the Commission on Centre-State Relations, Part I, Government of India, 1988, pp. 28–29)*

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> - `[Modern Example]` **Concurrent List Conflict — Farm Laws (2020–21):** The three controversial Farm Laws passed by Parliament in 2020 were argued by many states to be an encroachment on **State List subjects** (agriculture, markets). After massive protests, Parliament **repealed all three laws in November 2021** — a landmark example of Centre-State legislative tension.
> - `[Modern Example]` **International Agreements & Legislation:** India's obligations under the **Paris Agreement on Climate Change (2015)** have driven central legislation on renewable energy and environment — directly invoking Parliament's power under **Provision D** above.
> - `[Modern Example]` The **101st Constitutional Amendment Act (2016)** introducing GST is the biggest restructuring of India's legislative and financial federal architecture in recent decades, effectively creating a new concurrent taxing power.

# PART II: ADMINISTRATIVE RELATIONS
### *(Articles 256–263, Part XI)*

---

## 1. 🏛️ Distribution of Executive Powers

Executive power follows the **same pattern** as legislative power (with a few exceptions):

| Level | Executive Power Extends To |
|---|---|
| **Centre** | ① Subjects in the **Union List** ② Rights/jurisdiction under any treaty or agreement |
| **States** | Subjects in the **State List** |
| **Concurrent List** | Executive power rests with **States** — *unless* a Constitutional provision or Parliamentary law specifically gives it to the Centre |

> 💡 **Key Principle:** A law on a Concurrent subject, even if enacted by Parliament, is **executed by the States** — unless the Constitution or Parliament directs otherwise.
> *Example: Under the Essential Commodities Act (made by Parliament on a concurrent subject), executive power is vested in the Centre.*

---

## 2. ⚖️ Obligations of States and the Centre

The Constitution places **two restrictions** on states' executive power to allow the Centre to function without obstruction:

**Restriction 1 (General Obligation):**
- Every State must exercise its executive power so as to **ensure compliance** with Parliamentary laws and existing laws applicable in the State

**Restriction 2 (Specific Obligation):**
- Every State must ensure its executive power does **not impede or prejudice** the exercise of the Centre's executive power in the State

**Sanction (Punishment for non-compliance) — Article 365:**
- If a State fails to comply with Central directions, the President can hold that **constitutional machinery has broken down** in that State
- This means **President's Rule (Article 356)** can be imposed

---

## 3. 📋 Centre's Directions to the States

The Centre can give **binding directions** to states on these specific matters:

1. **Construction and maintenance** of communication routes declared to be of national or military importance
2. **Protection of railways** within the State
3. **Adequate facilities** for mother-tongue instruction at the primary level for **linguistic minority groups**
4. **Drawing up and executing specified schemes** for the welfare of **Scheduled Tribes** in the State

> ⚠️ The coercive sanction of **Article 365** (President's Rule threat) applies to these directions as well.

---

## 4. 🤝 Mutual Delegation of Functions

- Legislative power division is **rigid** — Centre cannot delegate legislative powers to states
- But **executive power** can be mutually delegated (shared) to avoid conflicts and deadlocks

**Two Methods of Delegation:**

| Method | Who Can Use It |
|---|---|
| **By Agreement (Presidential/Governor's consent)** | Both Centre AND States can use this |
| **By Parliamentary Legislation** (without state consent) | Only **Centre** can use this |

**How it works:**
- **President** may (with state's consent) entrust **Central executive functions** to a State government
- **Governor** may (with Centre's consent) entrust **State executive functions** to the Central government
- This delegation may be **conditional or unconditional**
- Parliament can also **confer powers and impose duties** on a State through legislation on a Union List subject — even **without the State's consent**

> 📝 **Note:** The power of states to entrust functions to the Centre was added by the **7th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1956**. Before that, only the Centre had this power.

---

## 5. 🤝 Cooperation Between the Centre and States

The Constitution includes these provisions for Centre-State coordination:

1. **Inter-State River Disputes:** Parliament can provide for adjudication of disputes over **use, distribution, and control of waters** of inter-state rivers and river valleys
2. **Inter-State Council (Article 263):** President can establish an Inter-State Council to **investigate and discuss subjects of common interest** — such a council was set up in **1990**
3. **Full Faith and Credit:** Public acts, records, and judicial proceedings of the Centre and every State must be given **full faith and credit** throughout India
4. **Interstate Trade Authority:** Parliament can appoint an authority for provisions relating to **interstate freedom of trade, commerce, and intercourse** — *but no such authority has been appointed so far*

---

## 6. 👔 All-India Services

### What Are They?

- India has **Central Services** (for Centre), **State Services** (for States), and additionally, **All-India Services**
- **All-India Services:** IAS (Indian Administrative Service), IPS (Indian Police Service), and **IFoS** (Indian Forest Service)
- Members occupy **top/key posts** under both Centre and States and serve them by turns
- **Recruited and trained by the Centre**
- **Control:** Jointly by Centre and States — ultimate control with **Centre**, immediate control with **State governments**

### Historical Background:

- **1947:** Indian Civil Service (ICS) replaced by **IAS**; Indian Police (IP) replaced by **IPS** — both recognised by the Constitution
- **1966:** **Indian Forest Service (IFoS)** created as the third All-India Service
- **Article 312:** Parliament is authorised to create **new All-India Services** on the basis of a **Rajya Sabha resolution**
- Each All-India Service forms a **single service** with:
  - Common rights and status
  - **Uniform scales of pay** throughout the country

### Do All-India Services Violate Federalism?

**Yes, in a way** — they restrict state autonomy and patronage. But they are defended on three grounds:

- ✅ Maintain **high standards of administration** in both Centre and States
- ✅ Ensure **uniformity of administrative system** throughout the country
- ✅ Facilitate **liaison, cooperation, coordination, and joint action** on common issues

### 📣 Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's Justification in the Constituent Assembly:

> *"The Indian federation, though a dual polity, will have a dual service, but with one exception... there shall be an all-India service, recruited on an all-India basis with common qualifications, with uniform scale of pay and members of which alone could be appointed to those strategic posts throughout the Union."*
> *(Constituent Assembly Debates, Volume VII, pp. 41–42)*

---

## 7. 📝 Public Service Commissions

| Provision | Details |
|---|---|
| **State PSC Chairman/Members** | Appointed by **Governor**, but can be removed only by **President** |
| **Joint State PSC (JSPSC)** | Parliament can establish for two or more states on request of concerned state legislatures — Chairman/Members appointed by **President** |
| **UPSC serving State needs** | UPSC can serve a state's needs on request of the **state governor** with **President's approval** |
| **Joint recruitment by UPSC** | UPSC assists states (when requested by two or more states) in framing joint recruitment schemes for services requiring special qualifications |

---

## 8. ⚖️ Integrated Judicial System

- India has a **dual polity** (two levels of government) but **NOT a dual judiciary**
- The Constitution established an **integrated judicial system** (unified court structure):
  - **Supreme Court** at the top
  - **State High Courts** below it
- This **single system enforces both Central and State laws** — done to eliminate diversities in remedial procedure

**Centre's Control Over Judiciary:**
- **High Court judges** are appointed by the **President** in consultation with:
  - Chief Justice of India
  - Governor of the State
- They can also be **transferred and removed** by the President
- Parliament can establish a **common High Court for two or more states**
  - Examples: **Maharashtra and Goa** share a High Court; **Punjab and Haryana** share a High Court

---

## 9. 🚨 Relations During Emergencies

| Type of Emergency | Centre's Power Over States |
|---|---|
| **National Emergency (Article 352)** | Centre can give executive directions to states on **'any' matter** — states come under complete Central control (but are NOT suspended) |
| **President's Rule (Article 356)** | President assumes the **functions of the State government** and powers of the Governor or any other executive authority in the State |
| **Financial Emergency (Article 360)** | Centre can direct states to observe **canons of financial propriety** (rules of fiscal discipline), including **reduction of salaries** of state employees |

---

## 10. 🔍 Other Provisions for Central Control

**(i) Article 355 — Two Duties on the Centre:**
- To **protect every State** against external aggression and internal disturbance
- To ensure that the government of every State is carried on **in accordance with the Constitution**

**(ii) Governor as Central Agent:**
- Governor is appointed by and holds office at the **pleasure of the President**
- Acts as **agent of the Centre** in the State
- Submits **periodical reports** to the Centre about the State's administrative affairs

**(iii) State Election Commissioner:**
- Appointed by the **Governor**
- Can be removed only by the **President**

# PART III: FINANCIAL RELATIONS
### *(Articles 268–293, Part XII)*

---

## 1. 💰 Allocation of Taxing Powers

| Authority | Taxing Power |
|---|---|
| **Parliament** | Exclusive power to levy taxes on **Union List subjects** (13 tax entries) |
| **State Legislature** | Exclusive power to levy taxes on **State List subjects** (18 tax entries) |
| **Both Parliament & State** | **Concurrent taxing power** for **GST** (introduced by 101st Amendment, 2016) — this is the *only* exception to the rule that there are no tax entries in the Concurrent List |
| **Parliament** | **Residuary power of taxation** (taxes not in any list) — Parliament has used this to impose gift tax, wealth tax, and expenditure tax |

> ⚠️ **Key Distinction:** The Constitution separates the power to **levy and collect** a tax from the power to **appropriate (keep)** its proceeds.
> Example: **Income Tax** is levied and collected by the Centre, but its proceeds are **distributed between Centre and States**.

---

### 🚫 Restrictions on States' Taxing Powers

States face the following restrictions:

1. **Profession Tax Cap:**
   - States can impose taxes on professions, trades, callings, and employments
   - But total amount payable by **any one person must NOT exceed ₹2,500 per annum**
   - *Originally this limit was only ₹250 per annum; the **60th Amendment Act of 1988** raised it to ₹2,500*

2. **GST Restriction on Inter-State/Import-Export Supply:**
   - States **cannot impose tax** on supply of goods/services occurring:
     - (a) **Outside the State**
     - (b) **In the course of import or export**
   - Parliament is empowered to formulate principles for determining when supply takes place outside the state
   - *(Contained in Article 286, as amended by the 101st Amendment Act of 2016)*

3. **Electricity Tax Restriction:**
   - States can impose tax on consumption/sale of electricity
   - **Exception:** No tax can be imposed on electricity:
     - (a) Consumed by the Centre **OR** sold to the Centre
     - (b) Consumed in construction, maintenance, or operation of any **railway** by the Centre/railway company, or sold to them for the same purpose

4. **Inter-State River Authority Electricity:**
   - A state can impose tax on electricity stored/generated/consumed/distributed/sold by any authority (established by Parliament) for regulating an **inter-state river or river valley**
   - BUT such a law must be **reserved for the President's consideration** and receive **Presidential assent** to be effective

---

## 2. 📊 Distribution of Tax Revenues

Two major constitutional amendments changed the scheme of tax revenue distribution:

### 80th Amendment Act, 2000:
- Enacted to give effect to the recommendations of the **10th Finance Commission**
- Commission recommended: **29% of total income** from certain Central taxes and duties should go to the States
- Known as the **'Alternative Scheme of Devolution'** — came into effect **retrospectively from April 1, 1996**
- Brought several Central taxes (Corporation Tax, Customs Duties) at par with Income Tax for the purpose of constitutionally mandated sharing with States
- This amendment **deleted Article 272**

### 101st Amendment Act, 2016:
- Introduced the **GST regime** — a completely new indirect tax architecture
- Conferred **concurrent taxing powers** on Parliament and State Legislatures for GST
- GST **replaced** numerous indirect taxes previously levied by both Union and States
- Purpose: Remove the **cascading effect of taxes** (tax-on-tax) and provide a **common national market**

**Central Indirect Taxes subsumed under GST:**
1. Central Excise Duty
2. Additional Excise Duties
3. Excise Duty under Medicinal and Toilet Preparations Act, 1955
4. Service Tax
5. Additional Customs Duty (Countervailing Duty)
6. Special Additional Duty of Customs
7. Central Surcharges and Cesses (related to supply of goods and services)

**State Indirect Taxes subsumed under GST:**
1. State Value Added Tax (VAT) / Sales Tax
2. Entertainment Tax (except levied by local bodies)
3. Central Sales Tax (levied by Centre, collected by States)
4. Octroi and Entry Tax
5. Purchase Tax
6. Luxury Tax
7. Taxes on lottery, betting, and gambling
8. State Surcharges and Cesses (related to supply of goods and services)

> 📝 The 101st Amendment also **deleted Article 268-A and Entry 92-C** in the Union List (both dealing with Service Tax, which were added by the **88th Amendment Act of 2003**).

---

### Current Position of Tax Revenue Distribution (Post-80th and 101st Amendments):

#### A. Taxes Levied by Centre but Collected and Appropriated by States (Article 268)
- **Examples:** Stamp duties on bills of exchange, cheques, promissory notes, insurance policies, share transfers, etc.
- Proceeds **do NOT go to the Consolidated Fund of India** — they are **assigned to the respective State**

#### B. Taxes Levied and Collected by Centre but Assigned to States (Article 269)
- **Includes:**
  - (i) Taxes on sale/purchase of goods (other than newspapers) in **inter-state trade**
  - (ii) Taxes on **consignment of goods** in inter-state trade
- Net proceeds do NOT form part of the Consolidated Fund of India
- Assigned to concerned States as per Parliament's principles

#### C. GST on Inter-State Trade (Article 269-A)
- **GST on inter-state supplies** is levied and collected by the **Centre**
- But divided between Centre and States as **Parliament decides on the recommendations of the GST Council**
- Parliament also formulates principles for determining the **place of supply**

#### D. Taxes Levied and Collected by Centre but Distributed Between Centre and States (Article 270)
- **Includes:** All taxes and duties in the Union List **EXCEPT**:
  - (i) Duties/taxes under Articles 268, 269, and 269-A
  - (ii) Surcharges under Article 271
  - (iii) Any cess levied for specific purposes
- Distribution prescribed by the **President on the recommendation of the Finance Commission**

#### E. Surcharge on Certain Taxes (Article 271)
- Parliament can levy **surcharges** (additional charges) on taxes under Articles 269 and 270
- Surcharge proceeds go **exclusively to the Centre** — States have NO share
- **Important Exception:** GST is **exempted from this surcharge** — no surcharge can be imposed on GST

#### F. Taxes Levied, Collected, and Retained Exclusively by States
- **18 taxes** belonging exclusively to States (enumerated in State List):

1. Land revenue
2. Taxes on agricultural income
3. Duties in respect of succession to agricultural land
4. Estate duty in respect of agricultural land
5. Taxes on lands and buildings
6. Taxes on mineral rights
7. Duties of excise on alcoholic liquors; opium, Indian hemp and other narcotic drugs and narcotics (excluding medicinal/toilet preparations containing alcohol or narcotics)
8. Taxes on consumption or sale of electricity
9. Taxes on sale of petroleum crude, high speed diesel, motor spirit (petrol), natural gas, aviation turbine fuel and alcoholic liquor (excluding inter-state or international trade)
10. Taxes on goods and passengers carried by road or inland waterways
11. Taxes on vehicles
12. Taxes on animals and boats
13. Tolls
14. Taxes on professions, trades, callings, and employments
15. Capitation taxes
16. Taxes on entertainments and amusements (to the extent levied by Panchayat, Municipality, Regional Council, or District Council)
17. Stamp duty on documents (except those specified in the Union List)
18. Fees on matters enumerated in the State List (except court fees)

> 📝 *Entries 52 and 55 (taxes on entry of goods into local areas, and taxes on certain advertisements) were omitted by the 101st Amendment Act of 2016.*

## 3. 💵 Distribution of Non-Tax Revenues

### Centre's Major Non-Tax Revenue Sources:
1. Posts and Telegraphs
2. Railways
3. Banking
4. Broadcasting

8. Others

1. Irrigation
2. Forests

5. Escheat and Lapse

## 4. 🎁 Grants-in-Aid to the States

Beyond tax sharing, the Constitution provides for **grants-in-aid** from Central resources to States. There are **two main types**:

### A. Statutory Grants (Article 275)

- Parliament can make grants to states **in need of financial assistance** (not every state automatically gets them)
- **Different amounts** may be fixed for different states
- Charged on the **Consolidated Fund of India** every year
- **Specific grants** also provided for:
  - Promoting welfare of **Scheduled Tribes** in a State
  - Raising the level of administration of **Scheduled Areas** (including Assam)
- Both general and specific statutory grants are given on the **recommendation of the Finance Commission**

### B. Discretionary Grants (Article 282)

- Both Centre AND States can make grants for **any public purpose** — even if it falls outside their respective legislative competence
- These are called **discretionary grants** because the Centre is under **no legal obligation** to give them — it is entirely the Centre's discretion
- **Two-fold purpose:**
  - Help states financially to fulfil **plan targets**
  - Give the Centre **leverage to influence and coordinate** state action for national planning
  *(M.P. Jain: Indian Constitutional Law, Wadhwa, Fourth Edition, pp. 342–43)*

### C. Other Grants (Temporary — Now Lapsed)

- Provision was made for grants **in lieu of export duties on jute and jute products** to the States of **Assam, Bihar, Orissa, and West Bengal**
- These were given for a **period of ten years** from the commencement of the Constitution
- Charged on the Consolidated Fund of India, given on recommendation of the Finance Commission

## 5. 🏛️ Goods and Services Tax (GST) Council

### Why Was It Created?

- Smooth administration of GST requires **cooperation and coordination** between Centre and States
- The **101st Amendment Act of 2016** established the **GST Council** under **Article 279-A**
- The President is empowered to constitute the GST Council by an order
- *(Article 279-A(1) states the President must constitute the Council within **60 days** of the commencement of the 101st Amendment)*

### What Does the GST Council Do?

It is a **joint forum of Centre and States** that makes recommendations on:

- (a) Which taxes, cesses, and surcharges of Centre, States, and local bodies will **merge into GST**
- (b) Which goods and services will be **subject to or exempted from GST**
- (c) **Model GST Laws**, principles of levy, and apportionment of inter-state GST
- (d) The **threshold limit of turnover** below which goods/services are exempted from GST
- (e) **GST rates** including floor rates with bands
- (f) Any **special rate** for a specified period to raise resources during a **natural calamity or disaster**

## 6. 🏦 Finance Commission (Article 280)

- **Nature:** A **quasi-judicial body** (functions like a court in some respects — gives recommendations based on evidence and principles)
- **Constituted by:** The **President** — every **fifth year** or even earlier
- **Role:** Makes recommendations to the President on:

1. **Distribution of net tax proceeds** between Centre and States, and allocation among States
2. **Principles governing grants-in-aid** to States from the Consolidated Fund of India
3. **Measures to augment Consolidated Funds of States** to supplement resources of **Panchayats and Municipalities** *(this function was added by the **73rd and 74th Amendment Acts of 1992**)*
4. **Any other matter** referred to it by the President in the interests of sound finance

> 📝 **Historical Note:** Till 1960, the Finance Commission also suggested amounts paid to Assam, Bihar, Orissa, and West Bengal in lieu of the share of export duty on jute and jute products.

> 💡 The Constitution envisions the **Finance Commission as the balancing wheel of fiscal federalism** in India.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> - `[Modern Example]` **15th Finance Commission (2020–2025):** Chaired by **N.K. Singh**, the 15th Finance Commission recommended allocating **41% of the divisible pool** of Central taxes to States (down from 42% by the 14th FC, adjusting for the newly formed UTs of J&K and Ladakh). Its recommendations cover the period **2021-26**.
> - `[Modern Example]` **GST Compensation Dispute (2020–22):** During COVID-19, several States demanded compensation for GST revenue shortfalls as mandated under the GST (Compensation to States) Act, 2017. The Centre's delay in releasing compensation led to a major Centre-State financial standoff — illustrating the critical importance of the **GST Council** as a cooperative federal forum.
> - `[Modern Example]` **Supreme Court on GST Council — Union of India v. Mohit Minerals (2022):** The Supreme Court held that the recommendations of the GST Council are **not binding** on the Centre or States — they are merely recommendatory. This judgment significantly rebalanced federal financial power.

## 7. 🛡️ Protection of States' Interest

The following bills can be introduced in Parliament **only on the recommendation of the President** (to protect State interests):

1. A bill that **imposes or varies any tax or duty** in which States are interested
2. A bill that **varies the meaning of 'agricultural income'** for income tax purposes
3. A bill that **affects the principles** on which money is distributed to States
4. A bill that imposes any **surcharge on any specified tax or duty** for the Centre's purposes

**"Tax or duty in which states are interested" means:**
- (a) A tax/duty whose **net proceeds (wholly or partly) are assigned** to any State; OR
- (b) A tax/duty by reference to whose net proceeds **payments are made from the Consolidated Fund of India** to any State

> 📝 **"Net Proceeds"** = Proceeds of a tax **minus** the cost of collection.
> The net proceeds are ascertained and certified by the **Comptroller and Auditor-General of India (CAG)** — whose certificate is **final**.

## 8. 🏦 Borrowing by Centre and States

| Entity | Borrowing Rules |
|---|---|
| **Central Government** | Can borrow within India OR abroad on the security of the **Consolidated Fund of India**; can give guarantees — both within limits **fixed by Parliament** (no such law enacted yet) |
| **State Government** | Can borrow **within India only** (NOT abroad) on the security of the **Consolidated Fund of the State**; can give guarantees — both within limits fixed by **State Legislature** |
| **Centre lending to States** | Centre can make loans to any State or give guarantees — sums required are charged on the **Consolidated Fund of India** |
| **State needing Centre's consent** | A State **cannot raise a loan without Centre's consent** if any part of a previous Central loan to that State is still outstanding |

## 9. 🔒 Inter-Governmental Tax Immunities

Like other federal constitutions, India's Constitution contains the rule of **'immunity from mutual taxation'** (neither level of government taxes the other's property without permission):

### A. Exemption of Central Property from State Taxation

- **Central government's property** is exempt from all taxes imposed by a State or any authority within a State (municipalities, district boards, panchayats, etc.)
- BUT Parliament is empowered to **remove this exemption**

**What does "property" include?**
- Lands, buildings, chattels (movable goods), shares, debts
- **Everything that has a money value**
- Both **movable and immovable**, both **tangible and intangible**
- Includes property used for **sovereign functions** (like armed forces) OR **commercial purposes**

**Important Exception:**
- **Corporations/companies** created by the Central government are **NOT immune** from state or local taxation — because a corporation is a **separate legal entity**

### B. Exemption of State Property or Income from Central Taxation

- **State property and income** are exempt from Central taxation
- Applies to income from both **sovereign functions** AND **commercial functions**
- BUT the Centre **can tax commercial operations** of a State if Parliament so provides
- However, Parliament can declare any particular trade/business as **incidental to ordinary government functions** — then it would **not be taxable**
- **Important:** Local authority property within a State is **NOT exempt** from Central taxation
- Similarly, **corporations/companies owned by a State** can be taxed by the Centre

---

### ⚖️ Key Supreme Court Case: In Re. Sea Customs Act (1963)

**Case Background:**
This was an advisory opinion (a reference made to the Supreme Court by the President under Article 143 of the Constitution, asking for its opinion on a legal question). The question was whether the immunity granted to States from Central taxation also extended to customs duties and excise duties.

**What the Court Held:**
The Supreme Court held that the **immunity granted to a State in respect of Central taxation does NOT extend to customs duties or duties of excise**.

**What This Means in Simple Terms:**
- The Centre **CAN impose customs duty** on goods imported or exported **by a State**
- The Centre **CAN impose excise duty** on goods produced or manufactured **by a State**
- Even though State property is generally immune from Central taxes, this immunity has a clear limit — it does not protect State trading/commercial activities from customs or excise duties

**Significance:**
This ruling was important because it prevented States from getting an unfair advantage in trade by claiming tax exemption on all their commercial activities. It maintained a level playing field between State-owned and privately-owned commercial enterprises.

## 10. ⚡ Effects of Emergencies on Financial Relations

### A. During National Emergency (Article 352)

- President can **modify the constitutional distribution of revenues** between Centre and States
- This means President can **reduce or cancel** the transfer of finances (both tax sharing and grants-in-aid) from Centre to States
- Such modification continues till the end of the **financial year in which the emergency ceases**

### B. During Financial Emergency (Article 360)

The Centre can direct States to:
1. Observe specified **canons of financial propriety** (rules of sound financial management)
2. **Reduce salaries and allowances** of all classes of persons serving in the State
3. **Reserve all money bills and financial bills** for the consideration of the President

### V. ⭐ Sarkaria Commission, 1983–88

**The Most Important Commission on Centre-State Relations**

- **Appointed by:** Central Government in **1983**

## ⚖️ KEY COURT CASES — DETAILED EXPLANATIONS

---

### 🏛️ Case 1: S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)

**Full Citation:** S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India, AIR 1994 SC 1918

#### Background — What Led to This Case?

S.R. Bommai was the Chief Minister of Karnataka (Janata Dal government). In 1989, some members of his legislative party defected, and the Governor of Karnataka sent a report to the President claiming that Bommai had lost his majority. Based on this report, President's Rule was imposed under **Article 356** — without giving Bommai any opportunity to prove his majority on the floor of the Assembly.

Similar situations had occurred in other states as well. Across India, Article 356 was being routinely misused by the party in power at the Centre to topple opposition-led state governments — regardless of whether the state government had actually lost its majority.

Bommai challenged the dismissal of his government in court.

#### What Questions Did the Court Consider?

The case raised fundamental questions about the nature of India's federalism:
1. Can the Presidential proclamation under Article 356 be questioned in court?
2. What are the limits on the President's power to impose President's Rule?
3. Is India truly federal? Can the Centre dismiss elected state governments at will?

#### What Did the Supreme Court Hold?

A 9-judge Constitutional Bench of the Supreme Court delivered a landmark unanimous judgment with several crucial holdings:

**1. Federalism is part of the Basic Structure:**
The Court held that **federalism is a basic feature of the Indian Constitution** — it cannot be destroyed even by constitutional amendment.

**2. Presidential Proclamation is Justiciable:**
The Court held that the Presidential proclamation under Article 356 is **not immune from judicial review**. Courts CAN examine whether the President had sufficient material to justify imposing President's Rule.

**3. Floor Test is the Only Test:**
The **floor of the House** (the State Assembly) is the **only place where a government's majority should be tested**. The Governor cannot rely on his own assessment or reports from defecting MLAs. Before recommending President's Rule, the Governor must give the Chief Minister an opportunity to **prove majority on the floor of the House**.

**4. Strict Conditions for Article 356:**
President's Rule can be imposed only when:
- There is a genuine **breakdown of constitutional machinery** in the State
- All **other alternatives** have been exhausted
- It is used as a **last resort**, not a first option

**5. The Assembly Cannot be Dissolved Immediately:**
When a Proclamation under Article 356 is issued, **the State Assembly must be kept in suspended animation** (not dissolved immediately). The Assembly should be dissolved only after Parliament approves the proclamation. This gives Parliament the chance to review the decision.

**6. Role of Rajya Sabha:**
The Proclamation must be approved by **both Houses of Parliament**. If the Lok Sabha is dissolved before approving the proclamation, the Rajya Sabha can still approve it. If Rajya Sabha disapproves, the proclamation lapses.

**7. Secularism and Article 356:**
The Court also held that if a State government acts against the **secular principles of the Constitution**, it would be a ground for invoking Article 356. This was in the context of the demolition of Babri Masjid in 1992, after which President's Rule was imposed in BJP-ruled states.

#### Why Is This Case Critically Important?

Before Bommai, Article 356 was imposed over **100 times** since 1950, often for purely political reasons to destabilise opposition governments. The **Bommai judgment fundamentally changed this** by:

- Making the courts the **guardians of federalism**
- Establishing that **elected state governments cannot be arbitrarily dismissed**
- Requiring **objective, verifiable material** before imposing President's Rule
- Protecting state governments from partisan Central interference

After Bommai, the **frequency of President's Rule impositions dropped dramatically**.

The **Punchhi Commission (2010)** recommended that the Bommai guidelines be **constitutionally incorporated into Article 356** through amendments.

---

### 🏛️ Case 2: In Re. Sea Customs Act (1963)

**Full Citation:** In Re. Sea Customs Act (1963) 3 SCR 787 — This was a Presidential Reference (Advisory Opinion) under Article 143

#### Background — What Was the Question?

This was not a dispute between two parties, but an **advisory opinion sought by the President** from the Supreme Court. The question was: Does the immunity granted to States under **Article 289** (exemption of State property and income from Central taxation) also cover **customs duties** and **excise duties**?

In other words: If a State government imports goods or manufactures goods commercially, can the Centre charge customs or excise duty on those goods?

#### What Did the Supreme Court Hold?

The Supreme Court gave a clear answer: **NO** — the immunity granted to States from Central taxation does NOT extend to:
- **Duties of Customs** (tax on imports/exports)
- **Duties of Excise** (tax on goods manufactured within India)

This means:
- The Centre **CAN** impose customs duty on goods **imported or exported by a State**
- The Centre **CAN** impose excise duty on goods **produced or manufactured by a State**

#### Reasoning

The Court reasoned that customs and excise duties are fundamentally different from direct taxes on property or income. They are **transaction-based taxes** that apply to specific activities (importing, exporting, manufacturing). The constitutional immunity for State property was not intended to give States a free pass on all commercial activities.

If States were exempt from customs and excise duties, they would have an **unfair competitive advantage** over private enterprises engaged in the same commercial activities — which would distort the national economy.

#### Significance

This case established an important limitation on State immunity and ensured that the Centre's ability to levy **indirect taxes** (customs, excise) was not undermined by claims of State tax immunity. It helped maintain a **level playing field** in commercial activities between State-run enterprises and private enterprises.

---

### 🏛️ Case 3: Union of India v. Mohit Minerals Pvt. Ltd. (2022) `[Modern Example]`

**Full Citation:** Union of India & Anr. v. Mohit Minerals Pvt. Ltd., 2022 SCC OnLine SC 657

#### Background

After the introduction of GST in 2017, a question arose about the **legal status of GST Council recommendations**. The Union government took the position that the GST Council's recommendations were **binding** on both the Centre and States. This was challenged in cases involving the levy of IGST on ocean freight.

#### What Did the Supreme Court Hold?

A 3-judge bench of the Supreme Court, in a landmark judgment, held that:

**GST Council recommendations are NOT binding — they are merely recommendatory.**

The Court held that:
- Both Parliament and State Legislatures have **simultaneous and co-equal powers** to legislate on GST under Article 246A
- The Constitution does not explicitly state that GST Council recommendations are binding
- Treating them as binding would be **inconsistent with the federal structure** of the Constitution
- The **Centre and States have equal, competing sovereignty** in the GST domain

#### Significance

This judgment was hugely significant for Indian federalism because:
- It **empowered State Legislatures** to deviate from GST Council recommendations
- It reinforced that **cooperative federalism** means equal partnership, not Central dominance
- It provided States with constitutional backing to resist Centre's attempts to override State GST decisions
- It is considered one of the most important federalism judgments after S.R. Bommai
