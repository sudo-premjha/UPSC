## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Main Takeaways before you begin reading:**
>
> 1. **Article 368** governs the amendment of the Indian Constitution — but it is not the *only* way the Constitution can be changed.
> 2. The Indian Constitution is **neither purely flexible nor purely rigid** — it is a **synthesis of both**.
> 3. There are **three types of amendment procedures** — Simple Majority, Special Majority, and Special Majority + State Ratification.
> 4. The **Parliament cannot amend the "Basic Structure"** of the Constitution — as ruled in the landmark *Kesavananda Bharati* case (1973).
> 5. The amendment process has been **criticised for being Parliament-centric**, but is widely acknowledged to strike a good balance between flexibility and rigidity.

## 🧭 Introduction: Why Amend a Constitution?

Every written Constitution needs a way to **update itself** as society, politics, and needs evolve over time. The Indian Constitution is no different.

However, the **method of amendment** matters a lot:
- In **Britain**, the Constitution is **unwritten and very flexible** — Parliament can change almost anything easily.
- In **USA**, the Constitution is **extremely rigid** — amendments require an overwhelming consensus.
- In **India**, the framers chose a **middle path** — neither too easy nor too difficult to amend.

**Article 368** in **Part XX** of the Constitution is the central provision. It gives Parliament the power to amend the Constitution by:
- **Addition** (adding new provisions)
- **Variation** (modifying existing provisions)
- **Repeal** (removing existing provisions)

> ⚠️ **Critical Limit:** Parliament's amendment power is **not absolute**. It **cannot amend provisions that form the "Basic Structure"** of the Constitution. This was established by the Supreme Court in the **Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973)** case.

---

## ⚖️ The Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973) — Explained

**Full Case Name:** *Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973)*

**What happened?**
Kesavananda Bharati, the head of a religious institution in Kerala, challenged the Kerala government's land reform laws that sought to restrict his right to manage religious property.

The broader constitutional question raised was: **Can Parliament amend ANY part of the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights?**

**What did the Supreme Court rule?**
A 13-judge bench — the largest ever in Indian constitutional history — ruled by a **7:6 majority** that:

- ✅ Parliament **CAN** amend any provision of the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights.
- ❌ Parliament **CANNOT** alter or destroy the **"Basic Structure"** (core framework) of the Constitution.

**What is the "Basic Structure"?**
The Court did not give a fixed, exhaustive list, but identified the following elements as part of the Basic Structure:
- Supremacy of the Constitution
- Republican and democratic form of government
- Secular character of the Constitution
- Separation of powers
- Federal character of the Constitution
- Unity and integrity of the nation
- Sovereignty of India
- Individual freedom and dignity
- Rule of law
- Judicial review
- Free and fair elections
- Parliamentary system of government
- Welfare State (Directive Principles)

**Why is this case so important?**
This case **reversed** the earlier ruling in *Golak Nath vs. State of Punjab (1967)*, where the Supreme Court had said Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights at all. Kesavananda Bharati gave a more nuanced ruling — amendment is allowed, but the *basic identity* of the Constitution cannot be destroyed.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` The Basic Structure doctrine remains highly relevant today. In **2015**, the Supreme Court struck down the **99th Constitutional Amendment Act** (which created the **National Judicial Appointments Commission — NJAC**) in the *Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India* case, holding that it violated the **independence of the judiciary** — a core element of the Basic Structure. This shows the doctrine is alive and actively applied.

## 📋 Procedure for Amendment (Step-by-Step)

As laid down in **Article 368**, here is how the Constitution is amended:

### Step 1: Introduction of the Bill
- The amendment bill can be introduced in **either House of Parliament** (Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha).
- It **cannot** be introduced in state legislatures.
- It can be introduced by **a minister or a private member** — no prior permission of the President is needed.

### Step 2: Passing the Bill with Special Majority
- The bill must be passed in **each House separately** by a **special majority**:
  - A majority of the **total membership** of the House, **AND**
  - A majority of **two-thirds of members present and voting**.
- **"Total membership"** = the full strength of the House, regardless of vacancies or absentees.
- ❌ There is **no provision for a joint sitting** of both Houses if there is a disagreement. This is unlike ordinary bills, where a joint sitting can resolve deadlocks.

### Step 3: Ratification by States (Only When Required)
- If the bill proposes to amend **federal provisions** of the Constitution, it must also be **ratified by the legislatures of at least half the states** by a **simple majority** (majority of members present and voting).
- If some states do not act on it — **it doesn't matter**. As soon as **half the states give consent**, the requirement is fulfilled.
- There is **no time limit** for states to give their consent.

### Step 4: Presidential Assent
- After passing both Houses (and state ratification, if required), the bill is sent to the **President for assent**.
- The President **must give assent** — they **cannot**:
  - Withhold assent, or
  - Return the bill for reconsideration.
- This was made **mandatory** by the **24th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1971**.

### Step 5: Constitution Stands Amended
- After the President's assent, the bill becomes a **Constitutional Amendment Act**.
- The Constitution is updated as per the terms of the Act.

## 🗂️ Types of Amendments: The Three-Track System

Not all parts of the Constitution need the same level of effort to amend. There are **three distinct tracks**:

---

### 🔵 Track 1: Amendment by Simple Majority of Parliament

These provisions can be amended just like **ordinary legislation** — a simple majority of members present and voting in each House.

> **Important:** These amendments are **NOT considered amendments under Article 368**. They fall outside its scope.

**Provisions covered include:**

| # | Provision |
|---|-----------|
| 1 | Admission or establishment of **new states** |
| 2 | Formation of new states and alteration of areas, **boundaries or names** of existing states |
| 3 | **Abolition or creation of Legislative Councils** in states |
| 4 | **Second Schedule** — emoluments, allowances, privileges of the President, Governors, Speakers, Judges, etc. |
| 5 | **Quorum** in Parliament |
| 6 | **Salaries and allowances** of Members of Parliament |
| 7 | **Rules of procedure** in Parliament |
| 8 | **Privileges** of Parliament, its members and committees |
| 9 | Use of **English language** in Parliament |
| 10 | **Number of puisne judges** (junior judges) in the Supreme Court |
| 11 | Conferment of **more jurisdiction** on the Supreme Court |
| 12 | Use of **official language** |
| 13 | **Citizenship** — acquisition and termination |
| 14 | **Elections** to Parliament and state legislatures |
| 15 | **Delimitation of constituencies** |
| 16 | **Union territories** |
| 17 | **Fifth Schedule** — administration of Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes |
| 18 | **Sixth Schedule** — administration of tribal areas |

---

### 🟡 Track 2: Amendment by Special Majority of Parliament

The **majority of constitutional provisions** fall in this category. They need:
- Majority of the **total membership** of each House, **AND**
- Majority of **two-thirds of members present and voting**

> The Lok Sabha Rules require this special majority to be maintained at **all effective stages** of the bill — not just the final vote.

**Provisions covered include:**
- **Fundamental Rights** (Part III)
- **Directive Principles of State Policy** (Part IV)
- **All other provisions** not covered under Track 1 or Track 3

---

### 🔴 Track 3: Special Majority of Parliament + Ratification by Half the States

These are provisions related to the **federal structure** (power-sharing between Centre and States). They need the **highest level of consensus**.

**Process:**
- Special majority in Parliament, **PLUS**
- Ratification by **at least half the state legislatures** by simple majority
- No time limit for states; remaining states' inaction doesn't block the amendment

**Provisions covered:**

| # | Provision |
|---|-----------|
| 1 | **Election of the President** and its manner |
| 2 | Extent of **executive power** of the Union and the states |
| 3 | **Supreme Court and High Courts** |
| 4 | **Distribution of legislative powers** between the Union and states |
| 5 | **Goods and Services Tax (GST) Council** *(added by the 101st Amendment Act of 2016, related to Article 279-A)* |
| 6 | Any of the **lists in the Seventh Schedule** (Union List, State List, Concurrent List) |
| 7 | **Representation of states** in Parliament |
| 8 | **Article 368 itself** — the power of Parliament to amend the Constitution |

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` The **101st Constitutional Amendment Act of 2016** introduced **GST (Goods and Services Tax)** — one of the biggest tax reforms in independent India. Since it fundamentally changed the financial relationship between the Centre and states (a federal matter), the GST Council was added to the list of provisions requiring **Track 3 amendment** (special majority + state ratification). This is a live example of a Track 3 amendment being used for a major economic reform.

## 🚨 Criticism of the Amendment Procedure

Despite working reasonably well, scholars and critics have pointed out **seven key weaknesses**:

### 1. 🏛️ No Special Body for Amendment
- India has **no Constitutional Convention** (like in the USA) or a special Constitutional Assembly for amending the Constitution.
- The **constituent power** (power to change the Constitution) is vested **mainly in Parliament** and only occasionally in state legislatures.

### 2. 📌 States Cannot Initiate Amendments
- Unlike in the **USA**, where state legislatures can propose constitutional amendments (if two-thirds of them petition the Congress to call a Constitutional Convention), **Indian states have no power to initiate amendments**.
- The **only exception**: A state legislature can pass a resolution requesting Parliament to **create or abolish a Legislative Council** in that state. Even then, Parliament is not bound to act on it.

### 3. ⚖️ Parliament-Centric Process
- The **bulk of the Constitution** can be amended by Parliament alone — either by a simple majority or special majority.
- State consent is required only for a **small set of federal provisions**.
- Even then, only **half the states** need to agree — compared to **three-fourths of states** required in the USA.

### 4. ⏰ No Time Limit for State Ratification
- The Constitution does **not prescribe** how long states have to ratify or reject an amendment.
- It is also **silent** on whether a state can **withdraw its approval** after having given it.

### 5. 🤝 No Joint Sitting for Deadlocks
- If the two Houses of Parliament **disagree** on a constitutional amendment bill, there is **no provision** for a **joint sitting** to resolve the deadlock.
- This is ironic because for **ordinary bills**, a joint sitting IS available to break deadlocks (Article 108).

### 6. 📝 Process Similar to Ordinary Legislation
- Except for the special majority requirement, the process of passing a constitutional amendment is very similar to passing an **ordinary law**.
- Critics argue this does **not adequately distinguish** between ordinary law-making and constitutional change.

### 7. 🕳️ Sketchy Provisions Leave Room for Judicial Intervention
- The provisions relating to the amendment procedure are **too brief and incomplete**.
- This leaves **wide scope** for parties to approach the judiciary, leading to frequent judicial involvement in constitutional matters.

## 💬 Defence of the Amendment Procedure: What the Founders Said

Despite these criticisms, the amendment procedure has proven **effective and balanced** over 75+ years.

### K.C. Wheare's Assessment
**K.C. Wheare** (*Modern Constitutions*, 1966, p. 43) praised the Indian approach:
> *"This variety in the amending process is wise but rarely found."*

He also said it **"strikes a good balance between flexibility and rigidity."**

### Jawaharlal Nehru's Vision
Speaking in the **Constituent Assembly Debates (Vol. VII, pp. 322–23)**, Nehru said:
> *"While we want this Constitution to be as solid and permanent as we can make it, there is no permanence in a Constitution. There should be a certain flexibility. If you make any Constitution rigid and permanent, you stop the nation's growth, the growth of a living, vital, organic people."*

### Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's Reasoning
In the **Constituent Assembly Debates (Vol. IX, p. 976)**, Ambedkar explained:
> *"The Assembly has not only refrained from putting a seal of finality and infallibility upon this Constitution by denying the people the right to amend the Constitution as in Canada or by making the amendment of the Constitution subject to the fulfilment of extraordinary terms and conditions as in America or Australia, but has provided for a facile procedure for amending the Constitution."*

**"Facile"** here means *easy and practical* — not "too simple."

## 📜 Important Cases: Explained in Detail

### Case 1: Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973)
*(Already explained in detail above — see Introduction section)*

**Key Outcome:** Established the **Basic Structure Doctrine** — Parliament can amend the Constitution but cannot destroy its essential identity.

---

### Case 2: Shankari Prasad vs. Union of India (1951)

**Background:** The **1st Constitutional Amendment Act (1951)** added the **Ninth Schedule** (a list of laws protected from judicial review) and restricted the right to property. It was challenged on the grounds that Parliament cannot curtail Fundamental Rights through an amendment.

**Supreme Court Ruling:**
- The Court held that Parliament's power to amend the Constitution under **Article 368 includes the power to amend Fundamental Rights**.
- The word **"law"** in Article 13 (which says the State shall not make any law that takes away Fundamental Rights) **does not include constitutional amendments**.

**Significance:** Upheld Parliament's supremacy to amend any part of the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights.

---

### Case 3: Sajjan Singh vs. State of Rajasthan (1964)

**Background:** The **17th Constitutional Amendment Act (1964)** was challenged. The question was the same — can Parliament amend Fundamental Rights?

**Supreme Court Ruling:**
- Reaffirmed the **Shankari Prasad ruling** — Parliament can amend Fundamental Rights.
- However, two judges (Justice Hidayatullah and Justice Mudholkar) dissented, expressing doubts and suggesting that the issue needed reconsideration.

**Significance:** While it upheld parliamentary supremacy, the dissenting opinions planted the seed for a future reconsideration — which came in the *Golak Nath* case.

---

### Case 4: I.C. Golak Nath vs. State of Punjab (1967)

**Background:** The Punjab government's land ceiling laws were challenged. The petitioners argued that constitutional amendments taking away Fundamental Rights were invalid.

**Supreme Court Ruling (11-judge bench, 6:5 majority):**
- Overruled *Shankari Prasad* and *Sajjan Singh*.
- Held that **Parliament CANNOT amend Fundamental Rights** at all.
- Declared that a **Constitutional Amendment is also "law"** under Article 13, and therefore must be consistent with Fundamental Rights.
- Applied the doctrine of **"prospective overruling"** — meaning this decision would apply to future amendments, not past ones (to avoid disturbing already-settled matters).

**Significance:** Severely limited Parliament's power. Led to a major political response — Parliament passed the **24th, 25th, and 26th Amendment Acts** to negate this judgment.

---

### Case 5: Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973) *(Detailed)*

**Background:** Passed in the aftermath of Golak Nath, Parliament had enacted:
- **24th Amendment (1971):** Asserted Parliament's absolute power to amend any part of the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights; made Presidential assent mandatory.
- **25th Amendment (1971):** Curtailed the right to property; said laws giving "equitable compensation" for property acquisition cannot be challenged in court.
- **26th Amendment (1971):** Abolished privy purses (allowances to former rulers of princely states).

Kesavananda Bharati challenged the Kerala land reform laws and, more broadly, the constitutional validity of these amendments.

**Supreme Court Ruling (13-judge bench — largest ever — 7:6 majority):**
- Overruled **Golak Nath** — Parliament CAN amend Fundamental Rights.
- However, introduced the **Basic Structure Doctrine** — Parliament CANNOT amend the "basic structure" or "essential features" of the Constitution.
- Upheld the **24th Amendment** as valid.
- Partially struck down the **25th Amendment** — the part that excluded judicial review was invalid.

**Elements identified as Basic Structure (from various judgments over time):**
- Supremacy of the Constitution
- Republican and democratic form of government
- Secular character
- Separation of powers
- Federal character
- Unity and integrity of India
- Sovereignty
- Individual freedom and dignity
- Rule of law
- Judicial review
- Free and fair elections
- Parliamentary system
- Welfare State objectives

**Significance:** This is arguably the **most important constitutional case** in Indian history. It created a permanent check on Parliament's amending power.

---

### Case 6: Indira Nehru Gandhi vs. Raj Narain (1975)

**Background:** Raj Narain challenged Indira Gandhi's election to the Lok Sabha from Rae Bareli on grounds of electoral malpractice. The Allahabad High Court found her guilty and declared her election void. In response, the **39th Constitutional Amendment (1975)** was passed, which placed the election disputes of the Prime Minister, President, Vice President, and Speaker beyond judicial review.

**Supreme Court Ruling:**
- Struck down the **39th Amendment** as unconstitutional.
- Held that placing the PM's election beyond judicial review violated **free and fair elections** and **democracy** — both part of the Basic Structure.
- Reaffirmed and expanded the Basic Structure Doctrine.

**Significance:** One of the most politically charged applications of the Basic Structure Doctrine. Demonstrated that even constitutional amendments made to protect political interests can be struck down.

---

### Case 7: Minerva Mills vs. Union of India (1980)

**Background:** The **42nd Constitutional Amendment Act (1976)** — passed during the Emergency — had made sweeping changes, including:
- Gave Parliament unlimited power to amend the Constitution (effectively nullifying *Kesavananda Bharati*).
- Gave Directive Principles (Part IV) absolute priority over Fundamental Rights (Part III).
- Excluded judicial review of constitutional amendments.

**Supreme Court Ruling:**
- Struck down two key clauses of the 42nd Amendment.
- Held that **Parliament's power to amend is limited** — an unlimited amending power would itself destroy the Basic Structure.
- Reaffirmed that **harmony between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles** is part of the Basic Structure — one cannot be made absolutely subservient to the other.
- Reaffirmed that **judicial review** is a Basic Structure element.

**Key Quote from Judgment:** *"A limited government under a controlled Constitution is the basic postulate of our constitutional system."*

**Significance:** Restored the balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles. Firmly entrenched the Basic Structure Doctrine against legislative overreach.

---

### Case 8: Waman Rao vs. Union of India (1981)

**Background:** Validity of laws placed in the **Ninth Schedule** (which protects laws from judicial review) after the Kesavananda Bharati judgment was challenged.

**Supreme Court Ruling:**
- Laws placed in the Ninth Schedule **before April 24, 1973** (the date of the *Kesavananda Bharati* judgment) were protected from judicial review.
- Laws placed in the Ninth Schedule **after April 24, 1973** could be challenged if they violated the Basic Structure.

**Significance:** Set a temporal limit on Ninth Schedule immunity.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` In **I.R. Coelho vs. State of Tamil Nadu (2007)**, a 9-judge Constitutional Bench reaffirmed the *Waman Rao* principle — any law added to the Ninth Schedule after **April 24, 1973** is open to judicial review if it violates Fundamental Rights that are part of the Basic Structure. This has direct relevance today as many land reform and reservation laws continue to be tested against this standard.

---

### Case 9: S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)

**Background:** This case dealt with the imposition of President's Rule (Article 356) in states and whether such proclamations are subject to judicial review.

**Supreme Court Ruling:**
- **Federalism** is a **Basic Structure** element of the Constitution.
- The imposition of President's Rule is subject to **judicial review**.
- The **floor of the House** (Legislature) is the proper place to test a government's majority — not the Governor's subjective assessment.

**Significance:** While not directly about the amendment procedure, this case powerfully reinforced **federalism** as an unamendable Basic Structure element, placing clear limits on what Parliament and the executive can do even through constitutional processes.

---

### Case 10: Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India (2015) — NJAC Case

**Background:** The **99th Constitutional Amendment Act (2014)** abolished the **Collegium System** (where senior judges recommend appointments of judges) and replaced it with the **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC)**, which included the Law Minister and two eminent persons.

**Supreme Court Ruling (4:1 majority):**
- Struck down the **99th Amendment** and the **NJAC Act** as unconstitutional.
- Held that the NJAC compromised the **independence of the judiciary** — a core Basic Structure element.
- The inclusion of the Law Minister (executive) in judicial appointments was seen as violating the separation of powers.

**Significance:** The most recent and prominent application of the Basic Structure Doctrine. Demonstrated that even a constitutional amendment passed with the required special majority and ratified by states can be struck down if it violates the Basic Structure.

🌍 **Modern Context:**
> `[Modern Example]` The debate over judicial appointments continues. As of **2023–2024**, the tension between the **government and the Supreme Court** over the Collegium system remains unresolved, with the government pushing for reforms while the Supreme Court maintains the Collegium's primacy under the Basic Structure principle.

## 📊 Quick Comparison: Amendment Procedures

| Feature | Simple Majority | Special Majority | Special Majority + States |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| **Type of majority needed** | Members present and voting | Total membership + 2/3 of those present & voting | Same as Special Majority |
| **State ratification needed?** | No | No | Yes — at least half the states |
| **Covered under Article 368?** | ❌ No | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| **Example** | Adding a new state | Amending Fundamental Rights | Amending election of President |
| **Presidential Assent required?** | Yes | Yes | Yes (mandatory) |

## 🌍 Modern Context: The Big Picture

> `[Modern Example]` **How many times has India amended its Constitution?**
> As of 2024, the Indian Constitution has been amended **106 times** (the 106th Amendment was passed in 2023, relating to reservation for women — **Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023** — the **Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam**, which provided 33% reservation for women in the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies).

> `[Modern Example]` **The 105th Amendment Act (2021)** restored the power of **states to identify Other Backward Classes (OBCs)** — this was passed after the Supreme Court's ruling in *Maratha Reservation case (2021)* which had struck down Maharashtra's OBC reservation. This is a live example of Parliament using its amendment power to respond to judicial decisions.

> `[Modern Example]` **The 101st Amendment Act (2016)** introduced **GST** — replacing a complex web of central and state taxes with one unified tax. This required Track 3 amendment (Special Majority + State Ratification) because it altered the Seventh Schedule lists, affecting the financial federalism of the country.

## 🧠 Memory Tricks

**To remember the THREE tracks of amendment:**
> **"SiSR"** = **Si**mple, **S**pecial, **S**pecial + **R**atification

**To remember what needs Track 3 (State Ratification):**
> Think **"FRED"** — **F**ederal structure, **R**epresentation of states, **E**lection of President, **D**istribution of powers

**To remember Kesavananda Bharati (1973):**
> **"Parliament can carve — but cannot demolish the building."** = Parliament can amend (carve) the Constitution, but cannot destroy (demolish) its Basic Structure.
