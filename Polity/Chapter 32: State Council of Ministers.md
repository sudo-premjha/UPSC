> **Top 5 Takeaways before you begin:**
>
> 1. **Articles 163, 164, 166, 167, and 177** are the core constitutional provisions governing the State Council of Ministers.
> 2. The **Governor** is the nominal (formal) head; the **Council of Ministers headed by the Chief Minister** is the real executive authority in a state.
> 3. **Collective Responsibility** to the State Legislative Assembly is the *fundamental principle* of the parliamentary system at the state level.
> 4. The **91st Amendment Act (2003)** capped the size of the council of ministers at **15% of the Legislative Assembly's strength** (minimum 12).
> 5. The **advice given by ministers to the Governor cannot be questioned in any court** — this protects the confidentiality of the cabinet.

---

## 🏛️ Introduction: Why Does This Chapter Matter?

India follows a **parliamentary system of government** (a system where the executive is drawn from and accountable to the legislature) in its states, modeled on the same pattern as the Union (Central) Government.

In this system, the **Council of Ministers headed by the Chief Minister** is the *real* executive authority in a state. The Governor, though the constitutional head, acts largely on their advice.

The principles of this parliamentary system are **not spelled out in detail** in the Constitution. Instead, **only two Articles — 163 and 164 — deal with them**, and that too in a broad, general manner.

---

## 📜 Section 1: Constitutional Provisions (Articles 163, 164, 166, 167, 177)

*Think of these articles as the legal "rulebook" for the State Council of Ministers.*

---

### ⚖️ Article 163 — Council of Ministers to Aid and Advise the Governor

This article defines the *relationship* between the Governor and the Council of Ministers.

**Three key rules under Article 163:**

- **Rule 1:** There shall be a Council of Ministers with the **Chief Minister as the head** to **aid and advise the Governor** in exercising his/her functions — *except* where the Governor is required to act in his/her **discretion** (independent judgment without ministerial advice).
- **Rule 2:** If any question arises about *whether* a matter falls within the Governor's discretion, the **Governor's decision is final**. No one can legally challenge an action of the Governor on the ground that he/she should or should not have used discretion.
- **Rule 3:** The **advice given by ministers to the Governor cannot be inquired into in any court**. This protects the confidentiality and intimacy of the Governor-minister relationship.

> 💡 **Simple Way to Remember:** The Governor *listens* to ministers for almost everything. But in a few special discretionary matters, the Governor *decides alone* — and that decision is final and unchallengeable in court.

---

### ⚖️ Article 164 — Other Provisions Regarding Ministers

This is the most detailed article. It covers **appointment, size, disqualification, tenure, collective responsibility, oath, membership, and salary.**

Here's each provision explained simply:

| Provision | What It Says |
|---|---|
| **Appointment** | Chief Minister is appointed by the Governor. Other Ministers are appointed by the Governor *on the advice of the CM*. |
| **Tribal Welfare Minister** | In **Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, and Odisha**, there must be a Minister in charge of **tribal welfare**. This minister may also handle SC/BC welfare. |
| **Bihar's Exemption** | Bihar was **removed** from the tribal welfare minister requirement by the **94th Amendment Act of 2006** because Scheduled Areas no longer exist there and the ST population fraction is very small. |
| **Size of Cabinet (Cap)** | Total ministers (including CM) **cannot exceed 15%** of the total strength of the State Legislative Assembly. Added by the **91st Amendment Act of 2003**. |
| **Minimum Strength** | The number of ministers (including CM) **shall not be less than 12**, even in smaller states. Also added by the **91st Amendment Act of 2003**. |
| **Anti-Defection Disqualification** | A legislator disqualified under the **anti-defection law** (expelled for switching parties) **cannot be appointed as a minister**. Added by the **91st Amendment Act of 2003**. |
| **Tenure** | Ministers hold office **during the pleasure of the Governor** (meaning the Governor can remove them — but only on the CM's advice). |
| **Collective Responsibility** | The Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible** to the **State Legislative Assembly**. |
| **Oath** | The **Governor administers** the oaths of office and secrecy to ministers. |
| **6-Month Rule** | A minister who is **not a member of the state legislature for 6 consecutive months** ceases to be a minister. |
| **Salary** | Salaries and allowances of ministers are determined by the **state legislature**. |

---

### ⚖️ Article 166 — Conduct of Business of the State Government

This article deals with *how* the state government formally functions.

- All **executive actions** of the State Government must be expressed as being taken **in the name of the Governor**.
- Orders and instruments executed in the Governor's name must be **authenticated** (officially verified/signed) in a manner specified by the Governor's rules.
- The **validity** of such authenticated orders **cannot be challenged** on the ground that the Governor did not personally make or execute them.
- The Governor shall make **rules** for the convenient transaction of government business and for **allocating work among ministers** — except for matters where the Governor acts at his/her discretion.

---

### ⚖️ Article 167 — Duties of the Chief Minister

The Chief Minister has **three specific constitutional duties** towards the Governor:

1. To **communicate** to the Governor all decisions of the Council of Ministers relating to state administration and proposals for legislation.
2. To **furnish information** about state administration and legislative proposals as and when the Governor asks for it.
3. If the Governor so requires, to **submit for cabinet consideration** any matter on which *a single minister* has decided but which has *not yet been discussed* by the full Council of Ministers.

> 💡 **Key insight:** Article 167 is essentially about *keeping the Governor informed*. The CM acts as the bridge between the cabinet and the Governor.

---

### ⚖️ Article 177 — Rights of Ministers Regarding the Legislature

- Every **minister has the right to speak and participate** in the proceedings of:
  - The **Legislative Assembly**, and
  - The **Legislative Council** (where it exists), and
  - Any **Committee of the State Legislature** of which he/she is a named member.
- However, a minister **cannot vote** in a House of which he/she is *not* a member.

> 💡 **Example:** A minister who is a member of the Legislative Council can still speak in the Legislative Assembly — but cannot vote there.

## 🤝 Section 2: Nature of Advice by Ministers

A very important concept — *what kind of advice do ministers give, and is it binding?*

**The core rule under Article 163:**
- The Council of Ministers, headed by the CM, **aids and advises the Governor** in *all* functions — **except** discretionary ones.
- If a dispute arises about whether something falls under discretion, **the Governor decides, and that decision is final** and cannot be challenged in court.
- The **nature and content of ministerial advice cannot be investigated by any court**. This ensures a confidential relationship between the Governor and ministers.

---

### ⚖️ Landmark Case: *Shamsher Singh vs. State of Punjab (1974)*

#### 🏛️ Background of the Case:
This is one of the most important Supreme Court judgments on the relationship between the Governor and the Council of Ministers. The case arose in the context of whether the Governor of Punjab could act *independently* (without ministerial advice) in certain service matters — specifically related to the appointment/confirmation of two District Judges.

#### 🔍 What Was the Core Question?
Can the Governor act *personally*, without the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers, in matters where the Constitution mentions "the satisfaction of the Governor"?

#### 📜 What Did the Supreme Court Decide?

The Supreme Court, in a **Constitution Bench judgment**, laid down the following landmark principles:

1. **Except in areas where the Governor is constitutionally required to act at his/her discretion**, the Governor **must** act on the **aid and advice of the Council of Ministers** in all exercises of power and functions.

2. The Governor is **not required — and in fact, not permitted — to act personally**, without or against the advice of the Council of Ministers, in normal governmental matters.

3. Wherever the Constitution uses the phrase **"satisfaction of the Governor,"** this does **not mean the personal, subjective satisfaction** of the Governor as an individual. It means the **satisfaction of the Council of Ministers** — i.e., it is the cabinet that must be satisfied, not the Governor personally.

4. This judgment **firmly established the supremacy of the elected Council of Ministers** over the Governor in day-to-day governance.

#### 🎯 Why It Matters for UPSC:
This case is frequently cited to explain that:
- India's Governor is a **constitutional head**, not a real executive.
- The **real executive power** rests with the Council of Ministers.
- "Governor's satisfaction" is, in reality, **cabinet's satisfaction**.

> 📌 **Remember the formula:** *Governor acts = Council of Ministers decides* (except in discretionary matters).

---

🌍 **Modern Context**
> `[Modern Example]` The tension between Governors and elected state governments has been a **major constitutional controversy in 2021–2024**. Governors in states like **Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Punjab, and Telangana** have been accused by elected state governments of *withholding assent to bills, delaying appointments, and acting beyond their constitutional role* — essentially violating the spirit of the *Shamsher Singh* judgment. In **2023**, the Supreme Court in **State of Punjab vs. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab** reaffirmed that Governors cannot sit indefinitely on bills passed by the legislature and reiterated the Shamsher Singh principle that Governors must act on ministerial advice. This shows that a 1974 case is still being actively enforced in 2023–24.

## 👥 Section 3: Appointment of Ministers

### How Are Ministers Appointed?

- The **Chief Minister is appointed by the Governor**.
- **Other ministers** are appointed by the Governor **on the advice of the Chief Minister** — meaning the Governor can only appoint those persons *recommended by the CM*. The Governor has no independent choice here.

### Who Can Be a Minister?

- **Usually:** Members of the State Legislature (either the Legislative Assembly or the Legislative Council).
- **Exception:** A **non-legislator can also be appointed as a minister**, but with a condition:
  - He/she **must become a member** (by election or nomination) of *either House* of the state legislature **within 6 months**.
  - If this condition is not fulfilled, he/she **ceases to be a minister** after 6 months.

### Rights of a Minister in the Legislature:
- A minister who is a member of **one House** has the right to **speak and participate** in the other House.
- But he/she can **vote only in the House of which he/she is a member**.

### The Tribal Welfare Minister Requirement:

| State | Required to Have Tribal Welfare Minister? |
|---|---|
| **Chhattisgarh** | ✅ Yes |
| **Jharkhand** | ✅ Yes |
| **Madhya Pradesh** | ✅ Yes |
| **Odisha** | ✅ Yes |
| **Bihar** | ❌ No (removed by 94th Amendment Act, 2006) |

> **Why was Bihar removed?** Because Bihar no longer has **Scheduled Areas** and its Scheduled Tribe population fraction is very small.
> **Why were Chhattisgarh and Jharkhand added?** The 94th Amendment Act of 2006 extended this obligation to these *newly formed states* (carved out of Madhya Pradesh and Bihar respectively), which have significant tribal populations.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**
> `[Modern Example]` The role of a Tribal Welfare Minister remains highly relevant today. The **PM JANMAN (Pradhan Mantri Janjati Adivasi Nyaya Maha Abhiyan)**, launched in **November 2023**, specifically targets the **75 most vulnerable tribal groups (PVTGs)** across states including Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, and MP. The existence of a dedicated tribal welfare minister in these states is directly aligned with the constitutional mandate and the current policy push for tribal development.

## 🙏 Section 4: Oath and Salary of Ministers

### Oath of Office

Before entering office, every minister takes **two oaths**, administered by the **Governor**:

**1. Oath of Office — The minister swears to:**
- Bear **true faith and allegiance** to the Constitution of India.
- Uphold the **sovereignty and integrity** of India.
- **Faithfully and conscientiously discharge** the duties of his/her office.
- **Do right** to all manner of people in accordance with the Constitution and the law, **without fear or favour, affection or ill-will**.

**2. Oath of Secrecy — The minister swears that:**
- He/she will **not directly or indirectly communicate or reveal** to any person(s) any matter that is brought under his/her consideration or becomes known to him/her as a state minister — *except as may be required for the due discharge of his/her ministerial duties*.

> 💡 **Why does the Oath of Secrecy matter?** It ensures that **cabinet deliberations remain confidential**. This is essential for frank, open discussion within the cabinet.

---

### Salary and Allowances

- Salaries and allowances are determined by the **state legislature** from time to time.
- A minister receives:
  - **Basic salary and allowances** payable to a member of the state legislature, **PLUS**:
  - A **sumptuary allowance** (a special allowance based on rank/status)
  - **Free accommodation**
  - **Travelling allowance**
  - **Medical facilities**

## 🤝 Section 5: Responsibility of Ministers

This is a critical section for understanding how the parliamentary system works.

### A. Collective Responsibility

**This is the FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE of the parliamentary system.**

**Article 164** clearly states: The Council of Ministers is **collectively responsible to the Legislative Assembly** of the state.

**What does "collective responsibility" mean in practice?**

- All ministers **own joint responsibility** for *all acts of omission and commission* — everything the government does or fails to do.
- Ministers **work as a team** — they swim or sink together.
- **Cabinet decisions bind all ministers**, even those who *disagreed* during the cabinet meeting.
- Every minister must **stand by and defend** cabinet decisions both inside and outside the legislature.
- If a minister **disagrees with a cabinet decision** and is not prepared to defend it, he/she **must resign**. Many ministers have resigned in the past due to such differences.

**What happens if the Legislative Assembly passes a No-Confidence Motion?**

- **All ministers must resign** — including ministers who are from the Legislative Council (upper house).
- ⚠️ **Important Note:** Each minister does *not* need to resign separately. The **resignation of the Chief Minister automatically amounts to the resignation of the entire Council of Ministers**.

**Can the Cabinet avoid a no-confidence vote?**
- Yes — the Council of Ministers can **advise the Governor to dissolve the Legislative Assembly** on the ground that it no longer faithfully represents the electorate, and call for **fresh elections**.
- However, the **Governor is not obliged** to accept this advice if the council of ministers has *already lost the confidence* of the assembly.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**
> `[Modern Example]` The principle of collective responsibility was dramatically tested during the **Maharashtra political crisis of 2022**. When the Uddhav Thackeray-led MVA government lost its majority due to a rebellion led by Eknath Shinde, the entire Council of Ministers fell — illustrating collective responsibility perfectly. Subsequently, the **Supreme Court in *Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra* (2023)** ruled on the Governor's role in this crisis, reaffirming constitutional norms about when a Governor can ask a CM to prove majority. This case is now a landmark judgment on the interface of collective responsibility, defection, and gubernatorial powers.

---

### B. Individual Responsibility

**Article 164** also contains the principle of **individual responsibility**:

- Ministers hold office **during the pleasure of the Governor**.
- This means: The Governor *can* remove a minister even when the Council of Ministers *as a whole* enjoys the confidence of the legislature.
- **BUT:** The Governor can remove a minister **only on the advice of the Chief Minister**. The Governor cannot act independently here.
- If a minister differs with the CM or performs poorly, the CM can:
  - Ask the minister to **resign**, OR
  - Advise the Governor to **dismiss** the minister.

> 💡 **This power gives the CM enormous authority** — it is through this power that the CM enforces collective responsibility. If a minister refuses to toe the cabinet line, the CM can remove him/her.

---

### C. No Legal Responsibility

- **There is no provision in the Constitution** for *legal responsibility* of ministers at the state level (just as there is no such provision at the Centre).
- It is **not required** that a Governor's order for a public act be **countersigned by a minister** (unlike in some other constitutional systems).
- Courts are **barred from inquiring** into the nature of advice given by ministers to the Governor.

---

## 🏗️ Section 6: Composition of the Council of Ministers

### Size and Structure

- The Constitution does **not specify** the size of the State Council of Ministers or the ranking of ministers.
- These are determined by the **Chief Minister** based on the needs and requirements of the time.

### Three Categories of Ministers

The Council of Ministers in every state consists of **three categories**:

---

#### 1. 🟡 Cabinet Ministers (Highest Rank)
- Head the **important departments** of the state government (e.g., Home, Education, Finance, Agriculture).
- Are **members of the Cabinet** and **attend cabinet meetings regularly**.
- Play an important role in **deciding all policies**.
- Their responsibilities cover the **entire gamut of state government** functioning.

---

#### 2. 🟠 Ministers of State (Middle Rank)
- Can be given **independent charge of departments**, OR
- Can be **attached to a Cabinet Minister** to assist him/her.
- They are **NOT members of the cabinet** and generally **do not attend cabinet meetings**.
- Exception: They may be invited to cabinet meetings when matters related to their departments are being discussed.

---

#### 3. 🔵 Deputy Ministers (Lowest Rank)
- Are **NOT given independent charge** of departments.
- Are **attached to Cabinet Ministers** and assist them in:
  - Administrative duties
  - Political duties
  - Parliamentary duties
- Are **NOT members of the cabinet** and **do not attend cabinet meetings**.

---

### The Chief Minister: Above All

- The **Chief Minister** stands at the **top of all these ministers** as the supreme governing authority in the state.

### Deputy Chief Minister
- At times, the Council of Ministers may also include a **Deputy Chief Minister**.
- Deputy CMs are appointed mostly for **local political reasons** (e.g., coalition governments, caste/regional balancing).

---

### ⚠️ Important Terminology Note:
> The term **'Ministry' or 'Ministries'** is used **only at the Centre** — not in the states.
>
> In other words, the state government is divided into **Departments** — not Ministries.
>
> So at the Centre, you have the "Ministry of Finance." At the state level, you have the "**Department** of Finance."

---

🌍 **Modern Context**
> `[Modern Example]` The 15% cap on council size (91st Amendment, 2003) was intended to prevent oversized, unwieldy cabinets formed for political patronage. However, larger states have larger assemblies and thus higher caps. For example, **Uttar Pradesh's Legislative Assembly has 403 seats**, allowing a maximum of roughly **60 ministers**. In contrast, smaller states can struggle with the *minimum of 12* requirement — a challenge highlighted in states like **Sikkim and Goa**, where getting 12 qualified legislators to serve as ministers can be politically complex.

## 🗂️ Section 7: The Cabinet

### What Is the Cabinet?

- The **Cabinet** is a **smaller body** that is the **nucleus (core) of the Council of Ministers**.
- It consists of **only the Cabinet Ministers** (not ministers of state or deputy ministers).
- It is the **real centre of authority** in the state government.

### 8 Key Functions of the Cabinet:

1. 🏆 It is the **highest decision-making authority** in the politico-administrative system of the state.
2. 📋 It is the **chief policy-formulating body** of the state government.
3. ⚙️ It is the **supreme executive authority** of the state government.
4. 🔗 It is the **chief coordinator** of the state administration.
5. 💬 It is an **advisory body to the Governor**.
6. 🚨 It is the **chief crisis manager** — dealing with all emergency situations.
7. 📜 It deals with all **major legislative and financial matters**.
8. 👔 It exercises **control over higher appointments** such as constitutional authorities and senior secretariat administrators.

---

## 🗃️ Section 8: Cabinet Committees

### What Are Cabinet Committees?

The Cabinet works through various **committees** called **Cabinet Committees**.

### Two Types:

| Type | Nature | Permanency |
|---|---|---|
| **Standing Committees** | Of a **permanent** nature | Continue across governments |
| **Ad Hoc Committees** | Of a **temporary** nature | Dissolved after their purpose is served |

### Key Features:

- They are **set up by the Chief Minister** based on the needs and requirements of the time.
- Their **number, nomenclature (names), and composition** vary from time to time and government to government.
- They **do two things:**
  1. **Sort out issues** and **formulate proposals** for the Cabinet to consider.
  2. **Take decisions themselves** on delegated matters.
- However, the **Cabinet can review and override** their decisions — the Cabinet remains supreme.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**
> `[Modern Example]` Cabinet Committees at the state level have gained importance in managing crisis situations. During the **COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021)**, virtually every state government formed **ad hoc Cabinet Committees** on health, economic relief, and migrant welfare. These committees coordinated responses to the pandemic at the state level, showing how the cabinet committee system is a living, adaptive tool of governance — not just a textbook concept.

## 🔬 Court Cases Summary — All Cases Explained in Detail

### Case 1: *Shamsher Singh vs. State of Punjab (1974)*

**📌 Citation:** Shamsher Singh & Anr. vs. State of Punjab — Supreme Court of India, 1974

**🧾 Background:**
The case arose when two District Judges (Shamsher Singh and Ishwar Chand Aggarwal) were confirmed/not confirmed in service. The question was whether the Governor of Punjab could make such decisions *personally* — independently of the Council of Ministers' advice — since service matters of High Court judges were, at the time, stated to be in the Governor's domain.

**⚖️ Core Legal Question:**
Does the phrase "the Governor shall act in his discretion" or "satisfaction of the Governor" in the Constitution mean the *Governor personally* decides, or does it mean the *Council of Ministers* decides?

**📜 Supreme Court Judgment (Constitution Bench):**
The Supreme Court held:

1. **The Governor is a constitutional head** — just like the President at the Union level. Both must act on the aid and advice of their respective Councils of Ministers in all matters *except* constitutionally specified discretionary matters.

2. **The Governor cannot act personally** (without ministerial advice) in matters of state governance — except in those specific areas where the Constitution explicitly grants discretion.

3. **"Satisfaction of the Governor"** = **Satisfaction of the Council of Ministers**. It is the cabinet that is satisfied; the Governor's personal opinion is irrelevant.

4. The Governor is **not an independent constitutional actor** in day-to-day governance — the elected government runs the state.

**🎯 Significance for UPSC:**
- Establishes the **subordinate role of the Governor** to the elected government.
- Defines the constitutional limits of gubernatorial discretion.
- Still cited in 2023–24 Supreme Court judgments on Governor-government conflicts.

---

### Case 2: *Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)*

*(Mentioned in Modern Context — explained here for completeness)*

**📌 Citation:** Subhash Desai vs. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra & Ors. — Supreme Court of India, May 2023

**🧾 Background:**
This arose from the **Maharashtra political crisis of June 2022**, when:
- A faction of Shiv Sena MLAs led by **Eknath Shinde** rebelled against CM **Uddhav Thackeray**.
- The Governor called upon Uddhav Thackeray to prove his majority.
- Uddhav Thackeray resigned before the floor test.
- Eknath Shinde was then sworn in as CM.
- The original Thackeray faction challenged the entire process.

**⚖️ Core Legal Questions:**
1. Was the Governor justified in calling for a floor test?
2. Was the Speaker's (later Deputy Speaker's) role in disqualification proceedings valid?
3. Who is the "real" Shiv Sena — the Thackeray group or Shinde group?

**📜 Supreme Court Judgment (Constitution Bench, May 2023):**
The 5-judge Constitution Bench held:

1. The **Governor did not have justifiable grounds** to call for a floor test — the Governor cannot act on the request of a *rebel faction* of the ruling party as evidence that the government has lost majority. Doing so was **constitutionally improper**.

2. However, since Uddhav Thackeray **resigned before the floor test**, the Court could not restore him to power.

3. The **disqualification petitions** against rebel MLAs were referred back to the **Speaker** for fresh decision.

4. The Election Commission's recognition of the Shinde faction as the "real Shiv Sena" was **upheld**.

**🎯 Significance for UPSC:**
- Reaffirms the *Shamsher Singh* principle that Governors must not act against the spirit of collective responsibility.
- Sets limits on when a Governor can call for a floor test.
- Crucial for understanding the **10th Schedule (anti-defection), collective responsibility, and gubernatorial discretion** together.

---

### Case 3: *State of Punjab vs. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab (2023)*

*(Mentioned in Modern Context — explained here for completeness)*

**📌 Citation:** State of Punjab vs. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab — Supreme Court of India, 2023

**🧾 Background:**
The Punjab government (led by CM Bhagwant Mann, AAP) passed several **Bills in the state assembly**. The Governor, **Banwarilal Purohit**, refused to give assent to these bills for a prolonged period — effectively keeping them in limbo. The Punjab government moved the Supreme Court.

**⚖️ Core Legal Question:**
Can a Governor withhold assent to bills passed by the state legislature *indefinitely*? What are the Governor's constitutional options when a bill is presented to him/her?

**📜 Supreme Court Judgment:**
The Supreme Court held:

1. The **Governor cannot indefinitely withhold assent** to bills passed by the state legislature. Such behaviour was termed **constitutionally impermissible**.

2. The Governor's options under **Article 200** are clearly defined: grant assent, withhold assent (send back for reconsideration), or reserve for Presidential consideration. "Pocket veto" (doing nothing) is not an option.

3. The Court **directed** the Governor to act on the pending bills expeditiously.

**🎯 Significance for UPSC:**
- Directly relevant to **Article 200** (Governor's assent to bills).
- Reaffirms that Governors must act within constitutional bounds and cannot obstruct the legislative will of elected governments.
- Part of a broader **2022–2024 trend of Supreme Court asserting limits on gubernatorial powers**.
