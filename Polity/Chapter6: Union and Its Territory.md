# 📘 Chapter 6: Union and Its Territory
### (Articles 1–4 | Part I of the Constitution)

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **India is a "Union of States"**, NOT a "Federation of States" — and this distinction is deeply intentional.
> 2. **Parliament has enormous power** to redraw India's map — create, merge, rename, or split states — without state consent.
> 3. **"Territory of India" ≠ "Union of India"** — the former is a broader term.
> 4. India's political map has evolved dramatically — from **29 units in 1950** to **28 States + 8 UTs today**.
> 5. India is rightly called **"an indestructible union of destructible states"** — states can be changed; the Union cannot be broken.

---

## 🗺️ Section 1: What Does Article 1 Say?

### The Name of the Country

**Article 1** declares India, that is, **Bharat** as a **'Union of States'**.

This provision covers two things:
1. The **name** of the country
2. The **type of polity** (how it is politically organized)

There was no agreement in the **Constituent Assembly** on what to call the country. Some members preferred the traditional name **Bharat**, while others advocated the modern name **India**. The Assembly resolved this by adopting **both names** — *"India, that is, Bharat."*

---

### Why "Union" and Not "Federation"?

The Constitution calls India a **'Union'** even though its structure is **federal** (power is shared between the Centre and states). Why?

**Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** gave two clear reasons:

- **Reason 1:** The Indian Union was **not created by an agreement among states**, unlike the American Federation where states voluntarily came together.
- **Reason 2:** Indian states have **no right to secede** (break away) from the Union.

> 💡 The Union is **indestructible**. India is an integral whole, divided into states only for **administrative convenience**.

---

### Three Categories of Indian Territory (Under Article 1)

| Category | Description |
|---|---|
| **1. Territories of the States** | The 28 states listed in the First Schedule |
| **2. Union Territories** | The 8 UTs directly administered by the Centre |
| **3. Acquired Territories** | Any territory the Government of India may acquire in the future |

> 📌 **Key Distinction:**
> - **"Territory of India"** = States + UTs + Acquired Territories *(wider term)*
> - **"Union of India"** = Only States *(narrower term)*
>
> States are **members of the federal system** and share power with the Centre.
> UTs and acquired territories are **directly run by the Central Government**.

---

### How Can India Acquire Foreign Territory?

As a **sovereign state** (a fully independent, self-governing nation), India can acquire foreign territories through modes recognized by **international law**:

- **Cession** — through treaty, purchase, gift, lease, or plebiscite (a public vote)
- **Occupation** — of territory not previously governed by any recognized ruler
- **Conquest or Subjugation** — through military means

> **Examples:** India acquired **Dadra and Nagar Haveli; Goa, Daman and Diu; Puducherry; and Sikkim** after the Constitution came into force.

---

### Special Provisions for Certain States

- The **Fifth and Sixth Schedules** contain separate rules for **scheduled areas and tribal areas** within states.
- **Part XXI** of the Constitution has **special provisions** for Maharashtra, Gujarat, Nagaland, Assam, Manipur, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Sikkim, Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, and Karnataka — these override the general rules applicable to all states.

---

## ⚖️ Section 2: Articles 2 & 3 — Parliament's Power Over States

### Article 2: Admitting or Establishing New States

**Article 2** gives Parliament **two powers**:

| Power | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Admit** new states into the Union | For states that **already exist** but are outside India |
| **Establish** new states | For states that **did not exist before** |

> 📌 Article 2 deals with states that are **NOT yet part of India**.
> Article 3 deals with states that are **already within India**.

---

### Article 3: Reorganizing Existing States

**Article 3** authorizes Parliament to:

1. **Form a new state** — by separating territory from a state, or uniting two or more states/parts of states
2. **Increase the area** of any state
3. **Diminish (reduce) the area** of any state
4. **Alter the boundaries** of any state
5. **Alter the name** of any state

> ✅ Parliament's power to form new states also includes the power to unite any part of a state or UT with another state or UT *(added by the 18th Constitutional Amendment Act of 1966)*.

---

### 🔒 Two Important Conditions Under Article 3

Before Parliament can act on the above:

1. **Condition 1:** The Bill can only be introduced in Parliament **with the prior recommendation of the President**.
2. **Condition 2:** Before recommending the Bill, the President must **refer it to the concerned state legislature** to give its views within a specified time.

> ⚠️ **But here's the catch:**
> - The President (or Parliament) is **NOT bound** by the state legislature's views. They can accept or reject them.
> - There is **no need** to refer the Bill to the state legislature again if an amendment to the Bill is later made in Parliament. *(Babulal vs. State of Bombay, 1959)*
> - For a **Union Territory**, no reference to any legislature is needed at all. Parliament can act on its own.

---

### India = "An Indestructible Union of Destructible States"

| Feature | India 🇮🇳 | USA 🇺🇸 |
|---|---|---|
| Can Parliament alter a state's territory without its consent? | **YES** | **NO** |
| Is the territorial integrity of a state guaranteed? | **NO** | **YES** |
| Description | *"Indestructible Union of Destructible States"* | *"Indestructible Union of Indestructible States"* |

> The Union Government can destroy or redraw states. But state governments cannot break the Union.

---

### Article 4: No Constitutional Amendment Needed!

**Article 4** declares that laws made under **Articles 2 and 3** are **NOT considered amendments to the Constitution** under Article 368.

This means:
- These laws can be passed by a **simple majority** in Parliament.
- They follow the **ordinary legislative process** — no special majority needed.

---

## ⚖️ Section 3: Can Parliament Cede (Give Away) Indian Territory to a Foreign Country?

### The Berubari Case (1960)

This was a landmark legal question. When India decided to transfer the **Berubari Union** (part of West Bengal) to Pakistan, it sparked political controversy and a Presidential Reference to the **Supreme Court**.

**The Supreme Court held:**
> The power of Parliament under **Article 3** to *"diminish the area of a state"* does **NOT** cover ceding (transferring) Indian territory to a foreign country.

**Consequence:** To transfer Berubari, a full **Constitutional Amendment** under **Article 368** was required. Hence, the **9th Constitutional Amendment Act (1960)** was enacted.

---

### Settlement of Border Disputes (1969 SC Ruling)

In a separate ruling in **1969**, the Supreme Court clarified:
> **Settling a boundary dispute** with another country does **NOT** require a Constitutional Amendment. It can be done by **executive action** because it does not involve ceding Indian territory.

---

## 🤝 Section 4: Exchange of Territories with Bangladesh

### The 100th Constitutional Amendment Act (2015)

This amendment gave effect to a land boundary agreement between India and Bangladesh.

**The Deal:**
- India transferred **111 enclaves** (small pockets of land) to Bangladesh.
- Bangladesh transferred **51 enclaves** to India.
- The deal also involved transfer of **adverse possessions** and demarcation of a **6.1 km undemarcated border stretch**.
- The amendment modified the **First Schedule** of the Constitution for **4 states: Assam, West Bengal, Meghalaya, and Tripura**.

---

### Background of This Amendment

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1947** | India-East Pakistan boundary determined by the **Radcliffe Award** |
| **1950** | Disputes addressed through the **Bagge Award** |
| **1958** | Further attempts through the **Nehru-Noon Agreement** |
| **1960** | **9th Constitutional Amendment Act** passed (re: Berubari) |
| **16 May 1974** | **India-Bangladesh Land Boundary Agreement** signed |
| **6 September 2011** | A **Protocol** signed to resolve remaining issues — enclaves, adverse possessions, undemarcated borders. State governments of Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and West Bengal gave their support. |
| **2015** | **100th Constitutional Amendment Act** enacted |

> The 1974 Agreement could not be ratified earlier because it involved territory transfer, which required a Constitutional Amendment, and the precise ground areas needed to be identified first.

---

## 🏛️ Section 5: Evolution of India's States and Union Territories

### Step 1: Integration of Princely States (1947)

At independence, India had two types of political units:
- **British Provinces** — directly under the British Government
- **Princely States** — under native princes, subject to British paramountcy (overall British authority)

The **Indian Independence Act (1947)** gave princely states **three options**:
1. Join India
2. Join Pakistan
3. Remain Independent

**Result:**
- Of **552 princely states** within India's geographical boundaries → **549 joined India**
- **3 refused:** Hyderabad, Junagarh, and Kashmir

**How were the 3 integrated?**

| State | Method of Integration |
|---|---|
| **Hyderabad** | Police action |
| **Junagarh** | Referendum (public vote) |
| **Kashmir** | Instrument of Accession |

---

### Step 2: The 1950 Constitution's Four-Fold Classification

When the Constitution came into force in 1950, India had **29 units** divided into 4 categories:

| Category | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Part A States (9)** | Former British Governor's Provinces | Assam, Bihar, Bombay, Madhya Pradesh, Madras, Orissa, Punjab, United Provinces, West Bengal |
| **Part B States (9)** | Former Princely States with legislatures | Hyderabad, J&K, Madhya Bharat, Mysore, Patiala & East Punjab (PEPSU), Rajasthan, Saurashtra, Travancore-Cochin, Vindhya Pradesh |
| **Part C States (10)** | Former Chief Commissioners' Provinces + some princely states; centrally administered | Ajmer, Bhopal, Bilaspur, Cooch-Behar, Coorg, Delhi, Himachal Pradesh, Kutch, Manipur, Tripura |
| **Part D Territories (1)** | Special territories | Andaman and Nicobar Islands |

---

### Step 3: Demand for Linguistic States — Dhar Commission & JVP Committee

The integration of princely states was an **ad hoc** (temporary, without long-term planning) arrangement. There were strong demands, especially from South India, to reorganize states on **linguistic lines** (based on language).

**Dhar Commission (June 1948):**
- Appointed by Government of India under **S.K. Dhar** to examine linguistic reorganization.
- Report submitted: **December 1948**
- **Recommendation:** Reorganize on the basis of **administrative convenience**, NOT language. *(This caused huge resentment.)*

**JVP Committee (December 1948):**
- Congress appointed a committee to re-examine the issue.
- Members: **Jawaharlal Nehru, Vallabhbhai Patel, Pattabhi Sitaramayya** (no chairman/convenor).
- Report submitted: **April 1949**
- **Conclusion:** Formally **rejected language** as the basis for reorganization.

---

### The First Linguistic State: Andhra (1953)

Despite the JVP rejection, the Government was **forced** to create the first linguistic state in **October 1953**.

- **Andhra State** was carved out of **Madras State** for Telugu-speaking people.
- This followed a long popular agitation and the death of **Potti Sriramulu** — a prominent Congress figure — after a **56-day hunger strike** for the cause.

---

### Step 4: Fazl Ali Commission (States Reorganisation Commission)

The creation of Andhra State intensified demands from all regions. The Government appointed a **three-member States Reorganisation Commission** in **December 1953**:

- **Chairman:** Fazl Ali
- **Members:** K.M. Panikkar and H.N. Kunzru

**Report submitted:** September 1955

**Key Findings:**
- **Broadly accepted language** as a basis for reorganization.
- **But rejected** the theory of **"one language – one state"**.
- Said **unity of India** must be the **primary consideration**.

**Four factors identified for reorganization:**

1. Preservation and strengthening of the **unity and security** of the country
2. **Linguistic and cultural homogeneity** (similarity)
3. **Financial, economic, and administrative** considerations
4. **Planning and promotion of welfare** of people in each state and the nation

**Recommendation:** Abolish the four-fold classification; create **16 states** and **3 centrally administered territories**.

---

### Step 5: The Great Reorganization of 1956

The Government accepted the Fazl Ali Commission's recommendations with minor modifications.

**Two key laws enacted:**
- **States Reorganisation Act (1956)**
- **7th Constitutional Amendment Act (1956)**

**Result:** The Part A/B/C/D distinction was abolished. **14 States and 6 Union Territories** were created on **November 1, 1956**.

**Key mergers and formations:**

| New Entity | How it was formed |
|---|---|
| **Kerala** | Travancore-Cochin + Malabar District (Madras) + Kasargode of South Canara |
| **Andhra Pradesh** | Telugu-speaking areas of Hyderabad + Andhra State |
| **Madhya Pradesh** | Madhya Bharat + Vindhya Pradesh + Bhopal merged in |
| **Bombay State** | Saurashtra + Kutch merged in |
| **Mysore** | Coorg merged in |
| **Punjab** | PEPSU (Patiala & East Punjab States Union) merged in |
| **Rajasthan** | Ajmer merged in |
| **Lakshadweep (UT)** | New UT of Laccadive, Minicoy & Amindivi Islands from Madras territory |

---

## 🗓️ Section 6: New States and UTs Created After 1956

### Maharashtra and Gujarat (1960)

- The **bilingual Bombay State** was divided into **two states** by the **Bombay Reorganisation Act, 1960**.
- **Maharashtra** → for Marathi-speaking people
- **Gujarat** → for Gujarati-speaking people (the **15th state**)

---

### Dadra and Nagar Haveli

- Ruled by the Portuguese until **liberation in 1954**.
- Administered by a **people-chosen administrator** until 1961.
- Converted to a **Union Territory** by the **10th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1961**.
- **In 2020:** Merged with Daman and Diu to form the **new UT of Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu** — via the **Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu (Merger of Union Territories) Act, 2019**.

---

### Goa, Daman and Diu

- Acquired from the Portuguese by **police action in 1961**.
- Made a **Union Territory** by the **12th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1962**.
- **In 1987:** Goa was granted **statehood** (25th state) by the **Goa, Daman and Diu Reorganisation Act, 1987**. Daman and Diu became a separate UT.
- **In 2020:** Daman and Diu was merged with Dadra and Nagar Haveli *(see above)*.

---

### Puducherry

- Comprises former French territories: **Puducherry, Karaikal, Mahe, and Yanam**.
- French handed over to India in **1954**.
- Administered as an **'acquired territory'** until 1962.
- Made a **Union Territory** by the **14th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1962**.

---

### Nagaland (1963)

- The **State of Nagaland Act, 1962** created the **State of Nagaland** (16th state), effective **December 1, 1963**.
- Formed by separating the **Naga Hills and Tuensang Area** from Assam.
- Created to address the demands of the hostile Naga movement.
- Before statehood, in **1961**, it was placed under the control of the **Governor of Assam**.

---

### Haryana, Chandigarh and Himachal Pradesh (1966 & 1971)

- The **Punjab Reorganisation Act, 1966** bifurcated Punjab:
  - **Haryana** (17th state) — Hindi-speaking areas
  - **Union Territory of Chandigarh** — made a common capital for both Punjab and Haryana
  - Hill areas merged with the UT of **Himachal Pradesh**

- This followed the demand for a **"Punjabi Subha" (Sikh Homeland)** by **Akali Dal** under **Master Tara Singh**.
- The **Shah Commission (1966)** made the recommendation for this division.
- **In 1971:** The **State of Himachal Pradesh Act, 1970** elevated Himachal Pradesh to full statehood (**18th state**, effective January 25, 1971).

---

### Manipur, Tripura and Meghalaya (1972)

A major reorganization of **Northeast India** occurred through the **North-Eastern Areas (Reorganisation) Act, 1971**, effective **January 21, 1972**:

| State | Status Change | State Number |
|---|---|---|
| **Manipur** | UT → Full State | 19th |
| **Tripura** | UT → Full State | 20th |
| **Meghalaya** | Sub-state → Full State | 21st |
| **Mizoram** | Carved from Assam → New UT | — |
| **Arunachal Pradesh** (formerly NEFA) | Carved from Assam → New UT | — |

> 📌 **Meghalaya's unique journey:** The **22nd Constitutional Amendment Act (1969)** had first created Meghalaya as an **'autonomous state' or 'sub-state'** within Assam, with its own legislature and council of ministers. This did not satisfy the people, and it was given full statehood in 1972.

---

### Sikkim's Unique Journey (1975)

- Till 1947: Sikkim was a **princely state** ruled by **Chogyal**.
- After 1947: Became a **'protectorate' of India** — India handled its defence, external affairs, and communications.
- **1974:** Sikkim sought greater association with India.
  - The **35th Constitutional Amendment Act (1974)** gave Sikkim the status of an **'associate state'** — a brand new class of statehood.
  - A new **Article 2-A** and a new **10th Schedule** were inserted into the Constitution.
- This still didn't fully satisfy the people.
- **1975 Referendum:** People voted to abolish the **institution of Chogyal** and merge with India fully.
- The **36th Constitutional Amendment Act (1975)** made Sikkim the **22nd full state** of India.
  - Added **Article 371-F** (special provisions for Sikkim's administration).
  - Repealed Article 2-A and the 10th Schedule added by the 35th Amendment.

---

### Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh and Goa (1987)

| State | Key Event | State Number |
|---|---|---|
| **Mizoram** (23rd) | Given statehood after **Mizoram Peace Accord (1986)** between Centre and **Mizo National Front**, ending a two-decade insurgency | 23rd |
| **Arunachal Pradesh** | Had been a UT from 1972; elevated to full statehood | 24th |
| **Goa** | Separated from the UT of Goa, Daman and Diu | 25th |

---

### Chhattisgarh, Uttarakhand, and Jharkhand (2000)

In **2000**, three new states were carved out:

| New State | Parent State | State Number |
|---|---|---|
| **Chhattisgarh** | Madhya Pradesh | 26th |
| **Uttarakhand** (originally Uttaranchal) | Uttar Pradesh | 27th |
| **Jharkhand** | Bihar | 28th |

---

### Telangana (2014)

- **Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act (2014)** bifurcated Andhra Pradesh into:
  - **Telangana** (29th state) — with Hyderabad as its capital
  - **Andhra Pradesh (residuary)**

**The journey of Andhra/Telangana:**
1. **Andhra State Act (1953)** → First linguistic state (Andhra) carved from Madras. Capital: Kurnool; High Court: Guntur.
2. **States Reorganisation Act (1956)** → Telugu-speaking areas of Hyderabad merged with Andhra to create enlarged **Andhra Pradesh**. Capital shifted to Hyderabad.
3. **Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act (2014)** → Split into Telangana and Andhra Pradesh.

---

### Jammu & Kashmir and Ladakh (2019)

**Before 2019:**
- J&K had its **own Constitution** and enjoyed a **special status** under **Article 370**.

**In 2019:**
- A presidential order — **"The Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order, 2019"** — superseded the earlier 1954 order.
- This extended **all provisions of the Indian Constitution** to J&K.
- Article 370's special status was effectively **abolished**.

**The Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019** then bifurcated the erstwhile state into:

| New Entity | Composition |
|---|---|
| **UT of Jammu and Kashmir** | All districts of erstwhile J&K *except* Kargil and Leh |
| **UT of Ladakh** | Kargil and Leh districts |

> 📊 **Net Result:** India's political map now has **28 States and 8 Union Territories**.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` *This section connects chapter concepts to recent events.*

### 🔹 J&K Reorganisation and Article 370 Abrogation (2019)
The most significant recent change to India's map: The **abrogation of Article 370** via the **Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order, 2019** and the **Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019** converted a state with special status into two Union Territories. The **Supreme Court upheld** this decision in **S.G. Vombatkere vs. Union of India** and the five-judge bench ruling in **December 2023**, which validated the presidential orders and the reorganisation, while also directing that statehood be restored to J&K at the earliest.

### 🔹 Merger of Dadra & NH with Daman & Diu (2020)
`[Modern Example]` The **Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu (Merger of Union Territories) Act, 2019** (effective **January 26, 2020**) reduced India's number of UTs from 9 to 8. This was done to improve administrative efficiency and reduce the financial burden of maintaining two small, separate UT administrations.

### 🔹 100th Constitutional Amendment and Land Boundary Agreement (2015)
`[Modern Example]` The exchange of enclaves with Bangladesh under the **100th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2015** was a landmark diplomatic achievement. India transferred **111 enclaves** and received **51 enclaves**. Over **50,000 residents** in the transferred enclaves were given a choice of citizenship — the vast majority chose Indian citizenship. This resolved a 68-year-old post-Partition anomaly.

### 🔹 Demand for New States — An Ongoing Debate
`[Modern Example]` Demands for new states continue to surface:
- **Greater Tipraland** demand in Tripura (for tribal areas)
- **Harit Pradesh / Purvanchal** demands in Uttar Pradesh
- Periodic demands for a **separate Vidarbha** state from Maharashtra
These debates keep Article 3's relevance very much alive in contemporary politics.

### 🔹 Statehood for Delhi?
`[Modern Example]` The **Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi (Amendment) Act, 2021** and the **Supreme Court's Constitution Bench ruling (May 2023)** reignited the debate over Delhi's unique status — neither a full state nor an ordinary UT. The court held that the **elected government of Delhi** has control over services (except land, police, and public order), though this was subsequently modified by the **Government of NCT of Delhi (Amendment) Ordinance, 2023**. This is a direct example of Article 239AA in action — the constitutional provision for Delhi's unique governance structure.
