### *Indian Polity by M. Laxmikanth — Simplified & Enhanced Study Notes*

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Top 5 Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. The Indian Constitution is the **longest written Constitution** in the world — currently ~470 Articles, 25 Parts, 12 Schedules.
> 2. It is a **unique blend** — borrowed from multiple countries but adapted to Indian conditions.
> 3. India follows a **Federal System with a Unitary Bias** — federal in form, unitary in spirit.
> 4. The Constitution has **17 Salient Features** including Fundamental Rights, DPSP, Secularism, and Emergency Provisions.
> 5. Despite criticisms (borrowed, bulky, legalistic), **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** defended it as a thoughtfully adapted document.

---

## ⚠️ Important Note Before We Begin

The Constitution **as originally adopted in 1949** has changed significantly due to amendments — especially the **7th, 42nd, 44th, 73rd, 74th, 97th, and 101st Amendments**.

- The **42nd Amendment Act (1976)** is called the **'Mini-Constitution'** because it made the largest number of changes across various parts of the Constitution.
- In the landmark **Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)**, the Supreme Court ruled that Parliament **cannot alter the 'basic structure'** of the Constitution under **Article 368**, even with its constituent (amendment-making) power.

## 🏛️ THE 17 SALIENT FEATURES — Explained Simply

---

### Feature 1: 📜 Lengthiest Written Constitution

Constitutions of the world are of two types:
- **Written** (e.g., American Constitution)
- **Unwritten** (e.g., British Constitution — based on conventions and traditions)

The Indian Constitution is the **longest of all written Constitutions** in the world. It is comprehensive, elaborate, and highly detailed.

**Then vs. Now:**

| Parameter | Originally (1949) | Currently |
|---|---|---|
| Preamble | 1 | 1 |
| Articles | 395 | ~470 |
| Parts | 22 | 25 |
| Schedules | 8 | 12 |

Since 1951, amendments have:
- **Deleted** ~20 Articles and 1 Part (Part VII)
- **Added** ~95 Articles, 4 new Parts (IVA, IXA, IXB, XIVA), and 4 new Schedules (9, 10, 11, 12)

> 📌 *For comparison: The US Constitution originally had only 7 Articles, Australia has 128, China 138, and Canada 147.*

**Why is it so long? 4 Key Reasons:**

- 🌍 **Geographical factors** — India's vast size and enormous diversity needed detailed provisions.
- 📜 **Historical factors** — Heavy influence of the lengthy Government of India Act of 1935.
- 🏛️ **Single Constitution for Centre and States** — Unlike the USA, India doesn't have separate state constitutions.
- ⚖️ **Legal experts dominated the Constituent Assembly** — They preferred detailed, precise legal language.

The Constitution also covers detailed administrative provisions and matters that other democracies leave to ordinary legislation or political conventions.

---

#### 🔖 Special Note: Jammu & Kashmir (Article 370)

Till 2019, J&K had its **own separate Constitution** and enjoyed special status under **Article 370**.

- In **2019**, a presidential order — **"The Constitution (Application to Jammu & Kashmir) Order, 2019"** — superseded the 1954 order.
- This extended **all provisions of the Indian Constitution** to J&K.
- The **Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019** then **bifurcated J&K into two Union Territories**:
  1. **Union Territory of Jammu & Kashmir** (with a legislature)
  2. **Union Territory of Ladakh** (without a legislature)

### Feature 2: 🌐 Drawn from Various Sources

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar proudly said the Constitution was framed after **"ransacking all the known Constitutions of the World."**

The Constitution draws from three broad areas:

- **Structural Part** → mostly from the **Government of India Act, 1935**
- **Philosophical Part** (Fundamental Rights & DPSP) → **American & Irish Constitutions**
- **Political Part** (Cabinet Government, Executive-Legislature relations) → **British Constitution**

> 📌 *The Government of India Act, 1935, is the MOST influential single source. Over 250 provisions were drawn from it. More than half the Constitution resembles this Act.*

**Quick Source Table (Table 4.4 — Simplified):**

| Source | What was Borrowed |
|---|---|
| **Govt. of India Act, 1935** | Federal Scheme, Judiciary, Governor's Office, Emergency Powers, Public Service Commissions, Advisory Jurisdiction of SC, administrative details |
| **British Constitution** | Parliamentary Government, Rule of Law, legislative procedure, single citizenship, cabinet system, prerogative writs, parliamentary privileges, bicameralism |
| **US Constitution** | Fundamental Rights, independence of judiciary, judicial review, impeachment of the President, removal of SC & HC judges, post of Vice-President |
| **Irish Constitution** | Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP), nomination of members to Rajya Sabha, method of election of President |
| **Canadian Constitution** | Federation with a strong Centre, residuary powers with Centre, appointment of State Governors by Centre |
| **Australian Constitution** | Concurrent List, freedom of trade/commerce/intercourse, joint sitting of both Houses of Parliament |
| **Weimar Constitution (Germany)** | Emergency provisions relating to suspension of Fundamental Rights |
| **Soviet Constitution (USSR/Russia)** | Fundamental Duties, ideal of social, economic and political justice in the Preamble |
| **French Constitution** | Republic; ideals of Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity in the Preamble |
| **South African Constitution** | Procedure for amendment of the Constitution, election of Rajya Sabha members |
| **Japanese Constitution** | Procedure established by Law |

### Feature 3: ⚖️ Blend of Rigidity and Flexibility

- A **Rigid Constitution** (like the USA) requires a *special procedure* to amend.
- A **Flexible Constitution** (like the UK) can be amended *just like ordinary laws*.

India's Constitution is **neither purely rigid nor purely flexible — it is a synthesis of both.**

**Article 368** provides for **two types of amendments:**

1. **Special Majority of Parliament alone** — Two-thirds majority of members present and voting + majority of total membership of each House.
2. **Special Majority + Ratification by half the states** — For provisions affecting federal features.

Additionally, **some provisions can be amended by a simple majority** of Parliament (like ordinary laws). Importantly, these do **not** come under **Article 368**.

---

### Feature 4: 🏛️ Federal System with Unitary Bias

India has a **federal system** with all the classic features:
- Two governments (Centre + States)
- Division of powers
- Written Constitution
- Supremacy of Constitution
- Rigidity of Constitution
- Independent Judiciary
- Bicameralism (two Houses of Parliament)

**BUT** it also has strong **unitary/non-federal features:**
- A strong Centre
- Single Constitution
- Single citizenship
- Flexible Constitution
- Integrated judiciary
- Centre appoints State Governors
- All-India Services
- Emergency provisions

> 📌 The word **"Federation"** is **never used** in the Constitution. **Article 1** calls India a **"Union of States"** — meaning:
> 1. The Indian federation is **not the result of an agreement** between states.
> 2. **No state has the right to secede** from the federation.

**How scholars describe India's federal system:**

| Scholar | Description |
|---|---|
| **K.C. Wheare** | Quasi-federal |
| **Morris Jones** | Bargaining federalism |
| **Granville Austin** | Co-operative federalism |
| **Ivor Jennings** | Federation with a centralising tendency |
| General description | "Federal in form but unitary in spirit" |

### Feature 5: 🗳️ Parliamentary Form of Government

India chose the **British Parliamentary System** over the **American Presidential System**.

| Basis | Parliamentary System | Presidential System |
|---|---|---|
| Principle | Cooperation between legislature & executive | Separation of powers |
| Example | India, UK | USA |

The Parliamentary system is also called:
- **Westminster Model** *(Westminster = place in London where the British Parliament is located)*
- **Responsible Government**
- **Cabinet Government**

India has this system at **both the Centre and in every State.**

**6 Key Features of Parliamentary Government in India:**
1. Presence of **nominal** (President/Governor) and **real** (PM/CM) executives
2. **Majority party rule**
3. **Collective responsibility** of the executive to the legislature
4. Ministers must be **members of the legislature**
5. **Leadership of the Prime Minister or Chief Minister**
6. **Dissolution of the lower House** (Lok Sabha or State Assembly)

**India vs. UK — Key Differences:**
- The Indian Parliament is **NOT sovereign** (unlike British Parliament) — it is subject to judicial review.
- India has an **elected head** (Republic) while the UK has a **hereditary head** (Monarchy).

> 📌 Political scientists call it a **'Prime Ministerial Government'** given the dominant role of the PM.

---

### Feature 6: ⚖️ Synthesis of Parliamentary Sovereignty and Judicial Supremacy

- **Parliamentary Sovereignty** → associated with the **British Parliament** (Parliament is supreme).
- **Judicial Supremacy** → associated with the **American Supreme Court** (Courts can strike down any law).

India has a **balanced synthesis of both.**

**Why is India's judicial review narrower than the USA?**
- The American Constitution has **"due process of law"** — courts can question both the *procedure and the substance* of a law.
- India's **Article 21** uses **"procedure established by law"** — originally, courts only checked if the *correct procedure* was followed.

**The balance works like this:**
- The **Supreme Court** can declare Parliamentary laws **unconstitutional** through **judicial review**.
- The **Parliament** can amend most parts of the Constitution through its **constituent power** — *but cannot touch the basic structure* (Kesavananda Bharati, 1973).

---

### Feature 7: ⚖️ Integrated and Independent Judiciary

India has a **single, unified judicial hierarchy:**

```
Supreme Court (Top)
        ↓
High Courts (State Level)
        ↓
District Courts & Subordinate Courts
```

This single system **enforces both Central and State laws** — unlike the USA, which has separate federal and state court systems.

**Role of the Supreme Court:**
- **Federal Court** — resolves Centre-State disputes
- **Highest Court of Appeal**
- **Guardian of Fundamental Rights**
- **Guardian of the Constitution**

**How is judicial independence ensured?**
- Security of tenure for judges
- Fixed service conditions
- Expenses charged on the **Consolidated Fund of India** (not subject to Parliamentary vote)
- Prohibition on discussing judges' conduct in legislatures
- Ban on practice after retirement
- Power to punish for **contempt of court**
- **Separation of judiciary from the executive**

### Feature 8: 🛡️ Fundamental Rights

**Part III** of the Constitution (Articles 12–35) guarantees **six Fundamental Rights** to all citizens:

| # | Right | Articles |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Right to Equality** | 14–18 |
| 2 | **Right to Freedom** | 19–22 |
| 3 | **Right against Exploitation** | 23–24 |
| 4 | **Right to Freedom of Religion** | 25–28 |
| 5 | **Cultural and Educational Rights** | 29–30 |
| 6 | **Right to Constitutional Remedies** | 32 |

> 📌 Originally, there were **7 Fundamental Rights**. The **Right to Property (Article 31)** was removed from the Fundamental Rights list by the **44th Amendment Act of 1978**. It now exists as a **legal right** under **Article 300-A** in Part XII.

**Key points about Fundamental Rights:**
- They promote **political democracy**.
- They act as **limitations on the executive** and the **legislature**.
- They are **justiciable** *(can be enforced in courts)* — unlike DPSP.
- If violated, the aggrieved person can go **directly to the Supreme Court** under Article 32.
- The SC can issue five types of **writs:** *habeas corpus* (produce the person before court), *mandamus* (command to perform duty), *prohibition* (stop lower court), *certiorari* (quash lower court order), *quo warranto* (challenge a person's authority).
- They are **NOT absolute** — subject to reasonable restrictions.
- They can be **curtailed or repealed** by Parliament through a Constitutional Amendment.
- They can be **suspended during a National Emergency** — **except** rights under **Articles 20 and 21**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The **Right to Life (Article 21)** has been expansively interpreted by the Supreme Court to include the right to health, clean environment, livelihood, and education.
> - In **2021**, the SC in *Gaurav Kumar Bansal vs. Union of India* and related cases addressed **COVID-19 oxygen shortage** as a violation of Article 21.
> - The **Right to Privacy** was declared a Fundamental Right under Article 21 by a 9-judge bench in **Justice K.S. Puttaswamy vs. Union of India (2017)** — a landmark ruling affecting everything from Aadhaar to surveillance.
> - The **Personal Data Protection Bill (now Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023)** was passed to operationalize the right to privacy.

---

### Feature 9: 📋 Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP)

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar called DPSP a **"novel feature"** of the Indian Constitution. They are in **Part IV** (Articles 36–51).

**Three Categories of DPSPs:**
1. **Socialistic** — Related to welfare, equal pay, adequate livelihood, etc.
2. **Gandhian** — Related to village panchayats, cottage industries, prohibition, etc.
3. **Liberal-Intellectual** — Related to uniform civil code, international peace, etc.

**DPSPs vs. Fundamental Rights:**

| Feature | Fundamental Rights | DPSP |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Political democracy | Social & economic democracy |
| Nature | **Justiciable** (enforceable in court) | **Non-justiciable** (not enforceable) |
| Force behind them | Legal | Political (public opinion) |
| Aim | Protect individual liberty | Build a welfare state |

> 📌 Even though DPSPs are non-justiciable, the Constitution declares they are **"fundamental in the governance of the country"** and it is the **duty of the state to apply them** in making laws.

> 📌 In the **Minerva Mills case (1980)**, the Supreme Court held that the **"Indian Constitution is founded on the bedrock of the balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles."**

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - **DPSP-inspired schemes:** The National Food Security Act (2013), MGNREGA (2005), Right to Education Act (2009), and PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (extended through 2028) are all direct implementations of DPSPs.
> - The **"welfare state"** vision of DPSP is visible in schemes like **PM-KISAN** (income support to farmers), **Ayushman Bharat** (health coverage), and **PM Awas Yojana** (housing for all).

---

### Feature 10: 📜 Fundamental Duties

The original Constitution had **no Fundamental Duties**. They were added in two stages:

| Amendment | Year | What it did |
|---|---|---|
| **42nd Amendment Act** | 1976 | Added 10 Fundamental Duties on recommendation of the **Swaran Singh Committee** (during the Emergency of 1975–77) |
| **86th Amendment Act** | 2002 | Added one more duty (relating to education for children) |

**Total: 11 Fundamental Duties** — listed in **Part IV-A, Article 51-A.**

Some key duties include:
- Respect the Constitution, national flag, and national anthem
- Protect sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India
- Promote the spirit of common brotherhood
- Preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture
- And more...

> 📌 Like DPSPs, Fundamental Duties are also **non-justiciable** — they cannot be enforced by a court. They are moral reminders that rights come with responsibilities.

### Feature 11: 🕌 A Secular State

India is a **secular state** — it does **not have an official religion.**

> 📌 The word **'secular'** was added to the **Preamble** by the **42nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1976.**

**Constitutional Provisions that Establish Secularism:**

1. Preamble guarantees **liberty of belief, faith and worship** to all citizens.
2. **Article 14** — Equality before law for all persons.
3. **Article 15** — No discrimination by the State on grounds of **religion**.
4. **Article 16** — Equal opportunity in public employment.
5. **Article 25** — Freedom to **profess, practice, and propagate** any religion.
6. **Article 26** — Religious denominations can **manage their own affairs**.
7. **Article 27** — No one can be **compelled to pay taxes** for promotion of a specific religion.
8. **Article 28** — No religious instruction in **state-maintained educational institutions**.
9. **Article 29** — Right to **conserve distinct language, script or culture**.
10. **Article 30** — Minorities have the right to **establish and administer educational institutions**.
11. **Article 44** — State shall endeavour to secure a **Uniform Civil Code (UCC)** for all citizens.

**Indian Secularism vs. Western Secularism:**

| Aspect | Western Secularism | Indian Secularism |
|---|---|---|
| Concept | *Negative* — Complete separation of Church and State | *Positive* — Equal respect and protection to ALL religions |
| Applicability | Works in homogeneous societies | Suited to India's multi-religious reality |

> 📌 The Constitution also **abolished communal representation** (reservation of legislative seats on basis of religion — which existed under the 1909, 1919, and 1935 Acts). However, it retains **temporary reservation of seats for SCs and STs**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The debate on **Uniform Civil Code (Article 44)** became prominent again after the **Law Commission of India (2023)** invited public feedback on UCC.
> - The **Uttarakhand Uniform Civil Code Act, 2024** made Uttarakhand the first state to implement a UCC, reigniting national debate.
> - The **Supreme Court in 2019 (Sabarimala case follow-up)** and various other judgments continue to define the boundaries of religious freedom vs. state regulation.

### Feature 12: 🗳️ Universal Adult Franchise

Every citizen who is **18 years of age or above** has the right to vote — regardless of:
- Caste
- Race
- Religion
- Sex
- Literacy
- Wealth

> 📌 The voting age was **reduced from 21 to 18 years** by the **61st Constitutional Amendment Act of 1988**, effective from 1989.

**Why was this a bold experiment in 1949?**
- Vast country, huge population
- High levels of poverty and social inequality
- Overwhelming illiteracy

> 📌 For context: Even Western democracies extended voting rights slowly. The USA gave franchise to women in **1920**, Britain in **1928**, France in **1945**, and Switzerland only in **1971**.

**Benefits of Universal Adult Franchise:**
- Makes democracy **broad-based**
- Enhances **self-respect** of common people
- Upholds the principle of **equality**
- Helps **minorities protect their interests**
- Opens up **new hopes for weaker sections**

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - In the **2024 General Elections**, approximately **96.8 crore voters** were registered — the largest electorate in world history.
> - The **Election Commission of India** runs the **Systematic Voters' Education and Electoral Participation (SVEEP)** programme to maximise voter turnout, especially among youth and first-time voters.

---

### Feature 13: 🪪 Single Citizenship

Even though India has a **federal system** with both Centre and States, it provides for only **one citizenship — Indian citizenship.**

**India vs. USA on citizenship:**

| Country | Citizenship |
|---|---|
| **USA** | Dual citizenship — citizen of the USA *and* citizen of their specific state |
| **India** | Single citizenship — only Indian citizen, regardless of which state one is from |

All Indian citizens enjoy the **same political and civil rights** throughout the country — no state can discriminate.

> 📌 However, the text also notes an honest observation: Despite single citizenship and uniform rights, India continues to face **communal riots, caste conflicts, linguistic clashes, and ethnic disputes** — showing that the constitutional goal of a "united and integrated nation" remains a **work in progress**.

---

### Feature 14: 🏛️ Independent Bodies

Beyond the three organs of government, the Constitution establishes **independent constitutional bodies** as pillars of democracy:

1. **Election Commission (Article 324)** — Ensures free and fair elections to:
   - Parliament
   - State Legislatures
   - Office of President
   - Office of Vice-President

2. **Comptroller and Auditor-General of India (CAG)** — Audits accounts of Central and State governments; acts as the **guardian of the public purse** and comments on the legality and propriety of government expenditure.

3. **Union Public Service Commission (UPSC)** — Conducts exams for **All-India Services** and higher Central services; advises the President on disciplinary matters.
   > 📌 Currently, there are **three All-India Services**: Indian Administrative Service (IAS), Indian Police Service (IPS), and Indian Forest Service (IFoS).

4. **State Public Service Commission** — In every state; conducts exams for state services; advises the Governor on disciplinary matters.

**How is their independence protected?**
- Security of tenure
- Fixed service conditions
- Expenses charged to the **Consolidated Fund of India** (not subject to parliamentary vote)

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The **Supreme Court in Anoop Baranwal vs. Union of India (March 2023)** ruled that the **Chief Election Commissioner and Election Commissioners** should be appointed by a committee (PM + Leader of Opposition + Chief Justice of India), instead of only by the President on PM's advice. This was seen as a landmark step towards protecting electoral independence.
> - The **Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023** was subsequently passed, modifying the composition of the appointment committee — replacing the CJI with a Union Cabinet Minister, which sparked fresh debate.

### Feature 15: 🚨 Emergency Provisions

The Constitution has **elaborate emergency provisions** to enable the President to handle extraordinary situations — to protect sovereignty, unity, integrity, security, democratic system, and the Constitution itself.

**Three Types of Emergencies:**

| Type | Article | Ground |
|---|---|---|
| **National Emergency** | 352 | War, external aggression, or **armed rebellion** |
| **State Emergency (President's Rule)** | 356 / 365 | Failure of constitutional machinery in states / failure to comply with Centre's directions |
| **Financial Emergency** | 360 | Threat to financial stability or credit of India |

> 📌 The original term "internal disturbance" in Article 352 was replaced by **"armed rebellion"** by the **44th Amendment Act (1978)** — making it harder to declare an emergency without serious justification.

**Effect of Emergency:**
- Central Government becomes **all-powerful**
- States come under **total central control**
- It converts the **federal structure into a unitary one** — without formally amending the Constitution!

> 📌 This transformation — from **federal (normal times) to unitary (emergency times)** — is a **unique feature** of the Indian Constitution.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - **President's Rule (Article 356)** has been invoked over 130 times since 1950, though its use has become more restrained after the **S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)** Supreme Court judgment, which laid down strict conditions for its imposition.
> - Most recently, President's Rule was imposed in **Jammu & Kashmir** from **December 2018 to October 2019** following the dissolution of the state assembly, before the reorganisation into Union Territories.

### Feature 16: 🏘️ Three-Tier Government

Originally, the Constitution provided for only a **two-tier (dual) government** — Centre and States.

The **73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendment Acts (1992)** added a historic **third tier — Local Government** — which is unique and **not found in any other Constitution in the world.**

**73rd Amendment Act (1992) — Rural Local Bodies (Panchayats):**
- Added **Part IX** and **Schedule 11** to the Constitution.
- Provided for a **three-tier Panchayati Raj system** in every state:
  - **Village Panchayat** (at village level)
  - **Panchayat Samiti / Intermediate Panchayat** (at intermediate/block level)
  - **Zila Parishad / District Panchayat** (at district level)
- Schedule 11 specifies **29 subjects** for Panchayats.

**74th Amendment Act (1992) — Urban Local Bodies (Municipalities):**
- Added **Part IX-A** and **Schedule 12** to the Constitution.
- Provided for **three types of municipalities** in every state:
  - **Nagar Panchayat** — for a *transitional area* (moving from rural to urban)
  - **Municipal Council** — for a *smaller urban area*
  - **Municipal Corporation** — for a *larger urban area*
- Schedule 12 specifies **18 subjects** for Municipalities.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The **15th Finance Commission (2021–26)** has allocated significant funds directly to local bodies — over ₹4.36 lakh crore — reflecting growing recognition of the third tier.
> - The **Svamitva Scheme (2020)** aims to provide property rights documentation to residents of rural areas — directly empowering Gram Panchayats.
> - Debates around **urban governance** have intensified post-COVID, with demands for greater financial and administrative autonomy for city corporations, especially after the **Delhi and Mumbai** governance disputes.

---

### Feature 17: 🤝 Co-operative Societies

The **97th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2011** gave **constitutional status and protection to co-operative societies.**

**Three key changes made:**

1. Made the **right to form co-operative societies** a **Fundamental Right** under **Article 19**.
2. Added a new **Directive Principle** on promotion of co-operative societies — **Article 43-B**.
3. Added a new **Part IX-B** (Articles 243-ZH to 243-ZT) titled **"The Co-operative Societies".**

**What does Part IX-B ensure?**
- Co-operative societies must function in a **democratic, professional, autonomous, and economically sound** manner.
- **Parliament** has power to legislate for **multi-state co-operatives**.
- **State Legislatures** have power to legislate for **other co-operatives**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`
> - The **Ministry of Co-operation** was created in **July 2021** as a separate ministry to give a new impetus to the co-operative movement — a first of its kind at the Central level.
> - **Amul and IFFCO** are among the world's largest co-operative societies, demonstrating the power of the co-operative model in India.
> - However, in **2021**, the Supreme Court in *Union of India vs. Rajendra N. Shah* struck down parts of the 97th Amendment that dealt with **state-level co-operatives**, ruling they required ratification by state legislatures — an important federal safeguard.

## ❌ CRITICISMS OF THE CONSTITUTION

Critics raised six major objections against the Constitution. Here's a simplified breakdown — along with the counters offered by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar:

---

### Criticism 1: A "Borrowed Constitution"

- **Critics said:** The Constitution has nothing new or original — it's a **"bag of borrowings"** or a **"hotch-potch Constitution"** or a **"patchwork"** from world constitutions.

- **Ambedkar's reply:** *"One likes to ask whether there can be anything new in a Constitution framed at this hour in the history of the world... The charge of producing a blind copy is based on an inadequate study of the Constitution."* — He argued that the framers made necessary modifications to suit Indian conditions and avoided the faults of borrowed provisions.

---

### Criticism 2: A "Carbon Copy of the 1935 Act"

- **Critics said:**
  - **N. Srinivasan:** The Constitution is *"both in language and substance a close copy of the Act of 1935."*
  - **Sir Ivor Jennings:** *"Many of its provisions are copied almost textually"* from the 1935 Act.
  - **P.R. Deshmukh (Constituent Assembly member):** *"The Constitution is essentially the Government of India Act of 1935 with only adult franchise added."*

- **Ambedkar's reply:** *"As to the accusation that the Draft Constitution has reproduced a good part of the provisions of the Government of India Act, 1935, I make no apologies. There is nothing to be ashamed of in borrowing. It involves no plagiarism. Nobody holds any patent rights in the fundamental ideas of a Constitution."*

---

### Criticism 3: Un-Indian or Anti-Indian

- **Critics said:** The Constitution does not reflect Indian political traditions or spirit, making it unsuitable.
  - **K. Hanumanthaiya (Constituent Assembly):** *"We wanted the music of Veena or Sitar, but here we have the music of an English band."*
  - **Lokanath Misra:** Called it a *"slavish imitation of the west."*
  - **Lakshminarayan Sahu:** Predicted it *"would not prove suitable and would break down soon."*

---

### Criticism 4: An Un-Gandhian Constitution

- **Critics said:** The Constitution doesn't embody Mahatma Gandhi's philosophy. It should have been built on **village panchayats and district panchayats** as Gandhi envisioned.
  - **K. Hanumanthaiya:** *"That is exactly the kind of Constitution Mahatma Gandhi did not want."*
  - **T. Prakasam:** Attributed this to Ambedkar's non-participation in the Gandhian movement and his antagonism towards Gandhian ideas.

---

### Criticism 5: Elephantine Size

- **Critics said:** The Constitution is **too bulky and too detailed** with unnecessary elements.
  - **Sir Ivor Jennings:** Called it *"too long and complicated."*
  - **H.V. Kamath (Constituent Assembly):** Quipped — *"The emblem that we have selected for our assembly is an elephant. It is perhaps in consonance that our Constitution too is the bulkiest that the world has produced."*

---

### Criticism 6: Paradise of the Lawyers

- **Critics said:** The Constitution is **too legalistic and complex** — a "**lawyer's paradise.**"
  - **Sir Ivor Jennings** coined the phrase "lawyer's paradise."
  - **H.K. Maheswari (Constituent Assembly):** Said the draft *"tends to make people more litigious"* and *"opens up vast avenues of litigation."*
  - **P.R. Deshmukh:** Called it *"ponderous like the ponderous tomes of a law manual"* — argued it should have been *"a socio-political document, a vibrating, pulsating and life-giving document."*
