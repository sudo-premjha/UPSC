# Chapter 12: Basic Structure of the Constitution

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **Parliament CAN amend** the Constitution under Article 368 — but it **CANNOT destroy** its 'Basic Structure.'
> 2. The **Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973)** is the most landmark judgment in Indian constitutional history — it gave us the **Basic Structure Doctrine.**
> 3. The journey was a **tug-of-war** between Parliament and the Supreme Court over who has the final say on constitutional amendments.
> 4. The Supreme Court has **never given a closed/fixed list** of what counts as 'Basic Structure' — it evolves case by case.
> 5. As of today, **20 elements** have been identified as part of the Basic Structure through various Supreme Court judgments.

---

## 🧭 Introduction: Why Does This Chapter Matter?

Imagine you are the Parliament of India. You have been elected by the people. Can you change *anything* in the Constitution — even delete Fundamental Rights like the Right to Equality or Freedom of Speech?

This is exactly the question that the courts and Parliament fought over for decades. The answer, as we will see, is: **Yes, you can amend — but No, you cannot destroy the soul of the Constitution.**

That "soul" is called the **Basic Structure (or Basic Features) of the Constitution.**

---

## PART 1: THE EMERGENCE OF THE BASIC STRUCTURE DOCTRINE

### 🔵 Round 1 — Parliament Wins: Shankari Prasad Case (1951)

**Full Case Name:** *Shankari Prasad vs. Union of India (1951)*

**Background:**
- The **First Amendment Act (1951)** was passed by Parliament. It **curtailed (reduced) the Right to Property** (a Fundamental Right at the time).
- This was challenged in the Supreme Court.

**What the Supreme Court Said:**
- Parliament's power to amend the Constitution under **Article 368** *includes* the power to amend **Fundamental Rights.**
- The word **'law'** in **Article 13** (which says laws violating Fundamental Rights are void) refers only to **ordinary laws**, NOT to **constitutional amendment acts.**
- Therefore, Parliament **can** reduce or remove any Fundamental Right by passing a constitutional amendment, and it will NOT be void under Article 13.

> 💡 **Simple Analogy:** Think of it like this — Article 13 is a watchdog for ordinary laws. But Parliament argued the watchdog doesn't apply to the Constitution's own family members (i.e., constitutional amendments).

**Verdict: Parliament wins. It can amend Fundamental Rights.**

---

### 🔵 Round 2 — Parliament Wins Again: Sajjan Singh Case (1964)

**Full Case Name:** *Sajjan Singh vs. State of Rajasthan (1964)*

**What Happened:**
The Supreme Court **re-affirmed** the position from the Shankari Prasad case. It again held that:
- A constitutional amendment act made under **Article 368** is **NOT a 'law'** within the meaning of **Article 13.**
- Therefore, Parliament retains the power to amend Fundamental Rights.

**Verdict: Parliament wins again. Same position maintained.**

---

### 🔴 Round 3 — Supreme Court Strikes Back: Golak Nath Case (1967)

**Full Case Name:** *Golak Nath vs. State of Punjab (1967)*

**Background:**
- The **Seventeenth Amendment Act (1964)** inserted certain state laws into the **Ninth Schedule** (a special list that protects laws from judicial review).
- This was challenged before the Supreme Court.

**What the Supreme Court Said (Reversing Earlier Stand):**
- **Fundamental Rights** have been given a **'transcendental and immutable' (eternal and unchangeable)** position in the Constitution.
- Therefore, Parliament **CANNOT** abridge (reduce) or take away any Fundamental Right.
- A constitutional amendment act is **ALSO a 'law'** within the meaning of **Article 13.**
- Hence, any amendment that violates a Fundamental Right will be **void.**

**Verdict: Supreme Court wins. Parliament CANNOT amend Fundamental Rights.**

---

### 🔵 Round 4 — Parliament Fights Back: 24th Amendment Act (1971)

Parliament reacted strongly to the Golak Nath judgment by passing the **24th Amendment Act (1971).**

**What this Act did:**
- It **amended both Article 13 and Article 368.**
- It explicitly **declared** that Parliament has the power to abridge or take away any Fundamental Rights under Article 368.
- It further stated that such a constitutional amendment act will **NOT** be considered a 'law' under Article 13.

> 💡 In simple words: Parliament essentially told the Supreme Court — "We disagree. We are overruling your Golak Nath judgment through a constitutional amendment itself."

---

### 🏆 The Landmark Moment: Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973)

**Full Case Name:** *Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973)*
*(Popularly known as the **Fundamental Rights Case**)*

This is **the most important constitutional case in Indian history.** A bench of **13 judges** (the largest ever) heard this case.

**What the Supreme Court Said:**

✅ It **overruled** the Golak Nath Case (1967) — so Parliament *can* amend Fundamental Rights.

✅ It **upheld the validity** of the **24th Amendment Act (1971)** — Parliament does have the power to abridge or take away Fundamental Rights.

✅ **BUT** — and this is the game-changer — it laid down the **doctrine of 'Basic Structure' (or 'Basic Features') of the Constitution.**

The ruling was:
> *"The constituent power of Parliament under Article 368 does NOT enable it to alter the 'basic structure' of the Constitution."*

**In Plain English:**
- Parliament can amend ANY part of the Constitution.
- BUT it **cannot destroy or alter the fundamental identity** of the Constitution.
- If a Fundamental Right forms part of the **Basic Structure**, even Parliament cannot take it away.

**Basic Structure Elements Identified in This Case:**
1. Supremacy of the Constitution
2. Separation of powers between legislature, executive, and judiciary
3. Republican and democratic form of government
4. Secular character of the Constitution
5. Federal character of the Constitution
6. Sovereignty and unity of India
7. Freedom and dignity of the individual
8. Mandate to build a welfare state
9. Parliamentary System

**Verdict: A balanced outcome — Parliament can amend, but cannot destroy the Basic Structure.**

---

### ⚖️ Application of the Doctrine: Indira Nehru Gandhi Case (1975)

**Full Case Name:** *Indira Nehru Gandhi vs. Raj Narain (1975)*
*(Popularly known as the **Election Case**)*

**Background:**
- The **39th Amendment Act (1975)** was passed. One of its provisions placed election disputes involving the **Prime Minister** and the **Speaker of Lok Sabha** completely **outside the jurisdiction of all courts.**
- In other words, no court — not even the Supreme Court — could hear challenges to the PM's election.

**What the Supreme Court Said:**
- This provision was **struck down** as **unconstitutional.**
- It violated the **Basic Structure** of the Constitution because it undermined:
  1. India as a **sovereign democratic republic**
  2. **Equality of status and opportunity** of individuals
  3. **Secularism** and freedom of conscience
  4. **Rule of Law** (government of laws, not of men)
  5. **Judicial Review**
  6. **Free and fair elections** (implied in democracy)

> 💡 **Simple Explanation:** Imagine if a law was passed saying "The Prime Minister's exam results can never be challenged in any court." That would destroy the very idea of equality before law — which is Basic Structure.

**Verdict: The Basic Structure Doctrine was reaffirmed and applied for the first time.**

---

### 🔵 Round 5 — Parliament Strikes Back Again: 42nd Amendment Act (1976)

Parliament reacted to the judicially invented Basic Structure doctrine by passing the **42nd Amendment Act (1976)** — the most sweeping constitutional amendment ever made.

**What this Act did:**
- It amended **Article 368** and declared:
  - There is **NO limitation** on Parliament's constituent (amending) power.
  - **No amendment** can be questioned in any court on **any ground** — including violation of Fundamental Rights.

> 💡 In other words: Parliament declared itself to be the **absolute master** of the Constitution with no checks.

---

### ⚖️ Supreme Court Responds: Minerva Mills Case (1980)

**Full Case Name:** *Minerva Mills vs. Union of India (1980)*

**What the Supreme Court Said:**
- The provisions of the **42nd Amendment Act (1976)** that gave Parliament unlimited amending power and excluded **judicial review** were **struck down as unconstitutional.**
- The court applied the Basic Structure doctrine and stated (in its own words):

> *"Since the Constitution had conferred a limited amending power on the Parliament, the Parliament cannot under the exercise of that limited power enlarge that very power into an absolute power. Indeed, a limited amending power is one of the basic features of the Constitution and, therefore, the limitations on that power cannot be destroyed."*

> *"The donee of a limited power cannot by the exercise of that power convert the limited power into an unlimited one."*

**Basic Structure Elements Identified in This Case:**
1. **Limited power of Parliament** to amend the Constitution
2. **Judicial Review** (power of courts to examine laws)
3. **Harmony and balance** between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles

> 💡 **Simple Analogy:** If someone gives you a limited power of attorney (authority to act on their behalf for *specific* tasks only), you cannot use that limited authority to grant yourself *unlimited* powers. Parliament is in the same position.

**Verdict: Supreme Court wins decisively. Parliament's amending power is LIMITED.**

---

### 📌 Clarification: Waman Rao Case (1980)

**Full Case Name:** *Waman Rao vs. Union of India (1980)*

**What the Court Said:**
- The Supreme Court **adhered to** (stuck with) the Basic Structure doctrine.
- It **clarified** that the doctrine would apply to all constitutional amendments enacted **after April 24, 1973** (the date of the Kesavananda Bharati judgment).

> 💡 This means amendments made *before* that date are protected from this test.

## PART 2: ELEMENTS OF THE BASIC STRUCTURE

### What is the Current Position?

Today, the rule is:
> **Parliament under Article 368 can amend ANY part of the Constitution — including Fundamental Rights — BUT it cannot affect the 'Basic Structure' of the Constitution.**

**Important Note:** The Supreme Court has **never given a fixed, closed list** of what constitutes the Basic Structure. The list **grows and evolves** through each new judgment.

---

### 📋 The Complete List of Basic Structure Elements (Identified So Far)

| # | Element of Basic Structure |
|---|---------------------------|
| 1 | **Supremacy of the Constitution** |
| 2 | **Sovereign, democratic, and republican** nature of the Indian polity |
| 3 | **Secular character** of the Constitution |
| 4 | **Separation of powers** between legislature, executive, and judiciary |
| 5 | **Federal character** of the Constitution |
| 6 | **Unity and integrity** of the nation |
| 7 | **Welfare state** (socio-economic justice) |
| 8 | **Judicial review** |
| 9 | **Freedom and dignity** of the individual |
| 10 | **Parliamentary system** |
| 11 | **Rule of law** |
| 12 | **Harmony and balance** between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles |
| 13 | **Principle of equality** |
| 14 | **Free and fair elections** |
| 15 | **Independence of the Judiciary** |
| 16 | **Limited power of Parliament** to amend the Constitution |
| 17 | **Effective access to justice** |
| 18 | **Principles (or essence) underlying Fundamental Rights** |
| 19 | **Powers of the Supreme Court** under Articles 32, 136, 141, and 142 |
| 20 | **Powers of the High Courts** under Articles 226 and 227 |

---

> 🔎 **Quick Reference for Articles:**
> - **Article 32** — Remedies for enforcement of Fundamental Rights (includes writs)
> - **Article 136** — Special leave to appeal by the Supreme Court
> - **Article 141** — Law declared by Supreme Court is binding on all courts
> - **Article 142** — Enforcement of decrees and orders of Supreme Court
> - **Article 226** — Power of High Courts to issue certain writs
> - **Article 227** — Power of Superintendence of High Courts over all courts

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *Added to connect the chapter's core ideas to present-day developments.*

---

### 1. 🏛️ Basic Structure and the NJAC Judgment (2015)

**The Supreme Court vs. Parliament — The Battle Continues:**

In **2014**, Parliament passed the **99th Constitutional Amendment Act** and created the **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC)** — a body that would give the government a role in appointing judges, replacing the existing **collegium system** (where judges appoint judges).

In **Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India (2015)**, the **Supreme Court struck down the NJAC** as unconstitutional.

**Why?** Because it violated the **Basic Structure** — specifically, the **independence of the judiciary.**

> 💡 This is a **live, real-world example** of the Basic Structure Doctrine in action — just as the chapter describes.

---

### 2. 🏛️ EWS Reservation and Basic Structure (2022)

**Janhit Abhiyan vs. Union of India (2022):**

The **103rd Constitutional Amendment Act (2019)** introduced **10% reservation for Economically Weaker Sections (EWS)** among the general category. This took total reservations beyond the 50% cap set in the Mandal Case.

The Supreme Court **upheld** the EWS reservation (3:2 majority) but the dissenting judges argued it violated the **Basic Structure** (specifically the **principle of equality**).

> 💡 This case shows that the debate over what violates Basic Structure is very much alive and ongoing.

---

### 3. 🏛️ Ninth Schedule and Judicial Review (Ongoing Relevance)

Following the **I.R. Coelho Case (2007)**, Parliament must now be careful that laws added to the Ninth Schedule do not violate the Basic Structure. **State governments** seeking to protect their reservation laws (e.g., Tamil Nadu's 69% reservation) by inserting them into the Ninth Schedule still face potential judicial scrutiny.

---

### 4. 🗳️ Free and Fair Elections — Election Commission's Independence

The Basic Structure element of **free and fair elections** was brought sharply into focus in **Anoop Baranwal vs. Union of India (2023)**, where the Supreme Court ruled that the **Chief Election Commissioner and Election Commissioners** should be appointed by a committee (not solely by the government) to protect electoral independence. This directly connects to the Basic Structure element of **free and fair elections.**

---

### 5. 💡 Federalism and Centre-State Tensions (Post-2019)

The **S.R. Bommai Case (1994)** principle that **federalism** is part of Basic Structure remains extremely relevant. The debates around:
- **Abrogation of Article 370** (2019) — special status of Jammu & Kashmir
- **The Supreme Court's hearing** in *In Re: Article 370 (2023)* where the court upheld the abrogation but the Basic Structure arguments were raised

These are direct real-world applications of the Basic Structure doctrine.
