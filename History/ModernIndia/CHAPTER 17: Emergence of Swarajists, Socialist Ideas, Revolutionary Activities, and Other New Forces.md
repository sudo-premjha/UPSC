# CHAPTER 17: Emergence of Swarajists, Socialist Ideas, Revolutionary Activities, and Other New Forces

---

## 🔍 Introduction: A Movement in Pause

After Gandhi's arrest in **March 1922**, the nationalist movement entered a difficult phase. There was **disintegration, disorganisation, and demoralisation** among nationalist ranks.

A critical question arose: *What should nationalists do during this passive phase of the movement?*

This question split the Congress into two distinct camps — the **Swarajists** and the **No-Changers**.

---

## 🏛️ Part 1: The Swarajist-No-Changer Debate

### Who Were the Swarajists?

The Swarajists believed that nationalists should **end the boycott of legislative councils** (law-making assemblies set up by the British) and instead **enter them**.

**Why?** Their logic was:
- Use the councils as an **arena of political struggle**.
- **Expose the basic weaknesses** of these assemblies from the inside.
- Arouse popular enthusiasm through council work.
- Their strategy: **"End or Mend"** — either force reforms or **obstruct the working of the councils** to make them dysfunctional.

**Key Leaders:** **C.R. Das, Motilal Nehru**, and **Ajmal Khan**.

---

### Who Were the No-Changers?

The No-Changers believed the opposite — that entering councils would be a mistake.

**Their Arguments:**
- Parliamentary work would lead to **neglect of constructive work**.
- It would cause a **loss of revolutionary zeal**.
- It would lead to **political corruption**.
- Instead, **constructive work** would prepare the masses for the *next phase* of civil disobedience.

**Key Leaders:** **C. Rajagopalachari, Vallabhbhai Patel, Rajendra Prasad**, and **M.A. Ansari**.

---

### The Split: Gaya Session (December 1922)

- The Swarajists' proposal was **defeated** at the **Gaya Session of the Congress (December 1922)**.
- In response, **C.R. Das** and **Motilal Nehru** resigned from the presidentship and secretaryship of the Congress respectively.
- They announced the formation of the **Congress-Khilafat Swarajya Party** (simply called the **Swarajist Party**):
  - **President:** C.R. Das
  - **Secretary:** Motilal Nehru

---

### Swarajists' Full Arguments for Council Entry

- ✅ Entering councils would **not negate** the non-cooperation programme — it was opening a *new front* of struggle.
- ✅ In a period of political vacuum, council work would **enthuse the masses** and keep up their morale.
- ✅ Nationalist entry would **deter the government** from filling councils with undesirable elements who could be used to legitimise British policies.
- ✅ The councils could be used as an **arena of political struggle** — there was no intention to use them as organs for the *gradual transformation* of colonial rule.

---

### Compromise at Delhi (September 1923)

Both sides wanted to avoid a **1907-type split** (like the Surat Split between Moderates and Extremists) and stayed in touch with Gandhi in jail.

Both sides agreed on:
1. The need to present a **united front** to force government reforms.
2. The **necessity of Gandhi's leadership** for a united nationalist front.

A compromise was reached at a **meeting in Delhi in September 1923**:
- The **Swarajists were allowed to contest elections as a group within the Congress**.
- The Swarajists accepted the full Congress programme — with only *one* difference: they would join legislative councils.
- Elections to the **Central Legislative Assembly** and **provincial assemblies** were scheduled for **November 1923**.

---

### 📜 The Swarajist Manifesto (Released October 1923)

The manifesto took a strong **anti-imperialist** line:

- 🔴 The **guiding motive** of British governance in India was the **selfish interests of Britain**, not India's welfare.
- 🔴 The so-called reforms were only a **blind** to further British interests under the pretence of granting responsible government — the real objective was **permanent exploitation** of India's unlimited resources.
- 🔴 The Swarajists would **present the nationalist demand for self-government** in the councils.
- 🔴 If this demand was **rejected**, they would adopt a policy of **uniform, continuous, and consistent obstruction** to make governance through councils impossible.
- 🔴 Councils would be **"wrecked from within"** by creating deadlocks on every measure.

---

### Gandhi's Changing Attitude

Gandhi was **initially opposed** to council entry. But after his **release from prison on health grounds in February 1924**, he gradually moved towards reconciliation:

- **Reason 1:** He felt public opposition to council entry would be **counterproductive**.
- **Reason 2:** In the **November 1923 elections**, the Swarajists had won:
  - **42 out of 141 elected seats** in the Central Legislative Assembly.
  - A **clear majority** in the provincial assembly of Central Provinces.
  - In cooperation with Liberals and independents like **Jinnah** and **Malaviya**, they won a majority in legislatures.
- **Reason 3:** Their **courageous and uncompromising** functioning in the councils convinced Gandhi they would not become a mere limb of colonial administration.
- **Reason 4:** A **government crackdown on revolutionary terrorists and Swarajists** towards the end of 1924 angered Gandhi, and he expressed solidarity with the Swarajists.

**Final Agreement (1924):** Both sides agreed that the **Swarajists would work in the councils as an integral part of the Congress**. This was endorsed at the **Belgaum Session of the Congress (December 1924)** — the only session over which **Gandhi himself presided**.

---

## 📉 Part 2: Decline of the Swarajists

The Swarajist position weakened over time due to multiple factors:

### Why They Declined

- **Widespread communal riots** weakened the coalition.
- A **split among Swarajists themselves** on communal lines and on the **Responsivist vs. Non-Responsivist** (whether to cooperate with government / hold office or not) question.
- The **government's divide-and-rule strategy** worked — it successfully separated the militant from the moderate, and Hindus from Muslims within the party.
- The **Swarajists lost Muslim support** when they failed to support the tenants' cause against zamindars in Bengal (most tenants were Muslims).
- **Death of C.R. Das in 1925** was a severe blow.

### The Responsivist Split

The **Responsivists** within the Swarajist Party — **Lala Lajpat Rai, Madan Mohan Malaviya**, and **N.C. Kelkar** — advocated:
- Cooperation with the government.
- Holding office wherever possible.
- Protection of so-called "Hindu interests."

This further alienated Muslim members and gave ammunition to communal forces on both sides — Muslim communalists called the Swarajists anti-Muslim, while Hindu communalists called leaders like Motilal Nehru (who didn't favour joining the council) anti-Hindu.

### Final Collapse

| Event | Year |
|---|---|
| Main Swarajist leadership withdraws from legislatures | March 1926 |
| 1926 elections fought as a disarrayed party | 1926 |
| Won 40 seats at Centre, some in Madras; routed in UP, Central Provinces, Punjab | 1926 |
| Final walkout from councils after Lahore Congress resolution on **purna swaraj** | 1930 |

---

## ✅ Part 3: Achievements of the Swarajists

Despite their decline, the Swarajists achieved significant things:

1. ✅ **Out-voted the government** several times with coalition partners, even on matters relating to **budgetary grants**, and passed **adjournment motions**.
2. ✅ Made **powerful speeches** on self-government, civil liberties, and industrialisation.
3. ✅ **Vithalbhai Patel** was elected **Speaker of the Central Legislative Assembly in 1925** — a landmark achievement.
4. ✅ Achieved the notable **defeat of the Public Safety Bill in 1928**, which was aimed at empowering the government to deport foreigners it deemed subversive (the government was alarmed by the spread of socialist/communist ideas and the role of foreign Comintern activists).
5. ✅ **Filled the political vacuum** when the national movement was rebuilding its strength.
6. ✅ **Exposed the hollowness** of the Montford (Montagu-Chelmsford) scheme.
7. ✅ Demonstrated that **councils could be used creatively** as a platform for nationalist politics.

---

## ❌ Part 4: Drawbacks of the Swarajists

1. ❌ **Lacked a policy to coordinate** their militancy inside the legislatures with mass struggle outside. They relied **totally on newspaper reporting** to communicate with the public.
2. ❌ An **obstructionist strategy** had inherent limitations — you can only block for so long.
3. ❌ **Conflicting ideas with coalition partners** further limited their effectiveness.
4. ❌ **Failed to resist the perks and privileges** of power and office.
5. ❌ **Failed to support the peasants' cause in Bengal** and consequently lost the support of Muslim members who were pro-peasant.

---

## 🔨 Part 5: Constructive Work by the No-Changers

While the Swarajists fought in the councils, the No-Changers devoted themselves to grassroots work that connected them directly to the masses:

1. 🏡 **Ashrams** sprang up where young men and women worked among **tribals and lower castes** (especially in **Kheda and Bardoli** areas of Gujarat) and popularised the use of **charkha and khadi**.
2. 🏫 **National schools and colleges** were set up where students were trained in a **non-colonial ideological framework**.
3. 🤝 Significant work was done for:
   - **Hindu-Muslim unity**
   - **Removing untouchability**
   - **Boycott of foreign cloth and liquor**
   - **Flood relief**
4. 💪 The constructive workers served as the **backbone of civil disobedience** as active organisers when the movement resumed.

### A Honest Critique of Constructive Work

It wasn't all perfect. Some limitations of the constructive programme:

- **National education benefited** mainly the urban lower middle classes and rich peasants — not the most marginalised.
- Enthusiasm for national education **surfaced only during the excitement of the movement**; in passive phases, the lure of degrees and jobs drew students back to official schools.
- **Khadi** was **costlier than imported cloth** — making its popularisation an uphill task for the poor.
- While campaigning on the **social aspect of untouchability**, no emphasis was placed on the **economic grievances** of landless labourers and agricultural workers who comprised mostly the untouchables.

> **Important Note:** Despite working separately, the Swarajists and No-Changers **maintained the best of terms** with each other and were able to **unite whenever a new political struggle was ripe**.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Swarajists and No-Changers

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The "Work Within the System vs. Boycott" Debate Lives On
The Swarajist-No-Changer debate about whether to engage with state institutions or boycott them echoes in contemporary politics:
- The **Aam Aadmi Party (AAP)'s rise (2013–2015)** reflected a similar tension — whether to work within existing electoral structures or continue civil society pressure from outside. AAP chose to enter the system, much like the Swarajists.
- `[Modern Example]` In 2023–24, debates around **boycotting the Electoral Bonds Scheme** vs. engaging with the **Supreme Court** to challenge it reflect the same strategic dilemma. The Supreme Court **struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme in February 2024** — suggesting that institutional engagement (via courts) can still yield results.

### 2. Constructive Work — A Living Legacy
- The No-Changers' emphasis on **grassroots constructive work** finds a modern echo in schemes like:
  - **PM SVANidhi Scheme (2020)** — for street vendors (grassroots economic empowerment)
  - **Jal Jeevan Mission** — village-level water infrastructure
  - **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020** — echoes the national education movement's vision of education in the mother tongue and rooted in Indian knowledge traditions.

## 🌊 Part 6: Emergence of New Forces in the 1920s

The **third decade of the 20th century** was a watershed in modern Indian history. While the masses were entering the national movement, the decade also saw the **crystallisation of distinct political currents** on the national scene.

These diverse currents were partly a reaction — positive or negative — to the **Gandhian philosophy of satyagraha** based on truth and non-violence. **International influence** on Indian political thinkers was also more pronounced than ever before.

### The Six New Forces of the 1920s

---

### 1. 📖 Spread of Marxist and Socialist Ideas

- **Ideas of Marx and socialist thinkers** inspired many groups to come into existence as socialists and communists.
- These ideas also led to the rise of a **left wing within the Congress**, represented by **Jawaharlal Nehru** and **Subhash Chandra Bose**.
- These young nationalists, inspired by the **Soviet Revolution** and dissatisfied with Gandhian political programme, began advocating **radical solutions** for India's economic, political, and social problems.

**What did these younger nationalists believe?**
- Were critical of **both Swarajists and No-Changers**.
- Advocated a more consistent anti-imperialist line — the slogan of **purna swarajya (complete independence)**.
- Were influenced by **international currents** (though still vaguely).
- Stressed combining **nationalism and anti-imperialism with social justice**.
- Raised the question of **internal class oppression** by capitalists and landlords — not just British oppression.

### Timeline of the Communist Party of India (CPI)

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1920** | **Communist Party of India (CPI)** formed in **Tashkent** (now capital of Uzbekistan) by **M.N. Roy, Abani Mukherji**, and others after the Second Congress of the Comintern (Communist International) |
| **1920** | **M.N. Roy** became the first Indian elected to the leadership of the **Comintern** |
| **1924** | Many communists — **S.A. Dange, Muzaffar Ahmed, Shaukat Usmani, Nalini Gupta** — jailed in the **Kanpur Bolshevik Conspiracy Case** |
| **1925** | **Indian Communist Conference at Kanpur** formalised the foundation of the CPI |
| **1929** | Government crackdown — **31 leading communists, trade unionists, and left-wing leaders** arrested and tried at Meerut in the famous **Meerut Conspiracy Case** |

- **Workers' and Peasants' Parties** were organised all over the country, propagating Marxist and communist ideas.
- All these communist groups remained an **integral part of the national movement** and worked alongside the Congress.

---

### 2. 🎓 Activism of Indian Youth

- **Students' leagues** were being established all over the country.
- **Students' conferences** were being held regularly.
- In **1928**, **Jawaharlal Nehru** presided over the **All Bengal Students' Conference**.

---

### 3. 🌾 Peasants' Agitations

Peasant agitations broke out across different regions over common demands:
- **Revision of tenancy laws**
- **Lower rents**
- **Protection against eviction**
- **Relief from indebtedness**

| Region | Movement/Nature |
|---|---|
| **United Provinces** | Agitations for tenancy law revision and rent relief |
| **Rampa region, Andhra** | Similar peasant agitations |
| **Rajasthan** | Peasant agitations |
| **Ryotwari areas of Bombay and Madras** | Peasant protests |
| **Gujarat** | **Bardoli Satyagraha (1928)** led by **Vallabhbhai Patel** |

---

### 4. 🏭 Growth of Trade Unionism

- The trade union movement was led by the **All India Trade Union Congress (AITUC)**, founded in **1920**.
  - **First President:** **Lala Lajpat Rai**
  - **General Secretary:** **Dewan Chaman Lal**
  - **Tilak** was also one of the moving spirits.

**Major Strikes of the 1920s:**
- Kharagpur Railway Workshops
- **Tata Iron and Steel Works (Jamshedpur)**
- **Bombay Textile Mills** — involved **1,50,000 workers** and went on for **5 months**
- **Buckingham Carnatic Mills**
- In **1928**, there were strikes involving **5 lakh workers** across the country.

> 📌 **Historic First:** In **1923**, the **first May Day** was celebrated in India — in **Madras**.

---

### 5. 🏛️ Caste Movements

The varied contradictions of Indian society found expression in caste associations and movements. These could be divisive, conservative, or potentially radical:

| Movement/Organisation | Region | Leader |
|---|---|---|
| **Justice Party** | Madras | — |
| **Self-respect Movement (1925)** | Madras | **"Periyar" — E.V. Ramaswamy Naicker** |
| **Satyashodhak activists** | Satara, Maharashtra | — |
| **Bhaskar Rao Jadhav movement** | Maharashtra | **Bhaskar Rao Jadhav** |
| **Mahars movement** | Maharashtra | **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** |
| **Radical Ezhavas** | Kerala | **K. Aiyappan** and **C. Kesavan** |
| **Yadav social improvement movement** | Bihar | — |
| **Unionist Party** | Punjab | **Fazl-i-Hussain** |

---

### 6. 💣 Revolutionary Activity with a Turn Towards Socialism

Those dissatisfied with the **non-violent nationalist strategy** turned to revolutionary activity. Two distinct strands emerged:

- **Hindustan Republican Association (H.R.A.)** — operating in **Punjab-UP-Bihar**
- **Yugantar, Anushilan groups**, and later the **Chittagong Revolt Group under Surya Sen** — operating in **Bengal**

---

## 🔫 Part 7: Revolutionary Activity During the 1920s

### Why Did Youth Turn to Revolution After the Non-Cooperation Movement?

The chain of events is important to understand:

1. Revolutionaries had faced **severe repression** during WWI.
2. In **early 1920**, many were **released under general amnesty** (to create a harmonious atmosphere for Montford Reforms).
3. Gandhi launched the Non-Cooperation Movement — many revolutionary groups either **joined it or suspended their activities** under persuasion by Gandhi and C.R. Das.
4. The **sudden withdrawal of the movement** (Chauri Chaura, 1922) left many **disillusioned**.
5. They began questioning the **basic strategy of non-violence** and looked for alternatives.
6. Since they were not attracted to:
   - The **parliamentary work** of the Swarajists, OR
   - The **patient, undramatic constructive work** of the No-Changers
   - ...they were drawn to the belief that **only violent methods would free India**.

### Key Revolutionary Leaders (Nearly All Were Former Non-Cooperation Movement Participants)

> **Jogesh Chandra Chatterjee, Surya Sen, Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, Chandrasekhar Azad, Shiv Verma, Bhagwati Charan Vohra, Jaidev Kapur**, and **Jatin Das**.

---

### Major Influences on the Revolutionaries

| Influence | Details |
|---|---|
| **(i)** Working class trade unionism | Revolutionaries wanted to harness the revolutionary potential of this emergent class for nationalist revolution |
| **(ii)** Russian Revolution (1917) | The success of the young Soviet state was a powerful inspiration |
| **(iii)** Newly sprouting communist groups | Emphasis on Marxism, socialism, and the proletariat (working class) |
| **(iv)** Revolutionary journals | *Atmasakti*, *Sarathi*, and *Bijoli* published memoirs extolling self-sacrifice of revolutionaries |
| **(v)** Books and novels | *Bandi Jiwan* by **Sachin Sanyal**; *Pather Dabi* by **Sharatchandra Chatterjee (Chattopadhyay)** — a government ban on the latter only enhanced its popularity |

---

## 🗺️ Part 8: Revolutionary Activity in Punjab-UP-Bihar

### The Hindustan Republican Association (HRA)

- **Founded:** October 1924, in **Kanpur**
- **Founders:** **Ramprasad Bismil, Jogesh Chandra Chatterjee**, and **Sachin Sanyal**
- **Aim:** To organise an **armed revolution** to overthrow colonial government and establish in its place the **Federal Republic of United States of India** based on the principle of **adult franchise** (voting rights for all adults).

---

### 🚂 Kakori Robbery (August 1925)

The **most important action** of the HRA:
- HRA men **held up the 8-Down train** at **Kakori**, a village near Lucknow.
- They **looted its official railway cash**.

**Aftermath — Government Crackdown:**
- Many were arrested.
- **17 were jailed**, **4 transported for life**.
- **4 were hanged: Bismil, Ashfaqullah, Roshan Singh**, and **Rajendra Lahiri**.

> Kakori proved to be a **major setback** for the revolutionary movement.

---

### The Historic Reorganisation: HRA → HSRA (September 1928)

Determined to overcome the Kakori setback, younger revolutionaries — inspired by **socialist ideas** — met at the **ruins of Ferozshah Kotla in Delhi (September 1928)** to reorganise.

Under the leadership of **Chandrashekhar Azad**:
- **HRA was renamed: Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA)**
- The HSRA decided to work under **collective leadership**.
- **Socialism** was adopted as the **official goal**.

**Key Participants:**
- From Punjab: **Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev Thapar (Sukhdev), Bhagwati Charan Vohra**
- From United Provinces: **Bejoy Kumar Sinha, Shiv Verma, Jaidev Kapur**

---

### 🔫 Saunders' Murder, Lahore (December 1928)

**Background:**
- **Lala Lajpat Rai** (Sher-i-Punjab) died from injuries sustained during a **lathi charge on an anti-Simon Commission procession** (October 1928).
- This tragedy, which humiliated the entire nation, led the HSRA to once again take to **individual assassination**.

**What Happened:**
- **Bhagat Singh** and **Shivram Rajguru** shot dead **John P. Saunders** (Deputy Superintendent of Police).
- They had **mistaken Saunders** for **Superintendent of Police James Scott**, who was actually responsible for the lathi charge on Lala Lajpat Rai.
- **Chandrashekhar Azad** shot dead an Indian constable who tried to pursue Bhagat Singh and Rajguru as they fled.

**Justification given by the HSRA:**
> *"The murder of a leader respected by millions of people at the unworthy hands of an ordinary police officer...was an insult to the nation. It was the bounden duty of young men of India to efface it... We regret to have had to kill a person but he was part and parcel of that inhuman and unjust order which has to be destroyed."*

---

### 💣 Bomb in the Central Legislative Assembly (April 8, 1929)

**Purpose:** The HSRA wanted to publicise its **changed objectives** and explain why a **revolution by the masses** was needed.

**What Happened:**
- **Bhagat Singh** and **Batukeshwar Dutt** threw a bomb in the **Central Legislative Assembly on April 8, 1929**.
- This was to **protest against the passage of the Public Safety Bill and Trade Disputes Bill**, which were aimed at **curtailing civil liberties** of citizens — especially workers.
- The **bombs were deliberately made harmless** — aimed at making *"the deaf hear"*.
- The objective was to **get arrested** and use the **trial court as a forum for revolutionary propaganda**.

> *"Force when aggressively applied is 'violence' and is, therefore, morally unjustifiable, but when it is used in the furtherance of a legitimate cause, it has its moral justification."* — **Bhagat Singh**

---

### ⚖️ Action Against the Revolutionaries

**The Trials:**
- **Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev**, and **Rajguru** were tried in the **Lahore Conspiracy Case**.
- Many other revolutionaries were tried in a series of other cases.
- The defence of the revolutionaries was **organised by Congress leaders**.

**In Jail:**
- Revolutionaries **protested against horrible prison conditions** through fasting.
- They demanded **honourable and decent treatment as political prisoners**.
- **Jatin Das** became the **first martyr on the 63rd day of his fast**.
- **Bhagat Singh** became a **household name** across India.

**Key Final Events:**

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **December 1929** | Azad involved in a bid to blow up **Viceroy Irwin's train** near Delhi |
| **1930** | **26 violent incidents** in Punjab alone; series of actions in UP towns |
| **February 27, 1931** | Chandrashekhar Azad met fellow revolutionaries at **Alfred Park (now Chandrashekhar Azad Park), Allahabad**; betrayed by an associate; besieged by British police; **shot himself dead** to fulfil his pledge of never being captured alive. *(His Colt pistol is displayed at the Allahabad Museum.)* |
| **March 23, 1931** | **Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev**, and **Rajguru** hanged for Saunders' assassination. *(March 23 is now observed as **Shaheed Diwas** and **Sarvodaya Day**.)* |

> It is said that the three young men walked towards the gallows **cheerfully**, chanting *"Inquilab Zindabad"* and *"Down with British Imperialism"*.

---

## 🗺️ Part 9: Revolutionary Activity in Bengal

### Reorganisation in the 1920s

During the 1920s, many revolutionary groups in Bengal:
- Reorganised their **underground activities**.
- Continued working **under the Congress**, getting access to the masses and providing organisational base.
- Many cooperated with **C.R. Das** in his Swarajist work.

**After C.R. Das's death (1925)**, the Bengal Congress split into two factions:
- One led by **J.M. Sengupta** (backed by the **Anushilan group**)
- One led by **Subhash Bose** (backed by the **Yugantar group**)

### Early Actions in Bengal

- **1924:** **Gopinath Saha** attempted to assassinate the notorious **Calcutta Police Commissioner Charles Tegart** — another man named **Day** was accidentally killed instead.
- The government cracked down heavily using a new ordinance.
- Many, including **Subhash Bose**, were arrested.
- **Gopinath Saha** was hanged.

---

### 🔫 Chittagong Armoury Raid (April 1930) — Surya Sen

**Who Was Surya Sen?**
- Participated in the **Non-Cooperation Movement**.
- Became a **teacher in the national school at Chittagong**.
- Imprisoned from **1926 to 1928** for revolutionary activity.
- **Secretary of the Chittagong District Congress Committee**.
- Was a **lover of poetry**, an admirer of **Tagore** and **Qazi Nazrul Islam**.
- Famous saying: *"Humanism is a special virtue of a revolutionary."*

**The Plan:**
Surya Sen, along with associates **Anant Singh, Ganesh Ghosh**, and **Lokenath Baul**, planned to:
- Occupy the **two main armouries** in Chittagong to seize arms for revolutionaries.
- **Destroy telephone and telegraph lines**.
- **Dislocate the railway link** of Chittagong with the rest of Bengal.

**The Raid:**
- Conducted in **April 1930**.
- Involved **65 activists** under the banner of **Indian Republican Army — Chittagong Branch**.
- The raid was **quite successful**:
  - Surya Sen **hoisted the national flag**.
  - Took salute.
  - **Proclaimed a provisional revolutionary government**.
- Afterwards, they dispersed into neighbouring villages and continued raiding government targets.

**Aftermath:**
- Surya Sen was **arrested in February 1933** and **hanged in January 1934**.
- But the Chittagong raid **fired the imagination of revolutionary-minded youth** across the country — recruits poured in steadily.

---

### 🌟 Noteworthy Aspects of Bengal's Revolutionary Phase

**(i) Large-scale Participation of Women:**

Especially under Surya Sen, women were active participants — not just supporters:

| Woman Revolutionary | Action |
|---|---|
| **Pritilata Waddedar** | Died conducting a raid |
| **Kalpana Dutt** | Arrested and tried alongside Surya Sen; given **life sentence** |
| **Santi Ghosh and Suniti Chandheri** | Schoolgirls from Comilla who **shot dead the district magistrate (December 1931)** |
| **Bina Das** | **Fired point blank at the Governor** while receiving her degree at convocation **(February 1932)** |

**(ii) Emphasis on Group Action:**
Instead of individual action, the focus was on **group action aimed at organs of the colonial State**. The objective: set an example for youth and **demoralise the bureaucracy**.

**(iii) Shedding Hindu Religiosity:**
- Some of the earlier tendency towards **Hindu religious rituals** (like oath-taking) was shed.
- This **facilitated participation by Muslims**.
- Surya Sen's group included Muslims: **Satar, Mir Ahmed, Fakir Ahmed Mian**, and **Tunu Mian**.

### Drawbacks of Bengal Revolutionary Movement
- ❌ Retained some **conservative elements**.
- ❌ Failed to **evolve broader socio-economic goals**.
- ❌ Those working with Swarajists failed to **support the cause of Muslim peasantry** against zamindars in Bengal.

### Official Reaction
- Government panic followed by **severe repression**.
- Armed with **20 repressive acts**, the government unleashed police on revolutionaries.
- In Chittagong, **several villages were burned** and **punitive fines** imposed on many others.
- In **1933**, **Jawaharlal Nehru** was arrested for **sedition** and given **two years' sentence** — for condemning imperialism and praising the heroism of the revolutionaries.

---

## 💡 Part 10: Ideological Rethinking — The Philosophical Revolution

This is perhaps the most intellectually important section of the chapter. The revolutionaries underwent a **profound transformation** in their thinking.

### The HRA's Early Ideological Commitments

- The **Founding Council of HRA** had decided to preach **revolutionary and communist principles**.
- The **HRA Manifesto (1925)** declared the **"HRA stood for abolition of all systems which made exploitation of man by man possible"**.
- HRA's main organ **Revolutionary** proposed:
  - **Nationalisation of railways** and other means of transport.
  - Nationalisation of **heavy industries** like ship-building and steel.
- HRA had also decided to **start labour and peasant organisations** and work for an **"organised and armed revolution"**.

---

### Bismil's Final Appeal

In his last days, **Ramprasad Bismil** made a remarkable call:
- **Gave up pistols and revolvers** — urged youth to do the same.
- Urged youth **not to work in revolutionary conspiracies** but in an **open movement**.
- Called for **strengthening Hindu-Muslim unity**.
- Urged **uniting all political groups under the Congress leadership**.
- Affirmed faith in **communism** and the principle that **"every human being has equal rights over the products of nature"**.

---

### Bhagat Singh's Ideological Evolution

This is perhaps the most important intellectual journey in the chapter:

**Key Text:** *The Philosophy of the Bomb* by **Bhagwati Charan Vohra** — contains the classic statement of the revolutionary position.

Even **before his arrest**, Bhagat Singh had moved away from violent, individual heroic action towards:
- **Marxism**
- **Belief that only a popular, broad-based mass movement** could lead to successful revolution.
- Revolution had to be **"by the masses, for the masses"**.

**Practical Expressions of This Shift:**
- **Punjab Naujawan Bharat Sabha (1926):** Bhagat Singh helped establish this as an **open wing** of revolutionaries to carry out political work among **youth, peasants, and workers**, with branches in villages.
- **Lahore Students' Union:** Bhagat and **Sukhdev** also organised this for **open, legal work among students**.

**But Why Continue Individual Heroic Action?**
Bhagat and his comrades understood they faced a dilemma — two reasons:
1. **Effective acquisition of new ideology is a prolonged, historical process** — but the need of the time was a **quick change** in the way of thinking.
2. They faced the **classic problem of how to mobilise people and recruit them** — and chose **"propaganda by deed"** (individual heroic action + using courts as forums for revolutionary propaganda).

> *"...real revolutionary armies are in villages and factories."* — **Bhagat Singh**

---

### Redefining "Revolution"

This is a crucial conceptual shift:

**Old definition:** Revolution = militancy, violence, individual heroism.

**New definition (Bhagat Singh's):**

> *"Revolution does not necessarily involve sanguinary strife (bloodshed), nor is there a place in it for personal vendetta. It is not the cult of bomb and pistol. By revolution we mean the present order of things, which is based on manifest injustice, must change."*

**Components of the New Revolutionary Vision:**

| Aspect | Bhagat Singh's Position |
|---|---|
| **Goal** | National liberation + a **new socialist order** ending "exploitation of man by man" |
| **Class approach** | *"Peasants have to free themselves not only from the foreign yoke, but also from the yoke of landlords and capitalists."* |
| **Universal anti-imperialism** | *"The struggle in India will continue, so long as a handful of exploiters continue to exploit labour of common people...It matters little whether these exploiters are British capitalists, British and Indian capitalists in alliance, or even purely Indians."* |
| **Definition of socialism** | Scientifically defined as **abolition of capitalism and class domination** |
| **Secularism** | Two of the six rules of the Punjab Naujawan Bharat Sabha: (1) members would have nothing to do with communal bodies; (2) they would propagate a **general feeling of tolerance**, considering religion a matter of **personal belief** |
| **Mental freedom** | *"To be a revolutionary, one required immense moral strength, but one also required criticism and independent thinking"* — need to free people from mental bondage of religion and superstition |

## 🌍 Modern Context — Revolutionary Ideas and Their Legacy

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. Bhagat Singh's Secular Legacy in Contemporary Discourse
- Bhagat Singh's insistence that religion is a **personal matter** and his opposition to **communal bodies** resonates deeply in contemporary India.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Supreme Court's judgment in the *Preamble case* (2024)** (challenges to the "secular" and "socialist" words in the Preamble, added by the 42nd Amendment) reiterated these as foundational values — precisely the values Bhagat Singh articulated almost 100 years ago.

### 2. Trade Unionism Today
- The AITUC (founded 1920) is still active. India today has **12 Central Trade Union Organisations (CTUOs)** recognised by the government.
- `[Modern Example]` In **2020**, a massive **All-India Strike** was called by trade unions against the government's labour reform — the **consolidation of 44 labour laws into 4 Labour Codes** — echoing the same debates about workers' rights that drove the 1920s trade union movement. As of 2024, the Labour Codes have not been fully implemented.

### 3. Peasant Agitations — An Unbroken Thread
- The peasant agitations of the 1920s (tenancy laws, rent relief, debt) find a direct modern parallel in the **Farmers' Protest (2020–21)**.
- `[Modern Example]` The demand for a **legal guarantee for Minimum Support Price (MSP)** — raised prominently in the **Farmers' Protest of 2024 (February–March 2024)** at Shambhu and Khanauri borders — is essentially a continuation of the same struggle for economic security for the rural poor that Vallabhbhai Patel championed in the **Bardoli Satyagraha of 1928**.

### 4. Caste Movements — Periyar's Legacy
- `[Modern Example]` The **Self-Respect Movement** founded by **Periyar in 1925** remains politically significant. The **Dravidam** (Dravidian) political tradition it inspired continues to dominate Tamil Nadu politics — the **DMK government (2021–present)** under **M.K. Stalin** frequently invokes Periyar's rationalist, anti-caste legacy.
- The demand for **caste census**, which gained national prominence in **2023–24** (Bihar caste survey, demands for national caste census), traces its intellectual roots directly to the caste movements of the 1920s that Ambedkar, Periyar, and others led.

### 5. Shaheed Diwas — Remembering the Revolutionaries
- `[Modern Example]` Every year on **March 23**, India observes **Shaheed Diwas** in memory of **Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev**, and **Rajguru**.
- In **2021**, on the 90th anniversary of their martyrdom, a special commemorative postal stamp was released by the Government of India.
- The **Bhagat Singh birthplace memorial at Khatkar Kalan, Punjab** was developed as a national memorial — inaugurated in **2022** by the Punjab government.
