# Chapter 1: Sources for the History of Modern India
## 📚 Study Notes for UPSC

## 📋 Chapter at a Glance

**5 Key Takeaways:**
1. **Archives** (government records & documents) are the most important primary sources for studying Modern India (1750-1950)
2. **Official records** of East India Company and British Crown provide detailed documentation of colonial administration
3. **Newspapers & journals** from 19th-20th centuries offer valuable insights into Indian society and freedom struggle
4. **Travel accounts, biographies, and memoirs** supplement official records with personal perspectives
5. **Creative literature, paintings, and oral history** help understand social conditions and cultural changes during colonial period

---

## 1. Understanding Historical Sources

### What Are Archives?
**Archives** are collections of historical records and documents—specifically **primary source documents** (documents created during the actual events, not written later).

**Key Features:**
- Created as part of actual activities (administrative, legal, social, or commercial)
- Original/unique documents
- Not written intentionally for future generations
- Most reliable source for historical research

**Example:** A letter written by Lord Dalhousie to the Court of Directors in 1850 is a primary source. A history textbook written in 2020 about Dalhousie is a secondary source.

## 2. Official Archives & Records

### A. Central Government Archives

#### 📍 National Archives of India (New Delhi)
The **primary repository** (main storage location) of Government of India records.

**What It Contains:**

**1. East India Company Records (1600-1857)**
- Detailed trading accounts
- Division by departments:
  - **Public/General Department**: General administration
  - **Revenue Department**: Tax collection, land revenue
  - **Political Department**: Relations with Indian states
  - **Military Department**: Army matters
  - **Secret Department**: Confidential matters
  - **Commercial Department**: Trade & business
  - **Judicial Department**: Legal matters
  - **Education Department**: Educational policies

**2. British Crown Records (1858-1947)**
- Large volume of administrative records
- Help trace decision-making processes
- Reveal psychology of British policymakers

**3. Survey of India Records (Started 1767)**
- **James Rennell** appointed as first Surveyor General of Bengal (1767)
- Scientific mapping of unknown regions
- Provides information on:
  - Geography
  - Contemporary socio-economic conditions
  - Border regions

**4. Department-Specific Records:**

| Department | What You'll Find |
|------------|------------------|
| **Public & Judicial Departments** | Social and religious policies of British |
| **Legislative Department** | Law-making process, Acts passed |
| **Educational Records** | Growth of education system under colonial rule |
| **Home Department - Public Series** | General administrative matters |
| **Home Political Series (1907 onwards)** | Political movements & communal issues |
| **Reforms Office** | Constitutional developments (1920-1937) |

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Example]** Just like the National Archives preserve colonial-era records, the **National Archives of India** today digitizes historical documents under the **Digital India** initiative. In 2021, the Archives launched an online portal making over 10 million pages accessible for research. This mirrors the archival function but uses modern technology for preservation and access.

**[Current Scheme]** The **Central Secretariat Library** (under Ministry of Culture) now works with National Archives to digitize and preserve government records, similar to how British kept meticulous records during colonial times.

**Three Categories of Records:**

#### 1. Former British Indian Provinces
Records from provinces like Bengal, Madras, Bombay under direct British rule.

#### 2. Erstwhile Princely States
Records from kingdoms that joined India after 1947.

**Major Collections:**

| Region | Important Archives | Period Covered |

| **Madhya Pradesh** | Gwalior, Indore, Bhopal, Rewa archives | Princely state period |
| **Kerala** | Travancore and Cochin archives | Princely state period |
| **Karnataka** | Mysore archives | Princely state period |
| **Maharashtra** | Kolhapur archives | Princely state period |

#### 3. Foreign Administrations (Non-British)
- Portuguese, Dutch, French, Danish records
- Located in various Indian cities

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Example]** The **Rajasthan State Archives** in 2023 launched a digitization project preserving 2 million documents from princely states. This helps researchers understand pre-Independence governance models.

**[Recent Development]** The Ministry of Culture's **Scheme for Safeguarding the Intangible Cultural Heritage** (ongoing) also covers preservation of historical records from former princely states, recognizing their cultural value.

### C. Archives of Three Presidencies

#### 1. Bengal Presidency (Fort William)
- **Early records lost** in sack of Calcutta (1756)
- **Post-Plassey records (after 1757)** survived completely
- Location: Partly in National Archives of India, partly in State Archives of West Bengal

#### 2. Madras Presidency (Fort St. George)
- **Records begin from 1670**
- **Valuable Information on:**
  - Rise of English East India Company as political power in South India
  - Anglo-French struggle (Carnatic Wars)
  - English conflicts with Indian powers

#### 3. Bombay Presidency
- **Location:** Maharashtra Secretariat Record Office, Mumbai
- **Useful for studying:**
  - Western India history
  - Maharashtra, Gujarat, Sindh
  - Kannada-speaking districts (later merged with Mysore in 1956)

---

### D. Archives of Other European Powers

#### 1. Portuguese Archives (Goa)
- **Period:** 1700-1900 mainly
- **Contents:**
  - Orders from Lisbon to Goa
  - Reports from India to Portugal
  - History of Portuguese possessions in India

#### 2. Dutch Archives
- **Cochin & Malabar records:** Madras Record Office
- **Chinsura records:** West Bengal State Archives

#### 3. French Archives
- **Original location:** Chandernagore (now Chandannagar) and Pondicherry (now Puducherry)
- **Current location:** Transferred to Paris by French authorities before they left India
- **Contents:** French colonial administration records

#### 4. Danish Archives
- **Danish possessions:** Tranquebar and Serampore
- **Transferred to:** Copenhagen when Danes sold these territories to English East India Company (1845)
- **Records in India:** Tranquebar records (1777-1845) in Madras Record Office

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Example]** The **Archaeological Survey of India (ASI)** under Ministry of Culture collaborates with European institutions to digitally share colonial-era records. In 2022, a MoU with Portugal facilitated exchange of digitized Portuguese records from Goa's colonial period.

### E. Judicial Records

**Location & Contents:**

| Court | Location | Period | Special Features |
|-------|----------|--------|------------------|
| **Mayor's Court, Fort St. George** | Madras Record Office | From 1689 | Earliest available judicial archives |
| **Mayor's Court, Fort William** | Calcutta High Court Record Room | 1757-1773 (pre-Plassey lost) | Post-Plassey Bengal records |
| **Supreme Court of Bengal** | Calcutta High Court | 1774-1861 | Important legal precedents |
| **Mayor's Court, Bombay** | Maharashtra Secretariat Record Office | From 1728 | Western India legal history |
| **Recorder's Court & Supreme Court, Bombay** | Maharashtra Secretariat Record Office | Various periods | Complete legal proceedings |

**What These Records Contain:**
- Court proceedings and minutes
- **Wills** (documents expressing how property should be distributed after death)
- **Probates** (legal proof that a will is valid)
- **Letters of administration** (authority to manage deceased's estate)
- **Useful for:**
  - Genealogical studies (family history)
  - Understanding social structure
  - Economic conditions of respective regions

### F. Published Archives

#### Parliamentary Papers (British Parliament)

**Contents:**
- Excerpts from East India Company records
- Government of India records under Crown
- Reports of Parliamentary Select Committees
- Royal Commission Reports on:
  - Education
  - Civil reforms
  - Famines
- Parliamentary debates on Indian empire

**Why Important:** These show how British Parliament viewed and controlled India from London.

#### Other Published Records:
- **Proceedings of Indian & Provincial Legislatures**
- **Weekly Gazettes** (official newsletters) from central & provincial governments
- **Collections of laws and regulations**

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Example]** Similar to Parliamentary Papers, today the **Lok Sabha Debates** and **Rajya Sabha Debates** are published and archived digitally. The **Parliament Library** maintains complete records accessible to researchers.

**[Current Practice]** The **Gazette of India** continues the tradition of official government publications, now available digitally on eGazette portal (launched 2016).

## 3. Private Archives

**Definition:** Papers and documents of **individuals and families** who played significant roles in modern India's development.

### Major Collections:

#### 1. Nehru Memorial Museum and Library (NMML), New Delhi
**Houses:**
- Papers of eminent nationalist leaders
- Records of **Indian National Congress**
- Personal correspondence of freedom fighters
- Organization documents from independence movement

**Why Important:** Provides perspective of Indian nationalists, not just British administrators.

#### 2. Business Archives
**Collections from:**
- Banks
- Business houses (e.g., Tata, Birla family archives)
- Chambers of Commerce

**Useful for:** Studying economic changes, industrialization, Indian capitalism development

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Institution]** The **NMML** was renamed **Pradhanmantri Sangrahalaya** (Prime Ministers' Museum) in 2022, expanding its scope to cover all Prime Ministers of India while continuing to preserve freedom struggle archives.

**[Recent Development]** In 2021, the Ministry of Culture launched the **Unsung Heroes of Freedom Struggle** project, digitizing personal archives of lesser-known freedom fighters from private collections.

**Historical material related to India stored abroad.**

#### 3. Church Missionary Society Archives, London
**Provides insights on:**
- Educational development in colonial India

### B. France

**Archives Nationale, Paris** and ministries of **Foreign Affairs, Colonies, and War**
- French possession history
- Socio-political conditions in India
- French perspective on Indian events

### C. Netherlands

**Rijksarchief, The Hague**
- Dutch East India Company records
- Dutch colonial activities in India

### D. Denmark & Portugal

**Copenhagen** (Danish archives) and **Lisbon** (Portuguese archives)
- Records of Danish and Portuguese possessions
- Trade and colonial administration

### E. Pakistan

**Extremely Important for Indian History:**

| Location | What It Contains |
|----------|------------------|
| **West Pakistan Record Office, Lahore** | Punjab region history |
| **Record Office, Peshawar** | Northwest frontier history |
| **Sind Records** | Sindh region history |

**Value:**

#### 🌍 Modern Context

**[Recent Initiative]** The **Ministry of External Affairs** in 2023 initiated discussions with Pakistan for exchange of digitized historical records from pre-Partition era for academic research.

## 5. Biographies, Memoirs & Travel Accounts

### Why These Sources Matter:
- Provide **personal perspectives** missing in official records
- Describe **everyday life and social conditions**
- **Supplement official records** with ground reality
- Generally reliable but may have personal biases

### Types of Writers:

#### 1. Missionaries
**Motivation:** Encouraging their societies to send more missionaries to India

**Famous Works:**
- **Bishop Heber's Journal**
  - Socio-economic life during decline of Indian powers
  - Rise of British power
- **Abbe Dubois's "Hindu Manners and Customs"**
  - Detailed account of Hindu society and customs

#### 2. British Travellers

| Traveller | Famous Work | Subject |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **George Forster** | Travel accounts | Various regions |
| **Benjamin Heyne** | Travel accounts | South India |
| **James Burnes** | "Narrative of a Visit to the Court of Sinde" | Sindh region |
| **Alexander Burnes** | "Travels Into Bokhara" | Central Asia & Northwest India |
| **C.J.C. Davidson** | "Diary of Travels and Adventures in Upper India" | Northern India |
| **John Butler** | "Travels and Adventures in the Province of Assam" | Northeast India |

#### 3. Non-British Travellers

| Traveller | Nationality | Famous Work | Time Period |
|-----------|------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Victor Jacquemont** | French | "Letters from India: Journey in British Dominions, Tibet, Lahore, and Kashmir" | 1828-1831 |
| **Baron Charles Hugel** | Austrian | "Travels in Kashmir and the Punjab" | Early 19th century |
| **William Moorcroft** | British (but independent traveler) | Travel accounts | Early 19th century |

### Value of Travel Accounts:
✅ Indispensable sources for modern Indian history
✅ Generally reliable
✅ Provide context missing from official documents
✅ Describe ordinary people's lives
✅ Record social customs and economic conditions

⚠️ **Limitation:** May reflect personal biases and cultural prejudices of writers

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Example]** Similar to how travel accounts provided outsider perspectives on India, modern travel literature and blogs by international visitors continue this tradition. The **Ministry of Tourism's** "Incredible India" campaign (ongoing since 2002) encourages documentation of India through visitors' eyes.

**[Academic Development]** Universities now offer courses in **Travel Writing** and **Colonial Literature**, analyzing how these historical accounts shaped Western perceptions of India.

## 6. Newspapers and Journals

### Historical Development

#### Early Period (1770s-1780s)

**First Attempts:**
- **William Bolts (1776)**: Disgruntled EIC employee tried to publish newspaper
  - Wanted to expose malpractices in private trade
  - Plan failed due to strong official opposition

**First Successful Newspaper:**
- **1780: "The Bengal Gazette" or "Calcutta General Advertiser"**
  - Publisher: **James Augustus Hickey**
  - **Fate:** Press seized within 2 years (1782)
  - **Reason:** Outspoken criticism of government officials

**Early Newspapers:**
- **The Calcutta Gazette** (1784)
- **The Madras Courier** (1788)
- **The Bombay Herald** (1789)

**Target Audience:** Europeans and Anglo-Indians (people of mixed British-Indian ancestry)
**Content:** Intellectual entertainment, not political commentary

---

#### Growth Period (Late 19th Century)

**Revolutionary Change:** Indians started publishing newspapers with nationalist perspectives.

**Amazing Fact:** Nearly **one-third of founding fathers of Indian National Congress (1885) were journalists!**

### Value as Historical Sources

**What Newspapers Provide:**

**1. From 1870s onwards:**
- Almost all aspects of colonial life
- Social conditions
- Economic changes
- Political developments
- Cultural movements

**2. From 1920s onwards:**
- Detailed coverage of freedom struggle
- Major events tracked day-by-day
- Grassroots perspectives on national movements

### ⚠️ Important Limitation: Bias

**Not Unprejudiced or Completely Objective**

**Example:**
- **London pro-British newspaper:** Portrayed 1857 Revolt as "Sepoy Mutiny" by disloyal soldiers
- **Indian nationalist newspaper:** Portrayed same event as "First War of Independence" by freedom fighters

**Historian's Task:** Compare different newspaper accounts to get balanced picture

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Example]** The **Press Council of India** (established 1966, re-established 1978) continues regulating and maintaining journalistic ethics, recognizing newspapers' role in democracy—similar to how newspapers were crucial during freedom struggle.

**[Current Data]** According to the **Registrar of Newspapers for India (RNI)**, as of 2022, India has over **1.35 lakh registered publications** in 22 scheduled languages, making it one of the world's largest newspaper markets.

**[Digital Transformation]** The **Ministry of I&B's** digital archives project (2020 onwards) is digitizing historical newspapers from colonial period, making them accessible online—similar to how these newspapers originally democratized information.

**[Recent SC Judgment]** The Supreme Court in **Romila Thapar vs Union of India (2018)** emphasized the importance of press freedom, echoing how nationalist newspapers fought British censorship.

## 7. Oral Evidence

### What is Oral History?
**Definition:** Construction of history using **non-written sources**, primarily **personal reminiscences** (memories shared by people who witnessed events).

### Advantages:
✅ **Broadens boundaries** of historical discipline
✅ **Corroborates** (confirms) findings from written sources
✅ **Fills gaps** where written records don't exist
✅ **Gives voice** to common people whose experiences weren't documented
✅ **Provides personal perspectives** on major events

### Limitations:
⚠️ **Veracity** (truthfulness) questioned by many historians
⚠️ Memory can be **unreliable or selective**
⚠️ **Personal biases** may color recollections
⚠️ **Difficult to verify** unlike written documents

**Example:** Interview with a freedom fighter who participated in Quit India Movement (1942) is oral history. Their account adds personal dimension but may not remember exact dates or may exaggerate their role.

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Initiative]** The **Oral History Association of India** (established 2009) promotes oral history research. The **Ministry of Culture** supports oral history projects documenting freedom struggle participants' memories before they're lost.

**[Recent Project]** The **National Council of Educational Research and Training (NCERT)** in 2019 launched an oral history project to record memories of people who witnessed Partition (1947), recognizing that written records don't capture human suffering adequately.

**[Technology Application]** Organizations now use **audio-visual recording** and **AI transcription** to preserve oral testimonies, making this source more reliable and accessible than before.

## 8. Creative Literature

### The Indo-European Contact & Novel Genre

**Historical Context:** The **novel** (as a literary form) emerged in India in the **latter half of 19th century** as a result of Indo-European contact.

**Why Important for History:**
- Novels provide **social commentary**
- Depict **real social conditions** of the time
- Show lives of **marginalized & oppressed** sections
- Reflect **cultural changes** during colonial period

---

### Major Writers & Their Works

#### A. Bengali Literature

**Bankim Chandra Chatterjee (1838-1894)**
- **First important novelist** of modern India
- **Genre:** Mostly historical novels

**Famous Works:**

| Novel | Year | Significance |
|-------|------|--------------|
| **Anandamath** | 1882 | • Contains powerful lyric **"Vandemataram"** (later became nationalist song)<br>• Depicts **Sanyasi Revolt** (1760s)<br>• Inspired nationalist movement |
| **Rajasimha** | Late career | Considered grand finale to his career |

**Historical Value:** Shows how Bengalis viewed historical events; "Vandemataram" became rallying cry for freedom movement.

---

#### B. Gujarati Literature

**Iccharam Suryaram Desai (1853-1912)**
- Fine scholar of medieval Gujarati literary history

**Famous Work:**
- **"Hind ane Britannia"**
  - One of **earliest Indian novels with political overtones**
  - Addressed British-Indian relationship

---

#### C. Tamil Literature

**Women Writers Breaking Barriers:**

| Writer | Novel | Year | Theme |
|--------|-------|------|-------|
| **Girija Devi** | Mohana Rajani | 1931 | Social experiences |
| **Ramatirthammal** | Dasikalin Mosavalai | 1936 | Social issues |

**Significance:** Made novel an effective vehicle for social commentary; women writers addressing societal issues was progressive for that era.

---

#### D. Telugu Literature

**G.V. Krishna Rao**
- **Novel:** "Keelubommalu" (The Puppets)
- **Year:** 1956
- **Focus:** Moral aspects and behavior of **rural people**
- **Historical Value:** Provides insight into village life and rural social structure

---

#### E. Malayalam Literature

**1. Vaikom Muhammad Basheer (1910-1994)**
- One of most eminent Malayalam writers

**Famous Work:**
- **"Balyakalasakhi" (The Childhood Friends)**
  - **Year:** 1944
  - **Genre:** Tragic love story
  - **Theme:** Inter-community relationships, social barriers

**2. Thakazhi Sivasankara Pillai**
- Prominent for realistic portrayal of society

**Famous Works:**

| Novel | Translation | Year | Theme |
|-------|-------------|------|-------|
| **Tottiyude Makan** | Son of a Scavenger | 1948 | **Caste discrimination**, life of lower castes |
| **Chemmeen** | Shrimps | 1956 | Lives of **fishing community** |

---

### Common Themes Across All Regional Literature:

Despite different educational backgrounds and regional contexts, these writers shared:

✅ **Strong sense of REALISM** (depicting actual conditions, not idealized versions)
✅ **Deep interest in MARGINALIZED sections** of society
✅ **Focus on OPPRESSED communities** (lower castes, women, poor)
✅ **Social commentary** through storytelling

### Historical Value:
These novels are like **windows into social milieu** (social environment) of their times:
- How people lived
- Social hierarchies
- Economic conditions
- Cultural practices
- Impact of colonial rule on society

#### 🌍 Modern Context
**[Modern Recognition]** The **Sahitya Akademi** (National Academy of Letters, established 1954) now recognizes and promotes literature in all Indian languages, continuing the tradition of regional literature documenting social realities.

**[Recent Development]** The **Prime Minister's Museum & Library** in 2022 created a special collection of colonial-era literature, recognizing these novels as historical sources, not just literary works.

**[Current Trend]** Contemporary Indian literature in regional languages continues addressing social issues. Writers like **Perumal Murugan** (Tamil), **Bama** (Dalit Tamil writer), and others carry forward this tradition of literature as social documentation.

**[Government Scheme]** The **National Book Trust's** translation schemes promote regional literature nationally, ensuring these historical perspectives reach wider audiences.

## 9. Paintings as Historical Sources

### Types of Paintings & Their Historical Value

---

#### A. Company Paintings (Also Called "Patna Kalam")

**What Are They?**
- Paintings created under **patronage** (financial support) of **East India Company**
- **Genre:** Realistic documentation of Indian life

**Also Known As:** "Patna Kalam" (Patna Style)

**What They Show:**
- **People** as they actually existed
- **Scenes** from daily life
- **Trades** and occupations
- **Festivals** and celebrations
- **Dances** and performing arts
- **Attire** (clothing) of different communities

**Time Period:** Popular throughout 19th century

**End:** Introduction of **photography in India (1840s)** gradually replaced these paintings

**Historical Value:**
- Visual documentation of social life
- Economic activities
- Cultural practices
- How different communities dressed and lived

---

#### B. Paintings of 1857 Revolt

**Historical Context:** The **Great Revolt of 1857** produced numerous pictorial images by both British and Indians.

**Types:** 
- Paintings
- Pencil drawings
- Etchings
- Posters
- Cartoons
- Bazaar prints (cheap popular prints)

---

##### British Perspective Paintings:

**Purpose:** Provoke emotions supporting British narrative

**1. "Relief of Lucknow" by Thomas Jones Barker (1859)**
- **Theme:** Commemorates British heroes who "saved" the English
- **Message:** British as saviors and heroes
- **Depicts:** British forces rescuing besieged British civilians
- **Historical Significance:** Shows how British wanted event to be remembered

**2. "In Memoriam" by Joseph Noel Paton**
- **Theme:** Portrays British as victims
- **Depicts:** English women and children huddled together, looking helpless and innocent
- **Message:** Waiting for "inevitable dishonour, violence, and death"
- **Purpose:** Generate sympathy for British, justify brutal suppression of revolt
- **Historical Significance:** Reveals British propaganda methods

---

##### Indian Perspective:
While fewer paintings from Indian side survived, bazaar prints and folk art showed Indian heroes and different narrative of events.

**Historical Value of 1857 Paintings:**
- Understand **different worldviews** of British vs Indians
- See how **same events interpreted differently**
- Study **propaganda techniques** used by colonial power
- Recognize **visual media as political tool**

---

#### C. Kalighat Paintings (19th Century, Calcutta)

**Origin:** Emerged in Calcutta (now Kolkata) in 19th century near Kalighat Temple

**Subjects:**

**1. Traditional Themes:**
- Mythological figures
- Gods and goddesses

**2. Contemporary Life (More Important Historically):**
- **Ordinary people** in everyday activities
- **Social changes** in Calcutta
- **Social commentary** on contemporary issues

**Unique Feature:** **Satirical paintings** (paintings that mock or criticize)
- Commented on **social evils**
- **Satirized** (mocked) certain behaviors and fashions adopted by people
- Showed impact of **Westernization** on Bengali society

**Historical Value:**
- Visual documentation of **urban social changes**
- Impact of **colonial modernity** on Indian society
- **Social criticism** through art
- Everyday life of common people in Calcutta

---

#### D. Nationalist Art Movement (Late 19th Century)

**Historical Context:** New art movement emerged, receiving stimulus from **growing nationalism** in India.

---

##### Key Artists:

**1. Raja Ravi Varma (1848-1906)**
- Pioneer of modern Indian art
- Combined European academic art techniques with Indian subjects
- Made mythological characters look realistic
- Made art accessible through **oleographs** (affordable prints)

**2. Nandalal Bose**
- Associated with Bengal School
- Nationalist themes
- Drew inspiration from Indian artistic traditions

---

##### The Bengal School Movement:

**Led By:** **Abanindranath Tagore** (nephew of Rabindranath Tagore)

**Key Supporters:**

| Name | Role | Contribution |
|------|------|--------------|
| **E.B. Havell** | Principal, Calcutta Art School | Promoted Indian art traditions over Western styles |
| **Ananda Kentish Coomaraswamy** | Art historian & philosopher (son of Tamil political leader in Sri Lanka) | Theorized Indian aesthetics; promoted Indian art internationally |

**Philosophy:** 
- **Rejection** of Western art dominance
- **Revival** of Indian artistic traditions
- Drawing inspiration from **Ajanta murals**, **Mughal miniatures**, and other Indian art forms

**Primary Themes:**
- Indian mythology
- Cultural heritage
- Nationalist symbols
- Spiritual subjects

**Historical Limitation:**
- Focused mainly on mythology and heritage
- Less documentation of contemporary social conditions
- But important for understanding **cultural nationalism**

**Historical Value:**
- Shows connection between **art and nationalism**
- **Cultural resistance** to colonial dominance
- Development of **modern Indian art movement**
- Important for **art historians** and understanding **cultural history**

---

#### 🌍 Modern Context

**[Modern Institution]** The **National Gallery of Modern Art (NGMA)** in New Delhi, Mumbai, and Bengaluru preserves colonial-era paintings. The **Ministry of Culture** allocated ₹250 crores in 2021-22 for NGMA expansion and collection preservation.

**[Recent Exhibition]** In 2022, NGMA organized "Company School to Independence" exhibition, showcasing how paintings documented India's transformation from colonial subject to independent nation.
