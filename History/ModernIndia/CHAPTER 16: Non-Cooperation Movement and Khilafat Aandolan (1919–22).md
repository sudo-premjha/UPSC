# CHAPTER 16: Non-Cooperation Movement and Khilafat Aandolan (1919–22)

---

## 🔍 Introduction: Two Movements, One Programme

During **1919–22**, the British faced resistance from two powerful mass movements: the **Khilafat Movement** and the **Non-Cooperation Movement**.

These two movements arose from *separate* causes:
- The **Khilafat issue** was about the treatment of Turkey and its Muslim Caliph (spiritual leader) by the British after World War I.
- The **Non-Cooperation Movement** was about India's broader political grievances — Punjab atrocities, failed reforms, and the demand for self-rule.

Yet, both adopted a **common programme of action: non-violent non-cooperation** with the British government.

The Khilafat issue, though not directly linked to Indian politics, provided a **golden bridge** for **Hindu-Muslim unity** against the British — a unity that was rare and historically significant.

---

## 📜 Part 1: The Background — Why Was Everyone Angry in 1919?

The post-World War I period shattered Indian hopes that Britain would reward their wartime loyalty with greater freedom. Instead, 1919 brought a series of events that deepened resentment across *all* sections of Indian society.

### Economic Grievances
- **Rise in prices** of essential commodities.
- **Decrease in production** of Indian industries.
- **Increase in the burden** of taxes and rents.
- Almost all sections of society — rich and poor — suffered economic hardship *because of* the war, which strengthened anti-British feeling.

### Political Atrocities
- The **Rowlatt Act** (a repressive law allowing detention without trial) was imposed.
- **Martial law** was declared in Punjab.
- The **Jallianwala Bagh massacre** exposed the brutal, uncivilised face of foreign rule.

### The Whitewash on Punjab
- The **Hunter Committee** (set up to investigate Punjab atrocities) proved to be an eyewash — it failed to deliver justice.
- Worse, the **House of Lords** (British Parliament's upper house) *endorsed* General Dyer's action.
- The British public even showed solidarity with Dyer — **The Morning Post** collected **£30,000** for him, proving where British sympathies truly lay.

### Failed Reforms
- The **Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms** with their ill-conceived scheme of **dyarchy** (a system of dual governance — partly Indian, partly British controlled) failed to satisfy India's rising demand for self-government.

---

## 🤝 Setting the Stage for Hindu-Muslim Unity

Even before the Khilafat issue emerged, the ground for joint Hindu-Muslim political action was being prepared:

| Event/Factor | Significance |
|---|---|
| **Lucknow Pact (1916)** | Stimulated Congress-Muslim League cooperation |
| **Rowlatt Act Agitation** | Brought Hindus, Muslims, and other sections together |
| **Rise of Radical Muslim Leaders** | **Muhammad Ali, Abul Kalam Azad, Hakim Ajmal Khan, Hasan Imam** became more influential than the conservative Aligarh school |

The younger, radical Muslim leaders advocated **militant nationalism** and active participation in the freedom struggle. They carried **strong anti-imperialist sentiments** — a sharp break from the older, loyalist Aligarh school tradition.

---

## ☪️ Part 2: The Khilafat Issue — What Was It About?

### Background of the Khilafat Issue
The **Khalifa** (Caliph) was the spiritual leader of Muslims worldwide. Muslims in India, like Muslims globally, regarded the **Sultan of Turkey** as the Khalifa.

- During **World War I**, Turkey had allied with Germany and Austria — against the British.
- After the war ended, Britain took a **stern and punitive attitude towards Turkey**:
  - Turkey was **dismembered** (broken up territorially).
  - The **Khalifa was removed from power**.
- This deeply **incensed Muslims across the world**, including in India.

### What Did Indian Muslims Demand from the British?
1. That the **Khalifa's control over Muslim sacred places** should be retained.
2. That the **Khalifa should be left with sufficient territory** after post-war territorial arrangements.

### Formation of the Khilafat Committee (Early 1919)
A **Khilafat Committee** was formed to force the British government to change its attitude towards Turkey. Its key leaders were:
- **Shaukat Ali and Muhammad Ali** (the Ali brothers)
- **Maulana Azad**
- **Ajmal Khan**
- **Hasrat Mohani**

This committee laid the groundwork for a country-wide agitation.

---

## 🗓️ Part 3: Development of the Khilafat-Non-Cooperation Programme

Initially, the Khilafat leaders limited themselves to **meetings, petitions, and deputations**.

However, a **militant trend** soon emerged, demanding active agitation — specifically, **stopping all cooperation with the British**.

### Key Milestones

**November 1919 — All India Khilafat Conference, Delhi:**
- A call was made for the **boycott of British goods**.
- Leaders warned that unless post-war peace terms were favourable to Turkey, they would **stop all cooperation with the Government**.
- **Gandhi**, who was the **President of the All India Khilafat Committee**, recognised this as a platform from which a **mass, united non-cooperation** could be launched.

---

## 🏛️ Part 4: Congress's Stand on the Khilafat Question

Gandhi was in favour of launching **satyagraha** (truth-force, non-violent resistance) and non-cooperation on the Khilafat issue. However, **the Congress was not united** on this.

### Opposition Within Congress
- **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** was opposed to:
  - Forming an alliance with Muslim leaders over a **religious issue**.
  - Using **satyagraha** as a political instrument (he was sceptical of its effectiveness).
- According to **Professor Ravinder Kumar**, Gandhi made a concerted bid to convince Tilak of both the virtue of satyagraha and the political wisdom of allying with Muslims on Khilafat.
- There was also opposition to specific parts of Gandhi's programme, such as the **boycott of councils**.

### Why Congress Eventually Agreed
Despite initial reservations, Congress came around and decided to support the non-cooperation programme on the Khilafat question for three main reasons:

- ✅ **Golden opportunity for Hindu-Muslim unity:** This was a chance to bring Muslim masses into the national movement. Different sections — Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, peasants, artisans, capitalists, tribals, women, students — could all fight for their own rights while realising that colonial rule was opposed to them all.
- ✅ **Loss of faith in constitutional methods:** Especially after the Punjab incidents and the partisan Hunter Committee Report, the Congress believed petitions and prayers were no longer enough.
- ✅ **Mass energy needed an outlet:** The Congress was fully aware that the masses were *eager* to express their discontent. This energy needed organised, disciplined direction.

> **Muslim League Support:** The Muslim League also decided to give **full support** to the Congress and its political agitation.

---

## 📅 Part 5: Timeline of the Movement — Key Events

### February 1920
- A **joint Hindu-Muslim deputation** was sent to the Viceroy to seek redress on the Khilafat issue.
- The mission **proved abortive** (failed completely).
- Gandhi announced he would lead a movement of non-cooperation if the peace treaty failed to satisfy Indian Muslims.

### May 1920
- The **Treaty of Sèvres** with Turkey was signed — it **completely dismembered Turkey**.
- This removed any last hope and made the movement inevitable.

### June 1920
- An **all-party conference at Allahabad** approved a programme of boycott of schools, colleges, and law courts.
- Gandhi was asked to lead it.

### August 31, 1920
- The **Khilafat Committee** started a campaign of non-cooperation.
- The movement was **formally launched**.
- *(Note: Tilak had breathed his last on **August 1, 1920**.)*

### September 1920 — Special Session, Calcutta
Congress approved a non-cooperation programme. It would continue until:
- Punjab wrongs were redressed.
- Khilafat wrongs were addressed.
- **Swaraj** (self-rule) was established.

**The Programme Included:**

| Phase | Actions |
|---|---|
| **First Phase** | Boycott of government schools and colleges |
| | Boycott of law courts; justice through **panchayats** instead |
| | Boycott of legislative councils *(C.R. Das disagreed but bowed to party discipline)* |
| | Boycott of foreign cloth; use of **khadi** (hand-spun cloth) instead; hand-spinning to be practised |
| | Renunciation of government honours and titles |
| **Second Phase** | Mass civil disobedience — including resignation from government service and **non-payment of taxes** |

Throughout the movement, participants were expected to:
- Work for **Hindu-Muslim unity**.
- Work for **removal of untouchability**.
- Remain **strictly non-violent** at all times.

---

### December 1920 — The Historic Nagpur Session of the INC

This session was a **turning point** in the history of the Indian National Congress. Four critical things happened:

**(i) Non-Cooperation Programme Endorsed.**

**(ii) Congress Creed Changed (Most Important):**
- **Old goal:** Attainment of self-government through *constitutional* means.
- **New goal:** Attainment of **swaraj** through *peaceful and legitimate* means.
- This meant the Congress now committed itself to an **extra-constitutional mass struggle** — a revolutionary shift.

**(iii) Major Organisational Reforms:**
- A **Congress Working Committee (CWC)** of **15 members** was set up to lead the Congress.
- Provincial Congress Committees were reorganised on **linguistic basis**.
- **Ward committees** were organised at the grassroots.
- Entry fee was reduced to **four annas** (making it accessible to the poor).

**(iv) Gandhi's Bold Promise:**
- Gandhi declared that if the non-cooperation programme was **implemented completely**, swaraj would be ushered in **within a year**.

### Leaders Who Left the Congress at This Stage
Some leaders who believed in *constitutional and lawful struggle* left the Congress:
- **Muhammad Ali Jinnah**
- **Annie Besant**
- **G.S. Kharpade**
- **B.C. Pal**

Others, like **Surendranath Banerjea**, founded the **Indian National Liberal Federation** and played only a minor role in national politics henceforward.

> Also note: Many **revolutionary terrorist groups from Bengal** pledged support to the Congress programme at this stage.

---

## 🌊 Part 6: Spread of the Movement — 1921–22

The adoption of the non-cooperation programme by Congress gave it enormous new energy. The years **1921 and 1922** saw an **unprecedented popular upsurge**.

### Gandhi & the Ali Brothers' Nationwide Tour
Gandhi and the Ali brothers undertook a nationwide tour, energising people across the country.

### Students
- Thousands of students **left government schools and colleges**.
- Around **800 national schools and colleges** were established.
- Key national institutions included:
  - **Jamia Millia** (at Aligarh)
  - **Kashi Vidyapeeth**
  - **Gujarat Vidyapeeth**
  - **Bihar Vidyapeeth**
- These were led by leaders like **Acharya Narendra Dev, C.R. Das, Lala Lajpat Rai, Zakir Hussain**, and **Subhash Bose** (who became the Principal of National College at Calcutta).

### Lawyers Who Gave Up Practice
Many prominent lawyers sacrificed their careers for the movement:
> **Motilal Nehru, Jawaharlal Nehru, C.R. Das, C. Rajagopalachari, Saifuddin Kitchlew, Vallabhbhai Patel, Asaf Ali, T. Prakasam, Rajendra Prasad**

### Economic Boycott
- **Heaps of foreign cloth were burnt publicly.**
- Imports of foreign cloth **fell by half**.
- **Picketing** of shops selling foreign liquor and toddy was undertaken at many places.
- The **Tilak Swaraj Fund** was oversubscribed — **one crore rupees** were collected.

### Congress Volunteer Corps
- Emerged as a **parallel police force**.
- In **July 1921**, the Ali brothers called on Muslims to **resign from the army** as it was "unreligious."
- They were **arrested in September 1921** for this.
- Gandhi echoed their call and asked local Congress committees to pass similar resolutions.

### Local Struggles and Strikes
The spirit of defiance spread and gave rise to several local movements:

| Region | Movement |
|---|---|
| Midnapore (Bengal) | **No-tax movement** against union board taxes |
| Guntur (Andhra) | No-tax movement |
| Assam | Strikes in **tea plantations, steamer services, and Assam-Bengal Railways** (led by **J.M. Sengupta**) |
| UP | **Awadh Kisan Movement** and **Eka Movement** |
| Malabar | **Mappila Revolt** |
| Punjab | **Sikh agitation** for removal of mahants (corrupt temple managers) |

### November 1921 — Prince of Wales Visit
The visit of the **Prince of Wales** to India was met with **strikes and demonstrations** across the country.

---

## 👥 Part 7: People's Response — Who Participated and How?

### Middle Class
- Led the movement initially.
- Later, showed increasing **reservations** about Gandhi's more radical programme.
- In cities like Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras (centres of elite politicians), the response to calls for resignation from government service and surrendering titles was **limited**.
- However, **newcomers to politics** — like **Rajendra Prasad** in Bihar and **Vallabhbhai Patel** in Gujarat — provided solid support. For leaders like them, non-cooperation was a viable, non-violent political alternative to terrorism.

### Business Class
- Supported the **economic boycott** because they had benefited from the emphasis on **swadeshi** (use of Indian-made goods).
- However, **big business remained sceptical** — they feared **labour unrest** in their own factories.

### Peasants
- Participation was **massive**.
- The Congress was against class war, but the masses broke this restraint.
- In Bihar, caste conflicts (lower castes taking up the sacred thread, challenging upper-caste authority) merged with the Non-Cooperation Movement.
- In general, peasants turned against both **landlords and traders**.
- The movement gave toiling masses a chance to express their real feelings — not just against the British, but also against their **Indian exploiters** (landlords and traders).

### Students
- Became active volunteers.
- Thousands left government institutions and joined newly opened national institutions like **Kashi Vidyapeeth, Gujarat Vidyapeeth**, and **Jamia Millia Islamia**.

### Women
- **Gave up purdah** (veil/seclusion).
- Offered their ornaments for the **Tilak Fund**.
- Joined the movement in large numbers.
- Actively participated in **picketing** before shops selling foreign cloth and liquor.

### Hindu-Muslim Unity — A Historic Achievement
- **Massive participation of Muslims** was one of the greatest achievements of the movement.
- Despite events like the **Moppila Uprising**, communal unity was broadly maintained.
- In many places, **two-thirds of those arrested were Muslims** — a level of participation neither seen before nor after.
- Gandhi addressed Muslim masses from **mosques**.
- Gandhi was even allowed to address **meetings of Muslim women** — where he was the **only male who was not blindfolded**.

---

## 🏛️ Part 8: Government Response

- Talks between **Gandhi and Reading (the Viceroy)** broke down in **May 1921**.
- The government wanted Gandhi to ask the Ali brothers to remove portions from their speeches that suggested violence.
- Gandhi saw through the strategy: **the government was trying to drive a wedge between him and the Khilafat leaders.** He refused.

**December 1921 — Government Crackdown:**
- Volunteer corps were declared **illegal**.
- Public meetings were **banned**.
- The press was **gagged**.
- **Most leaders, barring Gandhi**, were arrested.

---

## 🔚 Part 9: The Last Phase and Chauri Chaura

### Pressure on Gandhi to Launch Civil Disobedience
By late 1921, Gandhi was under **increasing pressure** from Congress rank and file to start the civil disobedience programme.

- The **Ahmedabad Session (1921)** (presided over by C.R. Das from jail; **Hakim Ajmal Khan** was acting president) appointed **Gandhi as the sole authority** on the issue.
- On **February 1, 1922**, Gandhi threatened to launch civil disobedience from **Bardoli (Gujarat)** unless:
  1. Political prisoners were released.
  2. Press controls were removed.

### 🔥 The Chauri Chaura Incident — February 5, 1922

**Chauri Chaura** is a small village in **Gorakhpur district, United Provinces** (present-day UP). Here is what happened:

1. A group of volunteers was campaigning against **liquor sale and high food prices**.
2. The **police beat up the leader** of this group.
3. They then **opened fire** on the crowd that came to protest in front of the police station.
4. The enraged crowd **torched the police station** with policemen inside.
5. Those who tried to flee were **hacked to death** and thrown back into the fire.
6. **Twenty-two policemen** were killed in the violence.

### Gandhi Withdraws the Movement
Gandhi, who was already unhappy with the **increasingly violent trend** of the movement, **immediately announced its withdrawal**.

The **Congress Working Committee** met at **Bardoli in February 1922** and resolved to:
- Stop all activity that led to breaking of the law.
- Focus on **constructive work** instead:
  - Popularisation of **khadi**
  - Setting up **national schools**
  - Campaigning for **temperance** (anti-liquor)
  - Promoting **Hindu-Muslim unity**
  - Fighting **untouchability**

### Reactions to the Withdrawal

> *"To sound the order of retreat just when public enthusiasm was reaching the boiling point was nothing short of a national calamity."*
> — **Subhas Chandra Bose**

> *"I would suffer every humiliation, every torture, absolute ostracism and death itself to prevent the movement from becoming violent."*
> — **M.K. Gandhi**, in *Young India*, February 16, 1922

> *(Marxist Interpretation):* "Gandhi and the Congress bigwigs sensed that a revolutionary mass movement was in the offing. They decided to take over the leadership to keep the movement a 'controlled' affair and 'within safe channels'. The movement was called off just when the masses seemed to be taking the initiative."

### Gandhi's Arrest — March 1922
- Gandhi was **arrested in March 1922** and sentenced to **six years in jail**.
- He made the occasion memorable with a magnificent court speech:

> *"I am here, therefore, to invite and submit cheerfully to the highest penalty that can be inflicted upon me for what in law is deliberate crime, and what appears to me to be the highest duty of a citizen."*

---

## 🤔 Part 10: Why Did Gandhi Withdraw the Movement?

There were multiple reasons, not just Chauri Chaura:

- **Non-violence not internalised:** Gandhi felt people had not fully understood or adopted non-violence. One incident like Chauri Chaura could trigger a generally violent movement.
- **Danger of violent movement:** A violent movement would give the colonial government the **perfect excuse** to use its full military might against the protestors.
- **Signs of fatigue:** The movement was showing signs of exhaustion — it is impossible to sustain any movement at a high pitch for very long.
- **Government's unwillingness to negotiate:** The government showed no interest in dialogue.
- **Khilafat issue dissolved:** In **November 1922**, the people of **Turkey** rose under **Mustafa Kamal Pasha** and deprived the Sultan of political power. **Turkey was made a secular state.** Extensive rights were granted to women, education was nationalised, and modern agriculture and industries were developed. In **1924, the caliphate was abolished.** This made the Khilafat question **completely irrelevant**.

---

## ⚖️ Part 11: Evaluation of the Khilafat Non-Cooperation Movement

### Positive Outcomes
- ✅ Brought the **urban Muslim population** into the national movement in a major way.
- ✅ **Politicised every stratum of the population** — artisans, peasants, students, urban poor, women, traders, etc. — reaching every nook and corner of the country.
- ✅ The mobilisation of millions of men and women imparted a **revolutionary character** to the national movement.
- ✅ **Shattered the myth of British invincibility.** Colonial rule rested on two myths:
  - **Myth 1:** British rule was *in the interest of Indians.* *(Demolished earlier by Moderate nationalists through economic critique.)*
  - **Myth 2:** British rule was *invincible.* *(Now challenged by satyagraha and mass struggle.)*
- ✅ The masses shed their **deep-seated fear** of colonial rule and its repressive machinery.

### Negative Outcomes / Critiques
- ❌ **Communalisation of politics:** While it brought Muslims into the movement, it also, to an extent, *communalised* national politics. National leaders failed to raise the **religious political consciousness of Muslims to a level of secular political consciousness**.

> **Critical View:** *"It (Khilafat movement) was a purely retrograde and reactionary movement, and more importantly for Indian nationalism, it was an intrinsically anti-nationalist movement pitting specifically Islamic interests against secular and non-Muslim interests."* — **Dr. Koenraad Elst**

## 🌍 Modern Context

`[Modern Example]` — *These connections have been added to relate historical concepts to present-day India.*

### 1. The Legacy of Non-Cooperation: Mass Movements Today
The Non-Cooperation Movement's spirit — of citizens withdrawing cooperation from the state as a form of protest — continues to echo in modern India:
- The **Farmers' Protest (2020–21)** at Delhi's borders was one of the largest sustained mass protests since Independence. Farmers boycotted government procurement, blocked highways, and demanded repeal of the three farm laws. Just as Gandhi's movement was eventually called off, the **three farm laws were repealed in November 2021** by the central government — showing that sustained, non-violent mass pressure can still work.

### 2. Khadi and Swadeshi — Still Relevant
- The Non-Cooperation Movement's emphasis on **khadi and swadeshi** finds a direct echo in the **"Vocal for Local"** campaign launched by PM Modi and the **Make in India** initiative.
- The **Khadi and Village Industries Commission (KVIC)** reported record khadi sales of **₹1.34 lakh crore** in 2022–23 — a significant increase from pre-2014 figures — reflecting renewed public interest in domestic products.
- `[Modern Example]` The **PLI (Production Linked Incentive) Scheme** is a modern-day policy instrument to reduce India's dependence on foreign goods, much like the swadeshi movement aimed to do.

### 3. Hindu-Muslim Unity — An Unfinished Project
- The Hindu-Muslim unity witnessed during 1920–22 remains one of the most studied and most debated periods of Indian communal history.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Supreme Court's Ayodhya Judgment (November 2019)** and subsequent construction of the **Ram Mandir** (inaugurated January 2024) are modern reminders that the project of communal harmony in India remains ongoing and complex.

### 4. The Question of Sedition and Protest
- Gandhi was tried under **sedition laws** in 1922.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Supreme Court of India** in **May 2022** (in *S.G. Vombatkere vs Union of India*) stayed the use of **Section 124A (Sedition) of the IPC**, noting its chilling effect on free speech. This mirrors the colonial-era debate about the state using legal tools to suppress legitimate protest. Note: The **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023** replaced the IPC, removing Section 124A (sedition) but introducing new provisions (Section 152) related to "acts endangering sovereignty."

### 5. Constructive Programme Lives On
- After withdrawing the movement, Gandhi focused on **constructive work** — khadi, education, temperance, removing untouchability.
- This philosophy of building from below finds resonance in schemes like the **Samagra Shiksha Abhiyan** (holistic school education), **Swachh Bharat Mission** (cleanliness), and **Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana** (financial inclusion) — all aimed at grassroots transformation rather than top-down governance alone.
