## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways to Remember Before You Read:**
>
> 1. **"Do or Die" — August 1942:** The **Quit India Movement** was the most radical mass struggle of the freedom movement — launched on **August 8–9, 1942** with the famous "Do or Die" call, it saw unprecedented spontaneous violence and the establishment of **parallel governments** even without its leaders.
> 2. **Leaderless but Unstoppable:** All top Congress leaders were arrested in the early hours of **August 9, 1942** — yet the movement spread wildly across India, proving how deep nationalism had penetrated ordinary people.
> 3. **Parallel Governments:** At **Ballia (UP), Tamluk (Bengal)**, and **Satara (Maharashtra)**, nationalists established actual functioning governments that replaced British administration for months.
> 4. **The INA — India's Armed Challenge:** Subhas Bose built the **Indian National Army** from Indian prisoners of war in Singapore — hoisted the national flag on Indian soil at **Moirang, Manipur (April 14, 1944)** — a direct armed challenge to British rule.
> 5. **Constitutional Deadlock Deepens:** The **CR Formula, Desai-Liaqat Pact**, and **Wavell Plan** — all failed to bridge the Congress-League divide, with Jinnah consistently demanding the Two-Nation Theory be accepted and Pakistan be explicitly conceded.

# CHAPTER 23: Quit India Movement, Demand for Pakistan, and the INA

---

## 🔍 Part 1: The Genesis of the Quit India Movement

### After Cripps' Departure

After Stafford Cripps left India empty-handed, **Gandhi framed a resolution calling for British withdrawal** from India and a non-violent non-cooperation movement against any Japanese invasion.

The **CWC meeting at Wardha on July 14, 1942** accepted the idea of launching a struggle.

---

### Why Start a Struggle NOW? — Five Reasons

**1. Failure of the Cripps Mission:**
- The Cripps Mission's failure to solve the constitutional deadlock exposed Britain's **unchanged attitude** on constitutional advance.
- Any more silence would amount to **accepting the British right to decide India's fate** without consulting Indians.

**2. Popular Discontent and Wartime Hardships:**
- **Rising prices** and shortage of rice, salt, and other essentials.
- **Commandeering of boats** in Bengal and Orissa (the British were taking civilian boats for military purposes — devastating the local economy).
- Fears of Britain following a **scorched earth policy** in Assam, Bengal, and Orissa against possible Japanese advance.

**3. Fear of British Collapse:**
- News of **British reverses in South-East Asia** and an imminent British collapse enhanced popular willingness to express discontent.
- Japanese troops were **approaching India's borders**.
- Popular faith in British stability was so low that **people were withdrawing deposits from banks and post offices**.

**4. British Racist Behaviour Exposed:**
- When the British evacuated from South-East Asia, they left their subjects to their fate.
- **Two roads were provided for refugees:** the **Black Road for Indian refugees** and the **White Road exclusively for European refugees** — a starkly racist arrangement.
- The rout of a **European power by an Asian power (Japan)** shattered white prestige.
- British behaviour towards Indian subjects in South-East Asia exposed the **racist attitude of the rulers**.

**5. Preparing the Masses:**
- The Congress leadership wanted to **condition the masses** for a possible Japanese invasion — to ensure Indians would resist Japan and not welcome them as liberators.

---

### 📜 The Quit India Resolution

- **July 1942:** The CWC met at Wardha and resolved to authorise Gandhi to take charge of a non-violent mass movement.
- The resolution was **proposed by Jawaharlal Nehru** and **seconded by Sardar Patel**.
- It was ratified at the **Congress meeting at Gowalia Tank, Bombay, on August 8, 1942**.

**The meeting resolved to:**
- ✅ Demand an **immediate end to British rule in India**.
- ✅ Declare free India's commitment to defend itself against **all types of Fascism and imperialism**.
- ✅ Form a **provisional Government of India** after British withdrawal.
- ✅ Sanction a **civil disobedience movement** against British rule.
- ✅ **Gandhi was named the leader** of the struggle.

---

### Gandhi's Instructions to Different Sections

Gandhi's special instructions were spelt out at the Gowalia Tank meeting (though not formally issued). They were directed at various sections of society:

| Section | Instruction |
|---|---|
| **Government servants** | Do not resign — but **declare your allegiance to the Congress** |
| **Soldiers** | Do not leave the army — but **do not fire on compatriots** |
| **Students** | If confident, **leave studies** |
| **Peasants (with anti-government zamindars)** | Pay **mutually agreed rent** |
| **Peasants (with pro-government zamindars)** | **Do not pay rent** |
| **Princes** | Support the masses and accept the **sovereignty of your people** |
| **Princely states' people** | Support the ruler **only if he is anti-government**; declare yourselves part of the Indian nation |

**Gandhi's Famous "Do or Die" Call:**

> *"Here is a mantra, a short one, that I give you. You may imprint it on your hearts and let every breath of yours give expression to it. The mantra is: 'Do or Die'. We shall either free India or die in the attempt; we shall not live to see the perpetuation of our slavery."*

> *"...though the need for non-violence was always reiterated, Gandhi's mantra of Do or Die represents the militant mood of Gandhi."* — **Sumit Sarkar**

## 🔥 Part 2: Spread of the Movement

### Arrests of All Leaders — August 9, 1942

Gandhi had been carefully building the tempo through:
- Individual civil disobedience
- Organisational revamping
- A consistent propaganda campaign

**But the government was in no mood to wait.**

In the **early hours of August 9, 1942**, in a **single sweep**, all top Congress leaders were arrested and taken to **unknown destinations**.

**Simultaneously:**
- The **Congress Working Committee, AICC**, and **Provincial Congress Committees** were declared **unlawful associations** under the **Criminal Law Amendment Act of 1908**.
- Assembly of public meetings was prohibited under **Rule 56 of the Defence of India Rules**.

**A Remarkable Consequence:**
- The removal of established leaders left **younger and militant elements to their own initiative**.
- **Aruna Asaf Ali** — till then relatively unknown — presided over the Congress committee session on **August 9** and **hoisted the flag** — becoming an icon of the movement.

---

### The Public on Rampage

With all leaders gone, the general public took matters into their own hands:

- 🚩 **National flags hoisted forcibly** on public buildings.
- 💣 **Bridges blown up**.
- 🛤️ **Railway tracks removed**.
- 📡 **Telegraph lines cut**.
- 🏛️ Symbols of authority were attacked throughout the country.
- Satyagrahis offered themselves up for arrest.

This activity was **most intense in eastern United Provinces and Bihar**.

**Students:**
- Went on strike in schools and colleges.
- Participated in processions.
- Wrote and distributed **illegal news sheets (patrikas)**.
- Acted as couriers for underground networks.

**Workers:**
- Went on strikes in **Ahmedabad, Bombay, Jamshedpur, Ahmednagar**, and **Poona**.

---

### Underground Activity

Many nationalists went underground and took to **subversive activities**. Participants included:
- Socialists
- Forward Bloc members
- Gandhi ashramites
- Revolutionary nationalists
- Local organisations in **Bombay, Poona, Satara, Baroda**, and other parts of **Gujarat, Karnataka, Kerala, Andhra, United Provinces, Bihar**, and **Delhi**

**Key Underground Leaders:**

> **Rammanohar Lohia, Jayaprakash Narayan, Aruna Asaf Ali, Usha Mehta, Biju Patnaik, Chhotubhai Puranik, Achyut Patwardhan, Sucheta Kripalani**, and **R.P. Goenka**

**Notable Achievement:**
- **Usha Mehta** started an **underground radio station in Bombay** — broadcasting nationalist news and maintaining morale.

**Purpose of Underground Activity:**
- Keep up popular morale.
- Provide a **line of command and guidance**.
- Distribute arms and ammunition.

---

### 🏛️ Parallel Governments — The Most Remarkable Feature

Nationalists established actual functioning **parallel governments** at several places:

**1. Ballia (United Provinces) — August 1942 (for one week)**
- Under **Chittu Pandey**.
- Got many **Congress leaders released** from jail.

**2. Tamluk (Midnapore, Bengal) — December 1942 to September 1944**
- Known as **Jatiya Sarkar** (National Government).
- Undertook **cyclone relief work**.
- Sanctioned **grants to schools**.
- Supplied **paddy from the rich to the poor**.
- Organised **Vidyut Vahinis** (volunteer corps).

**3. Satara (Maharashtra) — Mid-1943 to 1945**
- Named **"Prati Sarkar"** (Parallel Government).
- Organised under leaders like **Y.B. Chavan, Nana Patil**, etc.
- Set up **village libraries** and **Nyayadan Mandals** (people's courts).
- Ran **prohibition campaigns**.
- Organised **'Gandhi marriages'** (simple, inter-caste weddings).

**Support Provided by Various Groups:**

| Group | How They Helped |
|---|---|
| **Businessmen** | Donations, shelter, and material help |
| **Students** | Acting as couriers |
| **Simple villagers** | Refusing information to the authorities |
| **Pilots and train drivers** | Delivering bombs and other material |
| **Government officials (including police)** | Passing on secret information to activists |

---

## 👥 Part 3: Extent of Mass Participation

The Quit India Movement was remarkable for how broadly Indian society participated:

| Group | Nature of Participation |
|---|---|
| **Youth/Students** | In the forefront — strikes, processions, patrikas, couriers |
| **Women** | Actively participated — **Aruna Asaf Ali, Sucheta Kripalani, Usha Mehta** among the leaders |
| **Workers** | Went on strikes; faced repression |
| **Peasants (all strata)** | Heart of the movement; concentrated offensives on symbols of authority; **complete absence of anti-zamindar violence** |
| **Even some zamindars** | Participated |
| **Lower-level government officials** | Participated — eroding government loyalty from within |
| **Muslims** | Helped by giving shelter to underground activists; **no communal clashes** during the movement |

**Who Did NOT Join:**
- **Communists:** After Nazi Germany attacked Russia (where communists were in power), they began supporting the British war against Germany. The 'Imperialist War' became the 'People's War' for them.
- **Muslim League:** Feared that if the British left, minorities would be oppressed by Hindus.
- **Hindu Mahasabha:** Boycotted the movement.
- **Princely States:** Showed a low-key response.

---

## ⚔️ Part 4: Government Repression

Although **martial law was not formally applied**, repression was severe:

- Agitating crowds were **lathi-charged, tear-gassed**, and **fired upon**.
- Estimated **10,000 people killed**.
- The **press was muzzled**.
- The military took over many cities.
- **Police and secret service reigned supreme**.
- **Rebellious villages were fined heavily**.
- In many villages, **mass flogging** was carried out.

---

## 📊 Part 5: Evaluation of the Quit India Movement

### Key Assessments

- ❗ Left without leaders, there was **no restraint and violence became common**.
- 🔥 Main storm centres: **eastern United Provinces, Bihar, Midnapore, Maharashtra, Karnataka**.
- 💪 **Students, workers, and peasants** were the backbone; upper classes and bureaucracy remained largely loyal.
- 🏛️ Government loyalty suffered **considerable erosion** — showing how deep nationalism had penetrated.
- 📣 The movement established the truth that it was **no longer possible to rule India without the wishes of Indians**.
- ⚡ **Element of spontaneity was higher than before** — though a degree of popular initiative had been sanctioned by the leadership, and the Congress had been ideologically, politically, and organisationally preparing for a long time.
- 🎯 The movement placed the **demand for independence on the immediate agenda** of the national movement — after Quit India, there could be no retreat.
- 🦁 Common people displayed **unparalleled heroism and militancy** — facing the most brutal repression under the most adverse circumstances.

## 🍚 Part 6: Gandhi Fasts (February 1943)

**February 1943:** Gandhi started a fast as an answer to the government's exhortation that he condemn violence — which was essentially a government demand that he accept responsibility for the violence of the Quit India Movement.

- Gandhi directed the fast **against the violence of the State**, not the people.
- The popular response was **immediate and overwhelming**.
- Protests were organised at home and abroad through **hartals, demonstrations**, and **strikes**.
- **Three members of the Viceroy's Executive Council resigned** in protest.

**What the Fast Achieved:**
- ✅ **Public morale was raised**.
- ✅ **Anti-British feeling was heightened**.
- ✅ Provided an **opportunity for political activity**.
- ✅ **Government's high-handedness was exposed**.

Gandhi got the better of his opponents and refused to oblige by dying.

> 📌 **On March 23, 1943, Pakistan Day was observed** — a significant political marker by the Muslim League.

---

## 🌾 Part 7: The Famine of 1943

The horrors of war were compounded by the **great Bengal Famine of 1943**.

**Worst-Affected Areas:**
- South-west Bengal: the **Tamluk-Contai-Diamond Harbour region**
- **Dacca, Faridpur, Tippera**, and **Noakhali**

**Human Toll:**
- Around **1.5 to 3 million people perished** from the famine, along with epidemics (malaria, cholera, and smallpox), malnutrition, and starvation.
- This was **basically a man-made famine**.

**Fundamental Causes:**
1. The need to **feed a vast army** diverted foodstuffs away from the civilian population.
2. **Rice imports from Burma and South-East Asia had been stopped** (due to the Japanese occupation).
3. The famine was aggravated by **gross mismanagement and deliberate profiteering** — rationing methods were **belated** and confined to big cities only.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Quit India Movement and the Bengal Famine

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. "Do or Die" — The Spirit of Non-Negotiable Demands
- The Quit India Movement's absolute demand for immediate independence — no conditions, no further negotiation — finds a modern parallel in mass movements that refuse incremental compromise.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Farmers' Protest (2020–21)** similarly adopted a "complete repeal or nothing" position on the three farm laws. Like the Quit India Movement, it was a leaderless, decentralised movement sustained for over a year, ultimately resulting in the **repeal of all three farm laws (November 19, 2021)** — validating the strategy of non-negotiable mass pressure.

### 2. Parallel Governments — A Constitutional Concept Today
- The Prati Sarkar of Satara and Jatiya Sarkar of Tamluk were early experiments in grassroots self-governance.
- `[Modern Example]` These find their constitutional descendant in the **73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992)** establishing Panchayati Raj institutions, and in the **PM Gram Swaraj Abhiyan** — reflecting Gandhi's vision of village republics. The **Gram Panchayat Development Plans (GPDPs)** required under the **MGNREGA framework** are direct institutional descendants of the Jatiya Sarkar's work on relief and development.

### 3. The Bengal Famine — A Lesson for Food Security
- The man-made Bengal Famine of 1943 killed up to 3 million people due to mismanagement, profiteering, and supply disruption.
- `[Modern Example]` India's response to this historical trauma is the **National Food Security Act (2013)** — providing subsidised food grains to approximately **81.35 crore people** (about two-thirds of India's population) through the PDS. During the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022), the **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PMGKAY)** provided **free food grains** to 80 crore people — a direct policy legacy of the determination that no Indian should die of starvation as they did in 1943.

### 4. Underground Radio — Usha Mehta's Legacy
- Usha Mehta's **underground radio station** during the Quit India Movement was a pioneering act of communication defiance.
- `[Modern Example]` The spirit of this underground communication finds a modern echo in **encrypted messaging platforms and social media** being used by protest movements worldwide — and in India's debates about **internet shutdowns** (India had the highest number of internet shutdowns globally in multiple years 2019–2023, according to Access Now reports) as a tool of political control, reversing the lesson of 1942.

## 📜 Part 8: Rajagopalachari (CR) Formula (1944)

### Background

Efforts continued to solve the constitutional crisis. **C. Rajagopalachari (CR)** — the veteran Congress leader — prepared a formula for Congress-League cooperation in **1944**. It was a tacit acceptance of the League's demand for Pakistan. **Gandhi supported the formula**.

### Main Points of the CR Plan

- ✅ The **Muslim League** to endorse the Congress demand for independence.
- ✅ The League to cooperate with Congress in forming a **provisional government at the Centre**.
- ✅ After the end of the war, the **entire population of Muslim majority areas** in the North-West and North-East of India to decide by a **plebiscite** whether or not to form a **separate sovereign state**.
- ✅ In case of acceptance of partition, agreements to be made jointly for safeguarding **defence, commerce, communications**, etc.
- ✅ The above terms to be operative **only if England transferred full powers to India**.

### Jinnah's Objections

- Jinnah wanted the Congress to **accept the Two-Nation Theory** (the idea that Hindus and Muslims are two separate nations).
- He wanted **only the Muslims of North-West and North-East to vote** in the plebiscite — not the entire population.
- He also opposed the idea of a **common centre**.
- The Congress was ready to cooperate with the League for independence of the Indian Union — the **League was only interested in a separate nation**.

### Hindu Leaders' Reaction

- **Vir Savarkar** and Hindu leaders **condemned the CR Plan** — they were opposed to any concession towards partition.

## 🤝 Part 9: Desai-Liaqat Pact

Efforts to end the constitutional deadlock continued.

**Bhulabhai Desai** (leader of the Congress Party in the Central Legislative Assembly) met **Liaqat Ali Khan** (deputy leader of the Muslim League in that Assembly) and produced a draft proposal for forming an **interim government at the Centre**:

**Proposals:**
- An **equal number of persons** nominated by the Congress and the League in the central legislature.
- **20 per cent reserved seats** for minorities.

No final settlement was reached — but the **establishment of parity between Congress and League** had **far-reaching consequences**, effectively giving the League an equal constitutional status with the Congress.

## 🏔️ Part 10: Wavell Plan (Shimla Conference — June 1945)

### Context

- The war in Europe ended in **May 1945**, but the Japanese threat remained.
- The **Conservative government under Churchill** was keen to reach a solution on India.
- **Viceroy Lord Wavell** was permitted to start negotiations.
- Congress leaders were **released from jails in June 1945**.

### Why the Government Was Keen on a Solution

1. The **general election in England was scheduled for mid-1945** — Conservatives wanted to be seen as sincere on India.
2. **Pressure from the Allies** to seek further Indian cooperation in the war.
3. The government wanted to **divert Indian energies** into channels more profitable for the British.

### The Plan

A conference was convened by Viceroy Wavell at **Shimla in June 1945** to reconstruct the Governor General's Executive Council pending a new constitution.

**Main Proposals:**
- With the exception of the **Governor General and the Commander-in-Chief**, all members of the Executive Council were to be **Indians**.
- **Caste Hindus and Muslims were to have equal representation**.
- The reconstructed council was to function as an **interim government within the framework of the 1935 Act** (i.e., not responsible to the Central Assembly).
- The Governor General was to exercise his **veto on the advice of ministers**.
- Representatives of different parties to **submit a joint list** to the Viceroy for nominations. If a joint list was not possible, then **separate lists** were to be submitted.
- Possibilities were to be kept open for **negotiations on a new constitution** once the war was finally won.

### Muslim League's Stand

- The League wanted **all Muslim members to be League nominees**, fearing that since the aims of other minorities (depressed classes, Sikhs, Christians) were the same as those of the Congress, this arrangement would **reduce the League to a one-third minority**.
- Wavell wanted **Khizr Hyat Khan** as the Muslim representative from Western Punjab — the League opposed this.
- The League claimed **some kind of veto** in the council — decisions opposed to Muslims needing a **two-thirds majority for approval**.

### Congress Stand

- Congress objected to the plan as *"an attempt to reduce the Congress to the status of a purely caste Hindu party"*.
- It **insisted on its right to include members of all communities** among its nominees.

### Wavell's Fatal Mistake

- Wavell announced a **breakdown of talks** — effectively giving the League a **virtual veto** over the entire process.
- This **strengthened the League's position** enormously.
- It was evident in the **elections of 1945–46** — where the League swept Muslim seats.
- It **boosted Jinnah's position**.
- It **exposed the real character of Churchill's Conservative government** — which was clearly using the Muslim League to obstruct Indian independence.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Constitutional Deadlock

`[Modern Example]`

### The Demand for Constituent Assembly — Finally Realised
- The Congress's consistent demand throughout the Quit India era for a **Constituent Assembly elected on adult franchise** was finally conceded.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Constituent Assembly** was convened in **December 1946** and completed India's Constitution on **November 26, 1949** — now celebrated as **Constitution Day (Samvidhan Diwas)**. The **75th anniversary of the Constitution (November 26, 2024)** was marked with special sessions of both Houses of Parliament — a direct celebration of what the Congress demanded and the British consistently denied during 1942–1945.

## ⚔️ Part 11: Subhas Chandra Bose — Background

### The Man

- **Passed the Indian Civil Services examination** (securing **fourth position**) but resigned from the service in **1921** to join the freedom struggle.
- **Political guru: Chittaranjan Das** (Deshbandhu).
- Became **Mayor of Calcutta in 1923**.
- Jailed many times by the British.
- Once it was clear he could not follow Gandhi's way — and that Congress was determined to follow Gandhi — **Bose decided to go his own way**.

### Before Leaving India

- **March 1940:** Bose convened an **Anti-Compromise Conference at Ramgarh** — a joint effort of the **Forward Bloc** and the **Kisan Sabha**.
- Resolved that a **worldwide struggle should be launched on April 6** (first day of National Week) — people called upon not to help the Imperialist War with any resource.
- There was enthusiastic public participation on April 6.
- **July 1940:** Bose was arrested when he protested against a proposed **monument for Holwell in Calcutta**.
- **December 1940:** Released from prison and placed under **house arrest** after a hunger strike.

### Bose's Great Escape

- **January 1941:** Bose escaped from house arrest.
- **January 26, 1941:** He reached **Peshawar** under the pseudonym **Ziauddin**, helped by **Bhagat Ram**.
- Later reported to have left India *"to supplement from outside the struggle going on at home."*
- He approached **Russia for help** — but in **June 1941**, Russia joined the Allies, disappointing Bose.
- He then went to **Germany** and met Hitler under the pseudonym **Orlando Mazzotta**.

### In Germany

- With Hitler's help, a **'Freedom Army' (Mukti Sena)** was formed — consisting of Indian prisoners of war captured by Germany and Italy.
- **Dresden, Germany** was made the office of the Freedom Army.
- Bose came to be called **'Netaji'** by the people of Germany.
- He gave the famous slogan **'Jai Hind'** from the **Free India Centre, Germany**.
- Began **regular broadcasts from Berlin Radio in January 1942** — enthusing Indians back home.
- **Early 1943:** Left Germany — travelled by **German and Japanese submarines** to reach Japan and then **Singapore in July 1943**.

## 🪖 Part 12: Origin and First Phase of the Indian National Army

### The Original Idea — Mohan Singh

The idea of creating an army from Indian prisoners of war (POWs) was originally that of **Mohan Singh** — an Indian army officer who refused to join the retreating British army in Malaya.

- He turned to the Japanese for help.
- The Japanese had been encouraging Indian civilians to form **anti-British organisations**.
- Mohan Singh asked the Japanese for Indian prisoners of war.
- The Japanese handed over Indian POWs to Mohan Singh.
- After the **fall of Singapore**, several POWs were ready to join.
- By the end of **1942**, **40,000 men** were ready to join the INA.

**Important Condition:** The INA was intended to go into action **only on the invitation of the Indian National Congress and the people of India** — not on Japanese orders.

**Two Strategic Purposes:**
1. A **check against Japanese misconduct** towards Indians in South-East Asia.
2. A **bulwark against possible future Japanese occupation** of India.

The INA got a boost from the **outbreak of the Quit India Movement** in India.

**September 1942:** The **first division of the INA** was formed with **16,300 men**.

### End of Phase One

- Serious differences emerged between Mohan Singh and the Japanese over the INA's role.
- The Japanese wanted a **token force of only 2,000** — Mohan Singh wanted a much larger army.
- **Mohan Singh was taken into custody by the Japanese.**

## 🏳️ Part 13: Rashbehari Bose — The Unsung Hero

The role of **Rashbehari Bose** must be acknowledged:

- Had fled to **Japan in 1915** following failed revolutionary activities.
- Became a **naturalised Japanese citizen** over time.
- Made enormous efforts in **getting the Japanese interested in the Indian independence movement**.
- Became active in **Pan-Asian circles**.
- Founded the **Indian Club of Tokyo**.
- Gave lectures on the evils of **Western imperialism**.
- Was greatly excited when the INA was formed — left Tokyo for Southeast Asia.
- At a **conference in Bangkok** (under Japanese occupation), it was decided to place the INA under an **Indian Independence League** — with Rashbehari Bose as chairman.
- He had created this **League in 1942 in Tokyo**.
- When Subhas Bose was sought to lead the INA, Rashbehari Bose **happily transferred the control and leadership** of both the Indian Independence League and the INA to Subhas in **July 1943**.

> It was on **Rashbehari Bose's organisational spadework** that Subhas Bose could build the Indian National Army.

---

## 🗡️ Part 14: Second Phase — Subhas Bose Takes Command

### The Journey to Singapore

- **June 1943:** Subhas Bose (under pseudonym **Abid Hussain**) reached **Tokyo** and met **Japanese Prime Minister Tojo**.
- He then went to Singapore and met Rashbehari Bose.
- **Subhas Bose became Supreme Commander of the INA on August 25 (1943)**.

*(Note: In February 1944, Rashbehari's health deteriorated after a lung collapse. He died on **January 21, 1945, aged 58**.)*

---

### Key Events and Milestones of the INA

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **October 21, 1943** | Subhas Bose formed the **Provisional Government of Free India (Azad Hind)** at Singapore. Portfolio holders: **H.C. Chatterjee** (Finance), **M.A. Aiyar** (Broadcasting), **Lakshmi Swaminathan** (Women Department). The famous slogan *"Give me blood, I will give you freedom"* was given in Malaya. |
| **October 1943** | This provisional government **declared war on Britain and the United States** and was **recognised by the Axis powers** |
| **1943** | A **women's regiment — Rani Jhansi Regiment** — was also formed |
| **November 6, 1943** | The **Andaman and Nicobar Islands** were handed over by the Japanese to the INA; renamed as **Shahid Dweep** and **Swaraj Dweep** respectively |
| **January 1944** | INA headquarters shifted to **Rangoon (Burma)** — army recruits were to march from there with the war cry **"Chalo Delhi!"** |
| **July 6, 1944** | Subhas Bose **addressed Gandhi as 'Father of Nation'** from **Azad Hind Radio** — he was the **first person** to call Gandhi the Father of Nation. He asked for Gandhi's blessings for *"India's last war of independence."* |

---

### The Military Campaign

- One INA battalion commanded by **Shah Nawaz** was allowed to accompany the Japanese Army to the **Indo-Burma front** and participate in the **Imphal campaign**.
- However, the Indians received **discriminatory treatment from the Japanese**: denied rations and arms; made to do menial work for Japanese units — this **disgusted and demoralised** the INA units.
- **March 18, 1944:** The **Azad Hind Fauz crossed the Burma border** and stood on **Indian soil**.
- INA units advanced up to **Kohima and Imphal**.
- **April 14, 1944:** Colonel Malik of the Bahadur Group **hoisted the INA flag for the first time on the Indian mainland** at **Moirang, Manipur** (where the **INA Memorial Complex** stands today) — to enthusiastic cries of "Jai Hind" and "Netaji Zindabad".
- For **three months**, the INA carried out military administration duties at Moirang.
- Then **Allied forces reclaimed the territory**.

### The Retreat and Fall

- The INA met the same fate as the Japanese — **all brigades began their withdrawal on July 18, 1944**.
- The steady Japanese retreat quashed all hopes of the INA liberating India.
- The retreat continued till **mid-1945**.
- **August 15, 1945:** Japan **surrendered** — the INA also surrendered.
- **August 18, 1945:** Subhas Bose reportedly **died mysteriously in an air crash at Taipei (Taiwan)**.

### The INA Trials — A Boomerang for the British

When INA POWs were brought back to India after the war for **court-martial**, a **powerful movement emerged in their defence** — which we will study in the next chapter.

## 🌍 Modern Context — INA and Bose's Legacy

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. Netaji's Statue at India Gate — A National Recognition
- `[Modern Example]` On **September 8, 2022**, Prime Minister Modi unveiled a **grand granite statue of Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose** at the **India Gate canopy in New Delhi** — where King George V's statue had once stood. This was a powerful symbolic act — replacing a colonial monarch with India's most radical freedom fighter at the nation's most prominent war memorial.
- The **Netaji Research Bureau** in Kolkata and the **INA Memorial Complex at Moirang, Manipur** were developed as part of the **Azadi Ka Amrit Mahotsav (2021–22)** commemorations.

### 2. Women in Combat — Rani Jhansi Regiment's Legacy
- `[Modern Example]` The **Rani Jhansi Regiment** (1943) was India's first formal women's military unit. Its legacy was finally institutionalised when the **Indian Army inducted women into its Agniveer combat scheme (2022)**, and the **Supreme Court in 2022** upheld permanent commission for women officers across all branches of the armed forces — ending a decades-long discrimination that Bose had rejected way back in 1943.

### 3. Netaji's Files — Transparency and Historical Justice
- `[Modern Example]` In **January 2016**, the Government of India **declassified 100 files** related to Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose — making them publicly available for the first time. This was followed by further declassifications in subsequent years, reflecting the nation's ongoing effort to fully understand and honour Bose's legacy. The mystery of his death (air crash at Taipei, August 18, 1945) remains officially unresolved.

### 4. "Jai Hind" — From Bose to India's National Salutation
- The slogan **"Jai Hind"** given by Bose from the **Free India Centre, Germany** is now India's national salutation — used to conclude every major speech by the President and Prime Minister of India.
- `[Modern Example]` In the **2024 Budget speech and Republic Day addresses**, "Jai Hind" was the closing salutation — a living reminder that Bose's words, first spoken to inspire a beleaguered army in wartime, are now woven into the fabric of Indian state ceremonial.
