# CHAPTER 21: Congress Rule in Provinces

---

## 🔍 Introduction: Congress Takes Office

Following the **stunning electoral victory of 1937**, Congress ministries were formed in:

> **Bombay, Madras, Central Provinces, Orissa, United Provinces, Bihar** — and later in **NWFP** and **Assam** as well.

This was an unprecedented experiment — the Congress, which had fought the British for decades from the streets, now sat in government offices. The question was: what would it do with this power?

---

## 🕊️ Part 1: Gandhi's Advice — Hold Offices Lightly

Gandhi set the philosophical tone for how Congress ministers should approach their new roles. His guidance was clear and memorable:

- 🌿 Hold these offices **lightly, not tightly** — do not get attached to power.
- 🌿 These offices were to be seen as **"crowns of thorns"** — accepted not for comfort but to test whether they could **quicken the pace towards the nationalist goal**.
- 🌿 These offices should be used in a way **"not expected or intended by the British"** — subvert the system from within.
- 🌿 Congress should prove that it could **rule with the least assistance from the police and the Army** — demonstrating self-reliant, people-friendly governance.

> This advice was not just philosophical — it was a **strategic warning** against getting absorbed into the colonial administrative machine.

---

## 🏛️ Part 2: Work Under Congress Ministries — What Was Achieved?

The 28 months of Congress rule generated **great enthusiasm among the people** — suppressed mass energy had been released. The Congress showed that it could not only lead people in the streets but could also **use State power for their benefit**. This raised its prestige enormously.

**BUT — There Were Basic Limitations:**
- The ministries could **not change the basic imperialist character** of the system through their administration.
- They could **not introduce a radical era** of transformation.

With these limitations in mind, here is what was achieved across different areas:

---

### 🔓 A. Civil Liberties

The Congress ministries did much to ease the repressive colonial machinery:

**What Was Done:**
- ✅ Laws giving **emergency powers were repealed**.
- ✅ Ban on **illegal organisations** such as the **Hindustan Seva Dal** and **Youth Leagues** was lifted.
- ✅ Bans on **certain books and journals** were lifted.
- ✅ **Press restrictions were lifted**.
- ✅ Newspapers were taken **out of black lists**.
- ✅ **Confiscated arms and arms licences were restored**.
- ✅ **Police powers were curbed** and the **CID** (Criminal Investigation Department — the colonial-era intelligence/surveillance unit) **stopped shadowing politicians**.
- ✅ **Political prisoners and revolutionaries were released** — deportation and internment orders revoked.
- ✅ In **Bombay**, lands confiscated by the government during the **Civil Disobedience Movement were restored**.
- ✅ **Pensions of officials** associated with the Civil Disobedience Movement were restored.

**The Blemishes — Where Things Went Wrong:**

Despite this impressive record, there were instances where civil liberties were compromised:

- ❌ **Yusuf Maherally** (a socialist) was **arrested by the Madras government** for inflammatory speeches — and later released.
- ❌ **S.S. Batliwala** (a socialist) was **arrested by the Madras government** for seditious speech and given a **six months' sentence**.
- ❌ **K.M. Munshi**, the **Bombay Home Minister**, used the **CID against communists and leftists** — a clear irony given the earlier criticism of such practices.

---

### 🌾 B. Agrarian Reforms

This was the area where the greatest gap existed between aspiration and achievement.

**Why a Complete Overhaul Was Impossible — Seven Constraints:**

1. The ministries **did not have adequate powers** to abolish zamindari completely.
2. **Inadequate financial resources** — a lion's share was appropriated by the Government of India.
3. **Strategy of class adjustments** — zamindars and other powerful groups had to be conciliated and neutralised to maintain the coalition.
4. **Constraint of time** — the logic of Congress politics was confrontation and not cooperation with colonialism, making deep reform politically complex.
5. **War clouds hovering from 1938** — the approaching World War II distracted attention and resources.
6. The **reactionary second chamber** (Legislative Council) — dominated by landlords, moneylenders, and capitalists in **United Provinces, Bihar, Bombay, Madras**, and **Assam** — had to be conciliated as its support was necessary for legislation.
7. The **agrarian structure was too complex** to be transformed quickly.

**What Was Still Achieved:**
Despite these constraints, the Congress ministries legislated a number of laws relating to:
- **Land reforms**
- **Debt relief**
- **Forest grazing fees**
- **Arrears of rent**
- **Land tenures**

**Who Benefited — and Who Didn't:**
- Most benefits went to **statutory and occupancy tenants** (those with some legal security of tenure).
- **Sub-tenants** (those renting land from tenants) did **not gain much**.
- **Agricultural labourers** did **not benefit** at all — because they had not been politically mobilised.

---

### 🏭 C. Attitude Towards Labour

The Congress ministries' approach to labour was essentially **pro-worker but pro-industrial peace** — a careful balancing act:

**The Basic Approach:**
- Advance **workers' interests** while promoting **industrial peace**.
- Reduce strikes as far as possible.
- Advocate **compulsory arbitration** before any strike action — workers had to go through established conciliation machinery first.
- Create **goodwill between labour and capital** with ministry mediation.
- Make efforts to **improve workers' conditions and secure wage increases**.

**When This Broke Down:**
- Ministries treated **militant trade union protests as law and order problems**.
- Generally, ministries **took recourse to Section 144** (a law prohibiting assembly of four or more persons in an area) and **arrested leaders**.
- This approach was largely successful — **except in Bombay**.
- **Leftist critics were not satisfied** with this approach at all.

**Leaders' Reactions:**
- **Nehru was unhappy** about these repressive measures — but in public **supported the ministries** to protect them from what he called "petty and petulant criticism".
- **Gandhi** — though against militant and violent methods — stood for **political education of the masses**. He felt the Congress's popular base should not erode. He appealed to Congressmen **against frequent resort to colonial laws and machinery**.

---

### 🤲 D. Social Welfare Reforms

The Congress ministries undertook a range of social welfare initiatives:

- 🚫 **Prohibition** imposed in certain areas.
- 🙏 **Welfare measures for Harijans** — including:
  - Temple entry
  - Use of public facilities
  - Scholarships
  - Increase in their numbers in government service and police
- 📚 Attention given to **primary, technical, and higher education** and to **public health and sanitation**.
- 🧵 **Encouragement given to khadi** through subsidies and other measures.
- ⛓️ **Prison reforms** undertaken.
- 🏭 **Encouragement given to indigenous enterprises** (Indian-owned businesses).
- 📊 Efforts made to **develop planning** — the **National Planning Committee** was set up under **Congress President Subhas Chandra Bose in 1938** to begin the work of economic planning for India.

---

### 📣 E. Extra-Parliamentary Mass Activity of Congress

Beyond government work, Congress also maintained mass contact through:

- 📖 **Mass literacy campaigns** launched.
- 🏛️ **Congress police stations and panchayats** set up (parallel systems of justice and governance).
- 📜 **Congress Grievance Committees** presenting mass petitions to the government.
- 👑 **States peoples' movements** — supporting democratic movements in princely states.

---

## ⚖️ Part 3: Evaluation — Was Congress Rule Successful?

By 1939, **internal strifes, opportunism, and hunger for power** had started surfacing among Congressmen. Yet, the 28-month Congress rule was **significant and largely successful** for several reasons:

### What Was Achieved

- ✅ The contention that **Indian self-government was necessary for radical social transformation** got confirmed — self-rule worked better than colonial rule.
- ✅ Congressmen demonstrated that a **movement could use state power to further its ends without being co-opted** into the colonial machine.
- ✅ The ministries were able to **control communal riots**.
- ✅ The **morale of the bureaucracy came down** — it could no longer act with its old arrogance.
- ✅ Council work helped **neutralise many erstwhile hostile elements** (landlords, etc.).
- ✅ People were able to **perceive the shape of things to come** if independence was won — a taste of what Indian self-rule could look like.
- ✅ Administrative work by Indians **further weakened the myth that Indians were not fit to rule**.

---

## ⚠️ Part 4: The Complications and Challenges

### Labour Unrest
- The Congress's **huge victory had aroused the hopes of the industrial working class**.
- There was increased militancy and industrial unrest in **Bombay, Gujarat, the United Provinces**, and **Bengal**.
- At the same time, Congress was drawn into a **closer friendship with Indian capitalists**.
- This resulted in an **apparent anti-labour shift in Congress attitudes** — culminating in the **Bombay Traders Disputes Act of 1938**.

### The Princely States Dilemma
- The Congress leadership faced a difficult question: **how to react to the situation in the princely states**.
- Should Congress support the **Praja Mandal movement** (a democratic movement of the people — *praja* — in princely states demanding greater democratic rights) — or not?
- This dilemma was never fully resolved.

### The Muslim League's Pirpur Committee (1938)

This was a politically consequential development:

- The **All India Muslim League was annoyed** with the Congress for **not sharing power with them** after the elections.
- In **1938**, the League established the **Pirpur Committee** to prepare a detailed report on **alleged atrocities committed by the Congress ministries** against Muslims.

**The Pirpur Committee's Charges Against Congress:**
- ❌ Interference in **religious rites** of Muslims.
- ❌ Suppression of **Urdu in favour of Hindi**.
- ❌ **Denial of proper representation** to Muslims in governance.
- ❌ **Oppression of Muslims in the economic sphere**.

> These charges — whether fully accurate or politically motivated — had a deep and lasting impact on Muslim political consciousness and helped the Muslim League consolidate its base. They contributed significantly to the growing Hindu-Muslim political divide that ultimately led to Partition.

---

### The Lesson Learned

> The Congress was forced to realise that **being in power and actually running the administration was not easy** — all sections of the population had expectations so high that they could not be fulfilled all at once.

This is perhaps the most important political lesson of the Congress ministries' experience.

---

## 🕊️ Part 5: The End of Congress Ministries

**October 1939:** All Congress ministries **resigned** following the **outbreak of the Second World War**.

> The Viceroy had unilaterally declared India a belligerent in the war without consulting Indian elected representatives — the Congress viewed this as a fundamental violation of the principle of self-governance and resigned in protest.

---

## 🌍 Modern Context — Congress Rule in Provinces

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. "Crowns of Thorns" — Power Without Full Authority
- Gandhi's concept of holding offices "lightly, not tightly" and the structural limitations of the Congress ministries (governors' overriding powers, non-votable budgets) find a striking modern parallel.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Delhi government vs. Lieutenant Governor (LG) conflict (2015–2023)** is the most prominent modern example. The elected AAP government in Delhi repeatedly clashed with the LG over control of services, police, and administration — mirroring exactly the problem Congress ministries faced with governors under the 1935 Act. The **Supreme Court's landmark judgment in *Government of NCT of Delhi vs. Union of India* (May 2023)** ruled that the elected government (not the LG) has primacy over services in Delhi — a modern resolution of the power-without-full-authority problem that plagued Congress ministries.

### 2. The National Planning Committee — A Visionary Start
- The **National Planning Committee (1938)** under Subhas Chandra Bose was India's first serious attempt at economic planning.
- `[Modern Example]` This directly evolved into the **Planning Commission (1950–2014)** and later the **NITI Aayog (2015–present)**. The current **NITI Aayog's Vision Document 2047** — targeting India becoming a developed nation by 2047 — is a direct descendant of the planning impulse that the Congress first institutionalised under provincial rule in 1938.

### 3. Agrarian Reforms — The Unfinished Business
- The Congress ministries' failure to fully abolish zamindari due to structural constraints is historically important.
- `[Modern Example]` Zamindari was ultimately abolished after Independence through state legislation in the 1950s. However, agricultural labourers — who didn't benefit from Congress ministry reforms — remain the most vulnerable section of rural India. The **PM-KISAN Scheme (2019–present)** providing ₹6,000/year to farmer families, and the ongoing debates around **MSP legal guarantee** (demanded in the 2024 farmers' protests) are direct continuations of the unresolved agrarian question that the Congress ministries could not solve in 1937–39.

### 4. The Pirpur Committee and Communal Politics
- The Pirpur Committee's report (1938) alleging Congress atrocities against Muslims was a significant political weapon used by the Muslim League.
- `[Modern Example]` The pattern of **minority communities alleging discrimination under majority-led governments** — and the political use of such allegations — continues in contemporary Indian politics. The debates around the **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA, 2019)**, minority rights, and the **Supreme Court's various judgments** on minority rights reflect the same unresolved tension between majoritarianism and minority protection that the Pirpur Committee first articulated as a political issue in 1938.

### 5. Congress and Labour — A Tension That Persists
- The Congress ministries' ambivalence towards militant labour — using Section 144 against strikers while also improving wages — reflects a tension between capital and labour within the Congress that was never resolved.
- `[Modern Example]` The **consolidation of 44 labour laws into 4 Labour Codes (2019–2020)** under the current government, and the **All-India strike by central trade unions in 2022** opposing these codes, reflect the same fundamental tension: how should a government balance workers' rights with industrial peace and business interests — the exact dilemma that Congress ministries faced in 1937–39.
