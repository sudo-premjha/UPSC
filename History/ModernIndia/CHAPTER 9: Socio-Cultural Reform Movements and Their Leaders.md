## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **19th-century India** witnessed a wave of reform movements that challenged caste, idolatry, gender discrimination, and superstition — primarily driven by the educated middle class.
> 2. **Raja Rammohan Roy** is the founding figure — his Brahmo Samaj set the template for rational, humanistic reform rooted in Vedantic thought.
> 3. Reforms were not limited to Hindus — **Muslim, Parsi, and Sikh** communities all had their own parallel movements.
> 4. **Dalit reformers** like Jyotiba Phule, Savitribai Phule, and Gopal Baba Walangkar ran independent, grassroots movements that targeted caste oppression directly.
> 5. These movements had **both positive and negative outcomes** — while they modernised Indian society and built national consciousness, they also, inadvertently, contributed to **communal divisions**.

# PART 1: REFORM MOVEMENTS IN HINDU SOCIETY — BENGAL

## 🔷 Raja Rammohan Roy and the Brahmo Samaj

### Who Was He?

**Raja Rammohan Roy (1772–1833)**, widely called the **"Father of Indian Renaissance"** and the **"Maker of Modern India"**, was a man of extraordinary, wide-ranging talent. He believed in:

- The **modern scientific approach**
- The principles of **human dignity** and **social equality**
- **Monotheism** (the belief in one single God)

---

### His Key Writings and Early Work

| Work | Year | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Gift to Monotheists** | 1809 | Argued for belief in one God |
| Translated **Vedas** and **five Upanishads** into Bengali | Early career | Proved ancient Hindu texts support monotheism |
| **Precepts of Jesus** | 1820 | Separated the moral message of Christ from miracle stories |
| Bengali Grammar Book | — | Enriched Bengali language and prose style |

- In **1814**, he set up the **Atmiya Sabha** (Society of Friends) in Calcutta to:
  - Propagate the monotheistic ideals of the **Vedanta** (ancient philosophical tradition of Hinduism)
  - Campaign against **idolatry** (worshipping statues/images), caste rigidities, and meaningless rituals

---

### His Core Philosophy — Simplified

Roy was deeply influenced by **rationalist ideas** (using logic and reason to evaluate beliefs). He declared:

> *"Vedanta is based on reason — and if reason demands it, even a departure from scriptures is justified."*

He applied this reasoning to **all religions**, not just Hinduism. He tried to take the best moral lessons from Christianity, Islam, and Hinduism — a bold, creative synthesis.

This earned him **enemies on two fronts:**
- **Christian missionaries** were angry that he praised Christ's teachings but refused to convert.
- **Hindu orthodox groups** were angered by his rejection of idol worship and rituals.

---

### The Founding of the Brahmo Samaj

In **August 1828**, Roy founded the **Brahmo Sabha**, which was later renamed the **Brahmo Samaj** (Society of God).

**What the Samaj stood for:**

> *"The worship and adoration of the Eternal, Unsearchable, Immutable Being who is the Author and Preserver of the Universe."*

**Rules of Worship:**
- Prayer, meditation, and reading from the Upanishads
- **No** graven images, statues, sculptures, paintings, or portraits in Samaj buildings
- This firmly opposed idol worship

**The Twin Pillars of Brahmo Samaj:**
1. **Reason**
2. **The Vedas and Upanishads**

**Important:** Roy did **not** want to create a new religion. He only wanted to **purify Hinduism** of the evil practices that had crept into it over centuries.

---

### Features of the Brahmo Samaj (Key Points)

- ❌ Denounced **polytheism** (belief in many gods) and **idol worship**
- ❌ Discarded faith in divine **avataras** (incarnations of God)
- ❌ Denied that any scripture had **ultimate authority** over human reason and conscience
- 🔄 Took **no definite stand** on karma and transmigration of soul — left it to individual belief
- ❌ Criticised the **caste system**

---

### Opposition to Roy

**Raja Radhakant Deb** organised the **Dharma Sabha** to counter Brahmo Samaj propaganda. Roy's death in **1833** was a significant setback for the movement.

---

> **📢 Quote to Remember:**
> *"Raja Rammohan Roy and his Brahmo Samaj form the starting point for all the various reform movements — whether in Hindu religion, society or politics — which have agitated modern India."* — **H.C.E. Zacharias**

---

### Raja Rammohan Roy's Social Reform Work

Roy was not just a religious thinker — he was an **active social crusader.**

**1. Anti-Sati Campaign (started 1818)**
- **Sati** = the horrific practice of burning a widow alive on her husband's funeral pyre.
- Roy cited **sacred texts** to prove no religion sanctioned this.
- He visited cremation grounds, organised **vigilance groups**, and filed **counter-petitions** to the government.
- His efforts resulted in the **Government Regulation of 1829**, which declared sati a **crime**.

**2. Women's Rights**
- Condemned the general **subjugation of women**
- Opposed **polygamy** (a man having multiple wives) and the degraded state of widows
- Demanded the right of **inheritance and property** for women

**3. Education**
- Supported **David Hare's** efforts to found **Hindu College in 1817**
- His English school taught **mechanics** and **Voltaire's philosophy**
- In **1825**, established the **Vedanta College** — offering both Indian learning AND Western social and physical sciences
- Compiled a **Bengali grammar book** and developed a modern elegant prose style

**4. Languages**
- Roy knew **more than a dozen languages** including Sanskrit, Persian, Arabic, English, French, Latin, Greek, and Hebrew.

**5. Journalism and Freedom of Press**
- A bold supporter of **freedom of the press**
- Brought out journals in **Bengali, Hindi, English, and Persian** to educate the public and represent their grievances before the government.

**6. Political Activism**
- Condemned oppressive practices of **Bengali zamindars** (landlords)
- Demanded **fixation of maximum rents**
- Demanded abolition of taxes on tax-free lands
- Called for reduction of **export duties** on Indian goods
- Called for abolition of the **East India Company's trading rights**
- Demanded **Indianisation of superior services**
- Demanded **separation of the executive from the judiciary**
- Demanded **judicial equality** between Indians and Europeans and trial by jury

**7. Internationalism**
- Roy was an **internationalist** ahead of his times.
- He supported the **revolutions of Naples and Spanish America**
- Condemned the **oppression of Ireland** by English landlordism
- Threatened emigration from the empire if the **Reform Bill** was not passed

---

**Roy's Key Associates:** David Hare, Alexander Duff, Debendranath Tagore, P.K. Tagore, Chandrashekhar Deb, and Tarachand Chakraborty.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The spirit of Roy's anti-sati campaign lives on in modern legislation. The **Commission of Sati (Prevention) Act, 1987** criminalises the act of sati and its glorification. Although rare, sporadic incidents are still reported, reminding us that social reform is an ongoing project. Roy's demand for **women's property rights** found full constitutional expression in the **Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005**, which gave daughters equal rights in ancestral property. The **Supreme Court in Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020)** reinforced this, ruling that daughters have equal coparcenary rights regardless of whether the father was alive when the amendment came into force.

## 🔷 Debendranath Tagore and the Brahmo Samaj

**Maharishi Debendranath Tagore (1817–1905)** — father of the great poet Rabindranath Tagore — gave the Brahmo Samaj **new life and a definite shape** when he joined in **1842**.

- Before joining, he headed the **Tattvabodhini Sabha** (founded **1839**), along with its organ, the **Tattvabodhini Patrika** (in Bengali).
  - This was devoted to the systematic study of India's past with a rational outlook and propagation of Rammohan's ideas.
- The informal association of both sabhas brought **new vitality and strength** to the Brahmo Samaj.

**Gradual expansion:** The Samaj came to include prominent followers of Rammohan, the **Derozians** (discussed below), and independent thinkers such as **Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar** and **Ashwini Kumar Datta**.

**Two-front strategy under Tagore:**
1. **Within Hinduism** — Brahmo Samaj functioned as a reformist movement
2. **Outside** — Resolutely opposed Christian missionaries who criticised Hinduism and attempted conversions

**Reforms supported by the revitalised Samaj:**
- Widow remarriage
- Women's education
- Abolition of polygamy
- Improvement in ryots' (peasants') conditions
- Temperance (opposing alcohol)

---

## 🔷 Keshab Chandra Sen and the Brahmo Samaj

**Keshab Chandra Sen (1838–84)** was appointed **acharya** (spiritual head) by Debendranath Tagore soon after joining the Samaj in **1858**.

**His contribution:**
- Made the movement **popular** beyond Bengal
- Opened branches of the Samaj in **United Provinces, Punjab, Bombay, Madras**, and other towns

**Why the split happened:**
- Debendranath did not approve of Keshab's more radical ideas:
  - **Cosmopolitanisation** of the Samaj's meetings by including teachings from all religions
  - Strong views against the caste system
  - Open support to **inter-caste marriages**
- Keshab was **dismissed from the office of acharya in 1865**

**The First Split (1866):**
- Keshab and followers founded the **Brahmo Samaj of India**
- Debendranath Tagore's Samaj came to be known as the **Adi Brahmo Samaj**

**The Second Split (1878):**
- Keshab's inexplicable act of marrying his **13-year-old daughter** to the minor Hindu Maharaja of **Cooch-Behar** — with all orthodox Hindu rituals — caused a massive controversy.
- Also, some followers had started treating Keshab as an **incarnation** — which his progressive followers disliked.
- Further, Keshab was accused of **authoritarianism**.

**The Sadharan Brahmo Samaj (post-1878):**
- Founded by **Ananda Mohan Bose, Sib Chandra Deb**, and **Umeshchandra Dutta**.
- Reiterated core Brahmo doctrines: faith in one Supreme Being, no infallible scripture or man, belief in reason, truth, and morality.
- Brahmo centres were opened in **Madras province**.
- In Punjab, the **Dayal Singh Trust** implanted Brahmo ideas and opened **Dayal Singh College at Lahore in 1910**.

---

### Significance of the Brahmo Samaj — Summary

**Social Reforms championed:**
- Condemned **sati**
- Worked to abolish the **purdah system**
- Discouraged **child marriage** and polygamy
- Crusaded for **widow remarriage**
- Promoted **educational facilities** for women
- Attacked **casteism** and untouchability (with limited success)
- Condemned the Hindu prejudice against **going abroad**

**Limitation:** The Brahmo Samaj's influence largely did not go beyond **Calcutta and Bengal**. It did not have a lasting or widespread impact.

## 🔷 Prarthana Samaj

- Founded in **Bombay in 1867** by **Atmaram Pandurang**, with the help of **Keshab Chandra Sen**.
- A precursor was the **Paramahansa Sabha** — a secret society to spread liberal ideas and break caste and communal barriers.
- **Mahadeo Govind Ranade (1842–1901)** joined in **1870** and was the driving force behind its popularity.
- Other key leaders: **R.G. Bhandarkar (1837–1925)** and **N.G. Chandavarkar (1855–1923)**.

**Key features:**
- Emphasis on **monotheism** but more concerned with **social reforms** than religion
- Strongly attached to the **bhakti cult of Maharashtra**
- Relied on **education and persuasion** — NOT confrontation with Hindu orthodoxy

**Four-Point Social Agenda:**
1. Disapproval of the caste system
2. Women's education
3. Widow remarriage
4. Raising the **age of marriage** for both males and females

**Notable reformers alongside Ranade:** **Dhondo Keshav Karve** and **Vishnu Shastri** were key champions of social reform. Together with Karve, Ranade founded the **Widow Remarriage Movement** and the **Widows' Home Association** (to provide education and training to widows for self-sufficiency).

## 🔷 Young Bengal Movement and Henry Vivian Derozio

### The Context

During the **late 1820s and early 1830s**, a radical, intellectual trend emerged among the youth in Bengal — known as the **'Young Bengal Movement'**.

### The Leader

**Henry Vivian Derozio (1809–31)** was a young **Anglo-Indian** who taught at **Hindu College from 1826 to 1831**. He was the leader and inspirer of this progressive trend.

Drawing inspiration from the **French Revolution**, Derozio inspired his pupils to:
- Think freely and rationally
- Question **all authority**
- Love liberty, equality, and freedom
- Oppose decadent customs and traditions

The **Derozians** also supported:
- Women's rights and education
- Induction of Indians in higher grades of government services
- Protection of ryots from oppressive zamindars
- Better treatment to Indian labour abroad in British colonies
- Revision of the Company's charter
- Freedom of press
- Trial by jury

> Derozio was perhaps the **first nationalist poet of modern India**.

### Why the Movement Failed

- Derozio was removed from Hindu College in **1831** due to his radicalism.
- **Social conditions** were not yet ripe for radical ideas.
- No support from any other **social group or class**.
- The Derozians lacked a **real link with the masses** — they failed to take up the **peasants' cause**.
- Their radicalism was **bookish in character** — theoretical, not grounded.

### Their Legacy

Despite limitations, the Derozians carried forward **Rammohan Roy's tradition** of public education on social, economic, and political questions.

> *"The pioneers of the modern civilisation of Bengal, the conscript fathers of our race whose virtues will excite veneration and whose failings will be treated with gentlest consideration."* — **Surendranath Banerjea**

## 🔷 Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar

**Vidyasagar** was a great scholar and reformer whose ideas were a **happy blend of Indian and Western thought.** He believed in high moral values, was a **deep humanist**, and was generous to the poor.

**Key Contributions:**

**In Education:**
- In **1850**, became Principal of **Sanskrit College**
- Opened Sanskrit College to **non-Brahmins** — breaking the priestly monopoly on scriptural knowledge
- Introduced **Western thought** in Sanskrit College
- Evolved a new **methodology to teach Sanskrit**
- Devised a new **Bengali primer** and evolved a new prose style

**In Social Reform:**
- Started a **movement for widow remarriage** — this resulted in the **legalisation of widow remarriage** (Widow Remarriage Act)
- Crusader against **child marriage** and **polygamy**
- As government inspector of schools, organised **35 girls' schools**, many of which he ran at **his own expense**
- As secretary of **Bethune School** (established 1849), he was a pioneer of **higher education for women in India**

**The Struggle for Girls' Education:**
The powerful movement for women's education in the **1840s and 1850s** faced enormous resistance. Young girl students were:
- Shouted at and abused on streets
- Their parents were subjected to **social boycott**
- Many believed girls with Western education would make "slaves of their husbands"

Yet, the movement persisted and succeeded.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` Vidyasagar's legacy of women's education finds modern expression in the **Beti Bachao Beti Padhao** scheme (launched 2015) and the **Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana**. The **Gross Enrolment Ratio (GER)** for girls in higher education has steadily improved — India's GER in higher education reached **28.4%** in 2021-22 (AISHE Report 2021-22), with girls' GER surpassing boys in several states, reflecting the long-term fruits of educational reform movements.

## 🔷 Balshastri Jambhekar (1812–1846)

- A **pioneer of social reform through journalism** in Bombay
- Known as the **"Father of Marathi Journalism"**
- Started the newspaper **Darpan in 1832** — used it to awaken people to social reforms like **widow remarriage** and a **scientific approach to life**
- In **1840**, started **Digdarshan** — published articles on **scientific subjects and history**
- Founded the **Bombay Native General Library**
- Started the **Native Improvement Society** (with an offshoot — the Students Literary and Scientific Library)
- Was the **first professor of Hindi at Elphinstone College**
- Was a **director of the Colaba Observatory**

---

## 🔷 Paramahansa Mandali

- Founded in **1849 in Maharashtra**
- Founders: **Dadoba Pandurang, Mehtaji Durgaram**, and others
- Began as a **secret society** to reform Hindu religion and society
- Ideology closely linked to the **Manav Dharma Sabha**

**Key beliefs:**
- One God should be worshipped
- Real religion is based on **love and moral conduct**
- Freedom of thought and rationality were encouraged

**Caste Breaking in Practice:**
- At their meetings, food cooked by **lower-caste people** was eaten by all members — a revolutionary act at the time!
- Advocated **widow remarriage** and **women's education**
- Branches existed in **Poona, Satara**, and other towns of Maharashtra

---

## 🔷 Jyotirao Phule and Savitribai Phule

### Jyotirao (Jyotiba) Phule (1827–1890)

Born in **Satara, Maharashtra**, belonging to the **mali (gardener) community**, Jyotiba Phule organised one of the most powerful movements against **upper-caste domination** and **Brahminical supremacy**.

**Satyashodhak Samaj (Truth Seekers' Society) — Founded 1873**

- Leadership came from the **backward classes**: malis, telis, kunbis, saris, and dhangars.
- **Two main aims:**
  1. Social service
  2. Spread of education among women and lower-caste people

**Phule's Vision:**
- He wanted **social transformation** — not just social reform (a much deeper goal).
- Believed that without educating the **oppressed classes**, no mass awakening and no social revolution was possible.
- He aimed at a **society free of exploitation**.

**Phule's Key Contributions and Ideas:**
- Many believe Phule introduced the **Marathi word 'dalit'** (meaning "crushed") to describe those outside the varna system.
- Propagated the **Satyashodhak marriage ceremony** — simple, inexpensive, and making the **brahmin priest redundant**.
- His works, **Sarvajanik Satyadharma** and **Gulamgiri**, became sources of inspiration for the common masses.
- Used the symbol of **Rajah Bali** (as opposed to brahmins' symbol of Rama).
- Aimed at the **complete abolition of the caste system** and socio-economic inequalities.
- Was against **Sanskritic Hinduism**.
- Gave a **sense of identity** to depressed communities as a class.

> In **1888**, Jyotiba Phule was given the title of **Mahatma** by **Vithalrao Krishnaji Vandekar**, another Maharashtrian social reformer.

**How Phule Got His Education:**
- Jyotiba was not allowed to continue schooling due to his caste.
- With help from **Persian scholar Ghaffar Baig Munshi** and a **British official, Lizit Sahab**, he was admitted to a **Scottish missionary school**.

---

### Savitribai Phule (1831–1897)

Savitribai Phule was a truly remarkable figure — educator, poet, and social reformer — and the first female teacher in modern India.

**Background:**
- Born in **1831 at Naigaon, Satara district, Maharashtra** (also of the mali community)
- Married to Jyotiba Phule as a **child of nine** (child marriage was prevalent then)
- Jyotiba himself taught her at home. She later completed a **teacher's training course**.

**Her Brave Daily Struggle:**
- She faced **abuse and humiliation** from people on the streets as she walked to school to teach.
- She braved it all and **persisted** — becoming a symbol of courage.

**Her Key Achievements:**
- Taught women and children from **downtrodden castes including Mang and Mahar**
- With Jyotiba, established two educational trusts:
  - **Native Female School, Pune**
  - **Society for Promoting the Education of Mahars, Mangs, and others**
- Started the **Mahila Seva Mandal** to raise awareness about **women's rights**
- Rigorously campaigned against **dehumanisation of widows** and advocated widow remarriage
- **Organised a barbers' strike** to denounce the inhumane practice of shaving widows' heads
- Set up **Balhatya Pratibandhak Griha** (home to prevent infanticide) in **1863** — a shelter where unwed mothers and widows could have safe deliveries instead of resorting to infanticide or suicide
- Set an example against **untouchability** by opening their own water storage to everyone
- After Jyotiba's death, Savitribai took over the **Satya Shodhak Samaj** and worked for **plague victims**
- Died in **1897** after getting infected by the **plague** while caring for a patient — a martyr of service

**Her Literary Works:** Two poetry collections — **Kavyaphule** and **Bavankashi Subodh Ratnakar**

> 📌 In **August 2014**, the **University of Pune was renamed Savitribai Phule Pune University** in her honour.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` Jyotiba and Savitribai Phule's legacy of education for marginalised communities is directly reflected in modern policies. The **Post-Matric Scholarship Scheme for SC/ST students**, the **PM YASASVI scheme (2023)**, and **reservations in educational institutions** under Articles 15(4) and 46 of the Constitution are direct descendants of their philosophy. The Government of India celebrated **January 3rd as Savitribai Phule Jayanti** as a national tribute. `[Modern Example]` The **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020** also emphasises inclusivity and access for marginalised communities — echoing Phule's core vision.

## 🔷 Gopal Baba Walangkar (circa 1840–1900)

- Also known as **Gopal Krishna**
- A **pioneer in the cause of uplifting Dalits** from socio-economic oppression
- Considered by **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** to be the **pioneer of the Dalit movement**
- Born into a **Mahar family near Mahad** (now in Raigad district, Maharashtra)
- Served in the **army until retirement in 1886**
- Deeply influenced by **Jyotiba Phule**

**His Ideology:**
- Subscribed to Phule's **Aryan invasion theory**: untouchable people were the **indigenous inhabitants** of India, and brahmins were descendants of the **invading Aryans**.
- Argued that the **concept of caste was contrived by Aryan invaders** to subjugate the Anaryans (indigenous people).

**His Organisations and Work:**
- Formed a **group of Mahar astrologers** — to empower Mahars and reduce brahmin importance in ceremonies (brahmins only agreed to set ceremony timings for Mahars).
- Founded the **Anarya Dosh-Parihar Mandali** (Society for the Removal of Evils Among the Non-Aryans)
- Through this society, petitioned against the **government's policy of discontinuing Mahar recruitment in the army**
- In **1888**, began publishing the monthly journal **Vital Vidhvansak** (Destroyer of Brahmanical/Ceremonial Pollution)
- In **1889**, published the pamphlet **Vital Viduvansan** (Annihilation of Ceremonial Pollution)
- Also wrote **Hindu Dharma Darpan** (published 1894)
- Contributed to Marathi journals like **Sudharak** and **Deenbandhu** to arouse awareness among the depressed classes

> He has been called *"the first intellectual rebel from the Dalit community to have launched a scathing criticism of the caste system and the position of Dalits in it."*

**His approach** was not direct opposition to elites — it was an **appeal aimed at changing their attitude**. He faced upper-caste opposition when he was appointed to the **local taluk board of Mahad in 1895**.

---

## 🔷 Kisan Faguji Bansod (1879–1946)

- Born in a **Mahar family at Mohapa village near Nagpur, Maharashtra**
- Wanted **Dalit boys and girls** to be educated
- Established the **Chokhamela Girls' School at Nagpur**
- Set up his own **press in 1910** and published journals: **Nirashrit Hind Nagarik, Vital Vidhvansak**, and **Majur Patrika**
- Was one of the secretaries of the **All India Depressed Classes Conference in 1920**
- Influenced by the **Bhakti cult**, the **Brahmo Samaj**, and the **Prarthana Samaj**
- Subscribed to the **Aryan invasion theory** but advocated **reforms within Hinduism** for Dalit upliftment

---

## 🔷 Vitthal Ramji Shinde (1873–1944) — Maharshi

- Born in a **Marathi family in Karnataka**
- Spiritually influenced by works of **Tukaram, Eknath**, and **Ramdas**
- Intellectually influenced by **Hari Narayan Apte, Gopal Ganesh Agarkar, M.G. Ranade, Ramakrishna Gopal Bhandarkar, G.B. Kotkar** and others
- Joined the **Prarthana Samaj** and worked towards the **removal of untouchability**

**Key Work:**
- Established a **night school for untouchable children in Pune in 1905**
- Established the **Depressed Classes Mission in Bombay in 1906** — aimed at removing untouchability, providing education and solving problems of untouchables
- Gave evidence before the **Southborough Franchise Committee in 1919** — expressed the need for **special representation** for untouchable classes
- Favoured a **united front** of the depressed classes and caste Hindus — feared the British would **exploit divisions** otherwise
- Opposed the **caste system, idol worship, inequities on women**, hereditary priesthood, and meaningless rituals
- Participated in the **Civil Disobedience Movement** and was imprisoned in **Yerawda Central Jail**
- Author of **Bharatiya Asprushyatecha Prashna** (The Question of Indian Untouchability)

---

## 🔷 Gopalhari Deshmukh 'Lokahitawadi' (1823–1892)

- A social reformer and **rationalist** from Maharashtra
- Held the post of a **judge under British raj**
- Wrote for the weekly **Prabhakar** under the pen name **'Lokahitawadi'** on social reform issues
- Advocated reorganisation of Indian society on **rational, modern, humanistic, and secular values**
- Attacked **Hindu orthodoxy** and supported social and religious equality
- Wrote against the evils of the **caste system**

> His memorable statement: *"If religion does not sanction social reform, then change religion."*

- Started a weekly, **Hitechhu**, and played a leading role in founding the periodicals **Gyan Prakash, Indu Prakash**, and **Lokahitawadi**

---

## 🔷 Gopal Ganesh Agarkar (1856–1895)

- An educationist and social reformer from Maharashtra
- A strong advocate of the **power of human reason**
- Criticised blind dependence on **tradition** and false glorification of the past
- Co-founder of:
  - **New English School**
  - **Deccan Education Society**
  - **Fergusson College** (and was also its Principal)
- Was the **first editor of Kesari**, the journal started by **Lokmanya Tilak**
- Later started his own periodical, **Sudharak**, which spoke against **untouchability and the caste system**

---

## 🔷 Servants of India Society

- Founded by **Gopal Krishna Gokhale (1866–1915)**, a liberal leader of the Indian National Congress, in **1905** with the help of **M.G. Ranade**.

**Aims:**
- To train **national missionaries** for the service of India
- To promote, by all **constitutional means**, the true interests of the Indian people
- To prepare a cadre of **selfless workers** devoted to the country in a religious spirit

- In **1911**, the **Hitavada** began publication to project the society's views
- The society chose to remain **aloof from political activities** and organisations like the Indian National Congress
- After Gokhale's death in **1915**, **Srinivasa Shastri** took over as president
- **Still functioning today** — works in education, ashram schools for tribal girls, and balwadis

---

## 🔷 Social Service League

- Founded by **Narayan Malhar Joshi** (a follower of Gokhale) in Bombay
- Aim: To secure for the masses **better and reasonable conditions of life and work**

**Activities included:**
- Schools, libraries, reading rooms, day nurseries, and cooperative societies
- Police court agents' work, legal aid and advice to the poor and illiterate
- Excursions for slum dwellers
- Facilities for gymnasia and theatrical performances
- Sanitary work, medical relief, boys' clubs, and scout corps

> Joshi also founded the **All India Trade Union Congress (1920)**.

# PART 3: REFORM MOVEMENTS — ALL-INDIA SIGNIFICANCE

---

## 🔷 The Ramakrishna Movement and Swami Vivekananda

### Ramakrishna Paramahamsa (1836–1886)

While the Brahmo Samaj appealed to the intellectual elite, the average Bengali found more emotional satisfaction in **bhakti (devotion) and yoga**. This need was fulfilled by the teachings of **Ramakrishna Paramahamsa**.

- Born as **Gadadhar Chattopadhyay**, he was a poor priest at the **Kali temple in Dakshineshwar** on the outskirts of Calcutta.
- Experienced **spiritual trances (states of deep ecstasy)** from a very early age.
- Considered to have attained the **highest spiritual experience** available to Hindus.
- Did **not write books** — his conversations with people formed the basis of his teachings.
- Spoke simply through **parables and metaphors** drawn from ordinary life — but of universal relevance.

**His Core Teaching:**
- Recognised the **fundamental oneness of all religions**
- *"Krishna, Hari, Ram, Christ, Allah are different names for the same God"*
- *"There are many ways to God and salvation: As many faiths, so many paths."*
- *"Service of man is the service of God."*

**Two Objectives of the Ramakrishna Movement:**
1. To bring into existence a **band of monks dedicated to renunciation and practical spirituality** from whom teachers and workers would spread the universal message of Vedanta.
2. In conjunction with **lay disciples** to carry on **preaching, philanthropic, and charitable works**, looking upon all people — irrespective of caste, creed, or colour — as **manifestations of the Divine**.

- Paramahamsa himself laid the foundations of the **Ramakrishna Math** with his young monastic disciples.
- He was married to **Saradmani Mukherjee**, later known as **Sarada Devi** — whom Ramakrishna considered the embodiment of the **Divine Mother**. She played an important role in the work of the Math and encouraging young disciples.

---

### Swami Vivekananda (1862–1902)

**Narendranath Datta**, later known as **Swami Vivekananda**, spread Ramakrishna's message and reconciled it to the needs of **contemporary Indian society**. He became the preacher of **neo-Hinduism** (a modern, reformed version of Hinduism).

**Philosophical Foundations:**
- Based his message on the spiritual experiences of Ramakrishna, teachings of the **Upanishads and Gita**, and the examples of **the Buddha and Jesus**
- Subscribed to the **Vedanta** — which he considered a fully rational system with a superior approach
- His mission: to bridge the gap between **paramartha (service)** and **vyavahara (behaviour)**, and between spirituality and day-to-day life.
- Believed in the **fundamental oneness of God**: *"For our own motherland, a junction of the two great systems, Hinduism and Islam, is the only hope."*

**His Social Vision (Key Points):**
- Declared that **knowledge without action is useless**
- Lamented the **isolationist, touch-me-not attitude** of Hindus in religious matters
- Frowned upon religion's tacit approval of **oppression of the poor by the rich**
- Believed it was an **insult to God and humanity** to teach religion to a starving man
- Pointed out that masses needed **two kinds of knowledge**:
  - **Secular knowledge** — for economic uplift
  - **Spiritual knowledge** — faith in themselves and moral strength
- Called upon his countrymen to imbibe a spirit of **liberty, equality, and free thinking**

**The Parliament of Religions, Chicago, 1893:**
- Vivekananda made a **great impression** with his learned interpretations.
- The keynote: the need for a **healthy balance between spiritualism and materialism**
- His vision: blend the **materialism of the West** with the **spiritualism of the East** into a **new harmony** for mankind's happiness.

**Return to India and Founding of the Mission:**
- After lectures on Vedanta in the **USA and London**, he returned to India in **1897**
- In India, his lectures focused on:
  - Infusing **pride in India's past** and **faith in India's culture**
  - Unification of Hinduism by pointing to its common foundation
  - Making the educated see the **misery of the downtrodden** and work for their uplift through **practical Vedanta principles**
- In **1897**, he founded the **Ramakrishna Mission**

> *"So far as Bengal is concerned, Vivekananda may be regarded as the spiritual father of the modern nationalist movement."* — **Subhash Chandra Bose**

**The Ramakrishna Mission:**
- Used for **humanitarian relief and social work**
- Runs schools, hospitals, and dispensaries
- Offers help in natural calamities like **earthquakes, famines, floods, and epidemics**
- Has developed into a **worldwide organisation**
- Is **not a proselytising body** (does not convert people) — a key reason for its success
- Does **not consider itself a sect of Hinduism**
- Unlike the Arya Samaj, it **recognises the utility of image worship** in developing spiritual fervour
- Believes Vedanta philosophy will make *"a Christian a better Christian, and a Hindu a better Hindu"*
- In **1898**, Vivekananda acquired land at **Belur** where the **Ramakrishna Math** was finally shifted and registered. The monastic order is **open to all without discrimination** on basis of caste or creed.
- **Headquarters: Belur, near Calcutta** (for both Ramakrishna Math and Mission — twin organisations, though legally and financially separate)

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The Ramakrishna Mission continues to be one of India's most active humanitarian organisations. During the **COVID-19 pandemic (2020–21)**, the Mission ran extensive relief operations across Bengal and other states, distributing food, medicines, and running medical camps — living proof of Vivekananda's doctrine of "Service of man is the service of God." `[Modern Example]` Vivekananda's speech at Chicago (September 11, 1893) is commemorated as **National Youth Day on January 12** (his birth anniversary), observed across India since 1985.

---

## 🔷 Dayananda Saraswati and the Arya Samaj

### Dayananda Saraswati (1824–1883)

**Mulshankar** (later Dayananda Saraswati) was born in the **old Morvi state in Gujarat** in a brahmin family. He wandered as an **ascetic for 15 years (1845–60)** in search of truth.

- He received education on **Vedanta from a blind teacher named Swami Virajananda in Mathura**.
- The **first Arya Samaj unit** was formally set up at **Bombay in 1875**; headquarters later established at **Lahore**.
- His views were published in his famous work, **Satyarth Prakash** (The True Exposition).

**His Vision for India:**
- A **classless and casteless society**
- A **united India** — religiously, socially, and nationally
- An India **free from foreign rule**
- **Aryan religion** as the common religion of all

**His Slogan: "Back to the Vedas"**

> Important nuance: This was a call for a **revival of Vedic learning and Vedic purity of religion** — NOT a call for a revival of Vedic times. He accepted modernity and displayed a patriotic attitude to national problems.

**His Philosophy:**
- Considered the Vedas to be **"India's Rock of Ages"** — infallible, the true original seed of Hinduism
- Stressed the significance of **individual interpretation** of the scriptures — every person has the **right of access to God**
- Criticised **later Hindu scriptures** (Puranas) and **ignorant priests** for perverting Hinduism
- Strongly criticised the **escapist Hindu belief in maya** (illusion as the reality) and the sole aim of life being **moksha** (salvation) through escape from the world
- Instead advocated that **God, soul, and matter (prakriti)** were distinct and eternal entities — every individual had to **work out his own salvation**
- Attacked the prevalent popular belief in **niyati (destiny)** — that one gets back from society only according to destiny
- Believed in the theory of **karma and reincarnation**, but said good deeds should primarily be **for the good of others, not self**

**His Social Reforms:**
- Launched a **frontal attack** on Hindu orthodoxy, caste rigidities, untouchability, idolatry, polytheism, belief in magic, charms and animal sacrifices, taboo on sea voyages, feeding the dead through shraddhas, etc.
- Subscribed to the **Vedic notion of chaturvarna** — where one was identified as brahmin, kshatriya, vaishya, or shudra **not by birth but by occupation and merit**
- Fixed the minimum marriageable age at **25 years for boys** and **16 years for girls**

---

### The Ten Guiding Principles of the Arya Samaj

1. God is the primary source of all true knowledge
2. God — all-truth, all-knowledge, almighty, immortal, creator of Universe — is alone worthy of worship
3. The Vedas are the books of true knowledge
4. An Arya should always be ready to **accept truth and abandon untruth**
5. **Dharma** (due consideration of right and wrong) should guide all actions
6. The principal aim of the Samaj is to promote world's well-being — materially, spiritually, and socially
7. Everybody should be treated with **love and justice**
8. **Ignorance** is to be dispelled and knowledge increased
9. One's own progress should depend on the **uplift of all others**
10. **Social well-being** of mankind is to be placed above an individual's well-being

---

### The Arya Samaj's Social Ideals

- **Fatherhood of God and Brotherhood of Man**
- **Equality of the sexes**
- **Absolute justice and fair play** between man and man, and nation and nation
- Intercaste marriages and **widow remarriages** encouraged
- Social service in times of calamities — **earthquake, famine, floods**
- Promotion of **education**

---

### The DAV College Controversy and Split of 1893

After Dayananda's death in **1883**, a significant dispute arose over the curriculum of the **Dayananda Anglo-Vedic (D.A.V.) College** established in **1886 at Lahore**.

| Group | Leaders | Position |
|---|---|---|
| **College Party** (also called 'Culture' Party) | Lala Hansraj, Lala Lal Chand, Lala Lajpat Rai | Favoured government curriculum & English education for economic/professional needs; nothing against non-vegetarianism |
| **Mahatma Party** (later Gurukul Party) | Guru Datta Vidyarthi, Lala Munshi Ram (later Swami Shraddhanand) | Wanted Sanskrit, Vedic philosophy in the gurukul tradition; favoured strict vegetarianism for all Aryas |

The **Arya Samaj split in 1893** over these issues.

- The **College Party** retained control of the D.A.V. School and College.
- The **Mahatma Party** took over the **Arya Pratinidhi Sabha, Punjab**, and a majority of local Arya Samaj branches.

**Gurukul Kangri:**
- **Swami Shraddhanand** opened the Gurukul in **1900 at Gujaranwala** (West Punjab, now in Pakistan).
- In **1902**, it was moved to **Kangri near Haridwar** — hence **Gurukul Kangri**.
- Aimed at providing an **indigenous alternative to Lord Macaulay's education policy** — offering vedic literature, Indian philosophy, Indian culture, AND modern sciences.
- Founded the **Kanya Mahavidyalaya at Jalandhar in 1896** and sponsored **education for widows**.

---

### The Shuddhi Movement

- In its zeal to protect Hindu society from Christianity and Islam, the Arya Samaj started the **Shuddhi (purification) movement** — to **reconvert to Hinduism** those who had converted to Christianity or Islam.
- An aggressive shuddhi campaign led to **increasing communalisation** of social life during the 1920s, which later contributed to **communal political consciousness**.
- Shuddhi also attempted to uplift those regarded as **untouchables** and outside the caste system into the mainstream of caste Hindus.

---

## 🔷 Seva Sadan

- Founded in **1908** by **Behramji M. Malabari (1853–1912)**, a **Parsi social reformer**, along with **Diwan Dayaram Gidumal**.
- Malabari spoke vigorously against **child marriage** and for **widow remarriage** among Hindus.
- His efforts led to the **Age of Consent Act** (regulating the age of consent for females).
- Seva Sadan specialised in taking care of **women who were exploited and discarded by society**.
- Catered to **all castes** — provided education, medical, and welfare services to destitute women.
- Behramji Malabari also acquired and edited the **Indian Spectator**.

---

## 🔷 Dev Samaj

- Founded in **1887 at Lahore** by **Shiv Narayan Agnihotri (1850–1927)**, earlier a Brahmo follower.
- A religious and social reform society.

**Key teachings:**
- Eternity of the soul
- Supremacy of the guru
- Need for good action
- Ideal social behaviour: not accepting bribes, avoiding intoxicants and non-vegetarian food, keeping away from violent actions.

- Teachings compiled in the book **Deva Shastra**
- Agnihotri spoke against **child marriage**

---

## 🔷 Dharma Sabha

- Founded by **Radhakant Deb in 1830**
- An **orthodox** society
- Stood for **preservation of the status quo** in socio-religious matters
- Even **opposed the abolition of sati** — a starkly regressive position
- However, **favoured promotion of Western education**, even for girls (a progressive contradiction)

---

## 🔷 Bharat Dharma Mahamandala

- An **all-India organisation of orthodox educated Hindus**
- Stood for **defence of orthodox Hinduism** against the teachings of the Arya Samajists, the Theosophists, and the Ramakrishna Mission

**Similar organisations defending orthodox Hinduism:**
- **Sanatana Dharma Sabha (1895)**
- **Dharma Maha Parishad** in South India
- **Dharma Mahamandali** in Bengal

These combined in **1902** to form the **Bharat Dharma Mahamandala** with headquarters at **Varanasi**.

- Sought to introduce proper management of Hindu religious institutions and open Hindu educational institutions.
- **Pandit Madan Mohan Malaviya** was a prominent figure in this movement.

---

## 🔷 Radhaswami Movement

- Founded by **Tulsi Ram**, a banker from Agra (also known as **Shiv Dayal Saheb**) in **1861**

**Core beliefs:**
- One supreme being
- Supremacy of the guru
- Company of pious people (**satsang**)
- Simple social life
- Spiritual attainment does **not** require renunciation of worldly life
- All religions are considered true
- **No belief in temples, shrines, and sacred places** — but faith, charity, service, and prayer are necessary duties.

---

# PART 4: REFORM MOVEMENTS — SOUTH INDIA

---

## 🔷 SNDP Movement — Sree Narayana Guru

### Context

The **SNDP (Sree Narayana Guru Dharma Paripalana) Movement** was an example of a **regional movement** born out of conflict between the **depressed classes and upper castes**.

### Sree Narayana Guru Swamy (1856–1928)

- Started this movement among the **Ezhavas of Kerala**
- Ezhavas were a **backward caste of toddy-tappers** — considered untouchable, denied education, and barred from temple entry
- They were the **single largest caste group in Kerala, constituting 26% of the total population**
- Narayana Guru himself was from the Ezhava caste

**The Revolutionary Act of 1888:**
On **Sivaratri in 1888**, Narayana Guru took a stone from the **Neyyar river** and installed it as a **Sivalinga at Aruvippuram**.
- This was to show that **consecration of an idol was not the monopoly of higher castes**.
- This began a revolution that led to the **removal of many discriminations** in Kerala's society.

**The Aruvippuram Movement** drew the famous poet **Kumaran Asan** as a disciple of Narayana Guru.

**Formation of SNDP Yogam:**
- In **1889**, the **Aruvippuram Kshetra Yogam** was formed to help Ezhavas progress materially and spiritually.
- The **Aruvippuram Sree Narayana Guru Dharma Paripalana Yogam (SNDP)** was registered in **1903** under the Indian Companies Act.
  - **Narayana Guru**: Permanent Chairman
  - **Kumaran Asan**: General Secretary
  - *(Note: The efforts of **Dr. Palpu** — who had started the fight through movements like **Ezhava Memorial, Malayali Memorial**, etc. — must be acknowledged in the formation of SNDP.)*

**Narayana Guru's Beliefs:**
- Held all religions to be the **same**
- Condemned **animal sacrifice**
- Spoke against divisiveness on the basis of **caste, race, or creed**
- Famous inscription on the wall of Aruvippuram temple: *"Devoid of dividing walls of caste or race, or hatred of rival faith, we all live here in brotherhood."*
- Urged Ezhavas to **leave the toddy-tapping profession** and to **stop drinking liquor**

**Issues taken up by SNDP Yogam:**
1. Right of admission to **public schools**
2. Recruitment to **government services**
3. Access to **roads and entry to temples**
4. **Political representation**

**Overall Impact:**
The movement brought **transformative structural changes** — upward social mobility, shift in traditional distribution of power, and a federation of backward castes into a large conglomeration.

---

## 🔷 Vokkaliga Sangha

- Launched an **anti-brahmin movement in Mysore in 1905**

---

## 🔷 Justice Movement

- Started in **Madras Presidency** by **C.N. Mudaliar, T.M. Nair**, and **P. Tyagaraja**
- Aim: To secure **jobs and representation for the non-brahmins** in the legislature
- In **1917**, the **Madras Presidency Association** was formed — demanded **separate representation for lower castes** in the legislature

---

## 🔷 Self-Respect Movement

- Started by **E.V. Ramaswamy Naicker** (a Balija Naidu) in the **mid-1920s**
- Aimed at nothing short of a **rejection of brahminical religion and culture**, which Naicker felt was the **prime instrument of exploitation of the lower castes**
- Sought to undermine the position of brahmin priests by **formalising weddings without brahmin priests**

---

## 🔷 Temple Entry Movement

**Background:** Significant work had already been done by **Sree Narayana Guru** and **N. Kumaran Asan**.

**T.K. Madhavan**, a prominent social reformer and editor of **Deshabhimani**, took up the issue of temple entry with the **Travancore administration** — but nothing happened initially.

**Vaikom Satyagraha (1924):**
- Centre of agitation: **Vaikom** in the northern part of **Travancore, Kerala**
- Led by **K.P. Kesava**
- Demanded the **throwing open of Hindu temples and roads** to the untouchables
- Satyagraha was reinforced by **jathas from Punjab and Madurai**
- **Gandhi** undertook a tour of Kerala in support of the movement

**1931 Temple Entry Movement:**
- When the Civil Disobedience Movement was suspended, temple entry movement was organised again in Kerala.
- Inspired by **K. Kelappan**, poet **Subramaniyam Tirumambu** (the 'singing sword of Kerala') led a group of **16 volunteers to Guruvayur**.
- Leaders like **P. Krishna Pillai** and **A.K. Gopalan** were among the satyagrahis.

**The Historic Proclamation:**
- On **November 12, 1936**, the **Maharaja of Travancore** issued a proclamation **throwing open all government-controlled temples to all Hindus**.
- A similar step was taken by the **C. Rajagopalachari administration in Madras in 1938**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The spirit of the Temple Entry Movement lives on in modern legal battles. The **Supreme Court's Sabarimala Judgment (Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala, 2018)** ruled that the exclusion of women of menstruating age (10–50 years) from the Sabarimala temple was unconstitutional — a direct continuation of the principle that no group should be discriminated against in places of worship. `[Modern Example]` The **Constitution of India under Article 17** abolishes untouchability, and the **Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955** and **SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989** (amended in 2018) are the legislative heirs of these movements.

---

## 🔷 Indian Social Conference

- Founded by **M.G. Ranade** and **Raghunath Rao**
- Met annually from its **first session in Madras in 1887** — at the same time and venue as the **Indian National Congress**
- Focused on social issues — could be called the **"social reform cell"** of the Indian National Congress
- Advocated **inter-caste marriages**, opposed **polygamy** and **kulinism** (a practice in Bengal where brahmins had multiple wives)
- Launched the **'Pledge Movement'** — to inspire people to take a pledge against **child marriage**

# PART 5: MUSLIM REFORM MOVEMENTS

---

## 🔷 Wahabi / Walliullah Movement

**Inspiration:** The teachings of **Abdul Wahab of Arabia** and the preachings of **Shah Walliullah (1702–63)** inspired this essentially **revivalist response** (going back to basics of Islam) to Western influences and degeneration among Indian Muslims.

**Two-fold ideals of Shah Walliullah's Movement:**
1. **Desirability of harmony** among the four schools of Muslim jurisprudence that had divided Indian Muslims — he sought to integrate the best elements of all four schools
2. **Recognition of the role of individual conscience** in religion where conflicting interpretations were derived from the Quran and the Hadis

**Further Development:**
- Walliullah's teachings were further popularised by **Shah Abdul Aziz** and **Syed Ahmad Barelvi**, who also gave them a **political perspective**
- Un-Islamic practices that had crept into Muslim society were sought to be eliminated
- India was considered **dar-ul-Harb** (land of kafirs/non-believers) and needed to be converted to **dar-ul-Islam** (land of Islam)
- The movement initially targeted the **Sikhs in Punjab**, but after the **British annexation of Punjab (1849)**, it was directed **against the British**
- During the **1857 Revolt**, the Wahabis played an **important role** in spreading anti-British feelings
- The movement **fizzled out** in the face of British military might in the **1870s**

---

## 🔷 Titu Mir's Movement

- **Syed Mir Nisar Ali**, popularly known as **Titu Mir**, was a disciple of **Syed Ahmad Barelvi** of Rae Bareilly — an adherent of the Wahabi movement.
- Adopted **Wahabism** and advocated the **Sharia**
- Organised the **Muslim peasants of Bengal** against landlords (mostly Hindu) and the **British indigo planters**
- The movement was **not as militant** as British records made it out to be; only in the last year of Titu's life was there a confrontation with British police
- **Killed in action in 1831**

---

## 🔷 Faraizi Movement

- Also called the **Fara'idi Movement** (emphasis on Islamic pillars of faith)
- Founded by **Haji Shariatullah in 1819**
- Scene of action: **East Bengal**
- Aimed at the eradication of **social innovations and un-Islamic practices** among the Muslims and drawing their attention to their duties as Muslims

**Under Dudu Mian (Haji's son):**
- From **1840**, the movement became **revolutionary**
- Gave the movement an **organisational system** from the village to the provincial level with a **khalifa (authorised deputy)** at every level
- Organised a **paramilitary force armed with clubs** to fight zamindars (mostly Hindu, some Muslim) and indigo planters
- Asked followers **not to pay rent**
- Established its own **law courts**
- **Dudu Mian** was arrested several times — his arrest in **1847** finally weakened the movement
- After Dudu Mian's death in **1862**, the movement survived only as a **religious movement without political overtones**

---

## 🔷 Ahmadiyya Movement

- The Ahmadiyya forms a **sect of Islam** which originated in India.
- Founded by **Mirza Ghulam Ahmad in 1889**
- Based on **liberal principles**
- Described itself as the **"standard-bearer of Mohammedan Renaissance"**
- Like the Brahmo Samaj, based itself on the principles of **universal religion of all humanity**
- **Opposed jihad** (sacred war against non-Muslims)
- Spread **Western liberal education** among Indian Muslims
- The Ahmadiyya community is the **only Islamic sect** to believe that the **Messiah had come in the person of Mirza Ghulam Ahmad** — to end religious wars, reinstate morality, peace, and justice
- Believed in **separating the mosque from the State**, and in **human rights and tolerance**
- However, suffered from **mysticism** — like Baha'ism which flourished in West Asian countries

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The Ahmadiyya Movement remains **controversial in the Muslim world**. In **Pakistan**, Ahmadis were declared non-Muslims by constitutional amendment in 1974 and face severe legal discrimination. In India, they are free to practise their faith, though they face social hostility in some areas. This contrast illustrates the ongoing relevance of debates about **minority rights, religious freedom (Article 25 of the Indian Constitution)**, and the legacy of 19th-century reform movements. `[Modern Example]` The **Supreme Court of India** has consistently held (e.g., in the **Shirur Mutt case**) that essential religious practices are protected under Article 25 — echoing the reform movements' insistence on individual conscience and reason over blind orthodoxy.

---

## 🔷 Sir Syed Ahmed Khan and the Aligarh Movement

### The Context

After 1857, the British held **Muslims to be the main conspirators** behind the revolt. The activities of the Wahabis strengthened this view. Later, however, an opinion gained ground that **Muslims could be used as allies** against rising nationalist political activity (exemplified by the Indian National Congress). This was to be achieved through **thoughtful concessions** to the Muslims.

### Sir Syed Ahmed Khan (1817–1898)

- Born in a **respectable Muslim family**
- A **loyalist member of the judicial service** of the British government
- After retirement in **1876**, became a member of the **Imperial Legislative Council in 1878**
- His loyalty earned him a **knighthood in 1888**

**His Educational Vision:**
- Wanted to reconcile **Western scientific education** with the teachings of the **Quran** — to be interpreted in the light of **contemporary rationalism and science**
- Held the Quran to be the **ultimate authority**, but advocated a **critical approach and freedom of thought**
- Said that religion should be **adaptable with time**, or else it would become **fossilised**, and religious tenets were not immutable
- As an official, he opened **schools in towns**, got books translated into **Urdu**
- Started the **Mohammedan Anglo-Oriental College** (later the **Aligarh Muslim University**) at **Aligarh in 1875**

**His Social Reforms:**
- Struggled to improve the **position of women** through better education
- Opposed **purdah and polygamy**
- Advocated **easy divorce**
- Condemned the system of **piri and muridi** (spiritual leader-follower relationship that had become exploitative)
- Believed in the **fundamental underlying unity of religions** ('practical morality')
- Preached the **basic commonality of Hindu and Muslim interests**

**His Political Strategy:**
- Argued that Muslims should first **concentrate on education and jobs** and catch up with their Hindu counterparts who had an early start with Western education
- Felt that active participation in politics at that point would **invite government hostility** towards Muslim masses
- Therefore, he **opposed political activity** by Muslims in the short term

**The Shadow Side:**
- In his enthusiasm to promote Muslim educational and employment interests, Syed Khan **allowed himself to be used by the colonial government** in its divide and rule policy
- In later years, he began **propagating divergence of interests** between Hindus and Muslims — a tragic turn

**His Progressive Ideas were propagated through:** The magazine **Tahdhib-ul-Akhlaq** (Improvement of Manners and Morals)

---

### The Aligarh Movement

- Emerged as a **liberal, modern trend** among the Muslim intelligentsia based in the Mohammedan Anglo-Oriental College, Aligarh

**Aims:**
1. Spreading **modern education** among Indian Muslims without weakening their allegiance to Islam
2. Social reforms among Muslims — relating to **purdah, polygamy, widow remarriage, women's education, slavery, divorce**, etc.

**Ideology:** Based on a **liberal interpretation of the Quran** — seeking to harmonise Islam with modern liberal culture, and imparting a distinct **socio-cultural identity** to Muslims on modern lines.

> Soon, Aligarh became the **centre of religious and cultural revival** of the Muslim community.

---

## 🔷 The Deoband School (Darul Uloom)

**Context:** In contrast to the Aligarh Movement (which sought Western education and British support), the Deoband Movement was organised by the **orthodox section among the Muslim ulema** as a **revivalist movement**.

**Two objectives:**
1. Propagating **pure teachings of the Quran and Hadis** among Muslims
2. Keeping alive the **spirit of jihad** against the foreign rulers

**Founders and Date:**
- Begun at **Darul Uloom (Islamic academic centre), Deoband**, in **Saharanpur district, United Provinces** in **1866**
- Founded by **Muhammad Qasim Nanautavi (1832–80)** and **Rashid Ahmad Gangohi (1828–1905)**
- Aim: To train **religious leaders** for the Muslim community
- The instruction imparted was in **original Islamic religion**

**Political Stance:**
- Welcomed the formation of the **Indian National Congress**
- In **1888**, issued a **fatwa (religious decree)** against Syed Ahmed Khan's organisations — the **United Patriotic Association** and the **Mohammaden Anglo-Oriental Association**
- *(Some critics say Deoband's support to nationalists was more about its opposition to Syed Ahmed Khan than any positive political philosophy.)*

**Mahmud-ul-Hasan** — the new Deoband leader — gave **political and intellectual content** to the school's religious ideas. He worked out a synthesis of **Islamic principles and nationalist aspirations**. The **Jamiat-ul-Ulema** gave concrete shape to his ideas of protecting the religious and political rights of Muslims in the context of **Indian unity and national objectives**.

**Shibli Numani** — a supporter of the Deoband school — favoured including **English language and European sciences** in education. He founded the **Nadwatal Ulama** and **Darul Uloom at Lucknow in 1894–96**. He believed in the **idealism of the Congress** and in **cooperation between Muslims and Hindus** to create a state in which both could live amicably.

# PART 6: PARSI AND SIKH REFORM MOVEMENTS

---

## 🔷 Parsi Reform Movements

- The **Rahnumai Mazdayasnan Sabha** (Religious Reform Association) was founded in **1851** by a group of **English-educated Parsis**
- Purpose: *"Regeneration of the social conditions of the Parsis and the restoration of the Zoroastrian religion to its pristine purity"*

**Key Leaders:** Naoroji Furdonji, **Dadabhai Naoroji**, K.R. Cama, and S.S. Bengalee

**Message spread through:** The newspaper **Rast Goftar** (Truth-Teller)

**Reforms achieved:**
- Parsi religious rituals and practices were **reformed** and the Parsi creed **redefined**
- In the social sphere: upliftment of Parsi women through:
  - Removal of the **purdah system**
  - Raising the **age of marriage**
  - **Education** for women

> Gradually, the Parsis emerged as the **most westernised section of Indian society**

---

## 🔷 Sikh Reform Movements

### Singh Sabha Movement

- Founded at **Amritsar in 1873**

**Two-fold objective:**
1. To make available **modern Western education** to the Sikhs
2. To counter the **proselytising activities** of Christian missionaries, Brahmo Samajists, Arya Samajists, and Muslim maulvis

**For the first objective:** A network of **Khalsa schools** was established throughout Punjab.

**For the second objective:** Everything that went against the Gurus' teachings was rejected; rites consistent with **Sikh doctrine** were promoted.

---

### Akali Movement (Gurudwara Reform Movement)

- An **offshoot of the Singh Sabha Movement**
- Aim: To liberate Sikh gurudwaras from the control of **corrupt Udasi mahants** (the post had become hereditary)
- These mahants were a **loyalist and reactionary** lot, enjoying government patronage

**The Satyagraha:**
- Akalis launched a **non-violent non-cooperation satyagraha in 1921**
- The government used **repressive policies** against it
- But the government had to **bow before popular demands**

**The Resolution:**
- The government passed the **Sikh Gurudwaras Act in 1922** (amended in **1925**)
- This gave the control of gurudwaras to the **Sikh masses**
- Administered through the **Shiromani Gurudwara Prabandhak Committee (SGPC)** as the apex body

> The Akali Movement was a **regional movement but NOT a communal one**. Akali leaders played a notable role in the **national liberation struggle**, though some dissenting voices were heard occasionally.

---

# PART 7: THE THEOSOPHICAL MOVEMENT

---

## 🔷 The Theosophical Society

- Founded by **Madame H.P. Blavatsky (1831–91)** and **Colonel M.S. Olcott** — a group of **Westerners** inspired by Indian thought and culture
- Founded in **New York City, United States, in 1875**
- In **1882**, shifted headquarters to **Adyar, on the outskirts of Madras (Chennai), India**

**Core Beliefs:**
- A special relationship could be established between a **person's soul and God** through contemplation, prayer, revelation, etc.
- Accepted the Hindu beliefs in **reincarnation and karma**
- Drew inspiration from the **Upanishads** and the **Samkhya, Yoga, and Vedanta** schools of thought

**Aims:**
- Work for **universal brotherhood of humanity** without distinction of race, creed, sex, caste, or colour
- Investigate the **unexplained laws of nature** and the powers latent in man

**Social positions:**
- Opposed **child marriage**
- Advocated the **abolition of caste discrimination**
- Worked for the **uplift of widows**

**Annie Besant (1847–1933):**
- Came to India in **1893**
- Became the movement's president after the death of Olcott in **1907**, making the movement **somewhat popular in India**
- Laid the foundation of the **Central Hindu College in Benaras in 1898** — taught both Hindu religion and Western scientific subjects
- This college became the **nucleus for the formation of Benaras Hindu University in 1916**
- Did much for the cause of **education of women**

**Assessment of the Theosophical Movement:**

| Positive | Negative |
|---|---|
| Provided a common denominator for various Hindu sects | Philosophy seemed vague and lacking a positive programme — limited impact on common people |
| Fulfilled the urge of educated Hindus | Limited to a small segment of the westernised class |
| As Westerners glorifying Indian traditions, gave much-needed **self-respect** to Indians fighting British colonial rule | Also gave a **false sense of pride** in outdated and backward-looking traditions |

# PART 8: SIGNIFICANCE OF THE REFORM MOVEMENTS

---

## ✅ Positive Aspects

The orthodox sections of society could not accept the scientific onslaught of the socio-religious rebels. Despite abuse, persecution, fatwas, and even assassination attempts, these movements succeeded in contributing to:

- **Liberation of the individual** from conformity born out of fear and uncritical submission to priestly exploitation.
- The translation of religious texts into **vernacular languages**, emphasis on the **individual's right** to interpret scriptures, and simplification of rituals made worship a **more personal experience**.
- Movements emphasised the **human intellect's capacity to think and reason**.
- By weeding out corrupt elements, the reformers enabled their followers to **counter the colonial taunt** that their religions and society were decadent and inferior.
- Gave the rising middle classes the much-needed **cultural roots** — and served to reduce the **sense of humiliation** produced by conquest by a foreign power.
- Promoted a **modern, this-worldly, secular, and rational outlook** — reflected socially in a basic change in the notions of **'pollution and purity'**.
- Although traditional values were targeted, the reformers aimed at **modernisation, NOT outright westernisation** based on blind imitation of alien Western cultural values.
- Ended India's **cultural and intellectual isolation** from the rest of the world.
- The underlying concern was the **revival of native cultural personality** which had been distorted over the years.
- This cultural-ideological struggle proved to be an **important instrument in the evolution of national consciousness** and India's resolve to resist **colonial cultural and ideological hegemony**.

---

## ❌ Negative Aspects

- **Narrow social base** — the movements primarily catered to the **educated urban middle classes**; the vast masses of peasantry and the urban poor were largely ignored.
- The tendency to appeal to the **greatness of the past** and rely on scriptural authority encouraged **mysticism in new garbs** and fostered **pseudo-scientific thinking** — acting as a check on fully embracing the modern scientific outlook.
- These tendencies contributed — at least to some extent — to **compartmentalising Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, and Parsis**, and also **alienating high-caste Hindus from low-caste Hindus**.
- **Overemphasis on religious and philosophical aspects** of cultural heritage, while insufficiently emphasising art, architecture, literature, music, science, and technology.
- Hindu reformers confined their praise to **ancient Indian history** and looked upon the **medieval period as an era of decadence** — tending to create a notion of **two separate peoples** (Hindus and Muslims).
- **Uncritical praise of the past** was not acceptable to low-caste sections who had suffered under **religiously sanctioned exploitation** during the ancient period.
- The past itself was placed into **partisan compartments** — many in the Muslim middle class turned to the **history of West Asia** for their traditions and pride.
- The process of evolution of a **composite culture** (visible throughout Indian history) showed signs of being **arrested** with the rise of **communal consciousness** alongside national consciousness.
- These reform movements **undoubtedly contributed to the birth of communalism** in modern India — even if they were not the sole cause.

> **But on the whole:** It was out of this struggle that a **new society evolved in India**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The mixed legacy of reform movements — building national consciousness while inadvertently fostering communal identities — remains deeply relevant today. The tension between religious identity and national identity continues to manifest in debates around the **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA, 2019)**, the **National Register of Citizens (NRC)**, and questions about **Muslim Personal Law reform** (e.g., the **Triple Talaq judgment by the Supreme Court in Shayara Bano v. Union of India, 2017**, which declared instant triple talaq unconstitutional). The Arya Samaj's Shuddhi movement's legacy can be seen in ongoing debates about religious conversions and the **Freedom of Religion Acts** passed by several state governments between 2020 and 2023. The challenge of building a truly inclusive national identity — as dreamt of by Vivekananda, Phule, and Rammohan Roy — remains India's central unfinished business.
