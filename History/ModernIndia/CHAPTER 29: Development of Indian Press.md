# 📰 Chapter 29: Development of Indian Press

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **James Augustus Hickey** started India's first newspaper, *The Bengal Gazette*, in **1780** — and it was seized just 2 years later for criticizing the government.
> 2. The British enacted a **series of press laws** (1799 to 1931) to silence Indian voices, especially vernacular (local language) newspapers.
> 3. **Nationalist journalists** used the press as their most powerful weapon — not for profit, but as a tool for political education and mobilising public opinion.
> 4. The **Vernacular Press Act, 1878** was the most discriminatory law — it targeted only Indian-language newspapers and is nicknamed the **"Gagging Act."**
> 5. **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** became the symbol of the fight for press freedom — he was tried, jailed, and eventually transported to Burma, but emerged as a national hero.

---

## 🗞️ Section 1: The Birth of the Indian Press

### India's First Newspaper

- **James Augustus Hickey** launched **The Bengal Gazette** (also called *Calcutta General Advertiser*) in **1780** — this was the **first newspaper in India**.
- It was **seized in 1782** because it openly criticised the British government.
- The press grew quickly after this. More newspapers and journals followed:
  - *The Bengal Journal*
  - *The Calcutta Chronicle*
  - *The Madras Courier*
  - *The Bombay Herald*

### Why the British Worried

- Company officers feared these newspapers would **reach London** and **expose their corruption and misdeeds**.
- This fear of exposure became the primary reason for introducing **curbs (restrictions) on the press**.

---

## ⚖️ Section 2: Early Press Regulations (Timeline of Repression)

### 1. Censorship of Press Act, 1799

- Enacted by **Lord Wellesley**, who feared a **French invasion of India**.
- Imposed almost **wartime-level restrictions**, including **pre-censorship** (meaning the government had to approve content *before* it was published).
- These restrictions were later **relaxed under Lord Hastings**, who held **progressive views**.
- By **1818**, pre-censorship was **dispensed with (removed)**.

---

### 2. Licensing Regulations, 1823

- Enacted by **John Adams**, the acting governor general, who had **reactionary views** (meaning he strongly opposed change or reform).
- Key provisions:
  - Starting or using a press **without a licence** was a **penal (criminal) offence**.
  - The act was later extended to cover **journals, pamphlets, and books**.
- These restrictions were **specifically aimed at Indian-language newspapers** or those edited by Indians.
- **Impact:** **Rammohan Roy's** famous journal ***Mirat-ul-Akbar*** had to **stop publication** because of this act.

---

### 3. Press Act of 1835 (Metcalfe Act)

- **Metcalfe** served as governor general from **1835 to 1836**.
- He **repealed** the harsh 1823 ordinance and earned the title **"Liberator of the Indian Press."**
- The new **Press Act (1835)** simply required a printer or publisher to:
  - Give a **precise account of the premises** of a publication.
  - **Cease functioning**, if required, by making a similar declaration.
- **Result:** A liberal press policy led to a **rapid growth of newspapers** across India.

---

### 4. Licensing Act, 1857

- Passed due to the **emergency caused by the 1857 Revolt**.
- Imposed **licensing restrictions** on top of the existing registration process from the Metcalfe Act.
- The government reserved the right to **stop publication and circulation** of any:
  - Book
  - Newspaper
  - Printed matter

---

### 5. Registration Act, 1867

- This act **replaced Metcalfe's Act of 1835**.
- It was **regulatory** (rules-based), **not restrictive** (not designed to suppress).
- Key rules under this act:
  - Every book or newspaper had to **print the name** of the printer, publisher, and place of publication.
  - A **copy had to be submitted** to the local government **within one month** of publishing a book.

---

## ✊ Section 3: Early Nationalists Fight for Press Freedom

### Press as a Political Weapon

- From the **early 19th century**, **freedom of the press** was a top priority in the nationalist agenda.
- As early as **1824**, **Raja Rammohan Roy** had **protested** against a resolution that restricted press freedom.

### The Nationalist Press Strategy (1870–1918)

The early phase of the nationalist movement (roughly **1870 to 1918**) focused on:

- **Political propaganda and education**
- **Forming and spreading nationalist ideology**
- **Arousing, training, mobilising, and consolidating public opinion**

Rather than open mass agitation, they relied primarily on the press as their key tool.

> 🔑 **Key Point:** The **Indian National Congress**, in its early days, relied **solely on the press** to propagate its resolutions and proceedings.

---

### The Great Nationalist Newspapers and Their Editors

Many newspapers emerged during this period under distinguished and fearless journalists:

| Newspaper | Editor / Leader |
|---|---|
| *The Hindu* and *Swadesamitran* | **G. Subramania Aiyar** |
| *The Bengalee* | **Surendranath Banerjea** |
| *Voice of India* | **Dadabhai Naoroji** |
| *Amrita Bazar Patrika* | **Sisir Kumar Ghosh** and **Motilal Ghosh** |
| *Indian Mirror* | **N.N. Sen** |
| *Kesari* (Marathi) and *Mahratta* (English) | **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** |
| *Sudharak* | **Gopal Ganesh Agarkar** |
| *Hindustan* and *Advocate* | **G.P. Verma** |

**Other important newspapers:**

- **Punjab:** *Tribune* and *Akbhar-i-am*
- **Bombay:** *Gujarati*, *Indu Prakash*, *Dhyan Prakash*, and *Kal*
- **Bengal:** *Som Prakash*, *Banganivasi*, and *Sadharani*

---

### Press as National Service, Not Business

These newspapers were **not profit-making ventures** — they were seen as **national and public service**.

- They had a **wide reach**, even stimulating a **library movement** in India.
- Their impact was **not limited to cities and towns** — newspapers reached **remote villages**.
- In villages, a **single newspaper** would be read and discussed thoroughly in **'local libraries'** — informal community gatherings.
- These local libraries served the dual purpose of:
  - **Political education**
  - **Political participation**

### Press as Opposition

- In these newspapers, **government acts and policies** were put to **critical scrutiny**.
- They functioned as an **institution of opposition** to the British government.

---

### How Journalists Outsmarted the Law

The government had enacted harsh laws, including:

> **Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code (IPC):** Anyone trying to cause **disaffection (hatred or disloyalty) against the British Government** could be **transported for life** or **imprisoned up to three years**.

But nationalist journalists were clever. They evolved strategies to **subvert (bypass) these legal hurdles**:

- Writings hostile to the government were **prefaced (introduced) with statements of loyalty** to the government.
- **Critical writings of socialists or Irish nationalists** from newspapers in England were **quoted** — since it was hard to prosecute someone for quoting foreign press.

> This required an **intelligent mix of simplicity with subtlety** — a genuinely difficult skill.

## 🔇 Section 4: The Vernacular Press Act, 1878 — The "Gagging Act"

### Background: Why Was It Passed?

- A bitter legacy of the **1857 Revolt** was **racial bitterness** between the ruler and the ruled.
- After **1858**, the **European press** always **supported the government** in political controversies.
- The **vernacular press** (Indian-language newspapers), on the other hand, was **critical of the government**.
- Public opinion was strongly against:
  - The **imperialistic policies of Lord Lytton**
  - The terrible **famine of 1876–77**
  - The **lavish expenditure** on the imperial **Delhi Durbar** — all happening at the same time.
- Indian newspapers were **highly critical** of Lord Lytton's administration, particularly his **inhuman treatment of famine victims**.
- The government struck back with the **Vernacular Press Act (VPA), 1878**.

---

### What Did the VPA Do?

The VPA was designed to **"better control"** the vernacular press and **punish "seditious writing"** (writing that incites rebellion) in **"publications in oriental languages."**

**Key Provisions:**

1. The **district magistrate** was empowered to call upon any vernacular newspaper's **printer and publisher** to sign a bond (agreement) with the government, promising:
   - Not to cause **disaffection against the government**
   - Not to create **antipathy (hatred) between persons of different religions, caste, or race**
   - They could also be asked to **deposit security** (a sum of money).
   - If they violated the rules, the security was **forfeited (taken away)**, and if the offence happened again, the **press equipment could be seized**.

2. The **magistrate's action was final** — **no appeal could be made in a court of law**. *(This was deeply unjust — there was no judicial check.)*

3. A vernacular newspaper could get **exemption** from the act by submitting **proofs to a government censor** before publication (i.e., pre-censorship was back).

---

### Why Was It Called the "Gagging Act"?

The act was widely nicknamed the **"Gagging Act"** because it effectively **silenced (gagged) the vernacular press**.

**The two worst features of this act:**
1. **Discrimination between English and vernacular press** — English newspapers had full freedom while Indian-language papers were targeted.
2. **No right of appeal** — the magistrate's word was final.

---

### Impact and Response

- Proceedings were instituted against: **Som Prakash, Bharat Mihir, Dacca Prakash,** and **Samachar**.
- **Amrita Bazar Patrika** famously **turned overnight into an English newspaper** to escape the VPA!
- Later, the **pre-censorship clause was repealed**, and a **press commissioner** was appointed to supply authentic news to the press.
- There was **strong public opposition** to the act.
- Finally, **Lord Ripon repealed it in 1882**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The VPA's principle of **discriminatory treatment of media** finds a modern echo in debates around the **IT Rules, 2021** (Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021), which imposed a regulatory framework on digital news portals and OTT platforms. Critics argued these rules created **asymmetric regulation** — treating digital-native media differently from print. The **Supreme Court** stayed some provisions of these rules in **2021**, with the Kerala High Court also questioning whether they violated press freedom under **Article 19(1)(a)** of the Constitution.

## 🔒 Section 5: Continued Repression Against Nationalist Journalists

### Surendranath Banerjea — First Indian Journalist Imprisoned

- In **1883**, **Surendranath Banerjea** became the **first Indian journalist to be imprisoned**.
- In an angry editorial in ***The Bengalee***, he had criticised a **judge of the Calcutta High Court** for being **insensitive to the religious sentiments of Bengalis** in one of his judgements.

---

### Bal Gangadhar Tilak — The Face of the Fight for Press Freedom

**Tilak** is the figure **most frequently associated** with the nationalist fight for press freedom. His actions to build public anti-imperialist sentiment included:

- Starting **Ganapati festivals** (from **1893**)
- Starting **Shivaji festivals** (from **1896**)
- Editing **Kesari** (Marathi) and **Maharatta** (English)
- Being among the **first to advocate** bringing the **lower middle classes, peasants, artisans, and workers** into the Congress fold.

**His major campaigns:**
- **1896:** Organised an **all-Maharashtra campaign for boycott of foreign cloth** against the imposition of excise duty on cotton.
- **1896–97:** Initiated a **no-tax campaign in Maharashtra**, urging farmers to withhold revenue payment if their crop had failed.

---

### The Poona Plague and Tilak's Arrest (1897)

- In **1897**, **plague broke out in Poona**.
- Although **Tilak supported government measures** to check the plague, there was massive public resentment against the **harsh and heartless methods** used — such as **segregation** and **house searches**.
- Popular unrest resulted in the **murder of the chairman of the Plague Committee in Poona by the Chapekar brothers**.
- Government policies on **tariff, currency, and famine** also fuelled this resentment.

**Government Action Against Tilak:**
- The government had been looking for an opportunity to **suppress this militant trend**.
- Tilak was arrested after the murder of **Rand** (the Plague Committee chairman).
- The charges were based on:
  - Publication of a poem, **'Shivaji's Utterances'**, in *Kesari*.
  - A speech at the **Shivaji festival** where Tilak justified **Afzal Khan's murder by Shivaji**.
- The prosecution portrayed Tilak's historical defence of Shivaji's action as an **incitement to kill British officials**.
- **Verdict:** Tilak was found **guilty** and awarded **rigorous imprisonment of 18 months**.
- Several other editors in the Bombay presidency were tried and given similarly harsh sentences.
- **Outcome:** Overnight, Tilak became a **national hero** and was given the title **'Lokmanya'** — *respected and honoured by the people*.

---

### 1898: More Legal Tightening

- In **1898**, the government **amended Section 124A** and added a new **Section 153A** to the Penal Code.
- Section 153A made it a **criminal offence** to:
  - Bring into **contempt the Government of India**
  - Create **hatred among different classes** — especially vis-à-vis the English in India.
- This too led to **nationwide protests**.

> 💬 *"The government has converted the entire nation into a prison and we are all prisoners. Going to prison only means that from a big cell one is confined to a smaller one."*
> — **Bal Gangadhar Tilak**

## 📜 Section 6: Repressive Laws During the Swadeshi Era and Beyond

### Newspaper (Incitement to Offences) Act, 1908

- Aimed **against Extremist nationalist activity** during the **Swadeshi and Boycott Movement**.
- Empowered **magistrates to confiscate press property** that published objectionable material **likely to cause incitement to murder or acts of violence**.
- **Tilak**, as leader of militant nationalists, was tried on charges of **sedition** and **transported to Mandalay (Burma) for six years**.
- This led to **countrywide protests**.
- In **Bombay**, **textile workers and railway workshop workers** took on the army in the streets and went on **strike for days**.
- **Lenin** hailed this event as the **entrance of the Indian working class on the political stage**.

---

### Indian Press Act, 1910

- This act **revived the worst features of the Vernacular Press Act (VPA)**.

**Key provisions:**
- The **local government** was empowered to demand **security at registration** from the printer/publisher.
- Security could be **forfeited** or the paper **deregistered** if deemed an offending newspaper.
- The printer of a newspaper was required to **submit two copies of each issue** to the local government **free of charge**.

**Impact (According to Reba Chaudhuri):**
- The **Press Association of India** stated that **nearly 1,000 papers** were prosecuted under this act.
- The total amount of **securities forfeited** in the first **five years** was nearly **Rs 5 lakh** (per official returns of 1918).
- **Over 500 publications** were proscribed (banned) under the act.

---

### During and After the First World War

- **Defence of India Rules** were imposed to repress political agitation and free public criticism during **World War I**.
- In **1921**, on the recommendations of a **Press Committee chaired by Tej Bahadur Sapru**, both the **Press Acts of 1908 and 1910 were repealed**. ✅

---

### Indian Press (Emergency Powers) Act, 1931

- Gave **sweeping powers to provincial governments** to suppress propaganda for the **Civil Disobedience Movement**.
- Further **amplified in 1932** to include **all activities calculated to undermine government authority**.

---

### During the Second World War

- Under the **Defence of India Rules**:
  - **Pre-censorship was imposed** again.
  - Amendments were made to the **Press Emergency Act** and the **Official Secrets Act**.
  - At one point, **publication of all news related to Congress activity was declared illegal**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The struggle for **press freedom** that nationalists fought against in the colonial era continues to be relevant today. According to the **World Press Freedom Index 2024** (published by Reporters Without Borders), **India ranked 159th out of 180 countries** — a significant decline from earlier rankings. Key concerns include:
> - The use of laws like the **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA)** and **Sedition Law (Section 124A IPC)** against journalists.
> - In **2022**, the **Supreme Court stayed** the operation of **Section 124A (Sedition Law)** and directed the government to reconsider it, noting its colonial origins — a direct echo of the Tilak-era prosecutions.
> - The **Press and Registration of Periodicals Act, 2023** replaced the colonial-era **Press and Registration of Books Act, 1867** (the Registration Act), modernising the regulatory framework for print media.
>
> `[Modern Example]` The **Editors Guild of India** and the **Indian Journalists Union** continue to advocate for a dedicated **Media Freedom law** — connecting directly to the unfinished agenda that journalists like Tilak, Banerjea, and Subramania Aiyar fought for over a century ago.

## 🧩 Concept Clarity Corner

*(Quick explanations for technical terms used in this chapter)*

| Term | Simple Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Pre-censorship** | Government approves content *before* it is published |
| **Sedition** | Speech or writing that incites rebellion against the state |
| **Proscribed** | Officially banned or forbidden by the government |
| **Disaffection** | Active hostility or disloyalty towards the government |
| **Vernacular Press** | Newspapers published in Indian regional languages |
| **Bond/Security** | Money deposited as a guarantee of good behaviour |
| **Reactionary Views** | Strongly opposing political or social change/reform |
| **Forfeiture** | The government takes away money or property as a penalty |
