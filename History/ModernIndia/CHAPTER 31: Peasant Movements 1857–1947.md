# 🌾 Chapter 31: Peasant Movements 1857–1947

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **Colonial policies** destroyed the traditional agrarian (farming-based) structure of India, pushing peasants into extreme poverty, debt, and landlessness.
> 2. Early peasant movements (**Indigo Revolt, Pabna Leagues, Deccan Riots**) were **economic in nature** — they targeted immediate oppressors, not colonialism itself.
> 3. After 1900, peasant movements became increasingly **linked to the national freedom struggle** — from Champaran to Bardoli to the All India Kisan Sabha.
> 4. The **Vernacular Press Act** mentality extended to agrarian life — the British consistently used repressive laws to crush peasant resistance.
> 5. The **Telangana Movement** (1946–48) was the **biggest peasant guerrilla war** in modern Indian history, affecting **3,000 villages and 3 million people**.

---

## 🌱 Section 1: The Peasantry Under Colonialism — Why Did They Suffer?

### Root Causes of Peasant Impoverishment

The suffering of Indian peasants was not accidental — it was a **direct result of structural changes** brought about by British colonial rule. The four key causes were:

- **Colonial economic policies** that extracted maximum wealth from India
- **Ruin of handicrafts** (traditional crafts destroyed by cheap British imports), which led to **overcrowding of agricultural land** as artisans had nowhere else to go
- The **new land revenue system** (which demanded fixed cash payments regardless of harvest)
- The **colonial administrative and judicial system** that favoured landlords and moneylenders over peasants

### What Did Peasants Suffer?

**In Zamindari areas** (where landlords collected rent on behalf of the British):
- High rents
- Illegal levies (extra, unauthorised charges)
- Arbitrary evictions (being thrown off land without reason or notice)
- Unpaid labour (being forced to work for free)

**In Ryotwari areas** (where peasants paid revenue directly to the government):
- The **government itself** levied heavy land revenue

### The Debt Trap

The overburdened farmer, fearing loss of his only livelihood, would approach the **local moneylender**. The moneylender exploited the farmer's desperation by:

- Charging **very high rates of interest**
- Forcing the farmer to **mortgage his land and cattle**
- Sometimes **seizing the mortgaged belongings**

Over time, across large areas of India, actual cultivators were reduced to the status of:
- **Tenants-at-will** (could be evicted any time)
- **Sharecroppers** (giving a share of crops as rent)
- **Landless labourers** (no land at all)

### Peasant Resistance — From Protest to Crime

- Peasants often **resisted exploitation**.
- They gradually realised their **real enemy was the colonial state**.
- In desperate situations, some peasants turned to:
  - **Robbery**
  - **Dacoity**
  - What historians call **"social banditry"** (targeted crimes against oppressors seen as a form of popular justice)

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The colonial debt trap of the Indian peasant finds a direct parallel in the **agrarian distress crisis** of post-independence India. According to the **National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) data**, India recorded **5,563 farmer suicides in 2022**, with debt being cited as a primary cause. The **PM Kisan Samman Nidhi Yojana (PM-KISAN)**, launched in 2019, attempts to address this by providing **₹6,000 per year** directly to small and marginal farmers' bank accounts — a structural response to the same debt vulnerability that colonial-era peasants faced.
>
> `[Modern Example]` The **Swaminathan Commission Report** (submitted 2006, partially implemented after 2014) recommended fixing **Minimum Support Price (MSP) at 1.5 times the cost of production** — directly addressing the "exploitative pricing" dynamic that drove peasant movements in the 19th and 20th centuries. The **farmers' protest of 2020–21** demanding a legal guarantee for MSP echoed the same core demand: protection from exploitative market forces.

## 📜 Section 2: Early Peasant Movements — A Survey

### 2A. The Indigo Revolt (1859–60)

#### Background

- In **Bengal**, indigo planters — **nearly all Europeans** — exploited local peasants.
- They **forced peasants to grow indigo** (a blue dye crop) on their lands instead of more profitable crops like **rice**.
- Methods of coercion included:
  - Forcing peasants to **take advance sums** and sign **fraudulent contracts** that were then used against them
  - **Kidnappings** and **illegal confinements**
  - **Flogging** (beating)
  - Attacks on **women and children**
  - **Seizure of cattle**
  - **Burning and demolition of houses**
  - **Destruction of crops**

#### The Revolt

- Peasant anger **exploded in 1859**.
- Led by **Digambar Biswas** and **Bishnu Biswas** of **Nadia district**, peasants:
  - **Refused to grow indigo under duress**
  - **Resisted physically** the planters and their **lathiyals** (armed retainers) backed by police and courts
  - **Organised a counter-force** against planter attacks
  - Went on a **rent strike** when planters tried evictions and enhanced rents
  - **Refused to pay enhanced rents**
  - Physically resisted eviction attempts
  - **Learned to use the legal system** — filed legal cases supported by community fund-raising

#### Role of Intelligentsia

The **Bengali intelligentsia** (educated class) played a significant supporting role by:
- **Newspaper campaigns** for peasants' cause
- **Organising mass meetings**
- **Preparing memoranda** on peasant grievances
- **Supporting peasants in legal battles**

#### Outcome

- The government appointed an **Indigo Commission** to investigate.
- In **November 1860**, a government notification declared that **ryots could not be compelled to grow indigo** and all disputes would be settled legally.
- The planters were already **closing factories** — indigo cultivation was **virtually wiped out from Bengal by end of 1860**. ✅

---

### 2B. Pabna Agrarian Leagues (1870s–1880s)

#### Background

- During the **1870s and 1880s**, large parts of **Eastern Bengal** saw agrarian unrest.
- **Zamindars** were the oppressors here. Their tactics included:
  - **Enhanced rents** beyond legal limits
  - **Preventing tenants** from acquiring occupancy rights under **Act X of 1859**
  - **Forcible evictions**
  - **Seizure of cattle and crops**
  - **Prolonged, costly litigation** in courts where poor peasants were disadvantaged

#### The League

- Peasants of **Yusufshahi Pargana** in **Pabna district** formed an **agrarian league** to resist.
- The league organised a **rent strike** — ryots refused to pay enhanced rents.
- They **challenged zamindars in courts** and raised funds collectively to fight cases.
- The struggle spread throughout Pabna and to **other districts of East Bengal**.
- The **main form of struggle was legal resistance** — very little violence.

#### Outcome

- Most cases were resolved by **1885** through:
  - Official persuasion
  - Zamindars' own fears of continued resistance
- Many peasants were able to **acquire occupancy rights** and resist enhanced rents.
- In **1885**, the **Bengal Tenancy Act** was passed to protect tenants. ✅
- Supporters included **Bankim Chandra Chatterjee**, **R.C. Dutt**, and the **Indian Association under Surendranath Banerjea**.

---

### 2C. Deccan Riots (1875)

#### Background

- **Ryots of the Deccan region** suffered heavy taxation under the **Ryotwari system**.
- The moneylenders here were mostly **outsiders** — **Marwaris or Gujaratis**.
- Conditions worsened due to three specific factors:
  - **Crash in cotton prices** after the end of the **American Civil War in 1864**
  - Government's decision to **raise land revenue by 50 per cent in 1867**
  - A **succession of bad harvests**

#### The Social Boycott (1874)

- In **1874**, ryots organised a **social boycott movement** against the "outsider" moneylenders:
  - Peasants **refused to buy from their shops**
  - No peasant would **cultivate their fields**
  - **Barbers, washermen, and shoemakers** refused to serve them
- The boycott spread rapidly to villages of **Poona, Ahmednagar, Sholapur, and Satara**.

#### The Riots

- The social boycott transformed into **agrarian riots** with:
  - Systematic attacks on **moneylenders' houses and shops**
  - **Debt bonds and deeds seized and publicly burnt**
- The government suppressed the movement.
- As a conciliatory measure, the **Deccan Agriculturists Relief Act was passed in 1879**. ✅
- The **modern nationalist intelligentsia of Maharashtra** also supported the peasants.

## 🔄 Section 3: Changed Nature of Peasant Movements After 1857

After the 1857 revolt, peasant movements evolved. Here are the key characteristics:

### Positive Changes (What Was New)

- **Peasants emerged as the main force** — fighting directly for their own demands
- Demands were **almost wholly economic** in nature
- Movements targeted **immediate enemies**: foreign planters, indigenous zamindars, and moneylenders
- Struggles were directed towards **specific and limited objectives** — fixing particular grievances
- Peasants developed a **strong awareness of their legal rights** and asserted them both in and outside courts

### Limitations (What Was Still Missing)

- **Colonialism was not identified as the target** of these movements
- The **objective was not to end** the system of subordination or exploitation of peasants altogether
- **Territorial reach was limited** — no pan-India coordination
- **No continuity of struggle** or long-term organisation
- **Lack of an adequate understanding of colonialism** as the root cause
- **No new ideology** — no new social, economic, or political programme for the future
- Movements occurred **within the framework of the old societal order**, lacking a positive conception of an alternative society

> *Note: The Champaran Satyagraha and Kheda Satyagraha are covered in the Freedom Movement chapters.*

### 4A. The Kisan Sabha Movement (1918–1921)

#### Background

- After the **1857 revolt**, Awadh **taluqdars** had got back their lands — strengthening their hold over agrarian society.
- Most cultivators faced:
  - **High rents**
  - **Summary evictions (bedakhali)** — eviction without due process
  - **Illegal levies**
  - **Renewal fees or nazrana** (fees to renew tenancy agreements)
- **World War I** had spiked food prices, worsening conditions for UP peasants.

#### Organisation

- **Kisan sabhas** were organised in UP mainly by **Home Rule activists**.
- The **United Provinces Kisan Sabha** was set up in **February 1918** by:
  - **Gauri Shankar Mishra**
  - **Indra Narayan Dwivedi**
  - Supported by **Madan Mohan Malaviya**
- By **June 1919**, the UP Kisan Sabha had **450 branches**.
- Other prominent leaders: **Jhinguri Singh, Durgapal Singh**, and **Baba Ramchandra**

#### Nehru's Connection

- In **June 1920**, **Baba Ramchandra** urged **Nehru to visit these villages**.
- During these visits, **Nehru developed close contacts with villagers** — a turning point in his political evolution.

#### The Awadh Kisan Sabha (October 1920)

- The **Awadh Kisan Sabha** came into existence in **October 1920** due to differences in nationalist ranks.
- Its demands to kisans:
  - **Refuse to till bedakhali land** (land from which others had been evicted)
  - **Not offer hari and begar** (forms of unpaid labour)
  - **Boycott** those who did not accept these conditions
  - **Solve disputes through panchayats**

#### Decline

- By **January 1921**, the movement escalated to:
  - Looting of **bazaars, houses, and granaries**
  - Clashes with the police
- Centres of activity: **Rai Bareilly, Faizabad**, and **Sultanpur**
- Movement declined due to:
  - Government repression
  - Passing of the **Awadh Rent (Amendment) Act**

---

### 4B. The Eka Movement (1921–22)

#### Background

- Towards the **end of 1921**, peasant discontent resurfaced in northern districts of the United Provinces:
  - **Hardoi, Bahraich**, and **Sitapur**

#### Key Issues

1. **High rents** — 50 per cent higher than the recorded (official) rates
2. **Oppression of thikadars** (middlemen in charge of revenue collection)
3. Practice of **share-rents** (paying rent as a share of produce)

#### The Eka Pledge

The **Eka (Unity) Movement** used a **symbolic religious ritual** where assembled peasants vowed to:
- Pay only the **recorded rent** — but pay it **on time**
- **Not leave** when evicted
- **Refuse to do forced labour**
- **Give no help to criminals**
- **Abide by panchayat decisions**

#### Leadership and End

- Grassroot leadership came from **Madari Pasi** and other **low-caste leaders**, along with many **small zamindars**.
- By **March 1922**, **severe repression** by authorities brought the movement to an end.

---

### 4C. The Mappila Revolt (1921)

#### Background

- The **Mappilas** were **Muslim tenants** inhabiting the **Malabar region**.
- Most landlords in Malabar were **Hindus**.
- Their grievances included:
  - **Lack of security of tenure**
  - **High rents**
  - **Renewal fees**
  - Other **oppressive exactions**

#### Merging with Khilafat

- The Mappila tenants were encouraged by the local Congress body's demand for **legislation regulating tenant-landlord relations**.
- The Mappila movement **merged with the ongoing Khilafat agitation**.
- National leaders like **Gandhi, Shaukat Ali**, and **Maulana Azad** addressed Mappila meetings.

#### The Revolt

- After the **arrest of national leaders**, local Mappila leaders took charge.
- In **August 1921**, the **arrest of a respected priest leader, Ali Musaliar**, sparked off **large-scale riots**.
- Initial targets were symbols of British authority:
  - Courts, police stations, treasuries, offices
  - Unpopular landlords (**jenmies** — mostly Hindus)
- Once the British declared **martial law**, the character changed:
  - Many Hindus were seen as **helping the authorities**
  - The movement **acquired communal overtones**
  - This communalisation **isolated the Mappilas** from the Khilafat-Non-Cooperation Movement
- By **December 1921**, all resistance had stopped.

> ⚠️ **Important Note:** The Mappila Revolt is a complex event — it began as an **anti-landlord, anti-government peasant movement** but tragically transformed into a **communal conflict** due to the dynamics of repression and mistrust.

---

### 4D. The Bardoli Satyagraha (1926–1928)

#### Background

- The **Bardoli taluqa** in **Surat district** had been intensely politicised after Gandhi's arrival on the national scene.
- The movement sparked in **January 1926** when authorities decided to **increase land revenue by 30 per cent**.
- Congress leaders protested immediately. A **Bardoli Inquiry Committee** was set up.
- The committee found the **revenue hike to be unjustified**.

#### Vallabhbhai Patel Takes Charge

- In **February 1926**, **Vallabhbhai Patel** was called to lead the movement.
- The **women of Bardoli** gave him the title **"Sardar"** (meaning leader/commander).

#### How the Movement Was Organised

Under Patel, the movement was meticulously structured:
- **13 chhavanis** (workers' camps) set up across the taluqa
- ***Bardoli Satyagraha Patrika*** — a publication launched to mobilise public opinion
- An **intelligence wing** ensured all tenants followed the movement's resolutions
- **Social boycott** for those who opposed the movement
- **Special emphasis** on mobilisation of **women**
- **K.M. Munshi** and **Lalji Naranji** resigned from the **Bombay Legislative Council** in support

#### Resolution

- By **August 1928**, massive tension had built up. There were prospects of a **railway strike in Bombay**.
- **Gandhi reached Bardoli** to stand by in case of emergency.
- The government, seeking a face-saving exit, set a condition (that enhanced rent be paid first — which was not actually done).
- A committee reviewed the whole matter and found the revenue hike unjustified — recommending a rise of only **6.03 per cent** instead of 30 per cent. ✅

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The Bardoli Satyagraha's core demand — that **arbitrary revenue hikes must be independently reviewed** — resonates with the modern debate on **farm loan waivers and land acquisition compensation**. The **Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013** established an independent framework for determining "just" compensation — a principle Bardoli peasants fought for in 1928.
>
> `[Modern Example]` The **farmers' protest (2020–21)** against the three farm laws drew direct historical comparisons to Bardoli. Like Bardoli, it involved **organised non-cooperation**, a **clear set of specific demands**, extensive **mobilisation of rural communities**, and ultimately **resulted in the government backing down** — the farm laws were repealed in November 2021.

## 🏛️ Section 5: The All India Kisan Congress/Sabha (AIKS)

### Formation

- Founded in **Lucknow** in **April 1936**.
- **President:** **Swami Sahajanand Saraswati**
- **General Secretary:** **N.G. Ranga**
- A **kisan manifesto** was issued.
- A periodical was started under **Indulal Yagnik**.

### Political Influence

- The **AIKS and the Congress** held their sessions together at **Faizpur in 1936**.
- The **Congress manifesto** — especially the **agrarian policy** — for the **1937 provincial elections** was **strongly influenced by the AIKS agenda**.

## 🗺️ Section 6: Peasant Activity Under Congress Ministries (1937–39)

The period **1937–39** was the **high watermark of peasant movements** under Congress provincial rule.

**Key methods of mobilisation:**
- Holding **kisan conferences and meetings**
- Passing resolutions and airing demands publicly
- **Mobilisation campaigns** in villages

### Region-wise Peasant Activity

#### Kerala (Malabar Region)
- Peasants mobilised mainly by **Congress Socialist Party** activists.
- Many **"Karshak Sanghams"** (peasants' organisations) came into existence.
- Most popular method: **Marching of jaths** (groups of peasants) to landlords to get demands accepted.
- Significant campaign in **1938** for amendment of the **Malabar Tenancy Act, 1929**.

#### Andhra
- Zamindars' prestige had already declined after their defeat by Congressmen in elections.
- **Anti-zamindar movements** ongoing in several places.
- Many **provincial ryot associations** were active.
- **N.G. Ranga** had set up the **India Peasants' Institute** in **1933**.
- After 1936, **Congress socialists** began organising peasants.
- **Summer schools of economics and politics** were held and addressed by leaders like **P.C. Joshi, Ajoy Ghosh**, and **R.D. Bhardwaj**.

#### Bihar
- Led by **Sahjanand Saraswati**, joined by **Karyanand Sharma, Yadunandan Sharma, Rahul Sankritayan, Panchanan Sharma, Jamun Karjiti**, and others.
- In **1935**, the **Provincial Kisan Conference** adopted the **anti-zamindari slogan**.
- The Provincial Kisan Sabha developed a **rift with the Congress** over the **'bakasht land'** issue (land reclaimed by landlords from tenants) due to an unfavourable government resolution.
- The movement died out by **August 1939**.

#### Punjab
- Earlier mobilisation by: **Punjab Naujawan Bharat Sabha, Kirti Kisan Party, Congress**, and the **Akalis**.
- A new direction given by the **Punjab Kisan Committee in 1937**.
- Main targets: **Landlords of western Punjab** who dominated the unionist ministry.
- Immediate issues:
  - **Resettlement of land revenue** in **Amritsar and Lahore**
  - **Increase in water rates** in **canal colonies of Multan and Montgomery**
  - **Feudal levies** being demanded by private contractors
- Peasants went on **strike** and won concessions. ✅
- Activity concentrated in: **Jullundur, Amritsar, Hoshiarpur, Lyallpur**, and **Shekhupura**
- **Muslim tenants-at-will** of west Punjab and **Hindu peasants of south-eastern Punjab** (today's Haryana) remained largely unaffected.

#### Other Regions
Peasant activity was also organised in:
- **Bengal** (Burdwan and 24 Parganas)
- **Assam** (Surma Valley)
- **Orissa**
- **Central Provinces**
- **NWFP** (North-West Frontier Province)

## ⚔️ Section 7: Post-War Phase (1945–47)

### During World War II

- Because of a **pro-War line adopted by the communists**, the **AIKS was split** on communist and non-communist lines.
- Many veteran leaders — **Sahjanand, Indulal Yagnik**, and **N.G. Ranga** — left the sabha.
- The Kisan Sabha continued to work among the people and did **notable work during the famine of 1943**.

---

### 7A. The Tebhaga Movement (1946–47)

#### What Was Tebhaga?

- **Tebhaga** means **"two-thirds share"** — the demand that sharecroppers receive two-thirds of the harvest instead of the one-half they were getting.
- **Bargardars** (sharecroppers, also called *bagchasi* or *adhyar*) worked on lands rented from **jotedars** (rich peasant-landlords).

#### The Movement

- In **September 1946**, the **Bengal Provincial Kisan Sabha** called for implementing the **Flood Commission's recommendations** of tebhaga through mass struggle.
- **Communist cadres**, including **urban student militias**, went to the countryside to organise bargardars.
- **Central slogan:** **"nij khamare dhan tolo"** — meaning sharecroppers should take the paddy **to their own threshing floor** (not the jotedar's house), thereby enforcing the two-thirds share.
- **Storm centre:** North Bengal, principally among **Rajbanshis** — a low-caste community of tribal origin.
- **Muslims also participated in large numbers**.

#### Decline

The movement dissipated (faded away) due to:
- The League ministry's **sop (concession) of the Bargardari Bill** — a partial acceptance
- **Intensified repression**
- The popularisation of the **Hindu Mahasabha's agitation for a separate Bengal**
- **Renewed riots in Calcutta** — which ended prospects of sympathetic support from urban sections

---

### 7B. The Telangana Movement (1946–48) — The Biggest Peasant War

> 🔴 **This was the biggest peasant guerrilla war of modern Indian history.**

#### Scale
- Affected **3,000 villages** and a population of **3 million people**
- Located in the **princely state of Hyderabad** under the **Asajahi Nizams**

#### Nature of Oppression

The Hyderabad state combined multiple forms of oppression:
- **Religious-linguistic domination** — a small Urdu-speaking Muslim elite ruling over predominantly **Hindu-Telugu, Marathi, and Kannada-speaking groups**
- **Total lack of political and civil liberties**
- Grossest forms of **forced exploitation** by **deshmukhs, jagirdars**, and **doras** (various categories of landlords)
- **Vethi** (forced/unpaid labour)
- Numerous **illegal exactions** (unauthorised taxes and charges)

#### Build-up During the War

- Communist-led guerrillas built a strong base in Telangana villages through the **Andhra Mahasabha**.
- They led local struggles on:
  - Wartime exactions
  - Abuse of rationing
  - Excessive rent
  - Vethi

#### The Uprising

- Began in **July 1946** when a **deshmukh's thug murdered a village militant** in **Jangaon taluq of Nalgonda**.
- Quickly spread to **Warrangal and Khammam**.
- Peasants organised themselves into **village sanghams** (associations).
- Weapons used: **lathis, stone slings**, and **chilli powder** — against brutal repression.
- At its **greatest intensity between August 1947 and September 1948**.
- Peasants routed the **Razaqars** — the **Nizam's stormtroopers** (private militia).
- Once **Indian security forces took over Hyderabad**, the movement fizzled out.

#### Achievements of the Telangana Movement

- **Vethi and forced labour disappeared** in villages controlled by guerrillas
- **Agricultural wages were raised**
- **Illegally seized lands were restored**
- Steps taken to **fix ceilings and redistribute lands**
- Measures taken to **improve irrigation and fight cholera**
- An **improvement in the condition of women** was witnessed
- The **autocratic-feudal regime of India's biggest princely state was shaken up**, clearing the way for:
  - Formation of **Andhra Pradesh on linguistic lines**
  - Realising another aim of the national movement in this region ✅

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The Telangana Movement's demand for **land redistribution and ceiling on landholdings** directly inspired post-independence **land reform legislation**. However, its goals remain incompletely fulfilled. As per the **Agriculture Census 2015–16**, **86% of Indian farmers are small and marginal** (holding less than 2 hectares), suggesting the structural inequality that drove the Telangana Movement persists. The **state of Telangana** (carved out of Andhra Pradesh in **2014**) still grapples with farmer distress — with the **Rythu Bandhu scheme** (providing investment support per acre) being a modern policy response.
>
> `[Modern Example]` The formation of **Andhra Pradesh on linguistic lines** — one of the Telangana Movement's achievements — became the basis of the **States Reorganisation Act, 1956**, which reorganised all Indian states on linguistic lines. The later demand for a **separate Telangana state** (eventually granted in 2014) showed that the region's socio-political aspirations traced directly back to this movement.

---

## ⚖️ Section 8: Balance-Sheet of Peasant Movements

These movements had a profound long-term impact:

- **Created the atmosphere** for post-independence **agrarian reforms**, most importantly the **abolition of zamindari**
- **Eroded the power of the landed class**, contributing to the transformation of the agrarian structure
- Were **based on the ideology of nationalism** — increasingly linked freedom from landlords with freedom from colonial rule
- The **nature of these movements was similar across diverse areas** — showing the pan-India character of agrarian exploitation under colonialism

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The peasant movements' demand for **abolition of zamindari** bore fruit through post-independence legislation like the **Zamindari Abolition Acts** passed by various states between 1950–1955. However, the **Supreme Court** in the **Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)** upheld Parliament's power to enact agrarian reform laws, even if they affected property rights — a constitutional legacy of the peasant movements.
>
> `[Modern Example]` India's **National Policy for Farmers (2007)** and the ongoing debate on **Agricultural Land Reforms** continue the unfinished agenda of these movements. The **Economic Survey 2020–21** highlighted that **India's agricultural productivity remains far below its potential**, partly due to land fragmentation — a structural problem rooted in the colonial agrarian order that these movements resisted.
