## 1. Background: From Plassey to the Great Revolt

In **1757**, after the **Battle of Plassey**, the British took the first step towards gaining power in northern India.

Exactly **100 years later, in 1857**, a major **Revolt** erupted — a direct product of the character and policies of colonial rule since 1757.

After the revolt, **noteworthy changes** took place in how the British governed India.

---

## 2. Simmering Discontent (1757–1857)

The **cumulative effect** of British policies over 100 years adversely affected virtually every section of Indian society:

- **Rulers** of Indian states
- **Sepoys** (Indian soldiers)
- **Zamindars** (landed aristocrats)
- **Peasants**
- **Traders and artisans**
- **Pundits and maulvis** (religious scholars)

This simmering discontent **burst violently in 1857**, shaking the British Empire in India to its very foundations.

### The Period Was NOT Peaceful

The century between 1757 and 1857 saw a series of **sporadic popular outbursts**, including:

- **Religio-political violence**
- **Tribal movements**
- **Peasant uprisings and agrarian riots**
- **Civil rebellions**

### Key Triggers of Earlier Unrest

- **Enhanced revenue demands** — even during famine years — caused widespread anger.
- Movements against **local moneylenders** often turned into rebellion against Company rule, because moneylenders had **police backing**.
- **British interference** in native religious and traditional customs caused deep resentment.
- Major revolts took place in the **south, east, west, and north-eastern regions**, all of which were **brutally suppressed** by the Company.
- Some of these movements **continued even after 1857**.

## 3. The 1857 Revolt: Major Causes

The causes of the revolt emerged from **all aspects of daily existence** — socio-cultural, economic, and political — cutting through **all sections and classes** of Indian society.

---

### 3.1 Economic Causes

The colonial policies of the East India Company **destroyed the traditional economic fabric** of Indian society.

#### Impact on Peasantry
- The **new revenue settlement** was highly unpopular; peasants never recovered from the heavy taxation it imposed.
- Impoverished peasants had to take **loans from moneylenders/traders at usurious rates** (extremely high interest rates).
- On **non-payment of debt**, moneylenders often **evicted peasants from their land**.
- These moneylenders and traders emerged as the **new landlords**.
- The scourge of **landless peasantry and rural indebtedness** has continued to plague Indian society to this day.
- The **older system of zamindari was forced to disintegrate**.

#### Impact on Artisans and Handicrafts
- The **annexation of Indian states** cut off artisans' major source of patronage — native rulers and nobles could no longer afford to support craftsmen.
- **British policy discouraged Indian handicrafts** and promoted British goods.
- Highly skilled Indian craftsmen were **forced to look for alternate employment** — which hardly existed.
- The **destruction of Indian handicrafts was NOT accompanied** by the development of modern industries.

> **View — Karl Marx, 1853:**
> *"It was the British intruder who broke up the Indian handloom and destroyed the spinning-wheel. England began with depriving the Indian cottons from the European market; it then introduced twist into Hindustan and in the end inundated the very mother country of cotton with cottons."*

#### Impact on Indian Trade and Mercantile Class
- The British deliberately crippled them by imposing **high tariff duties on Indian-made goods**.
- Import of British goods into India attracted **low tariffs**, encouraging their entry.
- By **mid-19th century**, exports of **cotton and silk textiles** from India practically came to an end.
- **Free trade** — one way, that is — and refusal to impose **protective duties** against machine-made British goods simply **killed Indian manufacture**.

#### Impact on Zamindars (Landed Aristocracy)
- Their land rights were frequently forfeited using **quo warranto** (a legal instrument demanding proof of authority to hold a right) by the British administration.
- This caused a **loss of status** in villages.
- In **Awadh** (the storm centre of the revolt), **21,000 taluqdars** had their estates confiscated.
- They suddenly found themselves without income — *"unable to work, ashamed to beg, condemned to penury"*.
- These **dispossessed taluqdars** seized the opportunity of the sepoy revolt to oppose the British and try to **regain what they had lost**.

#### Overall Economic Impact
- The **ruin of Indian industry increased pressure on agriculture and land**, which could not support all the people.
- The **lopsided development** resulted in the **pauperisation** (impoverishment) of the country in general.

- The Company's **greedy policy of aggrandisement** (territorial expansion), accompanied by **broken pledges and promises**, created contempt and suspicion among Indian rulers.
- Key policies used for expansion:
  - **'Effective Control'**
  - **'Subsidiary Alliance'**
  - **'Doctrine of Lapse'** (a policy under which the British annexed Indian states whose rulers died without a natural heir)

- On **Prince Faqiruddin's death in 1856** (whose succession had been recognised conditionally by **Lord Dalhousie**), **Lord Canning** announced that the next prince on succession would have to:

  - In addition to renunciations already agreed upon by Prince Faqiruddin

#### Wider Consequences
- The collapse of native rulers also adversely affected those sections of society that derived their **sustenance from cultural and religious pursuits** (artists, scholars, religious figures who depended on royal patronage).

### 3.3 Administrative Causes

- **Rampant corruption** in the Company's administration — especially among the **police, petty officials, and lower law courts** — was a major cause of discontent.
- Many historians believe that the **rampant corruption seen in India today is a legacy of Company rule**.
- The character of British rule gave it a **foreign and alien look** — a kind of **absentee sovereignty** (rulers governing from afar with no real connection to the people).

- **Racial overtones and a superiority complex** characterised the British attitude towards Indians.
- Activities of **Christian missionaries** were looked upon with suspicion.
- Attempts at **socio-religious reform** — though progressive — were seen by a large section as **outside interference**:
  - **Abolition of sati**
  - **Support to widow-marriage**
  - **Women's education**
- These fears were compounded by:
  - The government's decision to **tax mosque and temple lands**
  - The **Religious Disabilities Act, 1856**, which modified Hindu customs — for instance, declaring that a **change of religion did not debar a son from inheriting** the property of his 'heathen' father.

#### Religious Grievances
- Restrictions on wearing **caste and sectarian marks**
- Secret rumours of **proselytising activities** (attempts to convert religion) by chaplains — often maintained at **Company's (i.e., Indian) expense**

- Indian sepoys were **unhappy with their emoluments** (pay and benefits) compared to British counterparts.

#### Emotional and Political Grievances
- The **annexation of Awadh** — home of many sepoys — further **inflamed their feelings**.
- Indian sepoys were made to feel **subordinate at every step** — racially discriminated in matters of **promotion and privileges**.

- The discontent was **not limited to military matters** — it reflected the **general disenchantment** with British rule.
- The sepoy was a **'peasant in uniform'** whose consciousness was connected to the rural population.

- **Barrackpore (1825)**
- **Afghan Wars (1838–42)**

## 4. Beginning and Spread of the Revolt

### 4.1 The Spark

Two developments enhanced the sepoys' growing disaffection:
1. Reports about **mixing of bone dust in atta (flour)**
2. Introduction of the **Enfield rifle**

The **greased wrapping paper** of the cartridge of the new rifle had to be **bitten off before loading**. The grease was reportedly made of:
- **Beef fat** — the cow was sacred to Hindus
- **Pig fat** — the pig was taboo for Muslims

The Army administration did **nothing to allay these fears**. The sepoys felt their **religion was in grave danger**.

> **Key Point:** The greased cartridges did **not create a new cause** of discontent — they merely supplied the **occasion** for the simmering discontent to come out in the open.

---

### 4.2 Starts at Meerut

- The revolt began at **Meerut**, 58 km from Delhi, on **May 10, 1857**.
- It rapidly spread across a vast area — from **Punjab in the north** to the **Narmada in the south**, and from **Bihar in the east** to **Rajputana in the west**.

#### Events Leading Up to Meerut

| Event | Date | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **19th Native Infantry at Berhampore** (West Bengal) | Feb 1857 | Refused to use Enfield rifle; mutinied; **disbanded March 1857** |
| **Mangal Pande** (34th Native Infantry) | April 8, 1857 | Fired at sergeant major at **Barrackpore**; overpowered and **executed**; regiment disbanded in May |
| **7th Awadh Regiment** | May 3, 1857 | Defied officers; met similar fate |

#### The Explosion at Meerut

- **April 24**: 90 men of the **3rd Native Cavalry** refused the greased cartridges.
- **May 9**: **85 of them** were dismissed, sentenced to **10 years' imprisonment**, and **put in fetters**.
- **May 10**: This sparked a **general mutiny**. Soldiers released imprisoned comrades, killed officers, and **unfurled the banner of revolt**. They set off for **Delhi after sunset**.

---

### 4.3 Choice of Bahadur Shah as Symbolic Head

- In **Delhi**, the local infantry joined the Meerut rebels.
- They killed European officers, including **Simon Fraser** (the political agent), and seized the city.
- **Lieutenant Willoughby**, officer-in-charge of the Delhi magazine, offered resistance but was overcome.
- The aged and powerless **Bahadur Shah Zafar** was proclaimed the **Emperor of India**.

#### Why This Was Significant
- Delhi became the **centre of the Great Revolt**; Bahadur Shah, its **symbol**.
- This was a **spontaneous recognition** that the Mughal dynasty had become the **traditional symbol of India's political unity**.
- With this single act, the sepoys **transformed a mutiny into a revolutionary war**.
- All Indian chiefs who joined the revolt **proclaimed loyalty to the Mughal emperor**.
- It signified that the rebels were **politically motivated**.
- Though **religion was a factor**, the broad outlook was shaped by the **perception of the British as the common enemy** — not by religious identity.

#### Bahadur Shah's Actions
- After initial vacillation (hesitation), he **wrote letters to all chiefs and rulers** urging them to organise a **confederacy of Indian states** to fight and replace the British regime.
- The **entire Bengal Army** soon rose in revolt.
- Regions that shook off British authority: **Awadh, Rohilkhand, the Doab, Bundelkhand, central India, large parts of Bihar, and East Punjab**.

---

### 4.4 Civilians Join

- The revolt was accompanied by a **rebellion of the civil population**, particularly in the **north-western provinces and Awadh**.
- Accumulated grievances found immediate expression — people rose **en masse** (in large numbers).

#### Who Participated?
- **Peasantry**
- **Artisans**
- **Shopkeepers**
- **Day labourers**
- **Zamindars**
- **Religious mendicants and priests**
- **Civil servants**

This widespread participation gave the revolt its **real strength** and the character of a **popular revolt**.

#### Actions of Civilians
- Peasants and petty zamindars attacked **moneylenders and zamindars** who had displaced them.
- They **destroyed moneylenders' account books and debt records**.
- They attacked **British-established law courts, revenue offices (tehsils), revenue records, and police stations**.

> **Key Statistic:** Of the total **~1,50,000 men** who died fighting the English in Awadh, over **1,00,000 were civilians**.

Within **a month** of the capture of Delhi by rebels, the revolt spread to different parts of the country.

## 5. Storm Centres and Leaders of the Revolt

### 5.1 Delhi

- **Symbolic Leader:** Bahadur Shah Zafar (nominal leadership)
- **Real Command:** A **court of soldiers** headed by **General Bakht Khan** (who led the revolt of Bareilly troops and brought them to Delhi)
- The court had **10 members** — 6 from the army, 4 from civilian departments
- Conducted affairs of state in the **emperor's name**

> **Weakness:** Emperor Bahadur Shah was perhaps the **weakest link** — his weak personality, old age, and lack of leadership qualities created **political weakness at the nerve centre** and did **incalculable damage**.

---

### 5.2 Kanpur

- **Leader:** **Nana Saheb** — adopted son of the last Peshwa, **Baji Rao II**
- He had been **refused the family title and banished from Poona**; was living near Kanpur
- Nana Saheb **expelled the English from Kanpur**, proclaimed himself the Peshwa, acknowledged **Bahadur Shah as Emperor**, and declared himself his governor
- **Sir Hugh Wheeler**, commanding the station, **surrendered on June 27, 1857** and was **killed the same day**

---

### 5.3 Lucknow

- **Leader:** **Begum Hazrat Mahal**
- Rebellion broke out on **June 4, 1857**; popular sympathy was **overwhelmingly in favour of the deposed nawab**
- Her son **Birjis Qadir** was proclaimed nawab
- A **regular administration** was organised with important offices **shared equally by Muslims and Hindus**
- **Henry Lawrence** (British resident) and European inhabitants took shelter in the **residency**
- The residency was **besieged**; Sir Henry was **killed during the siege**
- Command devolved on **Brigadier Inglis** who held out against heavy odds
- Early recovery attempts by **Sir Henry Havelock** and **Sir James Outram** failed
- **Sir Colin Campbell** (new commander-in-chief) evacuated Europeans with help of **Gorkha regiments**
- In **March 1858**, the city was recovered by the British, but **guerrilla activity continued till September 1858**

### 5.4 Bareilly

- **Leader:** **Khan Bahadur** — descendant of the former ruler of **Rohilkhand**
- Not enthusiastic about the British pension
- Organised an army of **40,000 soldiers**
- Offered **stiff resistance** to the British

---

### 5.5 Bihar

- **Leader:** **Kunwar Singh** — zamindar of **Jagdishpur**
- An old man in his **70s**
- Nursed a grudge — the British had **deprived him of his estates**
- Unhesitatingly joined the sepoys when they reached **Arrah from Dinapore (Danapur)**

---

### 5.6 Faizabad

- **Leader:** **Maulvi Ahmadullah**
- A native of **Madras** who had moved to **Faizabad** in the north
- Fought a stiff battle against British troops
- Emerged as one of the revolt's **acknowledged leaders** after it broke out in Awadh in **May 1857**

---

### 5.7 Jhansi

- **Leader:** **Rani Laxmibai** — the **most outstanding leader** of the revolt
- **Lord Dalhousie** had refused to allow her adopted son to succeed after her husband **Raja Gangadhar Rao** died
- Jhansi was annexed by applying the **Doctrine of Lapse**
- Driven out by British forces, she gave the battle cry: **"Main apni Jhansi nahin doongi"** (I shall not give away my Jhansi)
- Joined by **Tantia Tope** (close associate of Nana Saheb) after the loss of Kanpur
- Together they marched to **Gwalior**, where Indian soldiers hailed them
- **The Scindia** (local ruler) sided with the English and took shelter at Agra
- **Nana Saheb** was proclaimed Peshwa; plans were made for a march into the south
- Gwalior was recaptured by the English in **June 1858**

> **View — Sir Hugh Rose** (the man who defeated her):
> *"Here lay the woman who was the only man among the rebels."*

### 5.8 Shah Mal — Hero of Baghpat

- A **local villager** in **Pargana Baraut** (Baghpat, Uttar Pradesh)
- Organised headmen and peasants of **84 villages** (referred to as **chaurasi desh**)
- Marched **at night** from village to village, urging people to rebel

#### His Actions:
- Attacked **government buildings**
- Destroyed **bridges** and dug up **metalled roads** — partly to stop government forces, partly because these were **symbols of British rule**
- Sent **supplies to mutineers in Delhi**
- **Prevented official communication** between British headquarters and Meerut
- Made his headquarters at an **irrigation department bungalow** on the banks of the **Yamuna**
- Turned the bungalow into a **'hall of justice'** — resolving disputes and dispensing judgements
- Organised an effective **intelligence network**

For a short duration, people of the area felt that **British rule was over, and their own rule had come**.

> **Tragic End:** In **July 1857**, Shah Mal was killed by English officer **Dunlap**. It is alleged that his body was **cut into pieces** and his head **displayed on July 21, 1857** to terrify the public.

For more than a year, the rebels **carried on their struggle against heavy odds**.

## 6. Suppression of the Revolt

### 6.1 Fall of Delhi

- British captured Delhi on **September 20, 1857** after prolonged and bitter fighting.
- **John Nicholson**, leader of the siege, was badly wounded and later **succumbed to his injuries**.
- **Bahadur Shah was taken prisoner**.
- Royal princes were captured and **butchered on the spot** — publicly **shot at point-blank range** by **Lieutenant Hudson** himself.
- The emperor was **exiled to Rangoon** where he **died in 1862**.
- Thus, the great **House of Mughals was finally and completely extinguished**.
- **Terrible vengeance** was wreaked on the inhabitants of Delhi.

> With the fall of Delhi, the **focal point of the revolt disappeared**.

## 7. Why the Revolt Failed

### 7.1 All-India Participation Was Absent

- **Limited territorial spread** — no all-India character.
- The **eastern, southern, and western parts** of India remained more or less **unaffected**.
- Probably because **earlier uprisings in those regions** had been **brutally suppressed**.

### 7.2 All Classes Did Not Join

- **Big zamindars** acted as **'breakwaters to the storm'**.
- Even **Awadh taluqdars** backed off once promises of **land restitution** were made.
- **Moneylenders and merchants** — who suffered at the hands of mutineers — saw their interests better protected under British patronage.
- **Educated Indians** viewed the revolt as **backward-looking**, supportive of the feudal order, and as a reaction of conservative forces to modernity. They hoped the British would bring **modernisation**.
- **Most Indian rulers refused to join** and often gave **active help** to the British:
  - **Scindia of Gwalior**
  - **Holkar of Indore**
  - **Rulers of Patiala, Sindh**, and other **Sikh chieftains**
  - **Maharaja of Kashmir**

> **Key Statistic:** By one estimate, **not more than one-fourth of total area** and **not more than one-tenth of total population** was affected.

### 7.3 Poor Arms and Equipment

- Indian soldiers were **poorly equipped** — fighting generally with **swords and spears** and very few guns and muskets.
- European soldiers had **latest weapons** like the **Enfield rifle**.
- The **electric telegraph** kept the British commander-in-chief informed about rebel movements and strategy.

### 7.4 Uncoordinated and Poorly Organised

- **No coordination or central leadership**.
- Principal rebel leaders — **Nana Saheb, Tantia Tope, Kunwar Singh, Laxmibai** — were **no match** to their British counterparts in generalship.
- The British had **exceptional leaders**: the **Lawrence brothers, John Nicholson, James Outram, Henry Havelock**, etc.

### 7.5 No Unified Ideology

- Rebels lacked a **clear understanding of colonial rule**.
- They had **no forward-looking programme**, no **coherent ideology**, no **political perspective**, no **societal alternative**.
- They represented **diverse elements** with **differing grievances** and political concepts.
- **Modern nationalism was as yet unknown** in India.

> **However:** The revolt of 1857 played an important role in **bringing the Indian people together** and imparting the **consciousness of belonging to one country**.

---

## 8. Hindu-Muslim Unity Factor

This was one of the **most remarkable features** of the revolt:

- **Complete cooperation** between Hindus and Muslims at all levels — people, soldiers, leaders.
- All rebels acknowledged **Bahadur Shah Zafar (a Muslim)** as emperor.
- The first impulse of **Hindu sepoys at Meerut** was to march to **Delhi, the Mughal imperial capital**.

> **Maulana Azad** observed:
> *"Two facts stand out clearly... The first is the remarkable sense of unity among the Hindus and the Muslims... The other is the deep loyalty which the people felt for the Mughal Crown."*

### Examples of Unity
- Immediate **banning of cow slaughter** was ordered once the revolt was successful in any area.
- Both Hindus and Muslims were **well represented in leadership**:
  - **Nana Saheb** had **Azimullah** (a Muslim, expert in political propaganda) as an aide.
  - **Rani Laxmibai** had the solid support of **Afghan soldiers**.

> **Key Conclusion:** The events of 1857 demonstrated that the **people and politics of India were NOT basically communal or sectarian** before 1858.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The Hindu-Muslim unity of 1857 is often cited as a **counter-narrative** to communal division:
- The **Supreme Court's Ayodhya Verdict (2019)** in **M. Siddiq v. Mahant Suresh Das** emphasised the importance of constitutional values and peaceful coexistence — themes of interfaith cooperation that 1857 embodied.
- The spirit of composite culture is promoted through government initiatives like **Ek Bharat Shreshtha Bharat** (launched 2015, continued through cultural exchange programmes up to 2024).
- The **1857 revolt** is commemorated at the **Indian War Memorial Museum** at the Red Fort, Delhi, which was refurbished and opened to the public under the **Red Fort Festival** initiatives.

## 9. Nature of the Revolt — A Historiographical Debate

Views on the nature of the 1857 revolt differ widely:

| Historian/Thinker | View |
|---|---|
| **Sir John Seeley** (British) | A *"wholly unpatriotic and selfish Sepoy Mutiny with no native leadership and no popular support"* |
| **Dr K. Datta** | *"In the main a military outbreak"* taken advantage of by discontented princes and landlords; *"never all-Indian in character, but localised, restricted, and poorly organised"* |
| **V.D. Savarkar** | The **first war of Indian independence** — inspired by the lofty ideal of self-rule (*The Indian War of Independence, 1857*) |
| **Dr S.N. Sen** | *"Began as a fight for religion but ended as a war of independence"* (*Eighteen Fifty-Seven*) |
| **Dr R.C. Majumdar** | *"Neither the first, nor national, nor a war of independence"* — large parts unaffected, many sections did not participate |
| **Marxist historians** | *"Struggle of the soldier-peasant democratic combine against foreign as well as feudal bondage"* — but this is questionable since rebel leaders themselves came from feudal backgrounds |
| **Jawaharlal Nehru** | *"Essentially a feudal uprising though there were some nationalistic elements"* (*Discovery of India*) |
| **M.N. Roy** | A **last-ditch stand of feudalism** against commercial capitalism |
| **R.P. Dutt** | Saw significance of peasantry's revolt against foreign domination, while acknowledging it as a defence of the old feudal order |
| **L.E.R. Rees** | A war of **fanatic religionists** against Christians |
| **T.R. Holmes** | A conflict between **civilisation and barbarism** |
| **Eric Stokes** | **Elitist in character** — *"1857 stands firmly in a historical continuum"* |
| **S.B. Chaudhuri** | *"The first combined attempt of many classes of people to challenge a foreign power... a real, if remote, approach to the freedom movement of India of a later age"* |
| **Percival Spear** | *"The Mutiny became a Revolt and assumed a political character when mutineers of Meerut placed themselves under the king of Delhi"* |
| **Tara Chand** | *"The war against the British was not inspired by any sentiment of nationalism, for in 1857 India was not yet politically a nation"* — cooperation was based on **personal loyalties**, not loyalty to a common motherland |
| **John Lawrence** | *"Had a single leader of ability arisen among them, we must have been lost beyond redemption"* |
| **Stanley Wolpert** | *"Far more than a mutiny... yet much less than a first war of independence"* |

### Balanced Assessment

- One can **easily dismiss** extreme views (Rees' "fanatic religionists" or Holmes' "civilisation vs barbarism").
- But one **cannot quite accept** it fully as a **war for independence** either.
- It had **seeds of nationalism and anti-imperialism**, but the concept of **common nationality and nationhood** was **not inherent** to the revolt.
- It is doubtful if separate communities participated because they felt **common nationhood**.
- What about the **southern section**, which was **not a part** of the revolt?
- Each leader had a **personal cause** and **personal interest**.
- However, as **Dr Sen** points out, **national revolutions are mostly the work of a minority**, with or without active mass support. From that point of view, the 1857 rebellion can claim a **national character**.

> **Conclusion:** The revolt of 1857 was the **first great struggle of Indians to throw off British rule**. Not quite the first war of independence, but it **sowed the seeds of nationalism** and the quest for freedom from alien rule.

## 10. Consequences of the Revolt

The revolt of 1857 marks a **turning point** in the history of India. It led to far-reaching changes.

---

### 10.1 End of Company Rule — Crown Takes Over

- Even before the revolt was fully suppressed, the British Parliament passed the **Act for the Better Government of India** on **August 2, 1858**.

**Key provisions:**
- **Queen Victoria** declared sovereign of British India
- Appointment of a **Secretary of State for India** (a member of the British cabinet)
- **Direct responsibility** for administration assumed by the **British Crown**
- **Company rule was abolished**

The assumption of government was announced by **Lord Canning** at a durbar at **Allahabad** in the **'Queen's Proclamation'** issued on **November 1, 1858**.

> It was by this proclamation that the governor general acquired the additional title of **'Viceroy'**.

---

### 10.2 The Queen's Proclamation — Key Promises

| Promise | Details |
|---|---|
| **End of Annexations** | Era of annexations and expansion had ended |
| **Respect for Princes** | Dignity and rights of native princes to be respected |
| **Indian States** | To recognise **paramountcy** (supreme authority) of British Crown; treated as parts of a single charge |
| **Freedom of Religion** | Without interference from British officials |
| **Equal Protection Under Law** | Equal and impartial for all Indians |
| **Equal Opportunities** | In government services, irrespective of race or creed |
| **Respect for Indian Customs** | Old Indian rights, customs, and practices to be given due regard while framing law |

> **View — Bernard Cohn** (on the Queen's Proclamation):
> *"In conceptual terms, the British who had started their rule as 'outsiders', became 'insiders' by vesting in their monarch the sovereignty of India."*

---

### 10.3 Army Reorganisation

The army was **thoroughly reorganised**, and British military policy was dominated by **"division and counterpoise"** (divide to balance):

- **Number of Indian soldiers drastically reduced**; number of **European soldiers increased**
- **Divide and rule adopted**: separate units based on **caste/community/region**
- Recruits drawn from **'martial' races** of **Punjab, Nepal, and the north-western frontier** — who had proved loyal during the revolt
- Effort made to **keep the army away from civilian population**
- **Army Amalgamation Scheme, 1861**: Company's European troops transferred to the services of the Crown
- European troops **revamped by periodical visits to England** ('linked-battalion' scheme)
- **All Indian artillery units** (except a few mountain batteries) were **made defunct**
- **All higher posts** in the army and artillery reserved for **Europeans**
- Till the **first decade of the 20th century**, no Indian was considered fit for the **king's commission**; a new English recruit was considered **superior** to an Indian officer holding the viceroy's commission

---

### 10.4 The White Mutiny

In the wake of transfer of power from the East India Company to the British Crown, a section of **European forces** employed under the Company **resented** the move requiring the three **Presidency Armies** to transfer allegiance to the Queen.

**Background:**
- Before 1861, there were **two separate military forces** in India under British rule — the **Queen's army** and the **Company's units**
- Company troops received **batta** (extra pay allowances) for operations outside home territories
- With the transfer of power, **batta was stopped**
- **Lord Canning's legalistic interpretation** of the transfer laws infuriated the affected White soldiers

**Impact:**
- The White Mutiny was seen as a **potential threat** to the already precarious British position, with potential to incite **renewed rebellion** among the 'still excited population in India'
- Demands included an **enlistment bonus** or a **choice of release**
- Finally, the demand for **free and clear release with free passage home** was accepted
- It is also believed that the **open rebellion and physical violence** by European forces were such that there was **little possibility of acceptance** into the Queen's Army

---

### 10.5 End of the Era of Reforms

- The earlier **reformist zeal** of self-confident Victorian liberalism **evaporated**.
- Many British liberals began to believe that **Indians were beyond reform**.
- This new approach was called the **'conservative brand of liberalism'** by **Thomas Metcalf**.
- It had the support of **conservative and aristocratic classes** in England who favoured **complete non-interference** in traditional Indian society.
- **The era of reforms came to an end**.

---

### 10.6 Rise of the Divide and Rule Policy

- The **conservative reaction** in England made the British Empire in India **more autocratic**.
- It **denied the aspirations** of educated Indians for sharing power.
- In the long term, this proved **counter-productive** — it caused **frustrations** in the educated Indian middle classes and gave rise to **modern nationalism** very soon.
- The **policy of divide and rule** started in earnest after 1857 — the British used **one class/community against another** unscrupulously.
- Socially, there was **irremediable deterioration**.

---

### 10.7 Systematic Economic Exploitation

- While British territorial conquest ended, a period of **systematic economic loot** began.
- The Indian economy was **fully exploited without fear**.

---

### 10.8 Indian Civil Service Act, 1861

- Passed in accordance with Queen's Proclamation of 1858.
- Intended to give the impression that **under the Queen, all were equal** irrespective of race or creed.
- **In reality**, the detailed rules for the civil service examination had the effect of **keeping higher services a close preserve of the colonisers**.

---

### 10.9 Racial Hatred — The Worst Legacy

- **Racial hatred and suspicion** between Indians and English was probably the **worst legacy** of the revolt.
- British newspapers and journals **picturised Indians as subhuman creatures** who could be kept in check only by superior force.
- The entire Indian population was dubbed as **unworthy of trust** and subjected to **insults and contempt**.
- The complete structure of Indian government was **remodelled** and based on the notion of a **master race** — justifying the philosophy of the **'Whiteman's burden'**.
- This **widened the gulf** between rulers and ruled, causing political controversies, demonstrations, and violence in the coming period.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The administrative and political consequences of 1857 have had enduring impacts:
- The **Indian Army** today, the world's largest volunteer military force, has moved far beyond the colonial-era "martial race" theory. The **Agnipath Scheme (2022)** reformed military recruitment to make it more inclusive and youth-oriented — a sharp contrast to the divisive recruitment policies instituted after 1857.
- The **divide and rule** policy the British formally adopted after 1857 ultimately led to communal politics and eventually **Partition in 1947** — the consequences of which are still felt in India-Pakistan relations.
- The **Indian Civil Service Act, 1861** laid the foundation for what eventually became the **Indian Administrative Service (IAS)**. Today, the **UPSC Civil Services Examination** is open to all citizens regardless of race, creed, or gender — fulfilling the promise of the Queen's Proclamation that was denied in colonial practice.
- In **2022**, India celebrated **Azadi Ka Amrit Mahotsav** — 75 years of independence — with special commemorations of the **heroes of 1857** including Rani Laxmibai, Mangal Pande, and Tantia Tope, recognising their contribution as precursors of the freedom movement.

## 11. Significance of the Revolt

### For the British
- The revolt **exposed glaring shortcomings** in Company administration and its army.
- These defects were **promptly rectified** — they would never have been revealed without the revolt.

### For Indians
- The revolt had a **major influence on the course of the struggle for freedom**.
- It brought **grievances of people and sepoys** into the open — seen to be **genuine**.
- It became clear that **primitive Indian arms were no match** for advanced British weapons.
- The **senseless atrocities committed by both sides** shocked Indian intellectuals, who became increasingly convinced that **violence was to be eschewed** (avoided) in any struggle for freedom.
- The growing **educated middle class** preferred an **orderly approach** over violence.
- But the Revolt **did establish local traditions of resistance** to British rule, which were helpful in the **national struggle for freedom** that followed.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The lesson of 1857 — that armed, uncoordinated revolt was ineffective against a technologically superior power — profoundly influenced the **Indian freedom movement**:
- It paved the way for the founding of the **Indian National Congress (1885)** and the eventual adoption of **Gandhian non-violent resistance** (satyagraha) as the primary mode of struggle.
- The **idea of establishing local self-governance**, which rebels like Shah Mal briefly implemented, resonates with the **73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments (1992)** that formally empowered **Panchayati Raj** and **Municipal Bodies** — bringing governance to the grassroots.
- The spirit of the revolt is commemorated annually on **May 10** and through monuments like the **1857 Memorial** in Meerut and the **Shaheed Smarak** at various locations.

> 4. Movements fell into **two broad streams**: **Reformist** (Brahmo Samaj, Prarthana Samaj, Aligarh Movement) and **Revivalist** (Arya Samaj, Deobandi Movement) — both appealed to the "lost purity" of religion but differed in their reliance on tradition vs. reason.
> 5. The reform movements laid the groundwork for **Indian nationalism**, the **freedom struggle**, and the **constitutional guarantees of equality** in independent India.

## 1. Factors Giving Rise to the Desire for Reform

The dawn of the **19th century** witnessed the birth of a **new, modern vision** among some enlightened sections of Indian society. This vision shaped the course of events for decades and beyond.

This process of reawakening is sometimes defined as the **'Renaissance'** (rebirth of learning and culture), though the term is not fully justified. The process did not always follow the intended line and gave rise to some **undesirable by-products** as well, which have become as much a part of daily existence in the Indian subcontinent as the positive fruits of reform.

---

### 1.1 Impact of British Rule

The presence of a **colonial government** on Indian soil played a **complex yet decisive role** in this phase of modern Indian history.

**How the British conquest was different from earlier invasions:**

- Most earlier invaders **settled within India's frontiers**, were either **absorbed by its superior culture** or **interacted positively** with it, and became part of the land and its people.
- The **British conquest was different** — it came at a time when India, in contrast to an **enlightened Europe** of the 18th century (affected in every aspect by science and scientific outlook), presented the picture of a **stagnant civilisation** and a **static and decadent society**.

---

### 1.2 Social Conditions Ripe for Reform

#### Religious and Social Ills

Indian society in the 19th century was caught in a **vicious web** created by **religious superstitions** and **social obscurantism** (opposition to knowledge and enlightenment).

- **Hinduism** had become steeped in **magic and superstition**.
- **Priests** exercised an overwhelming and **unhealthy influence** on people's minds.
- **Idolatry** (worship of idols) and **polytheism** (worship of many gods) helped reinforce the priests' position.
- Their **monopoly of scriptural knowledge** imparted a deceptive character to all religious systems.
- There was nothing that religious ideology could not persuade people to do.

#### Depressing Position of Women

The position of women was the **most distressing** aspect of social conditions:

- **Female infanticide** — attempts to kill female infants at birth were not unusual
- **Child marriage** — another bane of society
- **Polygamy** — prevalent across communities
- **Kulinism in Bengal** — even old men took very young girls as wives
- **Sati** — widows (at least among higher castes) were expected to immolate themselves on their husband's funeral pyre
  - **Raja Rammohan Roy** described sati as *"a murder according to every shastra"*
- Widows who escaped sati were **condemned to a life of misery and humiliation**

#### The Caste Problem

**Caste** (a system of social segregation hierarchically ordained on the basis of ritual status) was another debilitating factor:

- At the bottom were the **untouchables** (or **scheduled castes**, as they came to be called later)
- They suffered from **numerous and severe disabilities and restrictions**
- The system **splintered people into numerous groups**
- In modern times, it became a **major obstacle** to the growth of:
  - A **united national feeling**
  - The **spread of democracy**
- **Important Note:** Caste consciousness, particularly regarding marriage, prevailed also among **Muslims, Christians, and Sikhs**, who also practised untouchability, though in a **less virulent form**
- Under a rigid caste system:
  - **Social mobility** was checked
  - **Social divisions** grew
  - **Individual initiative** was thwarted
- Above all, the **humiliation of untouchability** militated against **human dignity**

#### Opposition to Western Culture

- The establishment of colonial rule was followed by a **systematic attempt to disseminate colonial culture and ideology** as the dominant cultural current.
- Faced with this challenge, an attempt developed during the 19th century to **reinvigorate traditional institutions** and **realise the potential of traditional culture**.

#### New Awareness Among Enlightened Indians

The impact of **modern Western culture** and the **consciousness of defeat by a foreign power** gave birth to a new awakening.

- There was an awareness that a vast country like India had been colonised by a **handful of foreigners** because of **weaknesses within Indian social structure and culture**.
- For some time, it seemed India had **lagged behind in the race of civilisation**.

**This produced diverse reactions:**

- Some **English-educated Bengali youth** developed a **revulsion for Hindu religion and culture** — they gave up old religious ideas, deliberately adopting practices offensive to Hindu sentiments, such as **drinking wine and eating beef**.
- The response was varied, but the **need to reform social and religious life** was a **commonly shared conviction**.

**Key factors strengthening the resolve to reform:**

- Growth of **nationalist sentiments**
- Emergence of **new economic forces**
- Spread of **education**
- Impact of **modern Western ideas and culture**
- Increased **awareness of the world**

During the last decades of the 19th century, the rising tide of **nationalism and democracy** also found expression in movements to reform and democratise social institutions and the religious outlook of the Indian people.

> **Key Insight:** The socio-cultural regeneration of 19th century India was **occasioned by the colonial presence, but not created by it**.

---

## 2. Social and Ideological Bases of Reform

### 2.1 Middle-Class Base

The social base of the 19th century regeneration was the **newly emerging middle class** and the **educated intellectuals** (both traditionally educated and Western educated).

**A significant contrast existed:**

- Between the broadly **middle-class ideals** (derived from growing awareness of Western developments)
- And a predominantly **non-middle-class social base**

The 19th century **intelligentsia** (the intellectual class) searched for its model in the **European 'middle class'**, which (as they learnt through Western education) had brought about the great transformation from medieval to modern times through:

- The **Renaissance**
- The **Reformation**
- The **Enlightenment**
- **Democratic revolution or reform**

**However**, the intelligentsia of 19th century India:

- Did **not** grow from **trade or industry** (which were firmly under British control)
- Their roots lay in **government service** or the professions of **law, education, journalism, or medicine**
- Often combined with some connection with **land** in the form of intermediate tenures

---

### 2.2 The Intellectual Criteria

What gave the reform movements **ideological unity** were three principles:

1. **Rationalism** (using reason and logic to evaluate truth)
2. **Religious universalism** (finding common truths across all religions)
3. **Humanism** (emphasising human welfare and progress)

#### Rationalism in Practice

- **Raja Rammohan Roy** firmly believed in the **principle of causality** linking the whole phenomenal universe and **demonstrability as the sole criterion of truth**.
- **Akshay Kumar Dutt**, while declaring that *"rationalism is our only preceptor"*, held that all natural and social phenomena could be analysed by **purely mechanical processes**.
- They used a **rational approach** to study tradition and evaluated contemporary socio-religious practices from the standpoint of **social utility**.
- They sought to **replace faith with rationality**.

**Examples of rationalism across movements:**

- In the **Brahmo Samaj**, the **infallibility of the Vedas was repudiated** (rejected)
- The **Aligarh Movement** emphasised **reconciliation of Islamic teachings** with the needs of the modern age
- **Syed Ahmed Khan** went so far as to emphasise that **religious tenets were not immutable** (unchangeable)
- **Swami Vivekananda**: *"The same method of investigation which applies to sciences should be the basis on which religion must justify itself"*
- **Akshay Kumar Dutt** brought **medical opinion** to support his views against child marriage

Although some reformers appealed to **faith and ancient authority**, on the whole, a **rational and secular outlook** was evident in proposing alternatives to prevalent social practices.

> **Key Point:** Reference to the past was to be used only as an **aid and instrument**. Neither a **revival of the past** nor a **total break with tradition** was envisaged.

#### Religious Universalism

Though the reformers tried to reform their religions, there was a **universalistic aspect** to their religious perspective:

- **Raja Rammohan Roy** considered different religions as **national embodiments of universal theism** (belief in one God)
  - He defended the **monotheism of the Vedas** and the **unitarianism of Christianity**
  - While attacking the **polytheism of Hinduism** and **trinitarianism of Christianity**
- **Syed Ahmed Khan** said that all prophets had the same **'din' (faith)** and every country and nation had different prophets

The social reformers used the **universalist perspective** to contend with the influence of **religious identity** on the social and political outlook of the people, which was indeed strong.

#### Humanism

A new **humanitarian morality** was embodied in the social reform movements:

- It included the notion that **humanity can progress and has progressed**
- That **moral values** are ultimately those values which **favour human progress**
- The **humanist aspect** was seen in:
  - Emphasis on the **individual's right to interpret religious scriptures** in the light of **human reason and human welfare**
  - A general attack on **priestly domination** of religious practices

#### Broader Scope of the Movements

- **Religious reformation** was important but **not the exclusive concern**.
- Attention was focused on **worldly existence**, not on issues of **salvation or other-worldliness**.
- Because of the strong religious element in social practices and religion being the **dominant ideology**, it was not possible to undertake social action without engaging with it.
- These movements took into their ambit the **entire cultural existence, the way of life**.

**Two key concerns of these movements:**

1. The evolution of an **alternative cultural-ideological system**
2. The **regeneration of traditional institutions**

These concerns were manifest in:

- Attempts to **reconstruct traditional knowledge**
- The **use and development of vernacular languages**
- Creation of an **alternative system of education**
- **Defence of religion**
- Efforts to regenerate **Indian art and literature**
- Emphasis on **Indian dress and food**
- Attempts to revitalise **Indian systems of medicine**
- Research into **pre-colonial technology** for its potential

---

### 2.3 Two Streams of Reform

The reform movements could broadly be classified into two categories:

| Stream | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Reformist Movements** (greater reliance on reason and conscience) | Brahmo Samaj, Prarthana Samaj, Aligarh Movement |
| **Revivalist Movements** (greater reliance on tradition and return to past glory) | Arya Samaj, Deobandi Movement |

> **Key Point:** Both streams depended, to varying degrees, on an appeal to the **lost purity of the religion** they sought to reform. The only difference lay in the **degree** to which they relied on **tradition** or on **reason and conscience**.

---

## 3. Direction of Social Reform

The **humanistic ideals of social equality** and the **equal worth of all individuals** inspired the newly educated middle class and influenced the field of social reform in a major way.

**The link between social and religious reform:**

- Social reform movements were linked to religious reforms because nearly all social ills — like **untouchability** and **gender-based inequity** — derived legitimacy from religion.
- In later years, the social reform movement gradually **dissociated itself from religion** and adopted a **secular approach**.

**Evolution of the social base:**

- **Earlier:** The reform movements had a **narrow social base**, limited to **upper and middle classes** and **upper castes** who tried to balance modernised views with existing social conditions.
- **Later:** The movements **penetrated the lower strata** of society to revolutionise and reconstruct the social sphere.

**Key organisations and individuals in the early phase:**

- Organisations: **Social Conference**, **Servants of India Society**, **Christian missionaries**
- Individuals: **Jyotiba Phule**, **Gopalhari Deshmukh**, **K.T. Telang**, **B.M. Malabari**, **D.K. Karve**, **Sri Narayana Guru**, **E.V. Ramaswami Naicker**, **B.R. Ambedkar**

In later years, especially with the onset of the **20th century**, the **national movement** provided the leadership and organisation for social reform.

**Methods of propagation:**

- To reach the masses, reformers used **Indian languages**
- They used a variety of media:
  - **Novels, dramas, poetry, short stories**
  - **The press**
  - In the **1930s and later**, the **cinema**

### The Two-Point Agenda

Broadly, the social reform movements had a **two-point agenda**:

1. **Fight for the betterment of the status of women** in society
2. **Fight to remove disabilities** arising out of **untouchability**

---

## 4. Fight for Betterment of Position of Women

### 4.1 The Condition of Women

The reformers worked against great odds. Women were generally:

- Accorded a **low status** and considered **inferior adjuncts to men** with no identity of their own
- Suppressed by practices such as:
  - **Purdah** (veil/seclusion system)
  - **Early marriage**
  - **Ban on widow-marriage**
  - **Sati**

**Specific disabilities:**

| Aspect | Hindu Women | Muslim Women |
|---|---|---|
| **Economic dependence** | Dependent on male relatives | Dependent on male relatives |
| **Education** | Generally denied | Generally denied |
| **Property rights** | No right to inherit property | Could inherit, but only **half** as much as men |
| **Divorce** | No right to terminate undesirable marriage | No equality between men and women |
| **Polygamy** | Prevalent | Prevalent |

Their **glorification as wives and mothers** was the only way society recognised women's contribution.

### 4.2 Why Reform Was Considered Vital

- Improvement of women's status was vital because a radical change in the **domestic sphere** — where **initial socialisation of the individual** takes place and where **women play a crucial role** — was the need of the hour.
- It was clearly understood that:
  - Reformed homes would produce **reformed men**
  - **No country whose females were sunk in ignorance** could ever make significant progress in civilisation

### 4.3 The Reformers' Approach

The reformers basically appealed to the doctrines of **individualism and equality**. They argued that **true religion did not sanction an inferior status to women**.

**They worked against:**

- Polygamy
- Purdah
- Child marriage
- Restrictions on widow marriage

**They worked for:**

- Establishing **educational facilities** for women
- Persuading the government to enact **favourable legislation**
- Propagating the uselessness of **medieval, feudal attitudes**

**Major contributors to women's reform:**

- Social reform movements
- The freedom struggle
- Movements led by **enlightened women themselves**
- Free India's **Constitution**

---

### 4.4 Steps Taken to Ameliorate Women's Position

Because of the **indefatigable efforts** (untiring efforts) of reformers, a number of administrative measures were adopted.

---

#### A. Abolition of Sati

- Influenced by the frontal attack of enlightened Indian reformers led by **Raja Rammohan Roy**
- The government under Governor-General **William Bentinck** declared sati **illegal and punishable** by criminal courts as **culpable homicide**
- **Regulation XVII, A.D. 1829 of the Bengal Code**:
  - Applicable first to **Bengal Presidency** alone
  - Extended in slightly modified forms to **Madras and Bombay Presidencies in 1830**

> **Historical Note:** The practice of sati was sought to be banned in regions under the **Portuguese, Dutch, and French** in the **early 16th century**. In **1582**, the Mughal Emperor **Akbar** is said to have issued orders that sati was not to be coerced, and appointed inspectors to ensure no widow was compelled to follow the custom.

---

#### B. Preventing Female Infanticide

- The practice of murdering female infants at birth was common among **upper-class Bengalis and Rajputs**, who considered females an **economic burden**.
- **Bengal Regulations of 1795 and 1804** declared infanticide **illegal and equivalent to murder**.
- An **act passed in 1870** made it compulsory for parents to **register the birth of all babies** and provided for **verification of female children** for some years after birth, particularly in areas where the custom was practised in utmost secrecy.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The colonial-era concern about female infanticide has evolved into the modern fight against **sex-selective abortion**:
- The **Pre-Conception and Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques (PCPNDT) Act, 1994** (amended 2003) prohibits sex determination tests.
- The **Beti Bachao, Beti Padhao** scheme (launched 2015) addresses the declining Child Sex Ratio (CSR) and promotes education of girls.
- India's **Sex Ratio at Birth** improved from 918 (2014-15) to **934** (NFHS-5, 2019-21), showing progress but indicating the problem persists.
- In **2023**, the **Supreme Court** in *X v. Principal Secretary, Health and Family Welfare Department* expanded reproductive rights of women, reinforcing bodily autonomy.

---

#### C. Widow Remarriage

- The **Brahmo Samaj** had widow remarriage high on its agenda and did much to popularise it.
- It was mainly due to the efforts of **Pandit Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar (1820–91)**, the principal of **Sanskrit College, Calcutta**, that the **Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act, 1856** was passed.
  - It **legalised marriage of widows**
  - Declared **issues (children) from such marriages as legitimate**
- Vidyasagar **cited Vedic texts** to prove that Hindu religion sanctioned widow remarriage.

**Other prominent advocates of widow remarriage:**

| Person | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Vishnu Shastri Pandit** | Founded the **Widow Remarriage Association** in the **1850s** |
| **Karsandas Mulji** | Started **Satya Prakash** in Gujarati in **1852** to advocate widow remarriage |
| **Professor D.K. Karve** | Married a widow in **1893**; dedicated life to upliftment of Hindu widows; became secretary of the Widow Remarriage Association; opened a **widows' home in Poona** for vocational training of high-caste widows |
| **Veerasalingam Pantulu** | Made similar efforts in **Madras** |
| **B.M. Malabari** | Advocated the right of widows to remarriage |
| **Narmad** (Narmadashankar Labhshankar Dave) | Advocate of widow remarriage |
| **Justice Govind Mahadeo Ranade** | Advocate of widow remarriage |
| **K. Natarajan** | Advocate of widow remarriage |
| **Jyotiba Phule and Savitribai Phule** | Vociferous advocates; campaigned against degrading practices imposed on widows |

---

#### D. Controlling Child Marriage

- **The Native Marriage Act (or Civil Marriage Act), 1872**: Signified legislative action in prohibiting child marriage, but had **limited impact** as it was **not applicable to Hindus, Muslims, and other recognised faiths**.

**The Rukhmabai Case — A Landmark Moment:**

- **Rukhmabai Raut**, who went on to become **India's first woman physician to practise medicine**, was married to **Dadaji Bhikaji** at the age of **11**.
- With support of her stepfather **Sakharam Arjun**, she continued her education and did not join her husband.
- After **~12 years**, Dadaji demanded she join him; she **refused**.
- **Dadaji Bhikaji vs Rukhmabai case (1884)**: Dadaji petitioned the **Bombay High Court** for restitution of conjugal rights.
- Rukhmabai questioned the marriage's validity — it took place before she had *"arrived at years of discretion"*.
- **Justice Robert Hill Pinhey** dismissed Dadaji's petition, saying Rukhmabai had been married off in *"helpless infancy"*.
- **1887**: Pinhey's decision was **overturned** by **Chief Justice Sir Charles Sargent and Justice Farran**. Rukhmabai was told to live with her husband.
- **Rukhmabai declared** she would rather undergo **any punishment** than go to her husband.
- It is said that **Queen Victoria's intervention** set aside the sentence of imprisonment.
- Even conservatives among Indians and the British government did not want Rukhmabai punished for her stand.

**Impact of the case:**

- Became one of the **most publicised court cases** in 19th century India
- Brought the issue of **child marriage and women's rights** to the forefront
- A **Rukhmabai Defence Committee** was formed by reformers like **Behramji Malabari** and **Ramabai Ranade**

> After finishing her studies, Rukhmabai obtained a position as **Chief Medical Officer in Surat** and continued writing against child marriage and purdah during her long and distinguished medical career.

**The Age of Consent Act (1891):**

- Enacted due to the relentless efforts of Parsi reformer **B.M. Malabari**
- Forbade the **marriage of girls below the age of 12**
- The Rukhmabai case was a major push behind this legislation

**The Child Marriage Restraint Act, 1929 (Sarda Act):**

- Came into force in **1930**
- Pushed up marriage age to **18 for boys** and **14 for girls**
- Named after **Harbilas Sarda** — an Indian academic and judge, Member of the Central Legislative Assembly from **Ajmer-Merwara**, follower of **Dayanand Saraswati** and member of the **Arya Samaj**

**In free India:**

- **Child Marriage Restraint (Amendment) Act, 1978** raised the age of marriage:
  - Girls: from 15 to **18 years**
  - Boys: from 18 to **21 years**

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` Child marriage remains a significant challenge in India:
- The **Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006** replaced the old Sarda Act, making child marriages voidable and prescribing stricter punishments.
- In **2021**, the Government of India proposed the **Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Bill** to raise the minimum age of marriage for women from 18 to **21 years** (based on the recommendations of the **Jaya Jaitly Committee**). The bill was referred to a Parliamentary Standing Committee.
- According to **NFHS-5 (2019-21)**, **23.3%** of women aged 20-24 were married before the age of 18, down from 26.8% in NFHS-4.
- In **Independent Thought v. Union of India (2017)**, the **Supreme Court** struck down the exception in the rape law that permitted sexual intercourse with a minor wife aged 15-18, effectively criminalising marital rape of minors.

#### E. Education of Women

**Pioneers and milestones:**

- **Christian missionaries** were the first to set up the **Calcutta Female Juvenile Society in 1819**.
- **The Bethune School** (founded by **J.E.D. Bethune**, president of the Council of Education in Calcutta, in **1849**) was the **first fruit** of the powerful movement for women's education in the **1840s and 1850s**.
- **Pandit Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar** was associated with no less than **35 girls' schools** in Bengal — considered one of the **pioneers of women's education**.

**In Maharashtra:**

- **Jagannath Shankarsheth 'Nana'** and **Bhau Daji** were active promoters of girls' schools.
- **Jagannath 'Nana' Shankarsheth** (name spelt variously — Jugonnath Sunkersett, Jagannath Shankarshet, Jagannath Shankar Seth) was one of the founders of the **School Society and Native School of Bombay**.
  - The school changed names: **Bombay Native Institution (1824)** → **Board of Education (1840)** → **Elphinstone Educational Institution (1856)**
  - When **Dr Wilson** (Scottish) was unable to get accommodation for a girls' school, Nana provided it and even **sent female members of his family** to the school in **Girgaum**.
  - In **1856**, he took advantage of the British government's **grant-in-aid to private institutions** and started an **English Marathi school in Girgaum**.
  - He contributed funds to the girls' school started by the **Students' Literary and Scientific Society**.

- **Bhau Daji Lad** (Ram Krishna Lad): As the **first Indian president** of the **Students' Literary and Scientific Society**, he championed female education; a **girls' school** was founded in his name.

**Jyotirao Phule and Savitribai Phule — The Most Notable Pioneers:**

- **Jyotirao Phule** (also called Jyotiba Phule) held **radical views** on the necessity of education for girls.
- His first gender-sensitive act: encouraging his wife **Savitri** to read and write.
- Impressed by the school for girls run by **American missionaries in Ahmednagar**, he and his wife opened the **first school for girls at Bhidewada, Pune in 1848**.
- Not many were willing to teach (given the opposition), so Phule and Savitribai **shared the teaching work**.
- **Savitribai** could be said to have become the **first female school teacher of modern India**.
- She became **headmistress** and taught alongside her trainee **Fatima Sheikh** and Jyotirao's emancipated aunt, **Sagunabai**.
- The Phules opened **several more schools** in and around Pune.
- Phule also opened **night schools** for those working during the day.
- **Key distinction:** The schools established by the Students' Literary and Scientific Society were assisted by European officials, but **Phule's venture was without help from the authorities** — an **'indigenous' effort** by a **non-brahmin**, in the face of **huge opposition** from orthodox sections.

**Other milestones in women's education:**

| Year/Event | Details |
|---|---|
| **1863** | **Alexandra Society of Parsis** opened — aimed at educating Parsi girls |
| **1887** | **Cornelia Sorabji** — first woman graduate of **Bombay University** (a Parsi woman); later worked for equal opportunities for women in education |
| **Lord Dalhousie's declaration** | Female education must be given *"frank and cordial support"* |
| **1854** | **Charles Wood's Despatch on Education** laid great stress on need for female education |
| **1880s** | **Dufferin hospitals** opened — health facilities for women |
| **1914** | **Women's Medical Service** — trained nurses and midwives |
| **1916** | **Indian Women's University** set up by **Professor D.K. Karve** — an outstanding institution |
| **1916** | **Lady Hardinge Medical College** opened in Delhi |

> **Key Insight:** It was largely the **private enterprise of Indians themselves** that encouraged women's education.

**Women's participation in public life:**

- Participation in the **swadeshi and anti-partition** movements and the **Home Rule** movements during the early 20th century was a **major liberating experience** for otherwise home-centred Indian women.
- After **1918**, they faced **lathis and bullets**, were **jailed** during political processions, picketing, etc.
- They actively participated in **trade union and kisan movements**, and **revolutionary movements**.
- They **voted in, stood for, and got elected to** various legislatures and local bodies.
- **Sarojini Naidu**: became president of the **Indian National Congress (1925)** and later the **governor of the United Provinces (1947–49)**.
- After **1920**, aware and self-confident women led **a women's movement**.
- Many organisations and institutions came up, such as the **All India Women's Conference (established in 1927)**.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` Women's education and empowerment continue to be priority areas for the Indian government:
- **Beti Bachao, Beti Padhao (2015)** specifically targets the education of the girl child.
- Female literacy rate rose from **65.46%** (Census 2011) to higher levels as reflected in surveys — **NFHS-5 (2019-21)** shows significant improvements in women with 10+ years of schooling.
- The **New Education Policy (NEP) 2020** includes a **Gender Inclusion Fund** to build India's capacity in educating women and transgender students.
- **Women's Reservation Act, 2023 (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam)**: Parliament passed the **106th Constitutional Amendment** reserving **one-third of seats** in the Lok Sabha and state assemblies for women — a milestone echoing the 19th-century reformers' dreams of women's political participation.
- As of **2024**, India has had women serving as **President** (Pratibha Patil, Droupadi Murmu), **Prime Minister** (Indira Gandhi), **Speaker of Lok Sabha**, multiple Chief Ministers, and heads of constitutional bodies.

**AIWC Objectives:**

- Work for a society based on **social justice, integrity, equal rights and opportunities**
- Secure for every human being the **essentials of life**, not determined by **accident of birth or sex** but by **planned social distribution**

> **Note (critics' view):** According to critics, the philanthropic style followed by some of these women was that of **upper-class English women**. The AIWC was perhaps the first to adopt an **egalitarian approach**.

**Legislative reforms the AIWC worked towards:**

- **Sarda Act (1929)**
- **Hindu Women's Right to Property Act (1937)**
- **Factory Act (1947)**
- **Hindu Marriage and Divorce Act (1954)**
- **Special Marriage Act (1954)**
- **Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act (1956)**
- **Hindu Adoption and Maintenance Act (1956)**
- **Suppression of Immoral Traffic in Women Act (1958)**
- **Maternity Benefits Act (1961)**
- **Dowry Prohibition Act (1961)**
- **Equal Remuneration Act (1958, 1976)**

## 5. Struggle Against Caste-Based Exploitation

### 5.1 Origins of the Caste System

- The **later-Vedic conception** of the **four-fold division** of Hindu society got further subdivided into numerous **sub-castes** due to:
  - **Racial admixture**
  - **Geographical expansion**
  - **Diversification of crafts** (giving rise to new vocations)

- The concept of **Hindu chaturvarnashrama** (the four-fold division of society based on varna) dictated that a person's **caste determined their status** and relative purity.

**Caste determined:**

- Who could get **education** or **ownership of landed property**
- The **kind of profession** one should pursue
- Whom one could **dine with or marry**
- A person's **social loyalties even before birth**
- **Dress, food, place of residence, sources of water**, entry into temples — all regulated by caste

### 5.2 Impact on Untouchables

The worst hit were the **'untouchables'** (or **scheduled castes/dalits**, as they came to be called later):

- Disabilities imposed on them were **humiliating, inhuman**, and based on the principle of **inequality by birth**

---

### 5.3 Factors That Helped to Mitigate Caste-Based Discrimination

#### Factor 1: British Rule (Unintentional Undermining of Caste)

British rule created certain conditions that undermined caste consciousness to an extent — sometimes through **direct administrative measures**, sometimes **indirectly**:

- **Creation of private property in land** and **free sale of land** upset caste equations
- The close interlink between **caste and vocation** could not survive as **village autarchy** (self-sufficiency) crumbled
- **Modern commerce and industry** gave rise to several economic avenues
- **Growing urbanisation** and **modern means of transport** added to population mobility
- The British introduced the concept of **equality before law** in a uniformly applied system of law — a severe blow to social and legal inequalities
- **Judicial functions of caste panchayats** were taken away
- **Administrative services** were made open to all castes
- The **new education system** was on totally **secular lines**

---

#### Factor 2: Social Reform Movements

From the **mid-19th century** onwards, numerous organisations and individuals worked to:

- **Spread education** among untouchables
- **Remove restrictions** on entering temples or using ponds, tanks, etc.

**Key organisations:** Brahmo Samaj, Prarthana Samaj, Arya Samaj, Ramakrishna Mission, the Theosophists, the Social Conference

- Although many defended the **chaturvarna system**, they **criticised untouchability**
- They attacked:
  - The **rigid hereditary basis** of caste distinctions
  - The **law of karma** which formed the religio-philosophic defence of caste
- They called on people to work for **betterment in the real world**, rather than strive for **salvation after death**
- **Example — Arya Samaj**: While crusading against the disintegration of Hindu society into myriad sub-castes, it aimed at **reconstructing it on the original four-fold division** and upheld the **right of even the lowest castes** to study the scriptures

---

#### Factor 3: The National Movement

The national movement took inspiration from **liberty and equality** principles:

- National leaders and organisations **opposed caste privileges**, fought for **equal civic rights** and **free development of the individual**
- Caste divisions were diluted (though in a limited manner) through **mass participation** in demonstrations, meetings, and satyagraha struggles
- **Congress governments** in various provinces after **1937** did useful work for the depressed classes:
  - **Free education for Harijans** was introduced in some provinces
- Rulers of states like **Travancore, Indore, and Devas** took the initiative in **opening all state temples** by proclamation

**Gandhi's Campaign Against Untouchability:**

- Gandhi always had the objective of **eradicating untouchability by root and branch**
- His ideas were based on **humanism and reason**
- He argued that **the Shastras did not sanction untouchability** — and even if they did, they should be **ignored** since *"truth cannot be confined within the covers of a book"*
- In **1932**, he founded the **All India Harijan Sangh**

---

#### Factor 4: Stirrings Among the Lower Castes Themselves

With increasing opportunities of education and general awakening, there were **stirrings among the lower castes** that gradually developed into a **powerful movement** for their rights and against upper-caste oppression.

**Jyotiba Phule (Maharashtra):**

- Born in a **low-caste Mali family**
- Led a movement against the **brahminical domination** of Hindu society
- Accorded **highest priority to education of lower castes**, especially girls
- Opened **several schools** for them

**Gopal Baba Walangkar (also known as Gopal Krishna):**

- Considered a **pioneer** in the movement to uplift untouchable people from socio-economic oppression
- Worked against **caste discrimination** in **Raigad district**
- Expanded on **Phule's theory of the Aryan invasion** — that the indigenous inhabitants of India were actually the untouchable people, and caste was created by invading Aryans to **subjugate and control** them
- Founded the **Anarya Dosh-Parihar Mandali** (Society for the Removal of Evils Among the Non-Aryans)
- Became the **first Dalit to launch a newspaper** — the **Vital Vidhvasak** (Destroyer of Brahmanical or Ceremonial Pollution)
- Strove to bring caste Hindus to **consciousness of their inhuman behaviour**

**Kisan Faguji Bansod:**

- Another pioneer in the **Dalit uplift movement**
- Proponent of **upliftment of Dalits within the fold of Hinduism**
- Opened a **press** which published journals aimed at **awakening Dalits**, such as Walangkar's **Vital Vidhwansak**

**Babasaheb Ambedkar:**

- Experienced the **worst form of casteist discrimination** during childhood
- Fought against **upper-caste tyranny** throughout his life
- **Key milestones:**

| Year | Achievement |
|---|---|
| **1920** | Started the fortnightly newspaper **Mooknayak** (Leader of the Voiceless) — stated that nationalist consciousness could not develop if social divisions were ignored; presented sufferings of untouchables in the context of the caste system |
| **1924** | Formed the **Bahushkrit Hitakarani Sabha** (also referred to as **Bahishkrit Hitakarini Sabha**) with the motto **"Educate, Agitate and Organise"** — focused on mobilising the masses; marked the beginning of a new socio-economic and political movement for equality |
| **1927** | Started the newspaper **Bahishkrit Bharat** to defend the rights of depressed classes |
| **March 1927** | Led the **Mahad Satyagraha** — a procession of some **2,500 'untouchables'** through the town of **Mahad** to the **Chawdar tank** (a public water tank from which untouchables were barred). Dr Ambedkar **took water from the tank and drank it**. There were huge protests by caste Hindus. |
| **December 1927** | Ambedkar and colleagues **burnt the 'Manusmriti'** at the same place as a gesture of getting rid of inequalities |
| **1942** | Organised the **All India Scheduled Castes Federation** |

- Several other leaders of the depressed classes had founded the **All India Depressed Classes Association** in the **1920s**.
- Ambedkar **condemned the hierarchical and insular caste system as a whole** and advocated the **annihilation of the institution of caste** for the real progress of the nation.
- He stressed the necessity of removing ideas of **'high' and 'low'** and inculcating **self-elevation** through **self-help, self-respect, and self-knowledge**.
- The struggle of the depressed classes led to the provision of **special representation** in the **Government of India Act, 1935**.

**Other Movements:**

- In the **1900s**, the **Maharaja of Kolhapur** encouraged the **non-brahmin movement**, which spread to southern states in the first decade of the 20th century.
- It was joined by the **Kammas, Reddis, Vellalas** (powerful intermediate castes) and the **Muslims**.
- During the **1920s** in South India, the non-brahmins organised the **Self-Respect Movement** led by **E.V. Ramaswamy Naicker**.
- There were numerous movements demanding that the **ban on lower castes entering temples be lifted**.

**Sri Narayana Guru (Kerala):**

- Led a **lifelong struggle** against upper-caste domination
- Coined the slogan: **"One religion, one caste, one God for mankind"**
- His disciple **Sahadaran Ayyapan** changed it to: **"No religion, no caste, no God for mankind"**

---

#### Factor 5: The Constitution of Free India

- The struggle against caste discrimination **could not be fully successful during British rule**.
- The foreign government had its limitations — it could not afford to invite **hostile reaction from orthodox sections** by taking radical measures.
- Also, **no social uplift was possible without economic and political upliftment** — all of which could only be realised under a **free India's government**.

**Constitutional provisions:**

- The Constitution of free India **abolishes untouchability** and declares the endorsement of any disability arising from untouchability as **unlawful**
- It **forbids any restriction on access** to wells, tanks, bathing ghats, hotels, cinemas, clubs, etc.
- In one of the **Directive Principles**, the Constitution lays down: *"The State shall strive to promote the welfare of the people by securing and protecting as effectively as it may a social order in which justice—social, economic and political—shall inform all the institutions of the national life."*

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The fight against caste-based discrimination continues in modern India:
- **Article 17** of the Constitution abolishes untouchability; the **Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955** and the **Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989** (amended in 2015 and 2018) provide the legal framework.
- In **2018**, the Supreme Court in *Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra* initially diluted protections under the SC/ST Atrocities Act — but Parliament **restored the original provisions** through an amendment in the same year, showing the continued political salience of caste issues.
- Government schemes for Dalit upliftment include **Stand-Up India (2016)** (promoting entrepreneurship among SC/ST communities), **Post Matric Scholarship Scheme for SC Students** (revamped in 2021 with ₹35,534 crore allocation), and **Venture Capital Fund for Scheduled Castes**.
- Despite these measures, **NCRB data (2022)** recorded over **57,000 cases of crimes against Scheduled Castes**, highlighting that social reform remains a work in progress.
- The **Economic Survey 2022-23** noted the growing representation of SCs/STs in higher education, with their share in total enrolment increasing steadily — a direct continuation of the reform movement's emphasis on education as the tool for emancipation.

## 📋 Quick Reference Summary

### Factors Giving Rise to Reform Movements

- Presence of colonial government on Indian soil
- Various ills plaguing Indian society — obscurantism, superstition, polytheism, idolatry, degraded position of women, exploitative caste hierarchy
- Spread of education and increased awareness of the world
- Impact of modern Western culture and consciousness of defeat by a foreign power
- Rising tide of nationalism and democracy during the late 19th century

### Social Base
- Emerging **middle class** and **Western-educated intellectuals**

### Ideological Base
- **Rationalism**, **Religious universalism**, **Humanism**, **Secularism**

### Two Streams
- **Reformist**: Brahmo Samaj, Prarthana Samaj, Aligarh Movement
