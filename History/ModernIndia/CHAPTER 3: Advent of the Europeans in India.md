# 📖 Chapter 3: Advent of the Europeans in India
### *Unit 2 — Advent of Europeans and Consolidation of British Power in India*

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 big ideas of this chapter.**

1. **Europeans came to India primarily because Arab and Ottoman Turks blocked the old trade routes** — they needed a direct sea path to access India's spices, textiles, and luxury goods.
2. **The Portuguese arrived first (1498)** under Vasco Da Gama and established a maritime empire, but their religious intolerance and trade malpractices led to their decline.
3. **The Dutch came next, focused on trade not empire**, but were ultimately defeated by the English and retreated to Indonesia.
4. **The French were the most dangerous European rival to the English**, and three Carnatic Wars decided that the **English, not the French**, would rule India.
5. **The English succeeded** due to a combination of superior naval power, private enterprise structure, Industrial Revolution advantages, and smart use of debt markets.

---

## 🔑 A Note Before We Begin: History Is Continuous

We divide history into "ancient," "medieval," and "modern" periods for convenience — but history doesn't actually stop and restart. If we say modern Indian history begins with the Europeans, we must actually go back to the **15th century** — even before the Mughal Empire was established.

# 🇵🇹 PART 1: THE PORTUGUESE IN INDIA

---

## 1.1 Why Did Europeans Want a Sea Route to India?

### The Blocked Trade Routes
- After the **decline of the Roman Empire in the 7th century**, the **Arabs** established dominance over **Egypt and Persia**.
- Direct contact between Europe and India declined, cutting off easy access to Indian goods like **spices, calicoes (plain cotton cloth), silk, and precious stones** — all hugely in demand in Europe.
- In **1453**, **Constantinople fell to the Ottoman Turks**. This was a turning point.
- Indian merchandise now reached Europe **only through Arab Muslim intermediaries**.
- The **Red Sea trade route** was a **state monopoly** of Islamic rulers, earning them massive revenues.
- **Land routes to India** were also Arab-controlled.

> **Result:** Europeans desperately wanted to find a **direct sea route to India** — cutting out the middlemen.

### Why Europe Was Ready to Explore
The **15th century** in Europe was a period of extraordinary energy and change:

- **Spirit of the Renaissance** (a cultural and intellectual revolution) drove curiosity and exploration.
- Europe made **great advances in ship-building and navigation**.
- **Economic growth** was rapid — more land under cultivation, improved ploughs, **crop rotation** (a scientific farming method), and more meat production (which required spices for cooking and preservation).
- **Prosperity grew → demand for oriental luxury goods grew.**
- **Venice and Genoa** had earlier profited from oriental trade, but they were **too small** to confront the powerful Ottoman Turks or launch major explorations alone.
- **North Europeans** were ready to fund Portugal and Spain with money and men.
- The **Genoese** provided ships and technical knowledge.

### Portugal Leads the Charge
- **Portugal** took on the dual role of:
  - Leading **Christendom's resistance to Islam**
  - Embodying the **spirit of exploration** (inherited from Genoese tradition)
- **Prince Henry of Portugal** (nicknamed **"the Navigator"**) was obsessed with finding an ocean route to India and circumventing Muslim dominance of the eastern Mediterranean.
- **Pope Nicholas V** in **1454** issued a papal bull (an official decree) giving Prince Henry the right to navigate "to the distant shores of the Orient... as far as India" — to fight Islamic influence and spread Christianity.
- Henry died before his dream was realised.

### The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)
- In **1497**, under the **Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)**, Portugal and Spain divided the **non-Christian world** between themselves using an **imaginary line in the Atlantic, about 1,300 miles west of the Cape Verde Islands**.
- **Portugal** got everything to the **east** of the line.
- **Spain** got everything to the **west**.
- This legally and diplomatically prepared the ground for Portuguese entry into Indian waters.

### Bartholomew Dias & The Discovery
- In **1487**, Portuguese navigator **Bartholomew Dias** rounded the **Cape of Good Hope** in Africa and sailed up the eastern coast — convincing himself the sea route to India had been found.
- But it was **ten years later**, in **1497**, that a Portuguese fleet actually set out for India, arriving in **May 1498** — in just under **11 months**.

---

## 1.2 From Trading to Ruling: Vasco Da Gama (1498)

### The Arrival at Calicut
- Three ships under **Vasco Da Gama**, guided by a **Gujarati pilot named Abdul Majid**, arrived at **Calicut in May 1498**.
- This arrival **profoundly affected the course of Indian history**.
- The Hindu ruler of Calicut — the **Zamorin (Samuthiri)** — gave Vasco Da Gama a **friendly reception**. He had no suspicion of European intentions. Calicut's prosperity depended on its role as an **entrepôt** (a trading hub where goods are imported, stored, and re-exported).
- **Arab traders** on the Malabar Coast were worried — they did not want the Portuguese getting a foothold.

### The Old Trading System vs. The Portuguese Way
- For centuries, the Indian Ocean trade had multiple participants — **Indians, Arabs, Africans, Chinese, Javanese** — all operating by **unspoken rules of conduct**. No single power sought total dominance.
- **The Portuguese changed all of this.** They wanted to **monopolise** the trade by **excluding competitors**, especially the Arabs.

### Why the Pepper Trade Mattered
- When Vasco Da Gama returned to Portugal, he carried a **rich cargo** and made a **huge profit** in European markets.
- **Key fact:** Europeans buying pepper through Muslim middlemen had to pay **ten times more** for the same amount of pepper. Direct access eliminated this cost.
- This profit potential attracted other European merchants to India.

### Pedro Álvares Cabral (1500)
- **Pedro Álvares Cabral** came to trade for spices and **established a factory at Calicut in September 1500**.
- A conflict broke out — the **Portuguese factory at Calicut was attacked by locals**, killing several Portuguese.
- In retaliation, Cabral:
  - **Seized Arab merchant ships** in the harbour
  - **Killed hundreds of crew members**
  - **Confiscated their cargo**
  - **Burned the ships**
  - **Bombarded Calicut**
- Later, Cabral made **advantageous treaties** with the rulers of **Cochin and Cannanore**.

### Vasco Da Gama's Second Visit (1501)
- Da Gama returned in **1501** with "commercial greed combined with ferocious hostility" — attacking Arab ships wherever he found them.
- The **Zamorin refused** to exclude Arab merchants in favour of the Portuguese.
- Da Gama's rupture with the Zamorin became **total and complete**.
- He set up a **trading factory at Cannanore**.
- Gradually, **Calicut, Cannanore, and Cochin** became the key Portuguese trade centres.
- The Portuguese then obtained **permission to fortify** these centres under the pretext of protecting their factories.

---

## 1.3 Francisco De Almeida (1505–1509): First Governor

- In **1505**, the **King of Portugal appointed Francisco De Almeida** as the first governor in India for a **three-year term**, equipping him with sufficient force.
- His tasks:
  - **Consolidate** the Portuguese position in India
  - **Destroy Muslim trade** by seizing Aden, Ormuz, and Malacca
  - Build **fortresses at Anjadiva, Cochin, Cannanore, and Kilwa**

### The Egyptian Threat
- Encouraged by **Venetian merchants** (whose profitable trade was now threatened), the **Mameluke Sultan of Egypt** raised a fleet in the Red Sea to stop the Portuguese.
- In **1507**, a Portuguese squadron was **defeated in a naval battle off Diu** by combined **Egyptian and Gujarat navies**; Almeida's own son was killed.
- The next year, Almeida **avenged this defeat** by completely crushing both navies.

### Almeida's Blue Water Policy (Cartaze System)
- Almeida's vision: **make Portugal the master of the Indian Ocean**.
- His policy was known as the **Blue Water Policy (cartaze system)** — a sea-based strategy where control of the ocean (not land territory) was the key to power.

---

## 1.4 Alfonso de Albuquerque (1509–1515): Real Founder of Portuguese Power

- **Albuquerque** succeeded Almeida and is considered the **real founder of Portuguese power in the East**.
- He secured **strategic control of the Indian Ocean** by establishing bases overlooking all its entrances:
  - **East Africa**
  - **Red Sea**
  - **Ormuz**
  - **Malabar Coast**
  - **Malacca**

### How He Strengthened Portuguese Control
- Introduced a **permit system** for other ships.
- Exercised **control over major ship-building centres** in the region.
- Exploited the **lack of timber** in the Gulf and Red Sea regions — enemies couldn't build ships easily.

### Capture of Goa (1510)
- Albuquerque **acquired Goa from the Sultan of Bijapur in 1510** with relative ease.
- Goa became **"the first bit of Indian territory to be under European control since the time of Alexander the Great."**
- He strengthened Goa's fortifications and developed its commercial importance.

### Social Policies
- **Abolished sati** (the practice of widows self-immolating on their husband's funeral pyre) in his area of influence.
- Encouraged Portuguese men to **marry Indian women** to create a permanent Portuguese population.
- The Portuguese settlers introduced **new crops like tobacco and cashew nuts**, improved coconut plantations, and built roads and irrigation works.

> ⚠️ **Important note:** Albuquerque **bitterly persecuted Muslims** — a major drawback of his rule. This was driven by his goal to completely eliminate Muslim commercial interests in the East.

---

## 1.5 Nino da Cunha (1529–1538)

- **Nino da Cunha** assumed office as governor in **November 1529**.
- About a year later, he **shifted the headquarters of Portuguese government from Cochin to Goa** (making Goa the Portuguese capital in **1530**).

### Relations with Bahadur Shah of Gujarat
- **Bahadur Shah of Gujarat**, during his conflict with Mughal Emperor Humayun, sought Portuguese help.
- He **ceded the island of Bassein (with its revenues) to the Portuguese in 1534** and also promised a base in **Diu**.
- When Humayun withdrew from Gujarat in **1536**, Bahadur Shah's relations with the Portuguese soured.
- During negotiations, **Bahadur Shah was invited aboard a Portuguese ship and killed in 1537**.

### Portuguese Expansion into Bengal
- Da Cunha attempted to increase Portuguese influence in **Bengal** by settling many Portuguese nationals at **Hooghly** as their headquarters.

---

## 1.6 Why the Portuguese Had Favorable Conditions in India

- Except for **Gujarat** (ruled powerfully by **Mahmud Begarha / Begada from 1458 to 1511**), northern India was divided among many small, weak powers.
- The **Bahmani Kingdom** in the Deccan was breaking apart into smaller kingdoms.
- **No Indian power had a meaningful navy** — and none thought to develop one.
- In the Far East, a **Chinese imperial decree** limited Chinese ships' navigational reach.
- **Arab merchants and ship-owners** — until then dominant in the Indian Ocean — had no organisation or unity to match the Portuguese.
- Most crucially: **Portuguese ships carried cannons** — a decisive military advantage.

---

## 1.7 The Extent of the Portuguese State (Estado Português da India)

The Portuguese hold in India is generally **underestimated by historians**, but the **Estado Português da India (State of Portuguese India)** was actually a **significant element in Indian history**.

Within **fifty years of Vasco Da Gama's arrival**, many coastal parts of India were under Portuguese power:

- **Around Goa:** About **sixty miles of coast** were under Portuguese control.
- **West Coast (Mumbai to Daman and Diu):** A narrow tract with **four important ports and hundreds of towns and villages**.
- **South (Malabar Coast):** A chain of seaport fortresses and trading posts — **Mangalore, Cannanore, Cochin, and Calicut**.
- **East Coast:** Military posts at **San Thome (Chennai) and Nagapattinam (Tamil Nadu)**.
- **Bengal:** By the end of the 16th century, a wealthy settlement had grown at **Hooghly in West Bengal**.

### Diplomatic Role
- Envoys and ambassadors were exchanged between **Goa and many major Indian kingdoms**.
- Treaties were signed between **Goa and the Deccan Sultans in 1570**, regularly renewed.
- The Portuguese always played a role in battles for balance of power between **Vijayanagara and Deccan Sultans**, **Deccanis and Mughals**, and **Mughals and Marathas**.

> 🏁 **Interesting Fact:** The Portuguese were the **first Europeans to come to India** and also the **last to leave**. It was not until **1961** that the Government of India recaptured **Goa, Daman, and Diu** from them.

---

## 1.8 Portuguese Administration in India

| Position | Role |
|---|---|
| **Viceroy** | Head of administration; served for 3 years; had a secretary and later a council |
| **Vedor da Fazenda** | Responsible for revenues, cargoes, and dispatch of fleets |
| **Captains** | Commanded fortresses from Africa to China |
| **Factors** | Assisted the Captains; their power often used for personal benefit due to communication difficulties |

---

## 1.9 Religious Policy of the Portuguese

- The Portuguese brought from North Africa their **hatred of Moors (North African Muslims) and Arabs**, and their zeal to **promote Christianity** and **persecute Muslims**.
- Initially, they were **quite tolerant towards Hindus**.
- Over time, after the **introduction of the Inquisition in Goa** (a religious tribunal that persecuted non-Christians), **Hindus were also persecuted**.

### The Jesuits at Akbar's Court
Despite intolerance elsewhere, the **Jesuit missionaries** (members of the Society of Jesus, a Catholic order) made a **good impression at Akbar's court** due to the emperor's intellectual curiosity about theology.

- In **September 1579**, Akbar wrote to Goa requesting **two learned priests**.
- The Church sent **Jesuit fathers Rodolfo Aquaviva and Antonio Monserrate** to **Fatehpur Sikri (February 28, 1580)**, where they were received with honour.
- They returned in **1583** without converting Akbar — disappointing Portuguese hopes.
- A **second mission (1590–1592)** ended similarly.
- A **third mission (1595)** at Lahore became a sort of **permanent institution** under **Fathers Jerome Xavier and Emanuel Pinheiro**, influencing secular politics and providing detailed accounts of Akbar's later reign.

### Under Jahangir
- **Prince Salim (Jahangir)**, on taking the throne, initially **neglected the Jesuit fathers** to appease Muslims.
- By **1606**, he renewed his favours to them.
- The elegant **church at Lahore** was retained along with the priests' residence (**collegium**).
- In **1608**, baptisms were conducted in Agra with great freedom.
- Despite initial hope, **Jahangir was never converted** to Christianity.
- **Arrogant actions by Portuguese viceroys** created a rift with the Mughal emperor.

---

## 1.10 Portuguese Lose Favour with the Mughals

### The English Enter the Picture
- In **1608**, **Captain William Hawkins** arrived at Surat on his ship **Hector**, carrying a letter from **King James I of England** to **Jahangir**, seeking trading permission.
- The Portuguese (and Father Pinheiro) tried hard to prevent Hawkins from reaching the Mughal court — but failed.
- **Jahangir warmly received Hawkins in 1609**. Since Hawkins spoke **Turki (Turkic language)**, he conversed with the emperor directly.
- Jahangir appointed Hawkins as a **mansabdar of 400 (a Mughal military-administrative rank)** at a salary of **Rs 30,000** (which he apparently never received).
- Hawkins was also **married to the daughter of an Armenian Christian named Mubarak Shah**.

### The Portuguese React
- The granting of trade facilities to the English angered the Portuguese.
- A truce was negotiated between the Portuguese and the Mughals temporarily.
- The Portuguese managed to **stop English ships from entering Surat**.
- Hawkins left the Mughal court in **1611**, unable to counter Portuguese intrigues or Mughal vacillation.

### The English Fight Back
- In **November 1612**, the English ship **Dragon** under **Captain Best**, along with the **Osiander**, **successfully defeated a Portuguese fleet**.
- **Jahangir was greatly impressed** (he had no real navy).

### Portuguese Acts of Piracy Anger the Mughals
- In **1613**, the Portuguese **offended Jahangir** by:
  - Capturing Mughal ships
  - Imprisoning many Muslims
  - Plundering cargoes
- An enraged Jahangir ordered **Muqarrab Khan** (in charge of Surat) to seek compensation.

### Shah Jahan Ends Portuguese Influence
- During **Shah Jahan's reign**, the Portuguese **lost all the advantages** they had enjoyed at the Mughal court.
- The dream of converting the royal family to Christianity — nurtured since Akbar's time — was **finally extinguished**.

---

## 1.11 The Capture of Hooghly (1632)

### How It Began
- Based on an **imperial farman (royal decree) of circa 1579**, the Portuguese had settled near **Satgaon in Bengal** for trade.
- Over time, they built large buildings, drawing trade away from Satgaon to the **new port of Hooghly**.
- They **monopolised salt manufacture**, built a **custom house**, and strictly enforced duties on **tobacco** (which had become a major trade commodity since the early 17th century).

### The Slave Trade Provokes the Mughals
- The Portuguese ran a **cruel slave trade** — buying or seizing **Hindu and Muslim children** and raising them as Christians.
- In a particularly provocative act, they **seized two slave girls belonging to Mumtaz Mahal** (Shah Jahan's beloved queen).

### The Siege
- On **June 24, 1632**, the Mughal siege of Hooghly began.
- Shah Jahan ordered Bengal governor **Qasim Khan** to take action.
- The siege ended **three months later** — the Portuguese fled.
- The Mughals lost **1,000 men** but took **400 prisoners to Agra**.
- Prisoners were offered conversion to Islam or slavery.
- Persecution of Christians continued for a while before gradually dying down.

---

## 1.12 Decline of the Portuguese

### Causes of Decline (All Factors)

- **Emergence of powerful neighbours:** Powerful dynasties arose in **Egypt, Persia, and North India**; the **turbulent Marathas** became their immediate neighbours. *(The Marathas captured Salsette and Bassein from the Portuguese in 1739.)*
- **Religious policies backfired:** Jesuit missionary activities created political fears. Persecution of Muslims created resentment; the **forced conversion policy** also turned Hindus against the Portuguese.
- **Dishonest trade practices** evoked strong reactions.
- **Piracy and violence:** Portuguese reputation as sea pirates and their arrogance and violence made enemies of small states and the Mughal Empire alike.
- **Discovery of Brazil:** Diverted Portugal's colonising energy towards **the West** (Latin America).
- **Union of Spain and Portugal (1580–81):** This dragged the smaller Portugal into Spain's wars with **England and Holland**, **badly damaging the Portuguese trade monopoly**.
- **The sea route secret leaked:** Knowledge of the sea route to India could not be kept secret forever. The **Dutch and English** learned ocean navigation and the route.
- **Superior rivals:** The **Dutch and English** had greater resources and more motivation to expand overseas, and they overpowered Portuguese resistance.
- **Goa lost its importance** after the fall of the **Vijayanagara Empire**.
- **Spice trade was taken over by the Dutch**.
- **Brazil superseded Goa** as the economic centre of Portugal's overseas empire.
- In **1683**, the **Marathas invaded Goa** after two naval assaults.

## 1.14 Significance of the Portuguese

The Portuguese arrival wasn't just a trade story — it **changed the nature of power in Asia**. Key contributions and impacts:

### Military Innovation
- Their ships **carried cannon** — the first step toward monopolising trade through **threat or actual use of force**.
- Portuguese showed military innovation on the Malabar Coast in the 16th century: **body armour, matchlock men, and ship-landed guns**.
- They may have contributed by example to **Mughal use of field guns** and the "artillery of the stirrup."
- They introduced the **system of drilling infantry (on the Spanish model)** in the **1630s** — a practice adopted first by the French and English, and later by **Marathas and Sikhs**. These **sepoy armies** became new tools of empire in India.

### Naval Expertise
- Their **multi-decked ships** were heavily built to withstand Atlantic gales — this meant they could carry **heavier armament**.
- Their use of **castled prow and stern** was a unique method for repelling or launching boarding parties — **Indian builders adapted both**.
- Their skills at **organisation** — royal arsenals, dockyards, regular systems of pilots, mapping, and pitting state forces against private merchant shipping — were even more significant. These skills were inherited most by the **Dutch and English**.

### Cultural Contributions
- **Missionaries and the Church** were **teachers and patrons of the arts** — painting, carving, and sculpture.
- In music, they were interpreters of not just Portuguese, but broader **European art to India**.
- **Art of silversmith and goldsmith flourished in Goa** — elaborate filigree work and jewel-embedded metal work.
- Churches built under the Portuguese are notable for their **woodwork, sculpture, and painted ceilings** — though architecturally simple in plan.
- New crops like **tobacco and cashew nuts** were introduced to India by the Portuguese.

> ⚠️ The memory of **religious persecution and cruelty** unfortunately overshadows many of these contributions in historical memory.

# 🇳🇱 PART 2: THE DUTCH IN INDIA

---

## 2.1 Foundation of Dutch Commercial Enterprise

- **Commercial enterprise** led the Dutch to voyage to the East.
- **Cornelis de Houtman** was the first Dutchman to reach **Sumatra and Bantam in 1596**.
- In **1602**, the **States-General of the Netherlands** amalgamated many trading companies into the **East India Company of the Netherlands** (known by its Dutch acronym **VOC — Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie**).
- This company was empowered to:
  - **Carry on war**
  - **Conclude treaties**
  - **Take possession of territory**
  - **Erect fortresses**

---

## 2.2 Dutch Settlements in India

- First Dutch factory was established at **Masulipatnam (in Andhra) in 1605**.
- They captured **Nagapatam** (near Madras/Chennai) from the Portuguese, making it their **main stronghold in South India**.

### Full List of Dutch Factories in India:

| Factory Location | Year |
|---|---|
| Masulipatnam | 1605 |
| Pulicat (north of Madras) | 1609 |
| Surat | 1616 |
| Bimlipatam | 1641 |
| Karaikal | 1645 |
| Chinsura | 1653 |
| Baranagar | — |
| Kasimbazar (near Murshidabad) | — |
| Balasore | — |
| Patna | — |
| Nagapatam | 1658 |
| Cochin | 1663 |

### What the Dutch Traded:
- **From India to the Far East:** Various Indian articles and merchandise
- **From India:** Indigo (from the Yamuna valley and Central India), textiles and silk (from Bengal, Gujarat, Coromandel), saltpetre (from Bihar), opium and rice (from the Ganga valley)

---

## 2.3 Anglo-Dutch Rivalry

- The **English were also rising to prominence** in Eastern trade — posing a serious challenge to Dutch commercial interests.
- Commercial rivalry turned into **bloody warfare**.
- The climax: At **Amboyna (present-day Indonesia, captured from the Portuguese by the Dutch in 1605)**, the Dutch **massacred 10 Englishmen and 9 Japanese in 1623**.
- This further intensified Anglo-Dutch rivalry.
- After prolonged warfare, both sides reached a **compromise in 1667**:
  - The **British withdrew all claims on Indonesia**.
  - The **Dutch retired from India** to focus on the more profitable trade in Indonesia.
- The Dutch **monopolised the trade in black pepper and spices**.
- Their most important Indian trade commodities were: **silk, cotton, indigo, rice, and opium**.

---

## 2.4 Decline of the Dutch in India

- The Dutch became increasingly drawn into the trade of the **Malay Archipelago**.
- In the **Third Anglo-Dutch War (1672–74)**:
  - Communications between Surat and the new English settlement of Bombay were cut.
  - **Three homebound English ships were captured** in the Bay of Bengal by Dutch forces.
- The English retaliated: The **Battle of Hooghly (November 1759)** dealt a **crushing blow to Dutch ambitions in India**.
- The Dutch were **not interested in empire-building** — their focus was always trade, specifically the **Spice Islands of Indonesia**.

# 🇬🇧 PART 3: THE ENGLISH IN INDIA

---

## 3.1 Foundation of the English East India Company

### Background
- **Francis Drake's voyage around the world in 1580** and the **English victory over the Spanish Armada in 1588** generated a new spirit of enterprise in Britain.
- Knowledge of **high profits in Eastern trade** made English traders eager for a share.
- In **1599**, a group of English merchants calling themselves the **"Merchant Adventurers"** formed a company.
- On **December 31, 1600**, **Queen Elizabeth I** issued a charter giving the company:
  - The name: **"Governor and Company of Merchants of London trading into the East Indies"**
  - Rights of **exclusive trading**
  - Initial **monopoly of 15 years**, extended **indefinitely in May 1609** by a fresh charter
- Since the Dutch were already concentrated on the East Indies, the English **turned to India** in search of textiles and other trade commodities.

---

## 3.2 English Foothold in West and South India

### Captain Hawkins at Jahangir's Court (1609)
- **Captain Hawkins** arrived at Jahangir's court in **April 1609**, but the mission to establish a factory at Surat **failed due to Portuguese opposition**.
- Hawkins left Agra in **November 1611**.

### English Establish on the Malabar Coast
- In **1611**, the English began trading at **Masulipatnam** (south-eastern coast) and later established a factory there in **1616**.
- In **1612**, **Captain Thomas Best** defeated the Portuguese at sea off Surat.
- An impressed **Jahangir** granted permission in early **1613** to establish a **factory at Surat under Thomas Aldworth**.

### Sir Thomas Roe (1615–1619)
- In **1615**, **Sir Thomas Roe** came as the **accredited ambassador of King James I** to Jahangir's court, staying until **February 1619**.
- Though he **failed to conclude a commercial treaty**, he secured permission to set up factories at **Agra, Ahmedabad, and Broach**.

### Acquisition of Bombay
- **Bombay** was gifted to King **Charles II of England** by the King of Portugal as **dowry when Charles married Portuguese princess Catherine in 1662**.
- Bombay was handed over to the **East India Company in 1668** for an annual payment of just **10 pounds**.
- In **1687**, Bombay replaced Surat as the **headquarters of the Western Presidency**.

### Golden Farman from Golconda (1632)
- The **"Golden Farman"** issued by the **Sultan of Golconda** in **1632** gave the English the privilege of trading freely in Golconda's ports on a payment of **500 pagodas a year**.

### Fort St. George and Madras
- In **1639**, British merchant **Francis Day** received permission from the ruler of **Chandragiri** to build a **fortified factory at Madras** — which became **Fort St. George**.
- This replaced **Masulipatnam** as the headquarters of English settlements in south India.

### Eastern Expansion
- In **1633**, the English established factories at:
  - **Hariharpur in the Mahanadi delta** (first factory in east India)
  - **Balasore (in Odisha)**

---

## 3.3 English Foothold in Bengal

### Why Bengal Mattered
- Bengal was a **large, rich, and commercially advanced province** — and a major part of the Mughal Empire.
- Control over Bengal was an **attractive proposition for profit-seeking English merchants**.

### First Permissions
- **Shah Shuja**, Bengal's **subahdar (provincial governor)** in **1651**, allowed the English to trade in Bengal for an annual payment of **Rs 3,000** (in lieu of all duties).
- Factories were started at **Hooghly (1651)**, **Kasimbazar, Patna, and Rajmahal**.

### Trouble with Local Customs Officers
- Despite farman privileges, the Company's business was obstructed by **local customs officers** demanding tolls.

### Conflict with the Mughals
- The Company wanted a **fortified settlement at Hooghly** so it could use force if needed.
- **William Hedges** (first agent and governor of the Company in Bengal) appealed to **Shaista Khan** (Mughal governor) in **August 1682** — nothing came of it.
- Hostilities broke out between the English and the Mughals.
- In **October 1686**, **Hooghly was sacked by the imperial Mughals**.
- The English retaliated by:
  - Capturing **imperial forts at Thana (modern Garden Reach)**
  - Raiding **Hijli in east Midnapur**
  - Storming **Mughal fortifications at Balasore**
- The English were eventually **forced out of Hooghly** and pushed to an unhealthy spot at the **mouth of the River Ganga**.

### Job Charnock and the Return
- **Job Charnock** (a Company agent) negotiated with the Mughals to return to **Sutanuti**.
- Charnock signed a treaty in **February 1690** and returned to **Sutanuti in August 1690**.
- On **February 10, 1691**, an imperial farman was issued permitting the English to "continue contentedly their trade in Bengal" on payment of **Rs 3,000 a year**.

### Founding of Calcutta
- **Sobha Singh**, a zamindar in **Bardhaman district**, rebelled — giving the English the pretext to **fortify their settlement at Sutanuti in 1696**.
- In **1698**, the English bought the **zamindari (landlordship)** of three villages — **Sutanuti, Gobindapur, and Kalikata (Kalighat)** — for **Rs 1,200**.
- In **1700**, the fortified settlement was named **Fort William**, becoming the seat of the **Eastern Presidency (Calcutta)** with **Sir Charles Eyre as its first president**.

---

## 3.4 Farrukhsiyar's Farmans (1717): The Magna Carta of the Company

- In **1715**, an English mission led by **John Surman** went to the Mughal Emperor **Farrukhsiyar** and secured **three famous farmans** (royal decrees).
- These were called the **"Magna Carta of the Company"** — as significant for the Company as the Magna Carta was for English liberties.

### Key Terms of the Farmans:

**In Bengal:**
- Company's **imports and exports exempted** from additional customs duties beyond the annual **Rs 3,000 payment**.
- Company permitted to issue **dastaks (passes)** for transportation of such goods.
- Company permitted to **rent more lands around Calcutta**.

**In Hyderabad:**
- Company retained its **existing privilege of freedom from duties** in trade.
- Required to pay only **prevailing rent for Madras**.

**In Surat:**
- For an annual payment of **Rs 10,000**, the Company was **exempted from all duties**.

**Coinage:**
- **Coins minted by the Company at Bombay** were to have **currency throughout the Mughal Empire**.

---

## 3.5 Merging of Two English Companies

- After the **English Revolution of 1688**, the **Whigs** (a political faction), with enhanced influence, **opposed the monopoly** of the original East India Company.
- A **rival company was formed**, which sent **Sir William Norris** as ambassador to the court of **Aurangzeb (January 1701–April 1702)** to gain trading privileges.
- The new company proved a failure.
- Under pressure from the **Crown and Parliament**, the two companies were **amalgamated in 1708** under the title:
  > **"United Company of Merchants of England Trading to the East Indies"**
- This was the **East India Company (from 1708 to 1873)** that would establish **British political power in India**.

# 🇫🇷 PART 4: THE FRENCH IN INDIA

---

## 4.1 Foundation of the French East India Company

- The **French had harboured ambitions for Eastern trade since the early 16th century** — but actually arrived on Indian coasts very late.
- The **French were the last Europeans to come to India** for trade.
- During the reign of **King Louis XIV**, his famous minister **Colbert** established the **Compagnie des Indes Orientales (French East India Company) in 1664**. The king himself took great personal interest.
- The company was granted:
  - A **50-year monopoly** on French trade in the **Indian and Pacific Oceans**.
  - A **perpetual concession for the island of Madagascar** and any territories it could conquer.
- Much money was wasted trying to revive **Madagascar colonies** — without success.

### First Settlements
- In **1667**, **François Caron** headed an expedition to India and set up a **factory in Surat**.
- **Mercara** (a Persian who accompanied Caron) founded a **French factory in Masulipatnam in 1669** after obtaining a patent from the **Sultan of Golconda**.
- In **1673**, the French obtained permission from **Shaista Khan** (Mughal subahdar of Bengal) to establish a township at **Chandernagore near Calcutta**.

---

## 4.2 Pondicherry: The Nerve Centre of French Power

- In **1673**, **Sher Khan Lodi** (governor of Valikondapuram under the Bijapur Sultan) granted **François Martin** (director of the Masulipatnam factory) **a site for a settlement**.
- **Pondicherry was founded in 1674**.
- In the same year, **François Martin replaced Caron** as the French governor.
- Martin developed Pondicherry into a **place of great importance** — the **stronghold of the French in India**.
- Other important French trading centres: **Mahe, Karaikal, Balasore, and Qasim Bazar**.

---

## 4.3 Early Setbacks to the French East India Company

- War broke out between the **Dutch and the French** — badly affecting the French position in India.
- Bolstered by their **alliance with the English (since the Revolution of 1688)**, the Dutch **captured Pondicherry in 1693**.
- The **Treaty of Ryswick (September 1697)** restored Pondicherry to the French, but the **Dutch garrison held on for two more years**.
- Under **François Martin's able guidance**, Pondicherry flourished again as the **most important French settlement in India**.
- Another setback: The **War of Spanish Succession** in Europe forced the French to **abandon their factories at Surat, Masulipatnam, and Bantam** in the early 18th century.
- **François Martin died on December 31, 1706** — another blow to French fortunes.

---

## 4.4 Reorganisation of the French Company (1720)

- In **1720**, the French company was reorganised as the **"Perpetual Company of the Indies"** — reviving its strength.
- Under two active and wise governors — **Lenoir and Dumas (1720–1742)** — French power was further enhanced.
- French India was backed by French possession of **Mauritius and Reunion** in the southern Indian Ocean.

# ⚔️ PART 5: THE CARNATIC WARS — ANGLO-FRENCH STRUGGLE FOR SUPREMACY

---

## 5.1 Background of Anglo-French Rivalry in India

- Both the British and French came for **trade**, but were ultimately drawn into **Indian politics**.
- Both had visions of **establishing political power over the region**.
- The **Anglo-French rivalry in India** reflected their **traditional rivalry throughout history**.
- It began with the **Austrian War of Succession** and ended with the **Seven Years' War**.
- In India specifically, the rivalry took the form of **Three Carnatic Wars** — which decided once and for all that **the English, not the French, would become masters of India**.

### The Political Situation in South India (1740)
- **Nizam Asaf Jah of Hyderabad** was old and battling the Marathas; his subordinates were speculating on his death.
- South of Hyderabad: The **Coromandel Coast** had no strong ruler.
- Remnants of the old Vijayanagara Empire survived in **interior Mysore**.
- On the Malabar Coast: **Cochin and Travancore**.
- In the east: small states of **Madura (Madurai), Tanjore (Thanjavur), and Trichinopoly (Thiruchirapally)**.
- The decline of Hyderabad signalled the **end of Muslim expansionism** — and European adventurers began making plans.
- The **Maratha kingdom of Tanjore** gave the **Peshwa of Pune** excuses to interfere whenever he pleased.

---

## 5.2 First Carnatic War (1740–1748)

### Background
- **Carnatic** = the name Europeans gave to the Coromandel Coast and its hinterland.
- The First Carnatic War was an **extension of the Anglo-French War in Europe**, caused by the **Austrian War of Succession**.

### Immediate Cause
- France, aware of its weaker position in India, **did not want hostilities to extend to India**.
- But the **English navy under Barnet seized some French ships** — provoking France.
- France retaliated: **Madras was seized in 1746** with help from the fleet from Mauritius (the Isle of France) under **Admiral La Bourdonnais**, the French governor of Mauritius.
- Thus began the **First Carnatic War**.

### Result
- Ended in **1748** with the **Treaty of Aix-La Chapelle** (which ended the Austrian War of Succession).
- Under its terms: **Madras was returned to the English**; the French received their territories in **North America**.

### Significance: The Battle of St. Thome
- The First Carnatic War is remembered for the **Battle of St. Thome (in Madras)**, fought between the French and the forces of **Anwaruddin, the Nawab of Carnatic** (to whom the English appealed for help).
- A **small French army under Captain Paradise** defeated the **strong Indian army under Mahfuz Khan** at St. Thome on the banks of the **River Adyar**.
- **Eye-opener:** This revealed that even a **small, disciplined European army could easily defeat a much larger Indian army**.
- The war also demonstrated the **crucial importance of naval force** in the Anglo-French conflict in the Deccan.

---

## 5.3 Second Carnatic War (1749–1754)

### Background
- **Dupleix**, the French governor who had successfully led French forces in the First Carnatic War, sought to increase French political influence by **interfering in local dynastic disputes**.

### Immediate Cause
Two key events in **1748** provided the opportunity:

1. **Death of Nizam-ul-Mulk** (founder of independent Hyderabad) — **succession dispute followed**
2. **Release of Chanda Sahib** (son-in-law of Dost Ali, Nawab of Carnatic) by the Marathas

- **Nasir Jung** (Nizam's son) claimed the throne of Hyderabad.
- **Muzaffar Jung** (Nizam's grandson) opposed Nasir Jung, claiming the Mughal Emperor had appointed him governor.
- In Carnatic, **Chanda Sahib** resented the appointment of **Anwaruddin Khan** as Nawab.

**Sides:**
- **French** supported → **Muzaffar Jang** (Hyderabad) + **Chanda Sahib** (Carnatic)
- **English** supported → **Nasir Jang** (Hyderabad) + **Anwaruddin** (Carnatic)

### Course of the War
- Combined armies of **Muzaffar Jang, Chanda Sahib, and the French** defeated and **killed Anwaruddin at the Battle of Ambur (near Vellore) in 1749**.
- **Muzaffar Jang became the subahdar (governor) of Deccan**.
- **Dupleix was appointed governor of all Mughal territories south of the River Krishna**.
- A **French army under Bussy** was stationed at Hyderabad to protect French interests.
- Territories near Pondicherry and areas on the **Orissa coast (including Masulipatnam)** were **ceded to the French**.

### Robert Clive's Masterstroke: The Siege of Arcot (1751)
- The French and Chanda Sahib were besieging **Trichinopoly** where English-backed **Muhammad Ali** was trapped.
- **Robert Clive**, then just a Company agent (called a "factor"), proposed a **diversionary attack on Arcot** (the capital of the Carnatic) to relieve the pressure.
- His logic: If Arcot was attacked, **Chanda Sahib would rush back to save his capital**, lifting the siege of Trichinopoly.
- In **August 1751**, with only **210 men**, Clive attacked and captured Arcot.
- As predicted, Chanda Sahib rushed back with **4,000 men from Trichinopoly**.
- But he **failed to retake the fort even after a siege of 53 days (September 23 – November 14)**.
- **Mysore, Tanjore, and the Maratha chief Morari Rao** came to aid Trichinopoly, helping Clive and **Stringer Lawrence**.
- **Trichinopoly was relieved of its siege**.
- **General Law of France** with Chanda Sahib was **cooped up on the island of Srirangam** and forced to surrender in **June 1752**.
- **Muhammad Ali executed Chanda Sahib** — the British failing to interfere.

### Result
- French authorities, **annoyed at heavy financial losses**, recalled **Dupleix in 1754**.
- **Godeheu** succeeded Dupleix and adopted a policy of negotiations, concluding a treaty with the English.
- Both sides agreed **not to interfere in disputes between native princes**.
- Each side retained territories currently occupied by them.
- **Fear of repercussions in America** prompted France to suspend hostilities in India.

### Implications
- It became evident that **Indian rulers were no longer the patrons of Europeans — they were becoming dependent on European support**.
- **Muhammad Ali in Carnatic** and **Salabat Jang in Hyderabad** became **clients rather than patrons**.

---

## 5.4 Dupleix: Rise, Genius, and Fall

### Who Was Dupleix?
- **Joseph Francis Dupleix**, born **1697**, son of a wealthy Farmer-General of Taxes.
- Got a high post at Pondicherry in **1720** (allegedly through his father's influence).
- Made significant personal wealth through private trade (then permitted for Company servants).
- Suspended in **December 1726** due to constitutional changes in the French company.
- Won his case in **1730** and was appointed **Governor of Chandernagore** as compensation.
- In **1741**, appointed **Director-General of French colonies in India**.
- Conferred the **title of Nawab** by the Mughal emperor and the Subahdar of Deccan, Muzzaffar Jang.

### Dupleix as Administrator
- Found Pondicherry facing multiple crises: **Maratha invasion, famine, uncultivated land, and chaos**.
- Company directors demanded drastic **cuts in expenditure**.
- Dupleix **reduced public expenditure**, balanced income and expenditure, and **cut officer salaries** — despite opposition.
- He **defied the directors on fortification** — strengthened Pondicherry's defences using his **personal wealth**.
- Made Pondicherry the **commercial emporium of south India**.
- Even the directors later **praised his decisions**.

### Dupleix as Master Diplomat
- Used the **Nawab of Carnatic** to forbid the English from waging war, protecting Pondicherry while French forces built strength.
- Promised Madras to the Nawab after English defeat — but **used diplomacy to avoid keeping the promise**, even defeating the Nawab at St. Thome (1746).
- Convinced **Admiral La Bourdonnais** to break promises made to the English, using the argument that promises made in certain circumstances are not binding.
- **Was the first European to interfere in the internal politics of Indian rulers**.
- **Originated the practice of Subsidiary Alliance** (placing a European army in an Indian ruler's territory at the ruler's expense) — later famously used by the British under Lord Wellesley.

### Why Dupleix Failed
- *(i)* **Over-sanguine temperament** — hoped too much for too long, missing critical advantages.
- *(ii)* **Autocratic behaviour** — his peers frequently quarrelled with him.
- *(iii)* **Not a man of action in battle** — he planned campaigns and directed subordinates but **never personally led an army** (unlike Lawrence or Clive). The failure to capture Trichinopoly (1752–53) demonstrated this weakness.

---

## 5.5 Third Carnatic War (1758–1763)

### Background
- In Europe, Austria wanted to recover **Silesia** in **1756** — triggering the **Seven Years' War (1756–63)**.
- Britain and France were **once again on opposite sides**.

### Course of War in India
- In **1758**, the French army under **Count de Lally** captured English forts at **St. David and Vizianagaram**.
- The English became offensive and inflicted heavy losses on the **French fleet under Admiral D'Ache at Masulipatnam**.

### The Battle of Wandiwash (January 22, 1760)
- The **decisive battle** of the Third Carnatic War.
- Won by the English at **Wandiwash (or Vandavasi) in Tamil Nadu**.
- **General Eyre Coote** completely routed the French army under **Count Thomas Arthur de Lally**.
- **Bussy was taken prisoner**.
- **Pondicherry was gallantly defended by Lally for eight months** before he finally surrendered on **January 16, 1761**.
- With the loss of **Jinji and Mahe**, French power in India was at its lowest point.
- After the war, **Lally** was taken prisoner to London, returned to France, and was **imprisoned and executed in 1766**.

### Result and Significance
- The **Treaty of Peace of Paris (1763)** restored French factories in India — but **French political influence disappeared** after the war.
- The French, like the Portuguese and Dutch, **confined themselves to small enclaves and to commerce only**.
- **The English became the supreme European power in the Indian subcontinent.**
- The Dutch had already been defeated at the **Battle of Bidara in 1759**.

### The Real Turning Point?
> The **Battle of Plassey (1757)** is usually regarded as the decisive event leading to British rule over India. However, many historians argue that the **true turning point was the Battle of Wandiwash (1760)** — because it **eliminated the last European rival** of the English East India Company in India.

### The Sepoy Observation
- Significantly, in the **Battle of Wandiwash**, **natives (Indian soldiers) served in BOTH armies as sepoys (Indian soldiers in European armies)**.
- This makes a sobering point: **regardless of which European side won, India was going to fall to European invaders**. There was a fundamental **lack of geopolitical awareness and foresight** among Indian rulers.

---

## 5.6 Causes for English Success and French Failure

### Structure and Nature of the Companies
- The **English East India Company** was a **private enterprise** → created enthusiasm, self-confidence, and the ability to make **instant decisions without waiting for government approval**.
- The **French company was a State concern** → controlled by the French government, slowed by policies and bureaucratic delays.
- In the French company: the **monarch held more than 60% share**; directors were **nominated by the monarch** and were supposed to carry out decisions of two government-appointed high commissioners.
- **Shareholders took very little interest** because the State guaranteed dividends regardless.
- **Between 1725 and 1765, there was no meeting of French shareholders** — the company was run like a government department.

### Naval Superiority
- The **Royal Navy of Britain** was the **largest and most advanced** of its times.
- Victory over the **Spanish Armada** and at the **Battle of Trafalgar** put it at the peak of European naval power.
- The English had **learned from the Portuguese** the importance of naval power and **technologically improved their fleet**.
- Superior naval power helped **cut the vital sea link between French possessions in India and France**.

### Industrial Revolution
- The **Industrial Revolution began in England in the early 18th century** with inventions like the **spinning Jenny, steam engine, power loom**, and others.
- These improved production in **textiles, metallurgy, steam power, and agriculture**.
- The Industrial Revolution **reached other European nations much later** — giving England a lasting advantage.

### Military Skill and Discipline
- British soldiers were **well-trained and disciplined**.
- British commanders were **strategic innovators**.
- Technological development equipped the military well.
- **Smaller groups of English soldiers could defeat much larger armies**.

### Stable Government
- With the **exception of the Glorious Revolution of 1688**, Britain had **stable governance** with efficient monarchs.
- Compare this to:
  - France: Violent **Revolution of 1789** + **Napoleonic Wars**; **Napoleon's defeat in 1815** weakened France severely; France was then forced to side with Britain.
  - Italy: Unified as a nation only in **1861**.
  - Netherlands and Spain: Involved in an **80-years war** in the 17th century (weakening Portuguese imperialism).
  - The **Dutch East India Company** went **bankrupt in 1800**, followed by revolution in **1830** — forced to sell its possessions to Britain and quit Asia.

### Lesser Zeal for Religion
- Britain was **less zealous about religion** and less interested in spreading Christianity compared to Spain, Portugal, or the Dutch.
- This made British rule **far more acceptable** to Indian subjects than other colonial powers.

### Use of the Debt Market
- One of the most **innovative and decisive reasons** for British success was its use of **debt markets to fund wars**.
- The world's **first central bank — the Bank of England** — was established to **sell government debt to money markets**, promising decent returns upon defeating rivals like France and Spain.
- Britain could **spend much more on its military** than France.
- France, between **1694 and 1812**, went through monarchs, revolutionary governments, and Napoleon Bonaparte — and **simply went bankrupt** with its outdated methods of raising money.

- The **Danish East India Company** was established in **1616**.
- In **1620**, they founded a factory at **Tranquebar** (near Tanjore) on the eastern coast of India.
- Their principal settlement was at **Serampore (near Calcutta)**.
- The Danish factories were **never significant** at any point in time.
- They were **sold to the British government in 1845**.
- The Danes are **better known for their missionary activities** than for commerce.

# 🛒 PART 7: THE GOODS IN TRADE — WHAT INDIA OFFERED

### The Textile Trade
European travellers and traders left detailed accounts of the **complex production and trade of Indian textiles**. Greatly demanded goods included:

- **Cotton longcloth** (usually 35–50 metres in length)
- **Salempores** (staple cotton cloth)
- **Morees** (superior quality cotton cloth)
- **Painted cloths and prints**
- **Silks and dyes**

These were not just for Europe — **Indian textiles were popular across Asia**:
- In **China, Japan, and the Indonesian archipelago**, Indian cotton was prized for being **light yet strong**.
- When the Dutch, English, and French acquired Indian goods, they weren't just for home countries — they were also transported to **Malacca or Java** and traded for **spices**.
- By the **18th century**, the French had **coloured patterned handkerchiefs specially woven** for specific island markets.

### The Slave Trade Connection
- A corollary to the trade in textiles and spices was the **trade in slaves** — often ignored in Euro-Asia trade discussions.
- French ships took European goods to Asia, where they acquired **cowry shells and Indian textiles** (highly valued in West Africa).
- These were exchanged in **Africa for slaves**, who were sent to **French colonies in the Americas**.
- The Yale Center for the Study of Globalisation notes: *"The circle was completed when sugar and other goods from the Americas were loaded and shipped back to France."*

### The Already-Existing Indian Trade System
- When the French East India Company started trading, it entered an **already well-established, complex economic system**.
- **Indian merchants ran large commercial fleets and prosperous shore-based businesses**.
- Weavers and merchants worked with overland freight operators, brokers, exporters, and ship owners — all of whom had to **negotiate with local state officials for commercial privileges**.
- European traders **had to learn well-established rules** and successfully collaborate with **indigenous envoys**.
- At the peak of Indian trade, **demand for Indian goods actually exceeded supply** from weavers and artisans — initially without serious rivalry.

### How the English Won the Trade War
- As the **Dutch, English, and French companies grew more competitive**, the English — **better funded and better versed in local business practices** — expanded their factory outposts to **larger industrial towns under their jurisdiction**.
- Gradually, these **commercial strongholds turned into political enclaves**, enabling the English to **expand and consolidate power** across all of India.
