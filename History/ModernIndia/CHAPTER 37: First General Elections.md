# 📘 Chapter 37: First General Elections

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Big Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. India's **first general elections (1951–52)** were a gigantic democratic experiment — **17.32 crore voters**, over **70% illiterate**, voting for the first time in history.
> 2. The **Election Commission** was set up on **January 25, 1950**, with **Sukumar Sen** as the first Chief Election Commissioner — he managed the entire exercise with extraordinary skill.
> 3. Two landmark laws — the **Representation of the People Acts of 1950 and 1951** — formed the legal backbone of the elections.
> 4. The **Indian National Congress** won a landslide — **364 out of 489 seats** (~45% of votes), while most other parties were just getting started.
> 5. The elections proved the **sceptics wrong** — despite mass illiteracy and poverty, the voter turnout was **45.7%** and invalid votes were only **3–4%**.

---

## 🏛️ Part 1: Constitutional Foundation — Setting the Stage

### India Becomes a Sovereign Republic

When the **Constitution came into force in 1950**, India shed its **dominion status** (a semi-independent status under British authority) and became a fully **sovereign democratic republic** — with no remaining links to Britain.

**Key dates to remember:**
- **November 26, 1949** — Provisions relating to **citizenship** and **Article 324** (Election Commission) came into force
- **January 26, 1950** — Rest of the Constitution came into force (Republic Day)
- The government then moved to hold **general elections** to constitute the **House of the People (Lok Sabha)**, as required by the Constitution

### Electoral Provisions in the Constitution

The **founding fathers** of the Indian Constitution built the electoral framework directly into the Constitution itself:
- **Part XV**, **Articles 324 to 329** contain the core electoral provisions
- This ensured elections could not be easily tampered with by ordinary legislation

## 🗳️ Part 2: The Election Commission — Setting Up the Machinery

### Establishment:
- The **Election Commission of India** was set up on **January 25, 1950**
- It is a **permanent constitutional body** (a body established by the Constitution itself, not by ordinary law)
- Made **independent of the government of the day** — a crucial design choice

### First Chief Election Commissioner:
- **Sukumar Sen**, an **ICS officer**, assumed office on **March 21, 1950**
- He led the massive exercise of conducting India's first general elections

### Powers Under Article 324:
Under **Article 324**, the Election Commission has been vested with the power of:
- **Superintendence** (overall oversight)
- **Direction** (issuing instructions)
- **Control** (enforcement)

...over the **entire process** of elections to:
- Parliament
- Legislature of every state
- Offices of the **President** and **Vice-President** of India

> **Note:** Although the Constitution allows for multiple members in the Election Commission, for a long time after India became a republic, it remained a **single-member commission**.

---

## 📜 Part 3: The Legal Framework — Two Landmark Laws

Before elections could be held, Parliament passed **two major pieces of legislation**:

### Law 1: Representation of the People Act, 1950
This law dealt with:
- **Qualifications of voters**
- Preparation and publication of **electoral rolls**
- **Allocation of seats** in the House of the People to various states
- **Fixing the number of seats** in each state legislature

### Law 2: Representation of the People Act, 1951
This law dealt with:
- Qualifications and **disqualifications of members**
- **Conduct of elections**
- **Poll expenses**
- The **poll itself** and **counting of votes**
- Other related provisions

> Only after **both laws were passed** could the electoral machinery be fully put in place. Despite the government's desire to hold elections as early as **1950** or by **spring 1951**, the **first phase of elections could only begin on October 15, 1951**.

## 📊 Part 4: The Scale of the Exercise — By the Numbers

### Seats:

| House | Total Seats | Reserved for SC | Reserved for ST |
|---|---|---|---|
| **House of the People (Lok Sabha)** | **489** | **72** | **26** |
| **State Legislative Assemblies** | **3,283** | **477** | **192** |

### Voters:
- **Total enrolled voters** (excluding Jammu & Kashmir): **17,32,13,635** (~173 million / 17.32 crore)
- **Women voters**: approximately **45%** of total enrolled voters
- **Total population** (excluding J&K) as per **1951 census**: **35,66,91,760**
- This means **49% of the total population** was enrolled as voters

### Voting Age:
- **Universal adult franchise** (the right of every adult citizen to vote) — all persons **21 years of age or older** had the right to vote

## ⚡ Part 5: The Challenges — A Truly Unique Exercise

### The Big Scepticism:
- Most voters were **poor, illiterate, and rural**
- They had **no prior experience** of elections
- The exercise was described as:
  - *"A leap in the dark"*
  - *"Fantastic"*
  - *"An act of faith"*

### Challenge 1: Voter Registration
The Election Commission conducted a **house-to-house survey** to register voters. But many eligible voters couldn't be included because of:
- **Ignorance and apathy** of common voters
- **Lack of organisation and experience** of political parties
- **Inexperience and poor organisation** of government machinery in some states

### Challenge 2: The Women's Names Problem
This is a remarkable and uniquely Indian challenge documented in **Sukumar Sen's post-election report**:

- In several states, women voters were enrolled **not by their own names** but by descriptions like *"mother of so-and-so"* or *"wife of so-and-so"*
- Reason: **local custom** dictated that women should not disclose their names to strangers
- The Election Commission issued firm instructions: **correct name is essential** to identity; no elector to be registered without their proper name
- **Result:** The names of **nearly 2 to 3 million** women (out of ~80 million women voters) could NOT be registered — they couldn't vote
- This problem was most severe in: **Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Bharat, Rajasthan, and Vindhya Pradesh**

### Challenge 3: Displaced Persons (Partition Refugees)
- A major registration problem existed for **displaced persons** (migrants from Pakistan after partition) in:
  - **West Bengal**
  - **Punjab**
  - **Delhi**
  - (to some extent) **Assam**
- These migrants were a **considerable floating population** (people with no fixed address), making registration extremely difficult

### Challenge 4: Political Parties Lacked Experience
- In a **mature democracy** (a well-established democracy), parties help prepare electoral rolls and inform voters
- But India was a **nascent democracy** (a newly born democracy) — parties were inexperienced
- They took **little interest** in assisting the registration process
- Result: The **entire burden of preparing electoral rolls** for ~173 million voters fell almost entirely on the **government machinery in the states**, acting under Election Commission directions

### Challenge 5: Illiteracy and Symbols
- With **over 70% of voters being illiterate**, candidates could NOT be identified by written names
- The Election Commission had to **allot a symbol** to each major party and independent candidate
- Symbols were **painted on ballot-boxes** (this was later changed to symbols on ballot papers)
- Voting process: *"A voter had to simply insert the ballot paper given to him in the ballot box of the candidate of his choice in the voting compartment"* — as described by former Chief Election Commissioner **S.Y. Quraishi** in his book ***An Undocumented Wonder: The Making of the Great Indian Election***
- The ballot was **secret**

### Challenge 6: Mushroom Growth of Parties
- As symbols were allotted, political activity intensified
- A remarkable feature was the **formation of new political parties all over the country**
- There were as many as **178 parties** in total
- Most of these **virtually disappeared after the elections**, with many candidates **forfeiting their deposits**

## ⚙️ Part 7: How the Elections Were Conducted

### Constituency Structure:
| Type | Number | Seats |
|---|---|---|
| Single-seat constituencies | 314 | 314 |
| Two-seat constituencies | 86 | 172 |
| Three-seat constituencies | 1 | 3 |
| **Total** | **401 constituencies** | **489 seats** |

### Infrastructure:
- **Over 2,24,000 polling booths** — approximately one booth for every **1,000 voters**
- **Over 20 lakh (2 million) steel ballot-boxes** — one box per candidate
- **About 10 lakh (1 million) officials** supervised the polls

### Key Innovations:
- **Indelible ink** was introduced — a mark was put on the voter's finger to **prevent impersonation** (voting twice or in someone else's name)
  > *(This practice continues to this day)*

### Electoral System:
- **First-Past-the-Post (FPTP) system** (whoever gets the most votes in a constituency wins, even without a majority) — the winning candidate needed only a **plurality** (the largest number of votes), NOT an absolute majority

### Notable Winners and Losers:

**Notable Winners:**
- **Gulzari Lal Nanda** and **Lal Bahadur Shastri** — both future Prime Ministers of India
- **Chaudhry Brahm Prakash** — Delhi's first Chief Minister to be
- **Humayun Kabir, A.K. Gopalan, Rafi Ahmed Kidwai, K.D. Malviya, Subhadra Joshi**

**Notable Defeats:**
- **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** was **defeated** in Bombay (North Central) — a reserved constituency — as a Scheduled Castes Federation candidate, by his little-known former assistant and Congress candidate, **Narayan Sadoba Kajrolkar**
  > *(Dr. Ambedkar later entered Parliament as a **Rajya Sabha** member)*
- **Acharya Kripalani** (KMPP candidate) lost from **Faizabad, UP**
  > But his wife, **Sucheta Kripalani**, defeated Congress candidate **Manmohini Sahgal** in **Delhi**

### Key Constitutional Firsts After the Election:
- The **first Lok Sabha (House of the People)** was constituted by the Election Commission on **April 2, 1952**
  > *(Until this point, the Indian Constituent Assembly had served as an interim legislature)*
- **Speaker of the first Lok Sabha:** **Ganesh Vasudev Mavalankar**
- **First Prime Minister after general elections:** **Jawaharlal Nehru**

## 🏛️ Part 9: State Legislature Results

- The **Indian National Congress** swept the state polls as well
- **Total state assembly seats won: 2,248**
- Congress **formed the government in ALL states**
- However, it did **not get an outright majority** on its own in **four states**:
  1. **Madras**
  2. **Travancore-Cochin**
  3. **Orissa**
  4. **PEPSU** (Patiala and East Punjab States Union)

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *These sections connect historical concepts to present-day events and policies.*

### On the Election Commission's Independence:
- The **Election Commission (Amendment) Act, 2023** changed the process of appointing Election Commissioners — removing the Chief Justice of India from the selection panel and replacing it with a Cabinet Minister. This sparked a major national debate about the **independence of the Election Commission**, making Article 324 more relevant today than ever. The Supreme Court in **Anoop Baranwal vs. Union of India (2023)** had previously mandated a collegium system for appointments (including the CJI). `[Modern Example]`

### On Universal Adult Franchise and Voter Registration:
- India's voter base has grown from **17.32 crore in 1951** to approximately **96.88 crore registered voters** in the **2024 General Elections** — a nearly **6-fold increase** — demonstrating the deepening of democracy. `[Modern Example]`
- The **Election Commission's SVEEP programme** (Systematic Voters' Education and Electoral Participation) directly addresses the same challenge Sukumar Sen faced in 1951–52: low participation among women, youth, and marginalised communities. `[Modern Example]`

### On Women Voters — From Names Withheld to Political Participation:
- In 1951–52, **2–3 million women** couldn't vote because they refused to give their names. By the **2019 General Elections**, the **gender voter turnout gap almost closed** for the first time — women's turnout (67.18%) nearly matched men's (67.01%). `[Modern Example]`
- The **Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam (Women's Reservation Act, 2023)** — reserving **33% of Lok Sabha and State Assembly seats for women** — is the modern constitutional fulfillment of the democratic promise made in 1951. `[Modern Example]`

### On Ballot Symbols and Technology:
- India moved from **symbols painted on ballot boxes** (1951) to **Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs)** (nationally adopted from **2004**) to the current **Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT)** system. The Supreme Court in **Association for Democratic Reforms vs. ECI (2024)** dismissed petitions demanding a return to ballot papers, upholding EVMs. `[Modern Example]`

### On the Indelible Ink Tradition:
- **Indelible ink** introduced in 1952 is still used today — manufactured by the **Mysore Paints and Varnish Limited (MPVL)**, a government undertaking in Karnataka. During the **2024 General Elections**, MPVL supplied **26.66 lakh ink vials** for use across India. This 72-year-old innovation remains the simplest and most effective anti-fraud measure. `[Modern Example]`

### On the First-Past-the-Post System:
- The **FPTP system** used since 1952 continues to be debated. The **Law Commission's 255th Report (2015)** examined electoral reforms including the possibility of **Proportional Representation**. In 2024, the debate was reignited during discussions on the **One Nation One Election** proposal. `[Modern Example]`

### On Congress's Dominance and the Multi-Party System:
- The **Congress's 1952 performance (364/489 seats, ~45% votes)** established the era of **Congress dominance** that lasted until 1977. The contrast with **2024** — where the **BJP-led NDA** won 293 seats with the Congress winning only 99 — illustrates how dramatically India's party system has evolved over 70 years. `[Modern Example]`
