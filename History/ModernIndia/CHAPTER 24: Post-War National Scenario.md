# CHAPTER 24: Post-War National Scenario

---

## 🔍 Part 1: Two Basic Strands of National Upsurge (1945–47)

The last two years of British rule in India were characterised by two distinct but simultaneous streams of activity:

**Strand 1: Tortuous Negotiations**
- Increasingly complex negotiations involving the **government, Congress, and Muslim League**.
- Accompanied by **communal violence** on an unprecedented scale.
- Culminating in **freedom and partition**.

**Strand 2: Militant Mass Action**
- Sporadic, localised, and often extremely militant and **united mass action** by workers, peasants, and states' peoples.
- Took the form of a **countrywide strike wave**.
- Key events included:
  - **INA Release Movement**
  - **Royal Indian Navy (RIN) revolt**
  - **Tebhaga movement**
  - **Worli revolt**
  - **Punjab Kisan Morchas**
  - **Travancore peoples' struggle** (especially the **Punnapra-Vayalar episode**)
  - **Telangana peasant revolt**

---

## 🔄 Part 2: Change in the British Government's Attitude

### The Surprise the British Found

When the government lifted the ban on Congress and released Congress leaders in **June 1945**, it expected to find a **demoralised people**.

Instead, it found **tumultuous crowds impatient to do something** — popular energy had resurfaced after three years of repression.

### The Turning Point — Labour Comes to Power

- **July 1945:** The **Labour Party** formed the government in Britain.
  - **Clement Attlee** became Prime Minister.
  - **Pethick Lawrence** became the new Secretary of State for India.
- **August 1945:** Elections to central and provincial assemblies were announced.
- **September 1945:** It was announced that a **constituent assembly** would be convened after the elections, working according to the spirit of the Cripps Offer.

### Seven Reasons for the Change in British Attitude

1. **Change in global power balance:** The UK was no longer a great power. The **USA and USSR had emerged as superpowers** — both favoured freedom for India.

2. **New Labour government:** More sympathetic to Indian demands than the Conservatives.

3. **Socialist wave across Europe:** Throughout Europe, there was a wave of socialist-radical governments coming to power.

4. **British exhaustion and debt:** British soldiers were weary and tired and the **British economy lay shattered**. By 1945, the British government owed India **£1.2 billion** and was being drained by the **US Lend-Lease agreement** (which was finally paid off only in **2006**).

5. **Anti-imperialist wave in South-East Asia:** In **Vietnam** and **Indonesia**, there was fierce resistance to efforts to re-plant French and Dutch colonial rule.

6. **Fear of another revolt:** Officials feared another Congress revolt — a revival of the **1942 situation but much more dangerous** because of a likely combination of:
   - Attacks on communications
   - Agrarian revolts
   - Labour trouble
   - Army disaffection
   - Government officials and police joining the revolt
   - INA men with military experience supporting the movement

7. **Elections were inevitable:** The last elections had been held in **1934 for the Centre** and in **1937 for the provinces**. The British would have had to retreat anyway — *the Labour government only quickened the process somewhat*.

## 🗳️ Part 3: Congress Election Campaign and INA Trials

### What Made the 1945–46 Election Campaign Special

The most significant feature was that the Congress election campaign was **not just an appeal for votes** — it sought to **mobilise Indians against the British**.

**How the Campaign Expressed Nationalism:**
- ✅ Glorified martyrs of the 1942 Quit India Movement.
- ✅ Condemned officials responsible for repression.
- ✅ Lauded the brave leaderless resistance of the people.
- ✅ Set up **martyrs' memorials**.
- ✅ Collected **relief funds** for sufferers.
- ✅ Promised **enquiry** and threatened **punishment to guilty officials**.

This had a **devastating effect on the morale of the services**. The prospect of Congress ministries returning to power — especially in provinces where repression had been most brutal — **heightened the fears of those in government services**.

A **'gentleman's agreement'** with the Congress seemed necessary to the government.

---

### The INA Trials — A Massive Political Blunder by the British

Mass pressure against the trial of INA POWs — described as *"an edge of a volcano"* — brought about a **decisive shift in the government's policy**.

**The British Blunders:**
- Initially decided to hold public trials of **several hundreds** of INA prisoners, besides:
  - Dismissing them from service.
  - Detaining without trial approximately **7,000** of them.
- Then compounded this by **holding the first trial at the Red Fort in Delhi in November 1945**.
- Put on the dock together a **Hindu (Prem Kumar Sehgal), a Muslim (Shah Nawaz Khan)**, and a **Sikh (Gurbaksh Singh Dhillon)** — inadvertently creating a powerful symbol of **Hindu-Muslim-Sikh unity**.
- Additionally, using **Indian Army units to restore French and Dutch colonial rule** in Vietnam and Indonesia enhanced anti-imperialist feeling among urban populations and the army.

---

### Congress Support for INA Prisoners

- **September 1945 (Bombay):** At the first post-War Congress session, a strong resolution was adopted **declaring Congress support for the INA cause**.
- **Legal defence** of INA prisoners was organised by: **Bhulabhai Desai, Tej Bahadur Sapru, Kailash Nath Katju, Jawaharlal Nehru**, and **Asaf Ali**.
- **INA Relief and Enquiry Committee** distributed small sums of money, food, and helped arrange employment.
- **Fund collection** was organised across the country.

---

### The INA Agitation — Unprecedented in Scope

**Forms of Publicity and Participation:**
- Daily editorials and extensive press coverage.
- Distribution of pamphlets (often containing threats of revenge).
- Graffiti conveying similar messages.
- Public meetings held across the country.
- **INA Day (November 12, 1945)** and **INA Week (November 5–11)** celebrated.

**Geographic Spread:**
- Nerve centres: **Delhi, Bombay, Calcutta, Madras, United Provinces towns**, and **Punjab**.
- But spread to distant places: **Coorg, Baluchistan**, and **Assam**.

**Who Participated:**
- Film stars, municipal committees, Indians living abroad, gurudwaras, tongawallas.
- Shopkeepers closed shops.
- Student meetings and boycott of classes.
- Kisan conferences organised.
- **All India Women's Conference** demanded release of INA prisoners.

**Who Supported the INA Cause (Beyond Congress):**
> Muslim League, Communist Party, Unionists, Akalis, Justice Party, Ahrars in Rawalpindi, **RSS, Hindu Mahasabha**, and the Sikh League — a remarkable cross-party unity.

**Impact on Traditional Loyalists:**
- **Government employees** collected funds for the INA.
- **Loyalists** — the gentlemen with titles — appealed to the government to abandon the trials for good Indo-British relations.
- **Men of the armed forces** were unexpectedly sympathetic — attended meetings, received released prisoners (often in uniform), and contributed funds.
- The central theme: **questioning of Britain's right to decide a matter concerning Indians**.
- Britain realised the INA issue was taking on a purely **'Indian versus British'** colour with each passing day.

---

## ⚡ Part 4: Three Upsurges — Winter of 1945–46

Nationalist sentiment reached a crescendo and developed into **violent confrontations with authority** in the winter of 1945–46. There were three major upsurges:

1. **November 21, 1945** — in **Calcutta** over the INA trials.
2. **February 11, 1946** — in **Calcutta** against the seven-year sentence to INA officer **Rashid Ali**.
3. **February 18, 1946** — in **Bombay**, strike by the **Royal Indian Navy ratings**.

---

### Three-Stage Pattern (Common to All Three Upsurges)

**Stage I: A Group Defies Authority and Is Repressed**

*November 21, 1945 (Calcutta):*
- A student procession — comprising **Forward Bloc sympathisers, Student Federation of India (SFI)** activists, and Islamia College students — joined up with the League and the Congress, tying flags as a symbol of anti-imperialist unity.
- They marched to **Dalhousie Square** (the seat of government in Calcutta).
- When they refused to disperse, they were **lathi-charged**.
- They retaliated by throwing stones and brickbats.
- Police resorted to **firing** — **2 persons died**.

*February 11, 1946 (Calcutta):*
- Protest led by **Muslim League students**, joined by Congress and communist students' organisations.
- Arrests provoked students to defy **Section 144**.
- More arrests, lathi charges followed.

**The Royal Indian Navy Rebellion (February 18, 1946):**

Some **1,100 Royal Indian Navy (RIN) ratings of HMIS Talwar** went on a strike to protest against:
- **Racial discrimination** — demanding equal pay for Indian and white soldiers.
- **Unpalatable food**.
- **Abuse by superior officers**.
- Arrest of a rating for scrawling **'Quit India'** on HMIS Talwar.
- **INA trials**.
- **Use of Indian troops in Indonesia** — demanding their withdrawal.

**What Happened:**
- The rebellious ratings hoisted the **tricolour, crescent**, and the **hammer and sickle flags** on the mast of the rebel fleet.
- Other ratings joined — they went around Bombay in lorries holding Congress flags, **threatening Europeans and policemen**.
- **Crowds brought food** to the ratings.
- **Shopkeepers invited them** to take whatever they needed.

---

**Stage II: The City People Join In**

This phase was marked by a **virulent anti-British mood** resulting in virtual paralysis of Calcutta and Bombay:
- Meetings, processions, strikes, hartals.
- Attacks on Europeans, police stations, shops, tram depots, railway stations, banks.
- **Rail and road traffic stopped** by squatting on tracks.
- **Streets barricaded**.

---

**Stage III: People in Other Parts Express Sympathy and Solidarity**

- Students boycotted classes and organised hartals and processions.
- **Sympathetic strikes** in military establishments in:
  > Karachi, Madras, Visakhapatnam, Calcutta, Delhi, Cochin, Jamnagar, Andamans, Bahrain, and Aden.
- **Strikes by the Royal Indian Air Force** in Bombay, Poona, Calcutta, Jessore, and Ambala.

**The End of the Revolt:**
- **Patel and Jinnah persuaded the ratings to surrender** on **February 23** — with an assurance that national parties would prevent any victimisation.

---

### Evaluation of the Three Upsurges

**Significance:**
- ✅ Fearless action was an expression of **militancy in the popular mind**.
- ✅ Revolt in the armed forces had a great **liberating effect on the minds of people**.
- ✅ The RIN revolt was seen as an event **marking the end of British rule**.

**Concessions the Government Was Forced to Make:**
1. **December 1, 1946:** Government announced that only INA members accused of **murder or brutal treatment of fellow prisoners** would be brought to trial.
2. **January 1947:** Imprisonment sentences against the first batch were **remitted**.
3. **February 1947:** Indian soldiers were **withdrawn from Indo-China and Indonesia**.
4. Decision to send a **parliamentary delegation to India (November 1946)** was taken.
5. Decision to send the **Cabinet Mission** was taken in **January 1946**.

**Limitations of the Upsurges:**
- Only the **more militant sections** could participate — short-lived and confined to a few **urban centres** (while the general INA agitation reached the remotest villages).
- **Communal unity was more organisational** than among the general people — Muslim ratings went to the League for advice; the rest to the Congress and socialists.
- Despite considerable erosion of bureaucratic morale, the **British infrastructure to repress was intact**. They were soon able to control the situation — a **Maratha battalion in Bombay** rounded up the ratings.

**The Congress Strategy:**
- The Congress did **not officially support** the upsurges because of their **tactics and timing**.
- Negotiations were an integral part of Congress strategy — especially when the British were seen to be preparing to leave soon.
- Gandhi's opinion: the mutiny was **"badly advised"** — if they mutinied for India's freedom they were *"doubly wrong"*; if they had grievances, they should have waited for leaders' guidance.
- The Leftist critique: Congress indifference arose because it feared the situation would **go out of its control** and because a **disciplined armed force was vital** for a free India.

## 🌍 Modern Context — INA Trials and Mass Movements

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The INA and Anti-Racism — Still Relevant
- The RIN ratings' demand for **equal pay for Indian and white soldiers** was essentially an anti-racist demand.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Agnipath Scheme controversy (2022)** — where youth across India protested against a new short-term military recruitment scheme — echoes the same concerns about equitable treatment of Indian soldiers. The **Supreme Court dismissed petitions** challenging the Agnipath scheme in 2024, but the protests reflected a deep emotional connection between Indian youth and the armed forces that dates to the INA era.

### 2. RIN Revolt and Military Discipline
- `[Modern Example]` The RIN revolt's lesson — that colonial military forces cannot be relied upon to suppress popular uprisings — finds a modern echo in the **"Mandate of Heaven" problem** faced by authoritarian governments worldwide. The ongoing debates about the **role of the military in a democracy** (e.g., debates around the Agnipath scheme's impact on military loyalty) trace back to exactly the fears the RIN revolt produced in the British.

## 🗳️ Part 5: Election Results (Winter 1945–46)

### Congress Performance

- Got **91 per cent of non-Muslim votes**.
- Captured **57 out of 102 seats** in the Central Assembly.
- In provincial elections, got a majority in most provinces **except Bengal, Sindh, and Punjab**.
- The Congress majority provinces included the **NWFP and Assam** — which the League was claiming for Pakistan.

### Muslim League Performance

- Got **86.6 per cent of the Muslim votes**.
- Captured the **30 reserved seats** in the Central Assembly.
- Got a majority in **Bengal and Sindh** in provincial elections.
- Unlike in 1937, the League **clearly established itself as the dominant party** among Muslims.

### Punjab

A **Unionist-Congress-Akali coalition under Khizr Hayat Khan** assumed power.

### Why Elections Showed Communal Voting Despite INA Unity

Two structural reasons:
1. **Separate electorates** — forced voters into religious categories.
2. **Limited franchise** — for provinces, less than **10 per cent** of the population could vote; for the Central Assembly, less than **1 per cent** was eligible.

## 🏛️ Part 6: The Cabinet Mission (1946)

### Why Was It Sent?

The Attlee government announced in **February 1946** the decision to send a **high-powered mission of three British cabinet members** to India:
- **Pethick Lawrence** — Secretary of State for India (**Chairman of the Mission**)
- **Stafford Cripps** — President of the Board of Trade
- **A.V. Alexander** — First Lord of Admiralty

**Mission:** To find ways and means for a **negotiated, peaceful transfer of power to India**.

---

### Why British Withdrawal Seemed Imminent by 1946 — Six Reasons

1. **Success of nationalist forces:** Nationalism had penetrated into hitherto untouched sections and areas.

2. **Demoralisation of the bureaucracy:** European ICS recruits had been declining since WWI; the policy of Indianisation had ended British domination of the ICS. By 1939, there was **British-Indian parity** in the ICS. The long war had caused weariness. Now only a **depleted, war-weary bureaucracy** battered by the 1942 events remained.

3. **Contradictions in British strategy:**
   - After the Cripps Offer, there was **little left to offer** except full freedom.
   - When non-violent resistance was repressed with force, the **naked force behind the government** stood exposed.
   - If the government didn't clamp down on sedition or made truce offers, its **prestige suffered**.
   - Efforts to woo the Congress **dismayed the loyalists**.
   - Congress ministries coming to power in provinces **compounded the dilemma** for services.

4. **Constitutionalism boosted patriotism:** Congress Raj had proved a big morale-booster and helped patriotic sentiments penetrate deeper among the masses.

5. **Unreliable armed forces:** Demands for leniency for INA prisoners from within the army and the **RIN ratings' revolt** raised fears that the armed forces might not be reliable in a 1942-type mass movement aided by provincial ministries.

6. **Official rule was impossible:** The necessary numbers and efficient officials were simply not available for an entirely official rule.

> *"Our time in India is limited and our power to control events almost gone. We have only prestige and previous momentum to trade on and these will not last long."* — **Lord Wavell (October 1946)**

> *"The British Cabinet saw the growing rift between the Congress and the Muslim League as their trump card... Both Linlithgow and the Cabinet looked to the rivalry of the Congress and the League as their most useful weapon against the demands of either."* — **B.R. Tomlinson**

**The overarching aim** of British policymakers was now a **graceful withdrawal** — after a settlement on the modalities of transfer of power and the nature of post-imperial India-Britain relations.

---

### On the Eve of the Cabinet Mission Plan

**Congress Position:**
- Power should be transferred to **one centre**.
- Minorities' demands to be worked out in a framework ranging from **autonomy to Muslim-majority provinces** to self-determination or secession from the Indian Union — but **only after the British left**.

**British Position:**
- Britain bid for a **united and friendly India** — an active partner in defence of the Commonwealth.
- A **divided India would lack defence capacity** and would be a blot on Britain's diplomacy.
- British policy in 1946 clearly reflected a **preference for a united India** — in sharp contrast to earlier declarations.

**Attlee's statement (March 15, 1946):**
> *"...though mindful of the rights of minorities... cannot allow a minority to place their veto on advance of the majority."*
This was a far cry from the Shimla Conference where Wavell had allowed Jinnah to wreck the conference.

**Cabinet Mission reached Delhi: March 24, 1946**

---

### Cabinet Mission Plan — Main Points

The mission put forward its own plan in **May 1946**:

**1. Rejection of a Full Pakistan — Five Reasons:**
- (i) Pakistan as proposed would include **large non-Muslim populations** — 38% in the North-West and 48% in the North-East.
- (ii) The same principle of communal self-determination would logically demand separation of **Hindu-majority western Bengal** and **Sikh and Hindu-dominated Ambala and Jullundur divisions of Punjab** (some Sikh leaders were already demanding a separate state).
- (iii) Deep-seated **regional ties would be disturbed** if Bengal and Punjab were partitioned.
- (iv) Partition would create **economic and administrative problems** — e.g., the problem of communication between the western and eastern parts of Pakistan.
- (v) The **division of the armed forces** would be dangerous.

**2. Three-Section Grouping of Provinces:**

| Section | Provinces | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Section A** | Madras, Bombay, Central Provinces, United Provinces, Bihar, Orissa | Hindu-majority provinces |
| **Section B** | Punjab, North-West Frontier Province, Sindh | Muslim-majority provinces |
| **Section C** | Bengal and Assam | Muslim-majority provinces |

**3. Three-Tier Structure:**
- Executive and legislature at **provincial, section, and union levels**.

**4. Constituent Assembly:**
- Elected by provincial assemblies by **proportional representation** (voting in three groups: General, Muslims, Sikhs).
- A **389-member body**:
  - **292 members** from provincial assemblies
  - **4 members** from Chief Commissioner's provinces
  - **93 members** from princely states
- This was described as *"a good, democratic method not based on weightage."*

**5. Working of the Constituent Assembly:**
- Members from Groups A, B, and C were to sit **separately first** — to decide the constitution for provinces and if possible, for the groups.
- Then, the **whole constituent assembly** (all three sections combined) would sit together to formulate the **union constitution**.

**6. Common Centre:**
- Would control **defence, communication**, and **external affairs**.
- A **federal structure** was envisaged for India.

**7. Communal Questions:**
- In the central legislature, communal questions to be decided by a **simple majority of both communities** present and voting.

**8. Provincial Autonomy:**
- Provinces to have **full autonomy and residual powers**.

**9. Princely States:**
- No longer to be under **paramountcy of the British government**.
- Would be free to enter into an arrangement with **successor governments or the British government**.

**10. Future Flexibility:**
- After the **first general elections**, a province could come out of a group.
- After **10 years**, a province could call for reconsideration of the group or union constitution.

**11. Interim Government:**
- Meanwhile, an **interim government** was to be formed from the constituent assembly.

---

### The Critical Ambiguity — Different Interpretations of the Grouping Clause

The fatal flaw in the plan was that Congress and the League interpreted the key "grouping" clause **completely differently**:

| Party | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **Congress** | Grouping was **optional** — one constituent assembly was envisaged; the League no longer had a veto |
| **Muslim League** | **Pakistan was implied in compulsory grouping** — sections B and C would develop into solid entities with a view to future secession |

The Mission later clarified that the grouping was **compulsory** — deciding the matter in the League's favour.

---

### Objections of Different Parties

**Congress Objections:**
- Provinces should not have to wait till the first general elections to come out of a group — they should have the **option of not joining** a group in the first place (Congress had Congress-ruled **NWFP and Assam** in mind, which had been included in Groups B and C respectively).
- **Compulsory grouping contradicts provincial autonomy**.
- Absence of **elected members from princely states** in the constituent assembly (only nominated by princes) was not acceptable.

**League Objections:**
- Grouping should be **compulsory** with sections B and C developing into solid entities with a view to **future secession into Pakistan**.
- The League had thought Congress would reject the plan — prompting the government to invite the **League to form the interim government alone**.

---

### Acceptance, Rejection, and the Collapse of the Plan

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **June 6, 1946** | **Muslim League accepted** the long-term Cabinet Mission Plan |
| **June 24, 1946** | **Congress accepted** the long-term Cabinet Mission Plan |
| **July 1946** | Elections held in provincial assemblies for the Constituent Assembly |
| **July 10, 1946** | **Nehru stated**: *"We are not bound by a single thing except that we have decided to go into the Constituent Assembly... The big probability is that there would be no grouping as NWFP and Assam would have objections to joining sections B and C."* |
| **July 29, 1946** | The League **withdrew its acceptance** in response to Nehru's statement and gave a call for **"direct action" from August 16** to achieve Pakistan |

---

### Wavell's 'Breakdown Plan'

Wavell also presented his own plan to the Cabinet Mission in May 1946. It envisioned a middle course between **"repression"** and **"scuttle"** (hasty departure):

- Withdrawal of the **British Army and officials to the Muslim provinces** of North-West and North-East.
- Handing over the rest of the country to the Congress.

Though superseded by the Cabinet Mission Plan, Wavell's plan was evidence of:
- British recognition of the **impossibility of suppressing any future Congress-led rebellion**.
- Desire in some high official circles to make a **"Northern Ireland" of Pakistan** — a divided, conflict-ridden settlement.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Cabinet Mission Plan

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The Three-Section Grouping — A Template for Federal Solutions
- The Cabinet Mission's three-section grouping attempted to keep India united while accommodating regional/religious identities through a federal structure.
- `[Modern Example]` The **idea of asymmetric federalism** (giving different regions different levels of autonomy) remains relevant today. The **abrogation of Article 370 in August 2019** (which had given Jammu & Kashmir special autonomy) and the debates around **linguistic reorganisation of states** are modern echoes of the same fundamental question the Cabinet Mission tried to answer: *How much autonomy is enough to keep a diverse country together without breaking it apart?*

### 2. The "Grouping Clause" Ambiguity — A Lesson in Constitutional Drafting
- The Cabinet Mission's failure because of an ambiguously worded "grouping" clause is a powerful lesson in constitutional drafting.
- `[Modern Example]` India's own Constitution has faced similar interpretive disputes. The **Supreme Court's multiple judgments on the interpretation of Article 370** (especially the 2023 judgment upholding its abrogation in *Dr. Shah Faesal vs. Union of India*) demonstrate how **ambiguous constitutional language** can have enormous political consequences — a lesson first learned with the Cabinet Mission Plan's fatal grouping clause.

## 🔥 Part 7: Communal Holocaust and the Interim Government

### Direct Action and Communal Riots

From **August 16, 1946**, the Indian scene was rapidly transformed by communal riots on an **unprecedented scale** — leaving several thousands dead.

**Worst-hit areas:** Calcutta, Bombay, Noakhali, Bihar, and **Garhmukteshwar (United Provinces)**.

---

### Changed Government Priorities

Viceroy Wavell was now eager to somehow get the Congress into the Interim Government — even if the League stayed out. This was a departure from Wavell's stance at the Shimla Conference.

This attitude directly contradicted:
- The League's insistence that all settlements be acceptable to it.
- Earlier government postures of encouraging communal forces.
- Earlier denial of the legitimacy of nationalism and the representative nature of Congress.

*Continuance of British rule had demanded one stance; the prospect of withdrawal and post-imperial links dictated a contrary posture.*

---

### The Interim Government (September 2, 1946)

Fearing mass action by the Congress, a **Congress-dominated Interim Government headed by Nehru** was sworn in on **September 2, 1946** — with Nehru continuing to insist on his party's opposition to compulsory grouping.

Despite the title, the Interim Government was little more than a **continuation of the old executive of the Viceroy** — Wavell overruled the ministers on the issue of release of INA prisoners in his **very last cabinet meeting in March 1947**.

**October 26, 1946:** Wavell **quietly brought the Muslim League into the Interim Government** — allowing it to join:
- Without giving up **'direct action'**.
- Despite its **rejection** of the Cabinet Mission's long-term AND short-term plans.
- Despite insistence on compulsory grouping (which would reduce opponents of Pakistan in Assam and NWFP to a **helpless minority**).

### The League's Obstructionist Approach

The League did not attend the **Constituent Assembly** — which had its first meeting on **December 9, 1946**.

As a result, the Assembly had to confine itself to passing a general **'Objectives Resolution'** — drafted by **Jawaharlal Nehru** — stating the ideals of an independent sovereign republic with autonomous units, adequate minority safeguards, and social, political, and economic democracy.

**The League's Obstructionist Tactics:**
- ❌ Refused to attend **informal meetings** of the cabinet.
- ❌ **Questioned decisions and appointments** made by Congress members.
- ❌ **Liaqat Ali Khan** as Finance Minister restricted and encumbered the efficient functioning of **other ministries**.
- ❌ For the League, the Interim Government was *"a continuation of the civil war by other means"*.

**The Growing Crisis:**
- The Congress demanded that the British get the League to change its attitude or quit.
- **February 1947:** Nine Congress cabinet members wrote to the Viceroy demanding the **resignation of League members** and threatening their own withdrawal.
- The last straw: the League demanded the **dissolution of the Constituent Assembly**.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Communal Violence and Coalition Politics

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The Objectives Resolution — Foundation of India's Preamble
- The **Objectives Resolution** passed by the Constituent Assembly on **December 22, 1946** (moved by Nehru) became the philosophical foundation of the **Preamble to the Constitution**.
- `[Modern Example]` The Supreme Court in **Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973)** held that the Preamble (which embodies the Objectives Resolution's key ideas — sovereign, democratic, republic, social justice, liberty, equality) is part of the Constitution's Basic Structure and cannot be amended to alter its fundamental character. This was reaffirmed in 2024 challenges to the Preamble's "secular" and "socialist" words.

### 2. Communal Violence — An Unresolved Legacy
- The communal holocaust of 1946–47 killed tens of thousands and displaced millions.
- `[Modern Example]` India continues to grapple with communal violence. The **National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) 2022 report** recorded communal/religious riots across multiple states. The **Supreme Court's ongoing monitoring of the Manipur ethnic violence (2023)** and its intervention in communal cases demonstrates that the state still struggles with the problem of communal violence that the partition era unleashed.

## 📚 Part 8: Birth and Spread of Communalism in India

This section provides a comprehensive understanding of communalism as a **political phenomenon** — essential for any serious student of Indian history.

### What Is Communalism?

**Communalism** (more accurately called **'sectarianism'**) is basically an **ideology** that gives more importance to one's own ethnic/religious group rather than to the wider society as a whole.

It evolved through **three broad stages** in India:

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **(i) Communal Nationalism** | The notion that since a group belongs to a particular religious community, their **secular interests are the same** — even matters unrelated to religion affect them equally |
| **(ii) Liberal Communalism** | The notion that since two religious communities have different religious interests, they also have **different interests in the secular sphere** (economic, political, cultural) |
| **(iii) Extreme Communalism** | The notion that different communities not only have different interests but that these interests are **incompatible** — they cannot co-exist because one community's interests conflict with the other's |

---

### Key Characteristics of Indian Communalism

- **Not unique to India** — it is the result of conditions that have produced similar phenomena in other societies: Fascism, anti-Semitism, racism, Catholic-Protestant conflict in Northern Ireland, Christian-Muslim conflict in Lebanon.
- **A MODERN phenomenon** — rooted in modern social, economic, and political colonial structures. It emerged out of modern politics based on **mass mobilisation and popular participation**.
- Modern politics made it necessary for people to establish wider identities — involving spread of ideas of **nation, class, and cultural-linguistic identity**.
- In India, **religious consciousness was transformed into communal consciousness** in some parts and among some sections.
- **Social roots:** Rising middle classes propagated imaginary communal interests to further their own economic interests — *"communalism was a bourgeois question par excellence"* (Left critique).
- **Colonial support:** Communalists were backed by the colonial administration — it was the channel through which colonialists expanded their social base.
- **Socio-economic coincidence:** The sinister motives of communalists and colonialists were helped by the fact that **socio-economic distinctions in Indian society often coincided with religious distinctions** — giving inherent class contradictions a post-facto communal colouring.
- **Conservative support:** Conservative, socially reactionary elements gave full support to communalism.
- **Religion vs. Communalism:** Religiosity itself did not amount to communalism — but in a country where lack of education and low awareness of the outside world was a sad reality, religion had the potential to become a vehicle of communalism.

---

### Socio-Economic Reasons for Communalism's Growth

**The Key Insight:** Religion did NOT actually dictate the economic and political interests of Hindus and Muslims.

> *"A Bengali Muslim has much more in common with a Bengali Hindu than with a Punjabi Muslim."*

**Economic Competition and Colonial Exploitation:**
- Modern Western thought and scientific ideas were **not absorbed by Muslim intellectuals** — they remained traditional and backward.
- Even when modern education spread among Muslims (through reformers' efforts), the **proportion of educated was far lower** among Muslims than among Hindus, Parsis, or Christians.
- Muslims also **lagged behind in trade and industry**.
- Since the number of educated Muslims in business/professions was small, it was easy for **reactionary big landlords and the richer classes** to continue wielding influence over the Muslim masses.
- Among Hindus, **modern intellectuals and the rising commercial/industrialist class** had taken over leadership from the old landlord order — this did NOT happen among Muslims.
- Educated Muslims found few opportunities in business or professions — they **inevitably looked for government employment**.
- **British officials and loyalist Muslim leaders incited educated Muslims against educated Hindus**.
- **Syed Ahmad Khan** demanded special treatment for Muslims in government service and told Muslims that **loyalty to the British would be rewarded** with government jobs and special favours.
- Similar arguments were used by some loyalist Hindus and Parsis — but they were in a small minority.
- **Acute unemployment** (especially among the educated) and **intense competition for existing jobs** created a fertile ground for advocates of short-sighted solutions — **reservation of jobs on communal, provincial, or caste lines**.
- Economic backwardness and unemployment gave the colonial government **ample scope to use concessions, favours, and reservations to fuel communal and separatist tendencies**.
- Modern political consciousness **developed late among Muslims** — dominance of traditional reactionary elements over the Muslim masses helped a communal outlook take root.

> *"There has been a difference of a generation or more in the development of the Hindu and the Muslim middle classes... It is this lag which produces a psychology of fear among the Muslims."* — **Jawaharlal Nehru, The Discovery of India**

> *"He who does what is beneficial to the people of this country, be he a Muhammedan or an Englishman, is not alien. 'Alienness' has to do with interests."* — **Bal Gangadhar Tilak**

---

### British Policy of Divide and Rule

**Phase 1 (Before 1870s): Repression of Muslims**
- Muslims were looked upon with suspicion — especially after the **Wahabi and 1857 revolts**.
- **Introduction of English education** undermined Arabic and Persian learning — adding to economic backwardness and exclusion of Muslims from service.

**Phase 2 (After 1870s): Conciliation and Weaponisation of Muslims**
- With signs of **emerging Indian nationalism** and growing politicisation of the educated middle classes, the government reversed its policy.
- Decided to **rally Muslims behind the government** through concessions, favours, and reservations — and use them against nationalist forces.
- Used persons like **Sir Syed Ahmed Khan** to counter the growing influence of the Congress.
- Syed Ahmed Khan had a broad-minded and reformist outlook initially — but later **supported the colonial government**, exhorting Muslims to stay away from the Congress, talking of **separate interests of Hindus and Muslims**.

---

### Communalism in History Writing

**The Communal Interpretation of Indian History:**
- Initially suggested by **imperialist historians**, later adopted by some chauvinist Indian historians.
- **Ancient phase** portrayed as the **Hindu phase**.
- **Medieval phase** (Turks, Afghans, Mughals) portrayed as the **Muslim phase**.
- Conflicts of ruling classes in the medieval period **distorted and exaggerated as Hindu-Muslim conflicts**.
- Historians ignored the fact that **politics was based on economic and political interests, not religious considerations**.
- Both British and communal historians refused to acknowledge the notion of a **composite culture** in India.
- Hindu communal history: Indian society had reached **ideal heights in the ancient period** and decayed in the medieval period due to **'Muslim' rule** — refusing to acknowledge how Indian economy, technology, religion, philosophy, arts, literature, and culture had **developed and been enriched in the medieval period**.

> *"Communal harmony could not be permanently established in our country so long as highly distorted versions of history were taught in her schools and colleges, through the history textbooks."* — **M.K. Gandhi**

---

### Side-Effects of Socio-Religious Reform Movements

- Reform movements like the **Wahabi Movement** among Muslims and **Shuddhi** among Hindus — with their **militant overtones** — made religion more vulnerable to communalism.
- Reforms were sometimes seen as a process of **insulating one community from the influence of another**.

---

### Side-Effects of Militant Nationalism

- Early nationalists made conscious efforts to remove minority fears:
  - **Dadabhai Naoroji** (presiding over the second Congress session, 1886) declared the Congress would not raise socio-religious questions.
  - In **1889**, Congress decided not to take up any issue opposed by Muslims.
- But with the coming of **militant nationalism**, a distinct **Hindu nationalist tinge** became palpable:
  - **Tilak's Ganapati and Shivaji festivals** and anti-cow slaughter campaigns created much suspicion.
  - **Aurobindo's vision of an Aryanised world**.
  - Swadeshi Movement elements like **dips in the Ganga** and revolutionary activity with **oath-taking before goddesses**.
  - These were hardly likely to enthuse Muslims.
- The **Lucknow Pact (1916)** and the **Khilafat agitation (1920–22)** had a visible communal element.
- The Muslims' sympathy for Turkey was on **religious grounds** — even the heroes, myths, and cultural traditions they appealed to belonged not to the history of India but to the **history of West Asia**.
- Though this tendency made its supporters **anti-imperialist** in the short run, in the longer term it **encouraged the habit of looking at political questions from a religious point of view**.

---

### Communal Reaction by the Majority Community

- From the **1870s**, some Hindu zamindars, moneylenders, and middle-class professionals began giving expression to **anti-Muslim sentiments**.
- Some even declared the British had **"liberated the land from Muslim tyranny."**
- The **cause of Hindi was given a communal colour** by saying Urdu was the language of Muslims (which was not historically correct).
- **Punjab Hindu Sabha**, founded in **1909** by **U.N. Mukherjee** and **Lal Chand**, opposed the Congress for trying to unite Indians — argued Hindus should **side with the colonial government** against Muslims.
- **All-India Hindu Mahasabha** held its first session in **April 1915** with the **Maharaja of Kasim Bazar** as president.
- **Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS)** was established in **1925**.
- However, **Hindu communalism was not a strong force for a long time** — the modern secular intelligentsia and middle class among Hindus wielded greater influence.
- This was NOT the case with Muslims — Muslim communal elements (landlords, traditional religious leaders, bureaucrats) exercised a lot of influence.

## 📅 Part 9: Evolution of the Two-Nation Theory — A Timeline

| Year | Development |
|---|---|
| **1887** | Frontal attack on Congress by **Dufferin** (Viceroy) and **Colvin** (Lt. Governor UP). **Syed Ahmed Khan** and **Raja Shiv Prasad of Bhinga** propped up as anti-Congress front. Some Muslims joined Congress: **Badruddin Tyabji, Mir Musharraf Hussain, A. Bhimji**, and **Hamid Ali Khan** |
| **1906** | **Agha Khan led the Shimla delegation** to Viceroy **Lord Minto** — demanded separate electorates and Muslim representation commensurate with "political importance and contribution to the British Empire". Minto assured **special communal representation in excess of their population** for "extraordinary service." **All India Muslim League** founded by **Agha Khan, Nawab Salimullah of Dacca, Nawab Mohsin-ul-Mulk**, and **Nawab Waqar-ul-Mulk** — to preach loyalty to the British and keep Muslim intelligentsia away from the Congress |
| **1909** | Separate electorates awarded under **Morley-Minto Reforms**. **Punjab Hindu Sabha** founded by **U.N. Mukherji** and **Lal Chand** |
| **1915** | First session of **All India Hindu Mahasabha** under Maharaja of Kasim Bazar |
| **1912–24** | Muslim League dominated by younger Muslim nationalists — nationalism inspired by a communal view of political questions |
| **1916** | Congress accepted Muslim League demand of separate electorates — joint demands to government. Congress gave **political legitimacy** to the Muslim League's separate existence. Four mistakes: (1) Gave legitimacy to division of society into separate communities; (2) Undermined role of secular, nationalist Muslims; (3) Concessions to one community prompted other communities to demand similar; (4) All-out attack on communalism became difficult |
| **1920–22** | Muslims participated in Rowlatt and Khilafat-Non-Cooperation agitations — but communal element was present in political outlook |
| **1920s** | Shadow of communal riots. Arya Samajists started **Shuddhi and Sangathan** movements. Muslims started **Tabligh and Tanzeem** movements in retaliation. Swarajists divided along communal lines — many Responsivists joined Hindu Mahasabha. Ali brothers accused Congress of protecting only Hindu interests |
| **1928** | Nehru Report opposed by Muslim hardliners and Sikh League. **Jinnah's 14 Points** demanded separate electorates and reservation for Muslims in government service and self-governing bodies |
| **1930–34** | Some Muslim groups (Jamaat-ul-Ulema-i-Hind, Khudai Khidmatgars) participated in Civil Disobedience Movement — but overall Muslim participation far below Khilafat level. While Congress stayed away from two of three RTCs, communalists attended all three |
| **1932** | **Communal Award** accepted all Muslim communal demands from the 14 Points |
| **After 1937** | After poor performance in 1937 elections, Muslim League adopted **extreme communalism**. Began projecting Muslims not as a minority but as a **separate nation** (idea first proposed by **Rahmat Ali** in early 1930s, later developed by **Iqbal**). Communalism organised as a mass movement among middle and upper classes. Vicious propaganda by **Z.A. Suleri, F.M. Durrani, Fazl-ul-Haq**, etc. |
| **1937–39** | Jinnah blocked all avenues for conciliation — demanded Congress declare itself a **Hindu organisation** and recognise the League as the **sole representative** of Indian Muslims |
| **March 24, 1940** | **'Pakistan Resolution'** passed at Lahore session of Muslim League |
| **WWII Period** | British India Government gave the League a **virtual veto** on political settlement. League made full use of this throughout August Offer, Cripps' proposals, Shimla Conference, and Cabinet Mission Plan |
| **1947** | League finally got **independent Pakistan** — comprising Muslim majority areas of Punjab, Sindh, Baluchistan, NWFP, and Bengal |

---

### Why Extreme Communalism Emerged After 1937

**Three reasons:**
1. With increasing radicalisation, **reactionary elements searched for a social base** through channels of communalism.
2. The colonial administration had **exhausted all other means** to divide nationalists.
3. Earlier failures to challenge communal tendencies had **emboldened communal forces**.

> *"The question of majority and minority community is a creation of the British Government and would disappear with their withdrawal. We divide and they rule."* — **M.K. Gandhi and Maulana Mohammad Ali**

> *"The independent sovereign nation of Pakistan was born in the Muslim University of Aligarh."* — **Agha Khan**

## 🌍 Modern Context — Communalism

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. Communal Violence — Legal Framework
- `[Modern Example]` India still lacks a **Communal Violence (Prevention, Control and Rehabilitation of Victims) Bill** — which has been proposed since 2005 but never passed. The absence of such a law means communal violence is still handled under colonial-era provisions, a direct legacy of the failure to address communalism during the freedom movement.
- The **Supreme Court in 2022** (in multiple cases related to Manipur, Karnataka, and UP communal incidents) reaffirmed that the State has a **constitutional duty to prevent communal violence** under Article 21 — but the structural absence of a dedicated law reflects the unfinished business of partition-era communal politics.

### 2. Communal History Writing — Still a Battleground
- `[Modern Example]` The **National Council of Educational Research and Training (NCERT) textbook revision controversy (2023)** — where sections on the Mughal period, the emergency, and the RSS/Gandhi assassination connection were deleted from Class 12 History textbooks — directly mirrors the critique in this chapter about **communal interpretation of Indian history**. Gandhi's warning that *"communal harmony could not be established so long as highly distorted versions of history were taught"* remains profoundly relevant.

### 3. Separate Electorates — Abolished but the Debate Continues
- Separate electorates (the root cause of the League's political strategy) were abolished by the Indian Constitution.
- `[Modern Example]` However, the debate about **political representation for minorities** continues. The **Supreme Court in 2024** addressed the question of reservation for Other Backward Classes within Muslim communities (the **Bihar OBC reservation case**), reflecting the same unresolved question about how to provide adequate representation to marginalised religious and social groups without creating the divisive separate electorate system that helped cause Partition.
