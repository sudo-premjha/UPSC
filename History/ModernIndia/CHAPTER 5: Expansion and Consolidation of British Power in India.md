### *Unit 2 — Advent of Europeans and Consolidation of British Power in India*

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 big ideas of this chapter.**

1. **The British conquest of India was neither purely accidental nor purely planned** — it began as trade protection but evolved into deliberate empire-building, especially after 1798 under Lord Wellesley.
2. **Three military campaigns decided India's fate** — the Battle of Plassey (1757), the Battle of Buxar (1764), and the Battle of Wandiwash (1760) collectively eliminated all major rivals.
3. **Three administrative tools completed the conquest** — Warren Hastings' Ring-Fence Policy, Wellesley's Subsidiary Alliance System, and Dalhousie's Doctrine of Lapse systematically absorbed Indian states without always firing a shot.
4. **The Marathas, Mysore, Sindh, and Punjab were the last major resistances** — each fell for a combination of internal divisions, inferior diplomacy, and British military superiority.
5. **British India's conflicts with neighbours (Nepal, Burma, Afghanistan, Tibet)** were driven by imperial paranoia about Russian expansion and the desire to create "buffer states" and "scientific frontiers."

# 🌐 PART 1: THE BIG PICTURE — BRITISH IMPERIAL HISTORY

---

## 1.1 Two Phases of the British Empire

The entire imperial history of Britain can be divided into **two phases**:

- **The 'First Empire':** Stretched across the **Atlantic towards America and the West Indies**.
- **The 'Second Empire':** Beginning around **1783 (Peace of Paris)**, swinging towards the **East — Asia and Africa**.

The imperial history of Britain actually started with the **conquest of Ireland in the 16th century**. The English then presented themselves as the **"new Romans"** — supposedly "civilising" backward races throughout the world.

To justify this, **post-Enlightenment intellectuals** (thinkers of the era after the 18th-century European intellectual revolution) of Britain — and Europe generally — began certifying themselves as **"civilised" in contrast to "Oriental peoples"** and others. While the **nature of British imperial ideology changed** over time due to various forces, its **fundamentals remained the same**.

---

## 1.2 Was the British Conquest of India Accidental or Intentional?

This is one of the most debated questions in Indian history. There are two opposing schools of thought:

### School 1: The "Accidental" View
- Led by **John Seeley**
- Argues the British conquest was made **"blindly, unintentionally, and accidentally"** — in a **"fit of absent-mindedness"**
- The British came to **trade**, had no desire for territory, and were **unwillingly drawn into Indian political turmoil** created by Indians themselves

> 💬 *John Seeley: "Our acquisition of India was made blindly. Nothing great that has ever been done by Englishmen was done so unintentionally and so accidentally, as the conquest of India."*

### School 2: The "Intentional" View
- Argues the British came with the **clear intention of establishing a large and powerful empire**, working on the plan bit by bit
- Dismisses the "peaceful intent" claim of the early East India Company as **propaganda**

### The Balanced View (Most Accurate)
Both schools **overstate their case**. The reality is more nuanced:

- **Initially:** Company officials began acquiring territory simply to **promote and protect their trade interests**, especially seeing how factionalised Indian politics was. They realised they could **pit one ruler against another** and slowly started acquiring territories.
- **Later:** British politicians and administrators **worked on a clear, deliberate plan** to acquire territories and establish an empire.

> **Key factors that drove expansion:**
> - Desire for **quick profits**
> - **Personal ambitions** of individuals
> - Plain **avarice** (greed)
> - **Political developments in Europe** (especially the threat of France and Russia)
> - Wars to protect **commercial interests** and to protect **Indian allies** from rivals

> 💬 *B.L. Grover writes: "Lord Wellesley resorted to aggressive application of the subsidiary alliance system to extend British dominion in India as a defensive counter measure against the imperialistic designs of France and Russia. From 1798 to 1818 the British motives were consciously imperialistic."*

---

## 1.3 When Did the British Period Begin in India?

Historians disagree on the exact starting point:

| Year | Event | Who Considers It the Start |
|---|---|---|
| **1740** | Anglo-French struggle begins (Austrian War of Succession) | Some historians |
| **1757** | Battle of Plassey — British defeat Nawab of Bengal | Most historians |
| **1761** | Third Battle of Panipat — Marathas defeated | Some historians |

> 🔑 **Key Point:** All these dates are somewhat **arbitrary**. The political transformation that began around this time **took about eighty years to complete**. For instance, even in 1761, one cannot "write off" the Marathas or ignore the rising power of Haidar Ali. It was a period where multiple powers were still in play.

# ⚔️ PART 2: CAUSES OF BRITISH SUCCESS IN INDIA

---

The entire process of British expansion and consolidation **took almost a century**. Both war and administrative policies were used. The British were **not averse to using unscrupulous tactics** to exploit situations or regional rulers.

---

### (i) Superior Arms, Military, and Strategy
- English **firearms — muskets and cannons** — were superior to Indian weapons in both **speed of firing and range**.
- Many Indian rulers imported European arms and hired European officers — but **Indian military officers became mere imitators**, lacking original military thinking.

### (ii) Better Military Discipline and Regular Salary
- The English Company maintained **strict discipline** and paid **regular salaries** — ensuring loyalty from officers and troops.
- Most **Indian rulers lacked the funds** to pay salaries regularly.
- The Marathas sometimes had to **divert military campaigns just to collect revenue** to pay their troops.
- Indian rulers relied on **personal retinues (private bodyguards/attendants)** or **mercenary elements (hired fighters with no loyalty)** — who were **undisciplined and could turn rebellious or defect** when conditions worsened.

### (iii) Civil Discipline and Fair Selection System
- Company officers and troops were selected based on **reliability and skill** — not hereditary or caste/clan ties.
- They understood the **objectives of their campaigns** and maintained discipline.
- In contrast, **Indian administrators and military officers were appointed based on caste and personal relations** — often ignoring merit.
- This made their competence doubtful and they frequently pursued **their own interests over their ruler's**.

### (iv) Brilliant Leadership and Support of Second-Line Leaders
- **Top English leaders:** Clive, Warren Hastings, Elphinstone, Munro, Marquess of Dalhousie — all displayed **rare qualities of leadership**.
- **Secondary English leaders:** Sir Eyre Coote, Lord Lake, Arthur Wellesley — fought **for the cause and glory of their country**, not just for their personal leader.
- **India had brilliant leaders too:** Haidar Ali, Tipu Sultan, Chin Qilich Khan, Madhu Rao Scindia, Yashwant Rao Holkar.
- But Indian leaders **lacked a trained second-line team**. More critically, Indian leaders were **fighting against each other as much as against the British** — they lacked the spirit of a united cause.
- The **consciousness of 'India' as a nation was lacking**.

### (v) Strong Financial Backup
- The Company's income was **adequate to pay shareholders handsome dividends** AND finance wars in India.
- England was earning **fabulous profits from trade** with the rest of the world.
- These vast resources — money, materials, and men — were available to the British in times of need, thanks to their **superiority in sea power**.

### (vi) Nationalist Pride
- An economically thriving, materially ambitious British people with **strong national pride** faced "weak, divided-among-themselves Indians" who lacked **unified political nationalism**.
- The **lack of materialistic vision among Indians** was also a reason for the English Company's success.

# 🏰 PART 3: BRITISH CONQUEST OF BENGAL

---

## 3.1 Bengal on the Eve of British Conquest

- **Bengal** — the richest province of the Mughal Empire — included **present-day Bangladesh**, and its Nawab had authority over **present-day Bihar and Odisha**.
- **Exports from Bengal to Europe:** Saltpetre, rice, indigo, pepper, sugar, silk, cotton textiles, handicrafts, etc.
- The English East India Company had vital commercial interests in Bengal — **nearly 60 per cent of British imports from Asia consisted of goods from Bengal**.
- During the **1630s**, the British established factories at **Balasore, Hooghly, Kasimbazar, Patna, and Dacca**.
- By the **1690s**, the foundation of **Calcutta** completed the process of English commercial settlement in Bengal.

### The Financial Irony:
- The Company paid **Rs 3,000 (£350) per annum** to the Mughal emperor for the right to trade freely in Bengal.
- In contrast, the Company's **exports from Bengal were worth more than £50,000 per annum**.
- This enormous profit margin explains why Bengal was the prize the British fought for.

### The Nawabs of Bengal:
- **1700:** **Murshid Quli Khan** became the Dewan of Bengal, ruled till his death in **1727**.
- Succeeded by his son-in-law **Shujauddin**, who ruled till **1739**.
- **1739–40:** **Sarfaraz Khan** (an incapable son of Murshid Quli Khan) ruled for a year before being **killed by Alivardi Khan**.
- **Alivardi Khan** ruled till **1756** and even **stopped paying tributes to the Mughal emperor**.
- Under these rulers, **Bengal made unprecedented progress** — partly because Bengal escaped the Maratha invasions, Jat revolts, and external invasions (by Nadir Shah and Ahmad Shah Abdali) that devastated the rest of India.
- **Population of Calcutta** grew from **15,000 (in 1706) to 100,000 (in 1750)**. Cities like **Dacca and Murshidabad** also became highly populous.

### Root of Conflict:
- Almost all Bengal governors **strongly resented the special privileges** enjoyed by the English Company — as it meant a **huge loss to the provincial exchequer**.
- The **friction between English commercial interests and the Bengal government** became the chief cause of conflict.

---

## 3.2 Alivardi Khan and the English

- In **1741**, **Alivardi Khan** (the Deputy Governor of Bihar) **killed Nawab Sarfaraz Khan** in battle and confirmed his own position as the new **Subahdar of Bengal** by paying a large sum to **Mughal Emperor Muhammad Shah**.
- He ruled for **15 years**, during which he fought off the **Marathas**.
- The English took advantage of Maratha incursions into Bengal, obtaining **permission from the Nawab to dig a ditch and build an entrenchment around Fort William**.
- Later, Alivardi Khan became concerned about the **Carnatic region**, where European companies had usurped all power. He was urged to expel Europeans from Bengal.
- But he **died in April 1756** and was succeeded by his grandson, **Siraj-ud-Daulah** (the son of Alivardi's youngest daughter).

---

## 3.3 Challenges Before Siraj-ud-Daulah

Siraj-ud-Daulah inherited the throne as a **youth just in his twentieth year** — and inherited a mountain of troubles:

### Internal Rivals:
- **Shaukat Jang** — his cousin, the Nawab of Purnea
- **Ghasiti Begum** — his hostile aunt, a childless widow
- **Mir Jafar** — a rebellious commander of the army, husband of Alivardi Khan's sister
- A **dominant court group** comprising **Jagat Seth, Omichand, Rai Ballabh, Rai Durlabh**, and others — all opposed to him
- An **alarmed Hindu subject population**

### External Threat:
- The ever-growing **commercial activity of the English Company** threatened Siraj's political position.

### His Response:
- **Impulsive by nature and lacking experience**, Siraj acted in ways that proved counter-productive.
- He defeated and **killed Shaukat Jang** in battle.
- **Divested Ghasiti Begum of her treasures** and secured her.
- **Dismissed Mir Jafar** and appointed **Mir Madan** in his place.
- Appointed **Mohan Lal** (a Kashmiri officer) as the overall administrator — who acted almost like a prime minister.

---

## 3.4 The Battle of Plassey (June 23, 1757)

### Prelude to the Battle
A series of provocations preceded the battle:

- **Company officials made rampant misuse of trade privileges** — adversely affecting the Nawab's finances.
- The **English fortified Calcutta without the Nawab's permission**.
- The Company tried to mislead the Nawab and gave **asylum to a political fugitive, Krishna Das** (son of Raj Ballabh, who had fled with immense treasures against the Nawab's will).
- The Company suspected Siraj would **drastically reduce its trade privileges** in collusion with the French.
- When Siraj **attacked and seized the English fort at Calcutta**, it brought the hostility into the open.

### The 'Black Hole Tragedy' — A Note
- **Siraj-ud-Daulah is believed to have imprisoned 146 English persons** in a very tiny room, causing **123 of them to die of suffocation**.
- However, **historians either do not believe this story**, or say **the number of victims must have been much smaller**.

### The Battle and Its Secret Alliance
- A strong force under **Robert Clive** arrived from Madras and strengthened the English position.
- Clive forged a **secret alliance with the Nawab's own traitors:**
  - **Mir Jafar** (to be made Nawab as reward)
  - **Rai Durlabh**
  - **Jagat Seth** (an influential banker of Bengal)
  - **Omichand**
- Under the deal: **Mir Jafar would become Nawab and reward the Company for its services**.
- The **English victory at Plassey on June 23, 1757 was decided before the battle was even fought**.
- Due to the conspiracy, Siraj's **50,000-strong force was defeated by a handful of Clive's forces**.
- **Siraj-ud-Daulah was captured and murdered** by order of **Mir Jafar's son, Miran**.

### Significance of the Battle of Plassey

- **Mir Jafar became the Nawab of Bengal** — he gave the English large sums of money plus the **zamindari of 24 parganas**.
- **Political significance:** It **laid the foundation of the British Empire in India** — rightly regarded as the **starting point of British rule in India**.
- The English established **military supremacy in Bengal**. Their main rivals, the **French, were ousted**.
- They obtained a grant of territories for maintaining a properly equipped military force.
- **Sovereignty of the English over Calcutta was recognised** and the English posted a **resident at the Nawab's court**.
- However, there was **no apparent change in the form of government** — the supreme control now lay with Clive, on whose support the new Nawab Mir Jafar was entirely dependent.

---

## 3.5 Mir Kasim and the Treaty of 1760

- **Mir Jafar** was increasingly irritated by Clive's interference.
- He entered into a **conspiracy with the Dutch at Chinsura** — but the Dutch were **defeated and humbled by the English at Bedara in November 1759**.
- His failure to make payments due to the Company further annoyed the English.
- After Miran's death, a fight broke out between **Mir Kasim** (Mir Jafar's son-in-law) and Miran's son for the nawabship.
- **Vansittart** (the new Governor of Calcutta) agreed to support Mir Kasim's claim.

### Treaty of 1760 — Key Provisions:
- *(i)* Mir Kasim agreed to **cede the districts of Burdwan, Midnapur, and Chittagong** to the Company.
- *(ii)* The Company would get **half the share in chunam trade of Sylhet**.
- *(iii)* Mir Kasim agreed to **pay off all outstanding dues** to the Company.
- *(iv)* Mir Kasim promised to pay **five lakh rupees** towards financing the Company's war efforts in **southern India**.
- *(v)* It was agreed that **Mir Kasim's enemies were the Company's enemies**, and his friends, the Company's friends.
- *(vi)* Tenants of the Nawab's territory would **not be allowed to settle in Company lands**, and vice-versa.
- Under Company pressure, **Mir Jafar resigned** in favour of Mir Kasim with a **pension of Rs 1,500 per annum**.

### Mir Kasim's Steps as Nawab:
- Mir Kasim was the **ablest nawab** among the successors of Alivardi Khan.
- Shifted the capital from **Murshidabad to Munger in Bihar** — to create a safe distance from the Company at Calcutta.
- **Reorganised the bureaucracy** with men of his own choice.
- **Remodelled the army** to enhance its skill and efficiency.

---

## 3.6 The Battle of Buxar (October 22, 1764)

### Prelude to the Battle
The Company expected Mir Kasim to be a puppet — he was anything but:

- **Ram Narayan** (the deputy-governor of Bihar) refused to submit revenue accounts to the Nawab — and was backed by English officials in Patna.
- The **misuse of the dastak (trade permit — a permit exempting specified goods from duties)** by Company officials caused huge tensions:
  - It meant **loss of tax revenue** for the Nawab.
  - It made **local merchants face unfair competition** with Company merchants.
- The Company claimed **duty-free trade privileges** under an imperial farman — but then **Company servants also claimed the same for their private trade**, and even **sold dastaks to Indian merchants for a commission**.
- Company servants used **coercive methods to buy goods cheaply** — violating the spirit of duty-free trade.
- When **Mir Kasim abolished duties altogether** to level the playing field, the British protested and insisted on **preferential treatment** over other traders.
- This led to the **outbreak of wars between the English and Mir Kasim in 1763**.
- The English won successive victories at **Katwah, Murshidabad, Giria, Sooty, and Munger**.
- Mir Kasim fled to **Awadh** and formed a **confederacy with the Nawab of Awadh Shuja-ud-Daulah and Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II** to recover Bengal from the English.

### The Battle
- The combined armies of **Mir Kasim, Nawab of Awadh, and Shah Alam II** were defeated by English forces under **Major Hector Munro at Buxar on October 22, 1764**.
- **Significance:** Not only the Nawab of Bengal but also the **Mughal Emperor himself was defeated by the English** — making the English a great power in northern India and **contenders for supremacy over the entire country**.

### Aftermath:
- **Mir Jafar** (restored as Nawab in 1763 when Mir Kasim's relations with the Company broke down) agreed to hand over **Midnapore, Burdwan, and Chittagong** to the English for maintenance of their army.
- The English were permitted **duty-free trade in Bengal**, except a **2% duty on salt**.
- After Mir Jafar's death, his minor son **Najim-ud-daula** was appointed Nawab — but the **real power lay with the naib-subahdar, who could be appointed or dismissed by the English**.
- **Mir Kasim** spent the rest of his life as a homeless wanderer and **died in June 1777**.

---

## 3.7 The Treaty of Allahabad (August 1765)

**Robert Clive** concluded two important treaties at Allahabad in **August 1765**:

### With Nawab Shuja-ud-Daula (of Awadh):
- *(i)* Surrender **Allahabad and Kara** to Emperor Shah Alam II.
- *(ii)* Pay **Rs 50 lakh** to the Company as **war indemnity**.
- *(iii)* Give **Balwant Singh, Zamindar of Banaras**, full possession of his estate.

### With Shah Alam II (Mughal Emperor):
- *(i)* Reside at **Allahabad** (ceded by the Nawab of Awadh) under the Company's protection.
- *(ii)* Issue a farman granting the **Diwani** (the right to collect revenue — a crucial Mughal administrative function) **of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa** to the East India Company in lieu of an annual payment of **Rs 26 lakh**.
- *(iii)* Accept a provision of **Rs 53 lakh** to the Company in return for **nizamat functions** (military defence, police, and administration of justice) of the said provinces.

### Why Clive Did Not Annex Awadh:
- Annexing Awadh would have placed the Company under obligation to **protect an extensive land frontier** from Afghan and Maratha invasions.
- Instead, Clive made the Nawab **a firm friend of the Company**, turning Awadh into a **buffer state** (a territory that separates two potentially hostile powers).
- Similarly, the arrangement with Shah Alam II made the emperor a useful **"rubber stamp"** of the Company, while his farman **legalised the Company's political gains** in Bengal.

---

## 3.8 Dual Government in Bengal (1765–1772)

After Buxar, the East India Company became the real masters of Bengal. **Robert Clive introduced the Dual System of Government**:

### How It Worked:
- The Company exercised **diwani rights** (revenue collection) as the **diwan** (revenue minister).
- It exercised **nizamat rights** (police and judicial functions) through its right to **nominate the deputy subahdar**.
- **Diwani functions** were acquired from the emperor; **nizamat functions** from the subahdar of Bengal.

### Deputy Appointments:
- **Mohammad Reza Khan** — appointed deputy diwan for **Bengal** (also acted as deputy nazim/deputy subahdar)
- **Raja Sitab Roy** — appointed deputy diwan for **Bihar**

### The Great Advantage (and Its Flaw):
- **Advantage for the Company:** It left the **appearance of authority with the puppet Indian ruler** while keeping **sovereign power with the Company**.
- **Flaw:** The Nawab was responsible for maintaining peace and order — but he **depended on the Company for funds AND forces** (since the Company controlled the army and revenues).
- **Result:** Neither the Company nor the Nawab cared for administration and public welfare — leading to **administrative breakdown and disaster for the people of Bengal**.
- **Warren Hastings abolished the dual system in 1772**.

---

> The Battle of Plassey was essentially a case of a **corporation (the East India Company) becoming a state** — a phenomenon with disturbing modern parallels. # 🐯 PART 4: MYSORE'S RESISTANCE TO THE COMPANY

---

## 4.1 The Wodeyar/Mysore Dynasty

- After the **Battle of Talikota (1565)** dealt a deadly blow to the **Vijayanagara Empire**, many small kingdoms emerged from its remnants.
- In **1612**, a **Hindu kingdom under the Wodeyars** emerged in the region of Mysore.
- **Chikka Krishnaraja Wodeyar II** ruled from **1734 to 1766**.
- During the second half of the 18th century, **Mysore emerged as a formidable power** under Haidar Ali and Tipu Sultan.

### Why Mysore Threatened the British:
- Mysore's **proximity with the French** alarmed the English.
- **Haidar Ali and Tipu Sultan's control over the rich trade of the Malabar Coast** threatened English political and commercial interests in South India.
- Mysore's power was also seen as a threat to English control over **Madras**.

---

## 4.2 Rise of Haidar Ali

- In the early 18th century, two brothers — **Nanjaraj (the sarvadhikari)** and **Devaraj (the Dulwai)** — had reduced **Chikka Krishnaraja Wodeyar to a mere puppet**.
- **Haidar Ali**, born in **1721** in an obscure family, started his career as a **horseman in the Mysore army** under these two ministers.
- Though **uneducated**, he possessed a **keen intellect and great energy and determination**.

### Why Mysore Needed a Military Leader:
- Repeated **Maratha and Nizam's troop incursions** into Mysore led to **heavy financial demands** by the aggressors.
- Mysore became financially and politically weak — it needed someone with **high military and diplomatic skill**.

### Haidar Ali's Rise to Power:
- Haidar Ali **usurped royal authority** and became the **de facto ruler of Mysore in 1761**.
- He brilliantly analysed his enemies:
  - The **mobile Marathas** could be contained only by a **swift cavalry**.
  - The **Nizami army's French-trained cannons** could be silenced only by **effective artillery**.
  - **Superior Western arms** could only be matched by **arms from the same source or made with the same knowledge**.
- He took help of the **French** to set up an **arms factory at Dindigul** (now in Tamil Nadu).
- Introduced **Western methods of military training**.
- Used **considerable diplomatic skill** to outmanoeuvre opponents.
- **Captured Dod Ballapur, Sera, Bednur, and Hoskote in 1761–63**.
- **Brought to submission the troublesome Poligars of South India** (in present-day Tamil Nadu).

### Haidar Ali vs. The Marathas:
- Recovering from Panipat, the **Marathas under Madhavrao attacked Mysore** — defeating Haidar Ali in **1764, 1766, and 1771**. Haidar had to pay them large sums to buy peace.
- After **Madhavrao's death in 1772**, Haidar raided the Marathas **multiple times during 1774–76**, recovering lost territories and capturing new areas.

---

## 4.3 First Anglo-Mysore War (1767–1769)

### Background:
- After their easy success in Bengal, the English were **confident of their military strength**.
- They concluded a **treaty with the Nizam of Hyderabad (1766)**, giving the English the **Northern Circars** in exchange for protection against Haidar Ali.
- Haidar already had **territorial disputes with the Nawab of Arcot** and **differences with the Marathas**.

### The Shifting Alliances (Haidar's Diplomatic Masterstroke):
- The **Nizam, Marathas, and English allied together against Haidar Ali**.
- Haidar **paid the Marathas to turn them neutral**.
- By **promising to share conquered territories**, he **converted the Nizam into his ally**.
- Then joined the Nizam to attack the Nawab of Arcot.

### Course of War:
- The war continued for **a year-and-a-half without conclusion**.
- Haidar changed strategy and **suddenly appeared before the gates of Madras** — causing **complete chaos and panic**.
- The English were forced to conclude a **very humiliating treaty** with Haidar.

### Treaty of Madras (April 4, 1769):
- Exchange of prisoners
- Mutual restitution (return) of conquests
- **Haidar Ali was promised English help if he was attacked by any other power**

---

## 4.4 Second Anglo-Mysore War (1780–1784)

### Background:
- Haidar accused the English of **breach of faith and non-observance of the Treaty of Madras** — when in **1771, the Marathas attacked him and the English failed to come to his aid** as promised.
- He found the **French more helpful than the English** in providing guns, saltpetre, and lead.
- French war material was being brought to Mysore through **Mahe** (a French possession on the Malabar Coast).
- The **American War of Independence** had broken out — with the French on the rebel side against the English.
- This made **Haidar Ali's French friendship even more alarming** to the English.
- The English tried to **capture Mahe**, which Haidar regarded as under his protection — he considered this **a direct challenge to his authority**.

### Course of War:
- Haidar forged an **anti-English alliance with the Marathas and the Nizam**.
- He attacked the Carnatic, **captured Arcot**, and **defeated the English army under Colonel Baillie in 1781**.
- The English (under **Sir Eyre Coote**) detached both the Marathas and the Nizam from Haidar's side.
- But Haidar faced the English boldly — suffering a defeat at **Porto Novo in November 1781**.
- He regrouped and **defeated the English, capturing their commander Braithwaite**.

### Haidar Ali's Death and the Treaty of Mangalore (March 1784):
- **Haidar Ali died of cancer on December 7, 1782**.
- His son, **Tipu Sultan**, continued the war for one year without positive outcome.
- Both sides agreed to peace: the **Treaty of Mangalore (March 1784)** — each party returned territories taken from the other.

---

## 4.5 Third Anglo-Mysore War

### Background:
- A dispute arose between **Tipu and the state of Travancore**.
- Travancore had purchased **Jalkottal and Cannanore from the Dutch** in the Cochin state.
- Since **Cochin was a feudatory of Tipu**, he considered Travancore's act a **violation of his sovereign rights**.
- In **April 1790**, Tipu declared war against Travancore for restoration of his rights.

### Course of War:
- The English, siding with Travancore, attacked Tipu.
- In **1790**, Tipu **defeated the English under General Meadows**.
- In **1791**, **Cornwallis** took command and marched through **Ambur and Vellore to Bangalore (captured March 1791)** and then to **Seringapatam**.
- **Coimbatore fell** to the English — but was later lost again.
- With **Maratha and Nizam support**, the English attacked **Seringapatam a second time**.
- Tipu offered serious resistance — but the odds were against him.

### Treaty of Seringapatam (1792):
- **Nearly half of Mysore's territory** was taken by the victors:
  - **English got:** Baramahal, Dindigul, and Malabar
  - **Marathas got:** Regions surrounding the Tungabhadra and its tributaries
  - **Nizam got:** Areas from the Krishna to beyond the Pennar
- A **war damage of three crore rupees** was taken from Tipu.
- **Half the war indemnity was to be paid immediately**; the rest in instalments.
- **Tipu's two sons were taken as hostages** by the English to ensure payment.

> 💬 *Lord Cornwallis: "We have crippled our enemy effectively without making our friends too formidable."*

---

## 4.6 Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1799)

### Background:
- Both Tipu and the English used **1792–1799 to recoup their losses**.
- Tipu fulfilled all terms of the Treaty of Seringapatam and **got his sons released**.
- In **1796**, when the Hindu Wodeyar ruler died, Tipu **refused to place Wodeyar's minor son on the throne** and **declared himself Sultan**.
- He decided to **avenge his humiliating defeat**.
- In **1798**, **Lord Wellesley** succeeded Sir John Shore as the new governor general — an **imperialist to the core**.
- Wellesley was concerned about Tipu's **growing friendship with the French** and aimed at **annihilating Tipu's independent existence** or forcing submission through the **Subsidiary Alliance** system.
- The chargesheet against Tipu alleged he was:
  - Plotting against the English with the Nizam and Marathas
  - Sending emissaries to **Arabia, Afghanistan, Kabul, Zaman Shah, Isle of France (Mauritius), and Versailles** with treasonable intent
- Tipu's explanation did not satisfy Wellesley.

### Course of War:
- The war began on **April 17, 1799** and ended on **May 4, 1799** with the **fall of Seringapatam**.
- Tipu was defeated first by **English General Stuart** and then by **General Harris**.
- **Arthur Wellesley** (the brother of Lord Wellesley) also participated.
- The English were again helped by the **Marathas and the Nizam**.
- **Tipu laid down his life fighting bravely**.
- His family was interned at Vellore, and his treasures were confiscated.

### Mysore After Tipu:
- Wellesley offered **Soonda and Harponelly districts** to the Marathas — they refused.
- The **Nizam was given Gooty and Gurramkonda districts**.
- The English took possession of **Kanara, Wynad, Coimbatore, Dwaraporam, and Seringapatam**.
- A **boy from the earlier Hindu Wodeyar royal family** — **Krishnaraja III** — was chosen as the new Maharaja, who accepted the **subsidiary alliance**.
- In **1831**, **William Bentinck took control of Mysore** on grounds of misgovernance.
- In **1881**, **Lord Ripon restored the kingdom** to its ruler.

---

## 4.7 Estimate of Tipu Sultan

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| **Birth** | November 1750, to Haidar Ali and Fatima |
| **Languages** | Could freely converse in Arabic, Persian, Kanarese, and Urdu |
| **Military** | Organised army on European model with Persian words of command; known as the **'Tiger of Mysore'** |
| **Naval vision** | Set up a **Board of Admiralty in 1796**; planned a fleet of 22 battleships and 20 large frigates; 3 dockyards at **Mangalore, Wajedabad, and Molidabad** (plans did not fructify) |
| **Technology** | **Pioneer of rocket technology** in India; wrote a military manual explaining rocket operations |
| **Sericulture** | Pioneer in **introducing sericulture** (silk farming) to Mysore State |
| **Democracy** | Supported French soldiers at Seringapatam in setting up a **Jacobin Club in 1797**; ordered a salute of **2,300 cannons and 500 rockets**; became a member and allowed himself to be called **Citizen Tipu**; planted the **Tree of Liberty** at Seringapatam |
| **Religious nature** | Some colonial historians called him bigoted — **this is not fully correct**. He crushed Hindu Coorgs and Nairs but also punished Muslim Moplahs who defied him. He is reported to have demolished temples in Kerala during conquest — but also **protected Hindu temples within his own kingdom** and **sanctioned funds for repair of the Sringeri Temple** (idol had been damaged during a Maratha raid in 1791) |

> 🔑 **Important Nuance:** It is **necessary not to judge characters of the past with modern yardsticks of secularism and democracy**. Tipu was a man representing **multiple traditions**. He is also credited with beginning **capitalist development** at a time when feudalism was prevalent.

# 🏇 PART 5: ANGLO-MARATHA STRUGGLE FOR SUPREMACY

---

## 5.1 Rise of the Marathas

- As the Mughal Empire declined, the **Marathas rose to extraordinary power** — controlling a large portion of the country and receiving tributes from areas not directly under their control.
- By the **mid-18th century**, they were in **Lahore** — thinking of becoming rulers of the North Indian empire and playing the role of **kingmakers in the Mughal court**.
- The **Third Battle of Panipat (1761)** — where they were defeated by Ahmad Shah Abdali — changed the situation. But they **regrouped within a decade** and achieved a dominant position.

### The Maratha Confederacy:
- **Bajirao I (1720–40)** — considered the **greatest of all Peshwas** — started a confederacy of prominent Maratha chiefs.
- The purpose was to manage the rapidly expanding Maratha power AND partly appease the **Kshatriya section of the Marathas** (since the Peshwas were **Brahmins**) led by the senapati Dabodi.
- Each prominent family under a chief was assigned a sphere of influence to conquer and rule **in the name of the Maratha king Shahu**.

### The Five Prominent Maratha Families:

| Family | Base |
|---|---|
| **Gaekwad** | Baroda |
| **Bhonsle** | Nagpur |
| **Holkars** | Indore |
| **Sindhias** | Gwalior |
| **Peshwa** | Poona |

- The confederacy worked cordially from **Bajirao I to Madhavrao I** — but the **Third Battle of Panipat (1761)** changed everything.
- The death of **young Peshwa Madhavrao I in 1772** further weakened the Peshwas' control over the confederacy.
- Though the chiefs united occasionally (as against the British, **1775–82**), more often they **quarrelled among themselves**.

---

## 5.2 First Anglo-Maratha War (1775–1782)

### Background:
- After **Madhavrao's death in 1772**, his brother **Narayanrao** became the fifth Peshwa.
- Narayanrao's uncle, **Raghunathrao**, had his nephew **assassinated** and named himself Peshwa — though he was **not the legal heir**.
- Narayanrao's widow, **Gangabai**, gave birth to a son after her husband's death — named **'Sawai' Madhavrao** (legally the next Peshwa).
- **Twelve Maratha chiefs (Barabhai)**, led by **Nana Phadnavis**, tried to name the infant Peshwa and rule as regents.

### Treaties of Surat and Purandhar:
- **Raghunathrao** sought help from the English at Bombay and signed the **Treaty of Surat in 1775**:
  - Raghunathrao **ceded Salsette and Bassein** to the English, plus a portion of revenues from Surat and Bharuch.
  - The English would provide **2,500 soldiers** to Raghunathrao.
- The **British Calcutta Council condemned the Treaty of Surat** and sent Colonel Upton to Pune to make a new treaty.
- **Treaty of Purandhar (1776)**: The Calcutta Council annulled the Surat treaty, renounced Raghunath, and promised him a pension.
- The **Bombay government rejected this** and gave refuge to Raghunath.
- In **1777**, **Nana Phadnavis violated** his treaty with the Calcutta Council by granting the **French a port on the west coast**.
- The English retaliated by sending a force towards Pune.

### Course of War — The Treaty of Wadgaon:
- The Maratha army, though numerically larger, was commanded by the **brilliant general Mahadji Scindia (also known as Mahadji Shinde)**.
- Mahadji **lured the English army into the ghats (mountain passes)** near Talegaon, trapped them, and attacked the **English supply base at Khopali**.
- The Marathas used a **scorched earth policy** — burning farmland and poisoning wells.
- As the English retreated to Talegaon, the Marathas attacked, forcing them to retreat to the **village of Wadgaon**.
- Surrounded on all sides and cut off from food and water, the **English surrendered in mid-January 1779**.
- The **Treaty of Wadgaon** forced the Bombay government to **relinquish all territories acquired since 1775**.

### Treaty of Salbai (May 1782):
- **Warren Hastings (Governor General) rejected the Treaty of Wadgaon** and sent a large force under **Colonel Goddard**, who captured:
  - **Ahmedabad** (February 1779)
  - **Bassein** (December 1780)
- **Captain Popham** captured **Gwalior** (August 1780).
- In **February 1781**, General Camac **defeated Sindhia at Sipri**.
- **Sindhia proposed a new treaty** — the **Treaty of Salbai**, signed **May 1782**, ratified by Hastings in **June 1782** and by Phadnavis in **February 1783**.
- The treaty **guaranteed peace between the two sides for twenty years**.

### Main Provisions of Treaty of Salbai:
- *(i)* **Salsette** to remain with the English.
- *(ii)* All territory conquered since **Treaty of Purandhar (1776)**, including **Bassein**, to be restored to the Marathas.
- *(iii)* In Gujarat, **Fateh Singh Gaekwad** to retain pre-war territory and continue serving the Peshwa.
- *(iv)* English to **give no further support to Raghunathrao**; the Peshwa to grant him a maintenance allowance.
- *(v)* **Haidar Ali** to return all territory taken from the English and the Nawab of Arcot.
- *(vi)* The English to **enjoy trade privileges** as before.
- *(vii)* The **Peshwa not to support any other European nation**.
- *(viii)* The Peshwa and the English to ensure their **allies remain at peace** with one another.
- *(ix)* **Mahadji Scindia to be the mutual guarantor** for proper observance of treaty terms.

---

## 5.3 Second Anglo-Maratha War (1803–1805)

### Background:
- After **Peshwa Madhavrao Narayan committed suicide in 1795**, **Bajirao II** (the worthless son of Raghunathrao) became Peshwa.
- **Nana Phadnavis** (a bitter foe of Bajirao II) became chief minister.
- **Nana Phadnavis died in 1800** — giving the British an added advantage.
- The **dissensions among the Marathas** gave the English an opportunity to intervene.

### Course of War:
- On **April 1, 1801**, the Peshwa **brutally murdered the brother of Jaswantrao (Yashwantrao) Holkar** — Vithuji.
- A furious **Jaswantrao arrayed his forces** against the combined armies of Scindia and Bajirao II.
- On **October 25, 1802**, Jaswant **decisively defeated the Peshwa and Scindia at Hadapsar near Poona** and placed **Vinayakrao** (son of Amritrao) on the Peshwa's seat.
- A terrified **Bajirao II fled to Bassein** where on **December 31, 1802**, he signed a treaty with the English.

### Treaty of Bassein (1802) — Key Provisions:
- *(i)* Accept a **permanent Company infantry force** of not less than **6,000 troops** with field artillery in his territories.
- *(ii)* Cede territories yielding an income of **Rs 26 lakh**.
- *(iii)* **Surrender the city of Surat**.
- *(iv)* Give up all claims for **chauth on the Nizam's dominions**.
- *(v)* Accept the Company's **arbitration in all differences** with the Nizam or the Gaekwad.
- *(vi)* **Not employ Europeans** of any nation at war with the English.
- *(vii)* **Subject his relations with other states** to the Company's control.

### Reduced to Vassalage:
- After the Peshwa accepted the subsidiary alliance, **Scindia and Bhonsle attempted to save Maratha independence**.
- But the well-prepared English army under **Arthur Wellesley** defeated them and forced **separate subsidiary treaties**:
  - **Bhonsle:** Defeated **December 17, 1803 — Treaty of Deogaon**
  - **Scindia:** Defeated **December 30, 1803 — Treaty of Surji-Anjangaon**
  - **Holkar:** Defeated **1806 — Treaty of Rajpurghat**
- In **1804**, **Yashwantrao Holkar** attempted to form a coalition of Indian rulers — but **failed**.

### Significance of the Treaty of Bassein:
- Though signed by a Peshwa who lacked political authority, the **English gains were immense**.
- The Company already had troops in **Mysore, Hyderabad, and Lucknow** — adding Poona meant troops were **more evenly spread and could be rushed anywhere quickly**.
- The treaty was described as giving the English **"the key to India"** — perhaps an exaggeration, but understandable.

---

## 5.4 Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–1819)

### Background:
- **Lord Hastings** had the imperialistic design of imposing **British paramountcy** (supreme authority over all Indian states).
- By the **Charter Act of 1813**, the East India Company's **monopoly of trade in China (except tea) ended** — so the Company needed new markets.
- **The Pindaris** (mercenaries — hired fighters — of mixed caste composition, attached to Maratha armies) had lost regular employment as Marathas weakened. They began **plundering neighbouring territories, including Company territories**.
- The English **charged the Marathas with sheltering the Pindaris**.
- **Pindari leaders Amir Khan and Karim Khan surrendered; Chitu Khan fled into the jungles**.
- The **Treaty of Bassein** had **wounded the feelings of other Maratha leaders** — they saw it as absolute surrender of independence.
- Lord Hastings' actions against the Pindaris were seen as **transgression of Maratha sovereignty** — uniting the confederacy one last time.

### Course of War:
- A **repentant Bajirao II** made a last bid in **1817** by rallying Maratha chiefs against the English.
- The **Peshwa attacked the British Residency at Poona**.
- **Appa Sahib of Nagpur attacked the residency at Nagpur**.
- **Holkar made preparations for war**.
- But the Marathas had lost almost all elements needed to sustain a major power:
  - After **Jaswantrao Holkar's death**, **Tulsi Bai** (Holkar's favourite mistress) came to power — a clever woman, but influenced by unworthy men like **Balram Seth and Amir Khan**.
  - **Bhonsle at Nagpur and Scindia at Gwalior** had also become weak.

### Result:
- The Peshwa was defeated at **Khirki**
- Bhonsle was defeated at **Sitabuldi**
- Holkar was defeated at **Mahidpur**

### Key Treaties:
- **June 1817:** Treaty of Poona (with Peshwa)
- **November 1817:** Treaty of Gwalior (with Scindia)
- **January 1818:** Treaty of Mandasor (with Holkar)
- In **June 1818**, the **Peshwa finally surrendered** and the **Maratha confederacy was dissolved**.
- The **Peshwaship was abolished**.
- **Peshwa Bajirao became a British retainer at Bithur near Kanpur**.
- **Pratap Singh** (a lineal descendant of Shivaji) was made ruler of a small principality, **Satara**, formed from the Peshwa's dominions.

---

## 5.5 Why the Marathas Lost — Complete Analysis

| # | Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| *(i)* | **Inept Leadership** | Later Maratha leaders — Bajirao II, Daulatrao Scindia, Jaswantrao Holkar — were worthless and selfish; no match for Elphinstone, John Malcolm, and Arthur Wellesley |
| *(ii)* | **Defective Nature of Maratha State** | Cohesion was artificial and accidental, not organic; no effort since Shivaji's days for organised communal improvement, spread of education, or unification of people |
| *(iii)* | **Loose Political Set-up** | A loose confederation under Chhatrapati and then Peshwa; powerful chiefs carved semi-independent kingdoms; irreconcilable hostility between units; chiefs frequently took sides against each other |
| *(iv)* | **Inferior Military System** | Full of personal valour but inferior in organisation, weapons, disciplined action, and effective leadership; centrifugal divided command; neglected artillery; inadequate adoption of modern warfare |
| *(v)* | **Unstable Economic Policy** | No industries or foreign trade openings; economy not conducive to a stable political set-up |
| *(vi)* | **Superior English Diplomacy and Espionage** | Better diplomatic skill to win allies and isolate enemies; disunity among Marathas simplified English task; well-knit spy system vs. Maratha ignorance about their enemy |
| *(vii)* | **Progressive English Outlook** | Rejuvenated by Renaissance forces; focused on scientific inventions, ocean voyages, and colonies; Indians still steeped in medievalism and old dogmas; Maratha leaders paid little attention to mundane state matters; traditional social hierarchy based on priestly class dominance made empire-building difficult |

> 🔑 **Conclusion:** "The English attacked a 'divided house' which started crumbling after a few pushes."

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **The Maratha Legacy in Modern India:**
> The Maratha confederation's failure due to internal divisions finds a poignant modern echo. The **Maratha reservation movement** in Maharashtra — demanding OBC status for the Maratha community — led to massive protests in 2018 and again in 2023-24. The **Supreme Court of India in May 2021 struck down Maharashtra's Maratha reservation law** (*Dr. Jaishree Laxmanrao Patil vs. Chief Minister* case), ruling it violated the **50% ceiling on reservations** set in the *Indra Sawhney case (1992)*. The community that once nearly ruled all of India now fights for educational and employment reservations — a dramatic historical reversal that underscores the long-term human cost of the British conquest. `[Modern Example]`

# 🏜️ PART 6: CONQUEST OF SINDH

---

## 6.1 Background

- In the **early 19th century**, the English showed increasing interest in **Sindh**, where they already enjoyed some trade facilities authorised by a **Mughal Emperor's farman of 1630**.

### Rise of the Talpuras Amirs:
- Before the **Talpuras Amirs**, Sindh was ruled by the **Kallora chiefs**.
- In **1758**, an English factory was built at **Thatta** based on a parwana (permission) given by **Kallora prince Ghulam Shah**.
- In **1761**, Ghulam Shah **ratified the earlier treaty** and excluded other Europeans from trading.
- In **1775**, a not-too-friendly ruler **Sarfraz Khan** made the English **close their factory**.
- In the **1770s**, a **Baluch tribe called the Talpuras** descended from the hills and settled in Sindh's plains — excellent soldiers, adapted to hard life.
- In **1783**, under **Mir Fath (Fatah) Ali Khan**, the **Talpuras established complete hold over Sindh** and exiled the Kallora prince.
- The **Durrani monarch confirmed Mir Fath Khan's claims** and ordered him to share the country with his brothers (**popularly known as 'Char Yar'**).
- When **Mir Fath died in 1800**, the Char Yar divided the kingdom, **calling themselves the Amirs or Lords of Sindh**.
- The Amirs extended their dominion — conquering:
  - **Amarkot** from the Raja of Jodhpur
  - **Karachi** from the chief of Luz
  - **Shaikarpur and Bukkar** from the Afghans

---

## 6.2 Gradual Ascendancy Over Sindh

### Treaty of 'Eternal Friendship':
- A common belief in the late 18th century was that **Napoleon was conspiring with Tipu Sultan to invade India**.
- In **1799**, behind Lord Wellesley's efforts to revive commercial relations with Sindh was the hidden aim to **counteract the alliance of the French, Tipu Sultan, and Shah Zaman** (the Kabul monarch).
- In **October 1800**, the amir ordered the **British agent (Crow) to quit Sindh within ten days** — and the Company **quietly suffered the insult**.
- In **June 1807**, Napoleon joined the **alliance of Tilsit with Alexander I of Russia** — with conditions including a **combined invasion of India by land route**.
- The British now needed a barrier between Russia and British India.
- **Lord Minto** sent three delegations: **Metcalfe to Lahore**, **Elphinstone to Kabul**, and **Malcolm to Teheran**.
- **Nicholas Smith** visited Sindh, meeting the Amirs for a defensive arrangement.
- Result: The Amirs' **first-ever treaty with the English** — both sides professed **eternal friendship**, agreed to **exclude the French from Sindh**, and exchanged agents.
- The treaty was **renewed in 1820**, adding exclusion of Americans and resolving border disputes near Kachch.

### Treaty of 1832:
- In **1832**, **William Bentinck** sent **Colonel Pottinger** to Sindh.

**Treaty Provisions:**
- *(i)* **Free passage** through Sindh for English traders and travellers; use of the **Indus for trading** — but no warships or war materials.
- *(ii)* No English merchant to settle in Sindh; passports required for travellers.
- *(iii)* Tariff rates could be altered by the Amirs; no military dues or tolls to be demanded.
- *(iv)* The Amirs to work with the **Raja of Jodhpur** to suppress robbers of Kachch.
- *(v)* Old treaties confirmed; parties not to be jealous of each other.

---

## 6.3 Lord Auckland and the Subjugation of Sindh

- **Lord Auckland** (Governor General from **1836**) viewed Sindh as key to **saving India from a possible Russian invasion**.
- He wanted **counteracting influence over the Afghans**.
- **Ranjit Singh in Punjab was too strong** to coerce — the Amirs were not.
- When **Ranjit Singh captured the frontier town Rojhan**, Pottinger was sent to Hyderabad (Sindh) to sign a new treaty.
- The treaty offered **protection to the Amirs** on condition that Company troops be stationed in the capital at the Amir's expense — OR the English be given suitable concessions.
- The Amirs initially refused but **reluctantly agreed in 1838** when told about the possibility of Ranjit Singh getting help from others.
- The treaty permitted the English to **intervene in Amir-Sikh disputes** and establish a British resident with **freedom of movement escorted by English troops**.
- **Sindh was turned into a British protectorate in 1838**.

---

## 6.4 Tripartite Treaty of 1838 and Subsidiary Alliance (1839)

- In **June 1838**, the English persuaded **Ranjit Singh** to sign a **Tripartite Treaty** agreeing to British mediation in his disputes with the Amirs.
- Then they made **Emperor Shah Shuja** give up his sovereign rights on Sindh, provided the arrears of tribute were paid.
- The exact amount of tribute was to be determined by the English — whose main objective was to **obtain finances for the Afghan adventure** and secure Sindh for a line of operation against Afghanistan.

**Subsidiary Alliance (February 1839):**
- Under threat of superior force, the Amirs accepted: **a British subsidiary force stationed at Shikarpur and Bukkar** and annual payment of **Rs 3 lakh** for maintenance of Company troops.
- The Amirs were:
  - Barred from **negotiating with foreign states without Company knowledge**
  - Required to **provide a storeroom at Karachi** for military supplies
  - Required to **abolish all tolls on the Indus**
  - Required to **furnish auxiliary forces** for the Afghan war if called upon

---

## 6.5 Capitulation and Annexation of Sindh (1843)

- The **First Anglo-Afghan War (1839–42)** was fought on Sindh's soil — the Amirs never liked it or the British troop presence.
- They paid for all of this under treaty — but were **not rewarded or thanked**.
- They were instead **charged with hostility and disaffection** against the British government.
- **Ellenborough** (in a precarious position due to Afghan war reverses) sent **Outram** to Sindh to negotiate a new treaty.
- The new treaty required the Amirs to:
  - **Cede important provinces** as punishment for "past transgressions"
  - **Supply fuel** to Company steamers on the Indus
  - **Stop minting coins**
- **Napier intervened in a succession dispute** and started a war when the Amirs rose in revolt.
- The whole of Sindh capitulated quickly. **The Amirs were made captives and banished**.
- In **1843**, under **Governor General Ellenborough**, Sindh was **merged into the British Empire**.
- **Charles Napier** was appointed its **first governor**.

> 💬 *Charles Napier himself wrote: "We have no right to seize Sindh, yet we shall do so, and a very advantageous, useful, humane piece of rascality it will be."*

> 💬 *Elphinstone commented on the conquest coming after the Afghan War defeat: "Coming from Afghanistan it put one in mind of a bully who has been knocked in the street and went home to beat his wife in revenge."*

> 💬 *P.E. Roberts: "Under Auckland and his cabinet of secretaries British policy in India had fallen to a lower level of unscrupulousness than ever before."*

# ⚔️ PART 7: CONQUEST OF PUNJAB

---

## 7.1 Consolidation of Punjab Under the Sikhs

- After the **murder of the last Sikh Guru, Guru Gobind Singh**, a section of Sikhs under **Banda Bahadur** revolted against the Mughals during **Bahadur Shah's** reign.
- In **1715**, **Banda Bahadur was defeated by Farrukhsiyar** and **put to death in 1716**.
- The Sikh polity became leaderless and divided into two groups:
  - **Bandai (Liberal)**
  - **Tat Khalsa (Orthodox)**
- This rift ended in **1721** under the influence of **Bhai Mani Singh**.
- In **1784**, **Kapur Singh Faizullapuria** organised the Sikhs under **Dal Khalsa** — with the objective of uniting followers of Sikhism politically, culturally, and economically.
- The Dal Khalsa was formed into two sections:
  - **Budha Dal** — the army of the veterans
  - **Taruna Dal** — the army of the young
- The weakness of the Mughals and **Ahmad Shah Abdali's invasions** created confusion and anarchy in Punjab — helping the Dal Khalsa consolidate further.
- The Sikhs consolidated in **misls** (military brotherhoods — the word 'misl' is Arabic for "equal/alike" and also means "state") with a **democratic set-up**.
- During **1763 to 1773**, many misls ruled Punjab from **Saharanpur in the east to Attock in the west**, and from the mountains in the north to **Multan in the south**.

---

## 7.2 Sukarchakiya Misl and Ranjit Singh

At the time of **Ranjit Singh's birth (November 2, 1780)**, there were **12 important misls**:
> Ahluwaliya, Bhangi, Dallewalia, Faizullapuria, Kanhaiya, Krorasinghia, Nakkai, Nishaniya, Phulakiya, Ramgarhiya, **Sukarchakiya**, and Shaheed.

- The **central administration of a misl** was based on **Gurumatta Sangh** — essentially a political, social, and economic system.
- **Ranjit Singh was the son of Mahan Singh** (leader of the Sukarchakiya misl). Mahan Singh died when Ranjit Singh was only **12 years old**.
- Towards the close of the 18th century, **all important misls (except Sukarchakiya) were disintegrating**.
- **Afghanistan was also engulfed in civil war**.
- Ranjit Singh exploited these conditions, following a ruthless **"blood and iron" policy** to carve out a kingdom in central Punjab.
- In **1799**, Ranjit Singh was **appointed Governor of Lahore** by **Zaman Shah** (ruler of Afghanistan).
- In **1805**, he acquired **Jammu and Amritsar** — bringing both the political capital (**Lahore**) and religious capital (**Amritsar**) under his rule.
- He maintained good relations with the **Dogras and the Nepalese** and enlisted them in his army.

---

## 7.3 Ranjit Singh and the English

- The **prospects of a joint Franco-Russian invasion of India** alarmed the English.
- In **1807**, **Lord Minto** sent **Charles Metcalfe** to Lahore.
- Ranjit Singh offered to accept Metcalfe's proposal of an offensive-defensive alliance — **on condition** that the English remain neutral in a Sikh-Afghan war AND recognise him as the **sovereign of the entire Punjab including Malwa (cis-Sutlej) territories**.
- The negotiations **failed**.

### Treaty of Amritsar (April 25, 1809):
- With the **Napoleonic danger receding** and the English becoming more assertive, Ranjit Singh signed the **Treaty of Amritsar**.
- **Significance:** It **checked Ranjit Singh's cherished ambition** of ruling over the entire Sikh nation — by accepting the **River Sutlej as the boundary** between his dominions and the Company's.
- Ranjit Singh then directed his energies westward and captured:
  - **Multan (1818)**
  - **Kashmir (1819)**
  - **Peshawar (1834)**
- In **June 1838**, Ranjit Singh was compelled to sign the **Tripartite Treaty** with the English — but **refused to give passage to the British army** through his territories to attack **Dost Mohammad** (the Afghan Amir).
- Ranjit Singh **died in June 1839** — and with his death, the decline of his empire began.

---

## 7.4 Punjab After Ranjit Singh

### The Succession Crisis:
- **Kharak Singh** (Ranjit Singh's only legitimate son) was inefficient — court factions became active during his brief reign.
- **Kharak Singh's sudden death in 1839** and the **accidental death of his son Prince Nau Nihal Singh** (when he was returning from his father's funeral) led to anarchy.
- Ranjit Singh's able generals — **Mokham Chand, Dewan Chand, Hari Singh Nalwa, and Ram Dayal** — were already dead.
- Growing discontent among troops due to **irregularity of payment** and **appointment of unworthy officers**.
- The Lahore government (continuing the policy of friendship with the English) **permitted British troops to pass through its territory twice** — once fleeing from Afghanistan, once marching back — causing economic dislocation in Punjab.

### Rani Jindan and Daleep Singh:
- After **Nau Nihal Singh's death**, **Sher Singh** (another son of Ranjit Singh) succeeded — but was **murdered in late 1843**.
- **Daleep Singh** (a minor son of Ranjit Singh) was proclaimed Maharaja with:
  - **Rani Jindan** as regent
  - **Hira Singh Dogra** as wazir
- Hira Singh was **murdered in a court intrigue in 1844**.
- The new wazir, **Jawahar Singh** (brother of Rani Jindan), incurred the displeasure of the army and was **deposed and put to death in 1845**.
- **Lal Singh** (a lover of Rani Jindan) won over the army and became wazir.
- **Teja Singh** was appointed commander of the forces.

---

## 7.5 First Anglo-Sikh War (1845–1846)

### Causes:
- *(i)* **Anarchy in the Lahore kingdom** after Ranjit Singh's death — a power struggle between the Lahore court and the increasingly assertive local army.
- *(ii)* **Suspicions in the Sikh army** arising from English military campaigns to annex **Gwalior and Sindh in 1841** and the campaign in **Afghanistan in 1842**.
- *(iii)* **Increase in English troops** being stationed near the border with the Lahore kingdom.
- The immediate cause: The **Sikh army crossed the River Sutlej on December 11, 1845** — seen as an aggressive manoeuvre giving the English justification to declare war.

### Course of War:
- **English:** 20,000–30,000 troops
- **Sikhs:** About 50,000 men under overall command of **Lal Singh**
- The **treachery of Lal Singh and Teja Singh** caused five successive defeats to the Sikhs:
  - **Mudki** (December 18, 1845)
  - **Ferozeshah** (December 21–22, 1845)
  - **Buddelwal**
  - **Aliwal** (January 28, 1846)
  - **Sobraon** (February 10, 1846)
- **Lahore fell to the British forces on February 20, 1846** without a fight.

### Treaty of Lahore (March 8, 1846):
- War indemnity of **more than 1 crore rupees** to be given to the English.
- **The Jalandhar Doab** (between the Beas and Sutlej) **annexed** to the Company.
- A **British resident established at Lahore** under **Henry Lawrence**.
- **Sikh army strength reduced**.
- **Daleep Singh recognised as ruler** under Rani Jindan as regent and Lal Singh as wazir.
- Since the Sikhs couldn't pay the full war indemnity, **Kashmir (including Jammu) was sold to Gulab Singh** — who paid **75 lakh rupees** to the Company.
- The **transfer of Kashmir to Gulab Singh** was formalised by a **separate treaty on March 16, 1846**.

### Treaty of Bhairowal (December 1846):
- The Sikhs were not satisfied with the Treaty of Lahore over Kashmir — they rebelled.
- **Rani Jindan was removed as regent**.
- A **council of regency for Punjab** was set up — consisting of **8 Sikh sardars** presided over by **English Resident Henry Lawrence**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **The Kashmir Question — From 1846 to Today:**
> Kashmir was literally **sold for 75 lakh rupees** in the Treaty of Lahore (1846) — the origin of a dispute that continues to define South Asian geopolitics. The **abrogation of Article 370** of the Indian Constitution on **August 5, 2019** — reorganising Jammu & Kashmir into two Union Territories — was a landmark modern development. The **Supreme Court of India upheld the abrogation in its December 2023 judgment** (*In Re: Article 370 of the Constitution* case), while directing that **statehood be restored to Jammu & Kashmir** as soon as possible. This 2023 ruling directly connects to a chain of events that began in 1846. `[Modern Example]`

---

## 7.6 Second Anglo-Sikh War (1848–1849)

### Causes:
- The humiliating provisions of the **Treaties of Lahore and Bhairowal** created deep resentment.
- **Inhuman treatment of Rani Jindan** — sent to Benares as a pensioner — added to Sikh anger.
- **Mulraj** (governor of Multan) was replaced over the issue of increased annual revenue; he revolted and **murdered two English officers** accompanying the new governor.
- **Sher Singh** was sent to suppress the revolt but **joined Mulraj instead** — leading to a mass uprising in Multan.
- Governor General **Lord Dalhousie** (a hardcore expansionist) got the **pretext to annex Punjab completely**.

### Course of War:
Three important battles were fought:
- *(i)* **Battle of Ramnagar** — led by **Sir Hugh Gough** (commander-in-chief of the Company)
- *(ii)* **Battle of Chillhanwala** — January 1849
- *(iii)* **Battle of Gujarat** — February 21, 1849 (Gujarat here is a small town on the banks of River Jhelum — NOT the state of Gujarat)
- The **Sikh army surrendered at Rawalpindi**; their Afghan allies were **chased out of India**.

### Result:
- **Surrender of the Sikh army and Sher Singh in 1849**.
- **Annexation of Punjab**.
- The **Earl of Dalhousie** received thanks from the British Parliament and was promoted to **Marquess** in the peerage.
- A **three-member board to govern Punjab** was set up: **Henry Lawrence, John Lawrence, and Charles Mansel**.
- In **1853**, the board was dissolved and Punjab was placed under a **chief commissioner** — **John Lawrence** became the **first chief commissioner**.

### Significance of the Anglo-Sikh Wars:
- The wars gave both sides **mutual respect for each other's fighting prowess**.
- The **Sikhs fought loyally on the British side** in the **Revolt of 1857** and in many other campaigns and wars until **Indian independence in 1947**.

# 🏛️ PART 8: EXTENSION OF BRITISH PARAMOUNTCY THROUGH ADMINISTRATIVE POLICY

---

The Company's expansion (1757–1857) used a **two-fold method**:
- **(a)** Policy of **annexation by conquest or war**
- **(b)** Policy of **annexation by diplomacy and administrative mechanisms**

The three key administrative tools were:
1. **Ring-Fence Policy** (Warren Hastings)
2. **Subsidiary Alliance** (Lord Wellesley)
3. **Doctrine of Lapse** (Lord Dalhousie)

---

## 8.1 The Policy of Ring-Fence (Warren Hastings)

- **Warren Hastings** took charge as Governor General at a critical time — facing the powerful combination of the **Marathas, Mysore, and Hyderabad**.
- He followed a **Ring-Fence Policy** — creating **buffer zones (protective zones of allied states) to defend the Company's frontiers**.
- In simple terms: **defend their neighbours' frontiers to safeguard their own territories**.
- The **chief dangers** to the Company's territories were from the **Afghan invaders and the Marathas**.
- To safeguard against these, the Company organised the **defence of Awadh's frontiers** — on condition that the **Nawab would defray (pay for) the expenses of the defending army**.
- The defence of Awadh was essentially the defence of Bengal.

### Key Features of Ring-Fence:
- States brought under the system were **assured of military assistance against external aggression — but at their own expense**.
- These allies had to maintain **subsidiary forces** organised, equipped, and commanded by Company officers — **paid by the rulers of these states**.
- Wellesley's Subsidiary Alliance was, in fact, an **extension and perfection of the Ring-Fence system**.

---

## 8.2 The Subsidiary Alliance System (Lord Wellesley, 1798–1805)

Lord Wellesley was Governor General from **1798–1805** and used the Subsidiary Alliance to build an empire.

### How It Worked — What the Indian Ruler Had to Do:
- Accept the **permanent stationing of a British force** within his territory.
- **Pay a subsidy** for the maintenance of this force.
- Accept the **posting of a British resident** at his court.
- **Not employ any European** in his service without prior consultation with the Company.
- **Not go to war or negotiate with any other Indian ruler** without consulting the Governor General.

### What Britain Promised in Return:
- **Defend the ruler** from his enemies.
- Adopt a **policy of non-interference in internal matters** of the allied state.

### Why Wellesley Pushed This System:
- **Fear of Napoleon's expedition towards the East** — the British believed the French could attack India's western coast from their colony of **Mauritius**.
- The clause requiring Indian rulers to **dismiss non-British Europeans** from service was designed to keep the **French at bay**.
- It also **expanded the Company's hold** over Indian states and gradually brought more territory into the Company's fold.

### The Actual Impact on Indian Rulers:
- Lost their **independence** by buying security.
- Were **constantly interfered with** by the British Resident.
- Lost much of their **revenue** paying for British troops.
- Became **weak and irresponsible** rulers — their subjects were exploited, but it was **practically impossible to depose oppressive rulers** as they were protected by the British.

> 💬 *Sidney J. Owen: "Wellesley converted the British Empire in India to the British Empire of India."*

---

## 8.3 Evolution of the Subsidiary Alliance — Four Stages

| Stage | What Happened |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Company offered to help a friendly Indian state with its troops to fight wars |
| **Stage 2** | Made common cause with the now-friendly state — both fought together |
| **Stage 3** | The Indian ally was asked not for men, but for **money** — in return, Company would recruit, train, and maintain a fixed number of soldiers under British officers, available for the ruler's personal protection and defence |
| **Stage 4** | The protection fee was fixed at a **high level** — when the state failed to pay in time, it was asked to **cede territories in lieu of payment**. The Company's entry into state affairs had begun; the British Resident would then initiate and hasten eventual annexation |

### Origin:
- It was probably **Dupleix** who first **gave European troops on hire to Indian rulers** — making him the originator of the concept.
- **First Indian state to fall into this trap:** **Awadh (1765)** — which signed a treaty under which the Company pledged to defend Awadh's frontiers in return for the Nawab paying the cost.
- In **1787**, the Company first insisted that the subsidiary state should **not have independent foreign relations** — included in the treaty with the **Nawab of Carnatic** (which Cornwallis signed in **February 1787**).
- It was **Wellesley's genius** to make it a general rule to **negotiate for the surrender of territory in full sovereignty** for maintenance of the subsidiary force.

### States Which Accepted the Subsidiary Alliance:

| State | Year |
|---|---|
| **Nizam of Hyderabad** | September 1798 and 1800 |
| **Ruler of Mysore** | 1799 |
| **Ruler of Tanjore** | October 1799 |
| **Nawab of Awadh** | November 1801 |
| **Peshwa** | December 1801 |
| **Bhonsle Raja of Berar** | December 1803 |
| **Scindia** | February 1804 |
| **Rajput states of Jodhpur, Jaipur, Macheri, Bundi, and Bharatpur** | 1818 |
| **Holkars** (last Maratha confederation) | 1818 |

---

## 8.4 The Doctrine of Lapse (Lord Dalhousie, 1848–1856)

### What Was the Doctrine?
In simple terms:
- The **adopted son** of a ruler could inherit his **private property** — but **not the state**.
- It was for the **paramount power (the British)** to decide whether to give the state to the adopted son — or to **annex it**.

### The Controversy:
- The doctrine was claimed to be based on **Hindu law and Indian customs** — but **Hindu law was actually inconclusive** on this point.
- Instances of an Indian sovereign annexing a vassal's state on account of 'lapse' (dying without a male heir) were **quite rare**.
- **Maharaja Ranjit Singh** had annexed a few of his feudatory principalities on account of 'lapse'.
- The Company in **1820 acquired a few petty Cis-Sutlej states** on absence of heirs.
- But there was **no clear-cut instance** of an adopted son being deprived of an entire state.

### Was Dalhousie the Originator?
- **No** — this policy is attributed to **Lord Dalhousie (1848–56)**, but he was **not its originator**.
- It was a coincidence that during his governor-generalship, **several important cases arose** where the Doctrine could be applied.
- Dalhousie showed **too much zeal** in enforcing a policy that had previously been applied only theoretically.

### States Annexed Under the Doctrine of Lapse:

| State | Year of Annexation |
|---|---|
| **Satara** | 1848 |
| **Jaitpur (Bundelkhand)** | — |
| **Sambhalpur (Orissa)** | 1849 |
| **Baghat (Himachal Pradesh)** | 1850 |
| **Udaipur** | 1854 |
| **Jhansi** | 1854 |
| **Nagpur** | 1854 |

- Lord Dalhousie also **annexed Awadh in 1856** — but this was on **grounds of misgovernment**, not the Doctrine of Lapse.

---

## 8.5 Annexation of Awadh (1856)

- Awadh was the **oldest of the surviving states brought under the Subsidiary Alliance**.
- The **cruel impact of the Subsidiary Alliance** led to its **continuous maladministration** under profligate nawabs for **80 years**.
- People suffered from:
  - **Heavy taxes** imposed by the Nawab
  - **Illegal exactions** by officials and talukdars
  - The **chronic bankruptcy of the treasury** — partly due to heavy charges for maintaining subsidiary British troops
  - Large contributions extracted by **Lord Hastings, Lord Amherst, and Lord William Bentinck** for purposes unconnected with Awadh
- In **1819**, the Nawab was given the **title and status of a king**.
- **Lord Dalhousie** directed **Sleeman (the Resident)** to inspect the state personally — Sleeman's report described **anarchical conditions**.
- **Outram** (who succeeded Sleeman in **1854**) submitted a report supporting Sleeman's findings.
- Dalhousie initially **hesitated to annex** — he preferred permanent British administration while the Nawab retained his titles.
- But the **Court of Directors ordered annexation and abolition of the throne (1856)**.
- **Wajid Ali Shah refused to sign** a treaty giving away his rights and was **exiled to Calcutta**.
- This was a **political blunder** for which the British had to **pay a heavy price during the Revolt of 1857**.

### Dalhousie's Overall Record:
- During his **eight-year tenure (1848–56)**, Dalhousie **annexed eight states**.
- He annexed approximately **quarter million square miles** of Indian territory.
- His reign **almost completed the process of British expansion** that began with Plassey in **1757**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **The Doctrine of Lapse and Modern Inheritance Law:**
> The Doctrine of Lapse — which denied adopted sons the right to inherit states — was a direct violation of the prevailing Hindu personal law. Today, the **Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005** gives daughters equal rights in ancestral property — a radical departure from the patriarchal inheritance norms that the British exploited. The **Supreme Court in Vineeta Sharma vs. Rakesh Sharma (2020)** ruled that daughters have equal coparcenary rights in Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs) regardless of whether their father was alive when the 2005 amendment came into force — a landmark judgment in inheritance rights. `[Modern Example]`

# 🌏 PART 9: RELATIONS OF BRITISH INDIA WITH NEIGHBOURING COUNTRIES

---

## 9.1 Anglo-Bhutanese Relations

- The British **occupation of Assam in 1826** brought them into close contact with **Bhutan**.
- Frequent **Bhutanese raids into adjoining territories** in Assam and Bengal created friction.
- In **1863–64**, the British envoy was badly treated and a treaty was imposed on him — forcing the British to **surrender the passes leading to Assam**.
- In retaliation, the British **annexed these passes and stopped the allowance paid to the Bhutanese**.
- In **1865**, the Bhutanese were **forced to surrender the passes** in return for an **annual subsidy**.
- The surrendered district later became a **productive area with tea gardens**.

---

## 9.2 Anglo-Nepalese Relations

- The **Gorkhas** wrested control of Nepal from the successors of **Ranjit Malla of Bhatgaon in 1760**.
- They began expanding their dominion southward (the north was well defended by China).
- In **1801**, the English annexed **Gorakhpur** — bringing Gorkha and Company boundaries together.
- Conflict started when the Gorkhas **captured Butwal and Sheoraj** during the period of **Lord Hastings (1813–23)**.
- The war ended in the **Treaty of Sagauli in 1816** — in favour of the British.

### Treaty of Sagauli (1816) — Terms:
- **Nepal accepted a British resident**.
- Nepal **ceded the districts of Garhwal and Kumaon** and **abandoned claims to Terai**.
- Nepal **withdrew from Sikkim**.

### Advantages for the British:
- The British Empire now **reached the Himalayas**.
- Got **better facilities for trade with Central Asia**.
- Acquired sites for **hill stations: Shimla, Mussoorie, and Nainital**.
- The **Gorkhas joined the British Indian Army in large numbers**.

---

## 9.3 Anglo-Burmese Relations

Burma was a free country in the early 19th century wanting to expand westward. Three Anglo-Burmese Wars resulted in the **annexation of Burma into British India in 1885**.

### Causes of British Interest in Burma:
- **Forest resources of Burma** (timber)
- **Market for British manufactures** in Burma
- Need to **check French ambitions** in Burma and South-East Asia

### First Burma War (1824–1826):
- **Cause:** Burmese expansion westward — occupation of **Arakan and Manipur**, and threat to **Assam and the Brahmaputra Valley** — created continuous friction.
- British expeditionary forces **occupied Rangoon in May 1824** and reached within **72 km of the capital at Ava**.

**Treaty of Yandabo (1826) — Burma agreed to:**
- Pay **one crore rupees** as war compensation.
- Cede its coastal provinces of **Arakan and Tenasserim**.
- **Abandon claims** on Assam, Cachar, and Jaintia.
- Recognise **Manipur as an independent state**.
- Negotiate a **commercial treaty with Britain**.
- Accept a **British resident at Ava** while posting a Burmese envoy at Calcutta.

### Second Burma War (1852):
- Result of **British commercial need** and the imperialist policy of **Lord Dalhousie**.
- British merchants wanted **timber resources of upper Burma** and further market access.
- The British occupied **Pegu** (the only remaining coastal province of Burma).
- **Intense guerrilla resistance** had to be overcome before complete control of lower Burma was established.

### Third Burma War (1885):
- After **Burmese King Bhindan's death**, his son **Thibaw** succeeded — hostile to the British from the start.
- British merchants at Rangoon complained of **stepmotherly treatment** by Thibaw.
- Thibaw was **negotiating commercial treaties with France, Germany, and Italy**.
- The **French planned a rail link from Mandalay** to French territory — a serious threat.
- A **humiliating fine was imposed on a British timber company** by Thibaw.
- **Dufferin ordered the invasion and final annexation of upper Burma in 1885**.
- The British faced a **strong guerrilla uprising in all of Burma soon after**.
- **Burma was separated from India in 1935** — specifically to weaken links between Burmese nationalists and the **Indian National Congress**.
- The Burmese nationalist movement intensified under **U Aung San** during the Second World War.
- **Burma achieved independence on January 4, 1948**.

---

## 9.4 Anglo-Tibetan Relations

- Tibet was ruled by a **theocracy of Buddhist monks (lamas)** under nominal Chinese suzerainty.
- British efforts to establish commercial relations with Tibet had **yielded no result**.
- Chinese suzerainty over Tibet was **ineffective** and **Russian influence at Lhasa was increasing**.
- Reports of **Russian arms and ammunition coming into Tibet** alarmed **Curzon (Viceroy 1899–1905)**.
- Curzon sent a small **Gorkha contingent under Colonel Younghusband** on a special mission to Tibet.
- The Tibetans refused to negotiate and offered **non-violent resistance**.
- **Younghusband pushed into Lhasa (August 1904)** while the **Dalai Lama fled**.

### Treaty of Lhasa (1904) — Younghusband Dictated Terms:
- Tibet to pay an **indemnity of 75 lakh rupees** at the rate of **one lakh rupees per annum**.
- As security, the **Indian Government would occupy the Chumbi Valley** (between Bhutan and Sikkim) for **75 years**.
- Tibet to **respect the frontier of Sikkim**.
- **Trade marts to be opened at Yatung, Gyantse, and Gartok**.
- Tibet not to grant any concession for **railways, roads, or telegraph** to any foreign state — but give Great Britain some control over Tibet's foreign affairs.

### Later Revision:
- On insistence of the Secretary of State and a pledge given to Russia, the treaty was revised:
  - **Indemnity reduced from Rs 75 lakh to Rs 25 lakh**
  - **Chumbi Valley to be evacuated after three years** (actually evacuated only in **January 1908**)
- **Only China gained** from the whole affair — the **Anglo-Russian convention of 1907** provided that both great powers would **not negotiate with Tibet except through Chinese mediation**.
- However, **Curzon's policy counteracted all Russian schemes in Tibet**.

---

## 9.5 Anglo-Afghan Relations

### Background:
- In the early 19th century, **increased Russian influence in Persia** replaced British influence and **thwarted an English scheme for a new route via the River Euphrates to India**.
- After the **Treaty of Turkomanchai (1828)**, the English got alarmed about **possible Russian plans regarding India**.
- The **passes of the north-west** were seen as the key to enter India.
- The need: **Afghanistan must be under a ruler friendly to the British**.

### Lord Auckland's Forward Policy:
- **Auckland** (Governor General from **1836**) advocated a **forward policy** — meaning the Company government in India itself would take initiatives to **protect British India's boundary from a probable Russian attack** through treaties or annexation.
- The **Amir of Afghanistan, Dost Mohammed**, wanted British friendship — but **conditionally on British help to recover Peshawar from the Sikhs**. The British rejected this.
- Dost Mohammed then turned to **Russia and Persia for help**.
- This prompted the British to pursue the **forward policy** and the **Tripartite Treaty (1838)** was signed with the British, the Sikhs, and **Shah Shuja** (who had been deposed from the Afghan throne in **1809** and was living as a **British pensioner at Ludhiana**).

**Tripartite Treaty Provisions:**
- Shah Shuja to be **enthroned with Sikh armed help** — the Company remaining in the background, "jingling the money-bag"
- Shah Shuja to **conduct foreign affairs with the advice of the Sikhs and British**
- Shah Shuja to **give up sovereign rights over the Amirs of Sindh** in return for a large sum
- Shah Shuja to **recognise Ranjit Singh's claims** over Afghan territories on the right bank of the Indus

### First Anglo-Afghan War (1839–1842):
- Soon after the Tripartite Treaty, the original irritants disappeared — **Persia lifted its siege of Herat** and **Russia recalled its envoy from Kabul**.
- Yet the British proceeded with the war anyway.
- An **English army entered Kabul triumphantly (August 1839)** — most tribes had been won over by bribes.
- **Dost Mohammed surrendered (1840)** and **Shah Shuja was made the Amir of Afghanistan**.
- But **Shah Shuja was unacceptable to the Afghans**.
- As soon as the British withdrew, the **Afghans rose in rebellion**, killing the garrison commander in Kabul.
- The British **signed a treaty (1841)** with Afghan chiefs agreeing to evacuate Afghanistan and restore Dost Mohammed.
- But the plan failed. Under a new expedition, the British **re-occupied Kabul in September 1842** — but having learned their lesson, they recognised **Dost Mohammed as the independent ruler of Afghanistan**.
- **Cost of the First Afghan War: 1.5 crore rupees and nearly 20,000 men**.

### John Lawrence and the Policy of Masterly Inactivity (1864–1869):
- **John Lawrence** started a **policy of masterly inactivity** — a reaction to the disasters of the First Afghan War.
- Based on **practical common sense and intimate knowledge of frontier problems** and Afghan passion for independence.
- Even when **Dost Mohammed died in 1863**, there was **no interference in the war of succession**.
- **Two conditions** for this policy:
  - *(i)* Peace at the frontier must not be disturbed.
  - *(ii)* No candidate in civil war must seek foreign help.
- Lawrence tried to **cultivate friendship with Sher Ali** as he established himself on the throne.

> 💬 *R.B. Smith on Lawrence: "Sir John Lawrence's foreign policy was a policy of self-reliance and self-restraint, of defence not defiance, of waiting and watching."*

### Lytton and the Policy of Proud Reserve:
- **Lytton** (a nominee of the **Conservative government under Benjamin Disraeli, 1874–80**) became **Viceroy of India in 1876**.
- He started a new foreign policy of **"proud reserve"** — aimed at having **scientific frontiers** and safeguarding **"spheres of influence"**.
- According to Lytton, relations with Afghanistan **could no longer be left ambiguous**.

### Second Anglo-Afghan War (1878–1880):
- **Sher Ali** wanted friendship with both Russia AND British India while keeping both at arm's length.
- **Sher Ali refused to keep a British envoy in Kabul** while earlier having granted a **similar concession to the Russians**.
- Lytton was displeased. When the **Russians withdrew their envoy from Kabul**, Lytton **decided to invade Afghanistan**.
- Sher Ali fled in the face of the British invasion.
- The **Treaty of Gandamak (May 1879)** was signed with **Yakub Khan** (Sher Ali's eldest son).

**Treaty of Gandamak — Provisions:**
- The Amir to **conduct his foreign policy with the advice of the Government of India**.
- A **permanent British resident to be stationed at Kabul**.
- The Government of India to give the Amir **all support against foreign aggression and an annual subsidy**.

- But **Yakub had to abdicate under popular pressure** and the British had to **recapture Kabul and Kandhar**.
- **Abdur Rehman** became the new Amir.
- **Lytton's plan to dismantle Afghanistan** could not be carried out.
- **Ripon abandoned this plan** and decided to keep Afghanistan as a **buffer state**.

**Post-War:**
- After the First World War and the **Russian Revolution (1917)**, the Afghans demanded full independence.
- **Habibullah** (who succeeded Abdur Rahman in **1901**) was **killed in 1919**.
- The new ruler **Amanullah declared open war** on the British.
- **Peace came in 1921** when Afghanistan recovered independence in foreign affairs.

---

## 9.6 British India and the North-West Frontier

- The conquest of **Sindh (1843)** and annexation of **Punjab (1849)** carried British boundaries beyond the Indus — bringing contact with **Baluch and Pathan tribes** (mostly independent, though the Amir of Afghanistan claimed nominal suzerainty over them).
- During **1891–92**, the **British occupation of Hunza and Nagar in Gilgit Valley** alarmed **Abdur Rahman**.
- A compromise was reached: the **Durand Line** — a boundary between Afghan and British territories.
- **Amir received some districts** and his subsidy was increased.
- But the **Durand Agreement (1893)** failed to keep peace — **tribal uprisings soon followed**.
- A **permanent British garrison was established at Chitral** and troops were posted at the **Malakand Pass** — but tribal uprisings continued till **1898**.

### Curzon's North-West Frontier Policy (1899–1905):
- Curzon followed a **policy of withdrawal and concentration**.
- British troops **withdrew from advanced posts**, replaced by **tribal levies trained and commanded by British officers**.
- He **encouraged tribals to maintain peace**.
- He created the **North-West Frontier Province (NWFP)** directly under the **Government of India** (earlier under the Lieutenant-Governor of Punjab).
- Curzon's policies resulted in a **peaceful north-west frontier** — with occasional tribal uprisings continuing afterwards.
- In **January 1932**, it was announced that the **NWFP was to be constituted as a governor's province**.
- **Since 1947, the province belongs to Pakistan.**

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **The Durand Line and Modern Afghanistan-Pakistan Relations:**
> The **Durand Line (1893)** — drawn by the British as a boundary between Afghanistan and British India — became the **Pakistan-Afghanistan border** after 1947. Afghanistan has **never officially recognised the Durand Line** as its international border, making it one of the world's most contested boundaries. The **Taliban's return to power in Afghanistan in August 2021** and subsequent tensions between the Taliban and Pakistan (including cross-border shelling and fence disputes in 2022-23) are direct consequences of this 130-year-old British decision. India's strategic interests in Afghanistan and its concerns about Pakistan-sponsored terrorism in the region all trace back to British imperial boundary-drawing. `[Modern Example]`

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **The Subsidiary Alliance Legacy in Modern International Relations:**
> The Subsidiary Alliance system — where Indian rulers paid for the British troops that effectively controlled them — finds a modern parallel in debates about **military base agreements** and **security alliances**. The USA maintains military bases in over 80 countries, often with host nations sharing costs. India's own **Defence Cooperation Agreements** — including the **BECA (Basic Exchange and Cooperation Agreement) with the USA (signed 2020)** and the **Logistics Exchange Memorandum of Agreement (LEMOA, 2016)** — raise similar questions about sovereignty and strategic autonomy that Indian rulers faced when accepting the Subsidiary Alliance. `[Modern Example]`
