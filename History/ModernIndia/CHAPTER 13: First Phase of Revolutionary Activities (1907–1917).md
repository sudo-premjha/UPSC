# 💣 First Phase of Revolutionary Activities (1907–1917)
### Unit 6 | National Movement (1905–1918) | Chapter 13

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 core ideas of this chapter.**

1. Revolutionary activities emerged as a **direct byproduct of the Swadeshi Movement's failure** — when peaceful channels were shut, young nationalists turned to armed resistance.
2. Revolutionaries followed the model of **Russian nihilists and Irish nationalists** — individual heroic acts like assassinations and dacoities, NOT mass revolution.
3. Revolutionary activity had **three major regional centres in India** — Bengal, Maharashtra, and Punjab — each with distinct leaders, organisations, and methods.
4. **Indian revolutionaries operated globally** — in London, Paris, Berlin, the USA, Canada, and even Kabul — forming international networks to challenge British rule.
5. The **Ghadr Party** (based in North America) attempted the most ambitious armed revolt in India on **February 21, 1915**, which was foiled by a traitor at the last moment.

---

## 🔷 PART 1: Why Did Revolutionary Activities Surge?

### The Immediate Context

Revolutionary heroism began as a **byproduct of the growth of militant nationalism**. The first phase (1907–1917) was a direct fallout of the **Swadeshi and Boycott Movement**. The second phase would later emerge from the **Non-Cooperation Movement**.

### The Core Reason: No Outlet for Patriotic Energy

After the open (public) phase of the Swadeshi Movement declined, the younger nationalists who had been part of it **couldn't simply walk away and disappear into the background**. They desperately looked for new ways to express their patriotic energy.

The problem was twofold:
- The **Extremist leaders**, despite calling upon youth to make sacrifices, **failed to create effective organisations** or find new political forms to channel revolutionary energies
- **Government repression** had shut down all avenues of peaceful political protest

The conclusion the youth reached: **If Britain was to be driven out, it had to be done by physical force.**

---

## 🔷 PART 2: The Revolutionary Programme — What Did They Plan?

The revolutionaries considered two major options but found them **impractical at that stage**:
1. A **violent mass revolution** throughout the country
2. **Subverting the loyalties of the army**

Instead, they chose to follow the model of **Russian nihilists** (19th-century Russian radicals who used assassination and terror against the state) or **Irish nationalists** (who used armed resistance against British rule in Ireland).

### Their Strategy Involved:

- **Organising assassinations** of unpopular British officials
- **Eliminating traitors and informers** within the revolutionary groups themselves
- **Conducting swadeshi dacoities** (politically motivated robberies) to raise funds for revolutionary activities
- During the **First World War**: Organising **military conspiracies** with the expectation of help from Britain's enemies (mainly Germany)

### The Core Idea:
> Strike **terror in the hearts of rulers** → **Arouse the people** → **Remove the fear of authority** from public minds → Inspire the idealistic youth → Finally drive the British out

### A Critical Weakness:

The **Extremist leaders failed to ideologically counter the revolutionaries** because they never clearly explained the difference between:
- A **revolution based on mass activity** (broad-based, sustainable)
- A **revolution based on individual violent activity** (heroic but isolated)

This ideological gap allowed **individualistic violent activities** to take root and grow unchecked.

## 🔷 PART 3: Revolutionary Activities in Bengal

### Early Origins (1870s–1906)

- By the **1870s**, Calcutta's student community was full of **secret societies**, but they were not very active
- The **first revolutionary groups** were organised in **1902**:
  - In **Midnapore** — under **Jnanendranath Basu**
  - In **Calcutta** — the **Anushilan Samiti** (founded by **Promotha Mitter**, including **Jatindranath Banerjee, Barindra Kumar Ghosh**, and others)
- Till **1907–08**, activities were limited to physical and moral training — they remained insignificant

### The Yugantar Weekly (1906)

- In **April 1906**, an inner circle within Anushilan — led by **Barindra Kumar Ghosh** and **Bhupendranath Dutta** — started the weekly **Yugantar**
- Several other newspapers began openly advocating revolutionary violence by **1905–06**

After severe police brutalities at the **Barisal Conference (April 1906)**, Yugantar wrote:
> *"The remedy lies with the people. The 30 crore people inhabiting India must raise their 60 crore hands to stop this curse of oppression. Force must be stopped by force."*

---

### Key Revolutionary Actions in Bengal

#### Assassination Attempt on Sir Fuller (1907)
- The Yugantar group made an **abortive (failed) attempt** on the life of **Sir Fuller** — the first Lt. Governor of the new province of Eastern Bengal and Assam (who had actually resigned on **August 20, 1906**)

#### Attempt to Derail the Lt. Governor's Train (December 1907)
- Attempts were made to derail the train carrying the **Lieutenant-Governor Sir Andrew Fraser**

#### The Muzaffarpur Bombing (1908) — A Turning Point
- **Prafulla Chaki** and **Khudiram Bose** threw a bomb at a carriage supposed to be carrying a "particularly sadistic" white judge — **Kingsford** — in **Muzaffarpur**
- Kingsford was **not in the carriage**
- **Two British ladies were accidentally killed** — a tragedy that shocked public opinion
- **Prafulla Chaki** shot himself dead to avoid capture
- **Khudiram Bose** was tried and **hanged** — becoming one of the earliest martyrs of the revolutionary movement

#### The Alipore Conspiracy Case (1908)
The entire Anushilan group was arrested, including the famous **Ghosh brothers**:
- Case also called: **Manicktolla bomb conspiracy** or **Muraripukur conspiracy**
  - (Barindra Ghosh's house was on **Muraripukur Road** in the **Manicktolla** suburb of Calcutta)
- Charge: **'Conspiracy' or 'waging war against the King'** — equivalent to high treason, punishable by death

**Key outcomes of the trial:**
- **Chittaranjan Das** defended Aurobindo
- **Aurobindo Ghosh** was **acquitted** — judge condemned the flimsy nature of evidence against him
- **Barindra Ghosh** (head of the secret society) and **Ullaskar Dutt** (bomb-maker) were given the **death penalty**, later **commuted to life imprisonment**
- During the trial, **Narendra Gosain (Goswami)**, who had turned **approver and Crown witness** (turned informant against his own group), was **shot dead in jail** by two co-accused — **Satyendranath Bose** and **Kanailal Dutta**

#### Further Assassinations (1909–1910)
- **February 1909:** The **public prosecutor was shot dead** in Calcutta
- **February 1910:** A **deputy superintendent of police** was shot dead while leaving the Calcutta High Court

#### Barrah Dacoity (1908)
- Organised by **Dacca Anushilan** under **Pulin Das** to raise funds for revolutionary activities

---

### The Bomb Attack on Viceroy Hardinge (December 1912) — Spectacular Action

- **Rashbehari (Rash Behari) Bose** and **Sachin Sanyal** organised a **spectacular bomb attack on Viceroy Hardinge**
- It happened while Hardinge was making his **official entry into the new capital of Delhi** in a procession through **Chandni Chowk**
- **Hardinge was injured, but not killed**
- Investigations led to the **Delhi Conspiracy Trial**:
  - **Basant Kumar Biswas, Amir Chand, and Avadh Behari** were convicted and **executed**
  - **Rashbehari Bose** was known as the mastermind but **evaded arrest** — reportedly by escaping in disguise

---

### Bagha Jatin and the German Plot (World War I Era)

- The **western Anushilan Samiti** found a great leader in **Jatindranath Mukherjee**, popularly known as **"Bagha Jatin"**
- It emerged as the **Jugantar (Yugantar)** party
- Jatin revitalised links between the central organisation in **Calcutta** and other areas of **Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa**

**The German Plot / Zimmerman Plan:**
During the **First World War**, the Jugantar party arranged to **import German arms and ammunition** through sympathisers abroad. Jatin asked **Rashbehari Bose** to take charge of **Upper India** with the aim of an **all-India insurrection**.

The plan included:
- Organising a **guerrilla force** for an uprising
- **Seizure of Fort William**
- **A mutiny by armed forces**
- Funds raised through **taxicab dacoities** and **boat dacoities**

**How it failed:**
- The plot was **leaked by a traitor**
- Police discovered that **Bagha Jatin** was waiting in **Balasore** for delivery of German arms
- A **gunfight** followed — revolutionaries were either killed or arrested
- **Jatindranath Mukherjee (Bagha Jatin)** was shot and **died a hero's death in Balasore on the Orissa coast** in **September 1915**

> *"We shall die to awaken the nation."*
> — **Bagha Jatin**

---

### Newspapers Advocating Revolutionary Activity
- **Sandhya** and **Yugantar** in Bengal
- **Kal** in Maharashtra

---

### Assessment of Bengal's Revolutionary Movement

✅ Revolutionary activity became the **most substantial legacy of Swadeshi Bengal**
✅ Had a major **impact on educated youth for a generation or more**

❌ **Over-emphasis on Hindu religion** kept Muslims aloof
❌ Encouraged **quixotic heroism** (reckless, impractical bravery) — no involvement of the masses
❌ **Narrow upper-caste social base** severely limited its scope
❌ **Failed to withstand the weight of State repression**

## 🔷 PART 4: Revolutionary Activities in Maharashtra

Maharashtra had an older and deeper tradition of militant activism:

### Ramosi Peasant Force (1879)
- **Vasudev Balwant Phadke** organised the **Ramosi Peasant Force** in 1879
- Aim: Rid India of the British by instigating an **armed revolt** through disruption of communication lines
- Planned to raise funds through **dacoities**
- Was **suppressed prematurely**

### Tilak's Role in the 1890s
- **Tilak** propagated militant nationalism through:
  - **Ganapati and Shivaji festivals**
  - Journals — **Kesari** and **Maharatta**
- Two of his disciples — the **Chapekar brothers, Damodar and Balkrishna** — murdered:
  - **Rand**, the **Plague Commissioner of Poona**, and
  - **Lt. Ayerst** — in **1897**

### Savarkar and Abhinav Bharat
- **Savarkar and his brother** organised **Mitra Mela** (a secret society) in **1899**
- In **1904**, Mitra Mela merged with **Abhinav Bharat** (modelled after **Mazzini's 'Young Italy'**)
- **Nasik, Poona, and Bombay** emerged as centres of **bomb manufacture**

### Killing of Collector Jackson (1909)
- In **1909**, **A.M.T. Jackson**, the **Collector of Nasik** (also a well-known indologist), was killed by **Anant Lakshman Kanhere**, a member of **Abhinav Bharat**
- Investigation revealed a **conspiracy to overthrow the British government by armed revolution**
- **38 people were arrested**
- **Savarkar** (with his two brothers) was found to be the **brain, leader, and moving spirit** of the conspiracy
- At trial, Savarkar was sentenced to **transportation for life** and **forfeiture of all his property**

## 🔷 PART 5: Revolutionary Activities in Punjab

### Causes of Punjab Extremism
Punjab extremism was fuelled by a specific combination of local grievances:
- **Frequent famines**
- Rise in **land revenue** and **irrigation tax**
- Practice of **'begar'** (forced unpaid labour) by zamindars
- Anger over events in Bengal (partition, repression)

### Key Leaders and Their Activities

| Leader | Activity |
|--------|----------|
| **Lala Lajpat Rai** | Brought out **Punjabee** (motto: self-help at any cost) |
| **Ajit Singh** (Bhagat Singh's uncle) | Organised extremist **Anjuman-i-Mohisban-i-Watan** in Lahore with journal **Bharat Mata** |
| **Aga Haidar** | Active revolutionary |
| **Syed Haider Raza** | Active revolutionary |
| **Bhai Parmanand** | Active revolutionary |
| **Lalchand 'Falak'** | Radical Urdu poet and revolutionary |

Before turning to extremism, **Ajit Singh's group** was active in urging **non-payment of revenue and water rates** among **Chenab colonists** and **Bari Doab peasants**.

### Government Crackdown (May 1907)
- Punjab extremism **died down quickly** after the government struck in **May 1907** with:
  - A **ban on political meetings**
  - **Deportation of Lajpat Rai and Ajit Singh**

### Post-Deportation Radicalisation
After deportation, several leaders developed into **full-scale revolutionaries**: Ajit Singh, **Sufi Ambaprasad**, Lalchand, **Bhai Parmanand**, **Lala Hardayal**

### Punjab and the Ghadr Revolution
During the First World War, **Rashbehari Bose** was one of the leading figures of the **Ghadr Revolution**:
- At the close of **1913**, he met **Jatin (Bagha Jatin)** to discuss possibilities of an **all-India armed rising** on the model of **1857**
- Worked in cooperation with Bagha Jatin, extending the Bengal plan to **Punjab and upper provinces**
- When the plan failed, **Rashbehari Bose escaped to Japan in 1915** — where he would later play an important role in founding the **Indian National Army (INA)**

## 🔷 PART 6: Revolutionary Activities Abroad

Indian revolutionaries went abroad for three reasons:
1. Need for **shelter** from British persecution
2. Ability to publish **revolutionary literature** free from Press Acts
3. The **quest for arms**

---

### London — India House (1905)

- **Shyamji Krishnavarma** started in London in **1905**:
  - **Indian Home Rule Society** — called **'India House'** — as a centre for Indian students
  - A **scholarship scheme** to bring radical youth from India
  - A journal called **The Indian Sociologist**
- Revolutionaries like **Savarkar** and **Hardayal** were members of India House
- **Madanlal Dhingra** (from this circle) **assassinated India Office bureaucrat Curzon-Wyllie in 1909**

> *"Neither rich nor able, a poor son like myself can offer nothing but his blood on the altar of mother's deliverance... may I be reborn of the same mother and may I redie in the same sacred cause, till my mission is done and she stands free for the good of humanity and to the glory of God."*
> — **Madanlal Dhingra**

- **London became too dangerous** after **Savarkar was extradited in 1910** and transported for life in the Nasik conspiracy case

### Continental Europe — Paris, Geneva, Berlin

New centres emerged:
- **Paris and Geneva:** **Madam Bhikaji Cama** (a Parsi revolutionary with contacts among French socialists) published **Bande Mataram** journal; **Ajit Singh** also operated from here
- **Berlin (after 1909):** When **Anglo-German relations deteriorated**, **Virendranath Chattopadhyaya** chose Berlin as his base

### The Berlin Committee for Indian Independence (1915)

- Established in **1915** by **Virendranath Chattopadhyay, Bhupendranath Dutta, Lala Hardayal**, and others — with help from the **German foreign office** under the **'Zimmerman Plan'**

**Objectives:**
- Mobilise Indian settlers abroad to send **volunteers and arms to India**
- Incite **rebellion among Indian troops** in India
- Organise an **armed invasion of British India**

**Missions sent to:**
- Baghdad, Persia, Turkey, and Kabul — to work among Indian troops and **Indian prisoners of war (POWs)** and incite anti-British feelings

**Most notable mission:**
- A mission under **Raja Mahendra Pratap Singh, Barkatullah, and Obaidullah Sindhi** went to **Kabul** to organise a **'Provisional Indian Government'** there, with the help of **Crown Prince Amanullah**

## 🔷 PART 7: The Ghadr Party — Revolution from America

### Who Were the Ghadrites?

The **Ghadr Party** was a revolutionary group organised around a **weekly newspaper called The Ghadr** (**Ghadr** = Revolt/Mutiny in Urdu).

- **Headquarters:** San Francisco
- **Branches:** Along the US coast and in the Far East

The members were mainly:
- **Ex-soldiers and peasants** who had migrated from the **Punjab to the USA and Canada** in search of better employment
- Based in cities along the **western (Pacific) coast** of USA and Canada

### Pre-Ghadr Revolutionary Activity

Earlier activists who laid the groundwork:
- **Ramdas Puri, G.D. Kumar, Taraknath Das, Sohan Singh Bhakna**
- **Lala Hardayal** — arrived in **1911**

They set up:
- **'Swadesh Sevak Home'** at **Vancouver**
- **'United India House'** at **Seattle**

Finally, in **1913**, the **Ghadr Party was formally established**.

### The Ghadr Programme

The party aimed to:
- **Organise assassinations** of officials
- **Publish revolutionary and anti-imperialist literature**
- Work among **Indian troops stationed abroad**
- **Procure arms**
- Bring about a **simultaneous revolt in all British colonies**

### The Moving Spirits of the Ghadr Party

**Lala Hardayal, Ramchandra, Bhagwan Singh, Kartar Singh Saraba, Barkatullah, Bhai Parmanand**

---

### Two Events That Triggered the Revolt Plan (1914)

Two events in **1914** encouraged the Ghadrites to act:

#### 1. The Komagata Maru Incident (1914)

- **Komagata Maru** was a ship carrying **370 passengers** — mainly **Sikh and Punjabi Muslim** would-be immigrants — from **Singapore to Vancouver**
- **Canadian authorities turned them back** after **two months of deprivation and uncertainty**
- It was believed the Canadian authorities were **influenced by the British government**
- The ship **finally anchored at Calcutta in September 1914**
- Passengers **refused to board the Punjab-bound train**
- In the conflict with police at **Budge Budge near Calcutta**, **22 persons died**

#### 2. Outbreak of the First World War (1914)

The combination of the **Komagata Maru outrage + WWI** convinced Ghadr leaders: **the time to strike had come**.

---

### The Plan for Armed Revolt

- **Kartar Singh Saraba** and **Raghubar Dayal Gupta** left for India
- **Bengal revolutionaries were contacted**
- **Rashbehari Bose and Sachin Sanyal** were asked to lead the movement
- **Political dacoities** were committed to raise funds

**An interesting social dimension:** In at least **3 out of 5 major dacoity cases** in Punjab (January–February 1915), the raiders **targeted moneylenders and their debt records** before taking cash — giving the movement a **quasi-peasant, anti-moneylender character**

**The Date Fixed: February 21, 1915** — Armed revolt planned in **Ferozepur, Lahore, and Rawalpindi garrisons**

---

### Why the Plan Failed

The plan was **foiled at the last moment due to treachery (betrayal from within)**.

**Government response — swift and brutal:**
- Used the **Defence of India Rules, 1915**
- **Rebellious regiments were disbanded**
- Leaders were **arrested and deported**
- **45 revolutionaries were hanged**
- **Rashbehari Bose fled to Japan** (from where he and **Abani Mukherji** made many efforts to send arms)
- **Sachin Sanyal** was **transported for life**

**Beyond the Ghadrites, the government also interned radical pan-Islamists:**
- **Ali brothers, Maulana Azad, Hasrat Mohani** — all interned for years

### The Defence of India Act, March 1915

The British responded to the wartime revolutionary threat with **the most intensive repression since 1857**:
- The **Defence of India Act** was passed in **March 1915** — primarily to **smash the Ghadr movement**
- **Large-scale detentions without trial**
- **Special courts** giving extremely severe sentences
- **Numerous court-martials** of army men

---

### Evaluation of the Ghadr Movement

**What it achieved:**
✅ Its achievement lay in the **realm of ideology**
✅ It preached **militant nationalism with a completely secular approach** — a major strength compared to the Hindu-centric Bengal revolutionaries

**Where it failed:**
❌ Lacked **organised and sustained leadership**
❌ **Underestimated the extent of preparation** required — organisationally, ideologically, financially, and tactically
❌ **Lala Hardayal** was perhaps **unsuited for the role of an organiser**

## 🔷 PART 8: The Singapore Mutiny (1915)

Among scattered mutinies of this period, the most notable was:

- **Date:** February 15, 1915
- **Place:** Singapore
- **Units involved:** Punjabi Muslim **5th Light Infantry** and the **36th Sikh Battalion**
- **Leaders:** **Jamadar Chisti Khan, Jamadar Abdul Gani, and Subedar Daud Khan**
- Was **crushed after a fierce battle** in which many were killed
- **37 persons were executed** and **41 transported for life**

---

## 🔷 PART 9: Decline of the Revolutionary Movement

A **temporary respite in revolutionary activity** came after the First World War because of three reasons:

1. **Release of prisoners** held under the Defence of India Rules — cooled down popular passions
2. **Atmosphere of conciliation** after **Montagu's August 1917 statement** and talk of constitutional reforms
3. **Coming of Gandhi** on the political scene with the programme of **non-violent non-cooperation** — which promised new hope to the frustrated youth

## 🌍 Modern Context

### [Modern Example 1] — The Ghadr Legacy and Diaspora Nationalism Today
The **Ghadr Party's use of the Indian diaspora** in North America to fund and organise revolution against British rule finds a striking modern parallel in debates about **diaspora influence on Indian politics**. Today, the **Indian diaspora** (over 32 million people worldwide as per Ministry of External Affairs, 2023) plays a significant role through remittances ($111 billion in 2022, making India the world's top remittance receiver per World Bank data), political lobbying, and cultural influence. The government now engages the diaspora formally through the **Pravasi Bharatiya Divas** convention and the **Pravasi Bharatiya Samman Award** — a far cry from the Ghadrites, who were hunted by the British Empire.

### [Modern Example 2] — Defence of India Act vs. UAPA (2019)
The **Defence of India Act (1915)** — passed primarily to crush the Ghadr movement and allow **detention without trial** — finds a troubling modern echo in debates around the **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), as amended in 2019**. The 2019 amendment allowed the government to designate **individuals (not just organisations) as terrorists** without trial. Critics, including the **Supreme Court** (in several bail judgments), have expressed concern about prolonged detention without trial. The **Supreme Court in Union of India vs. KA Najeeb (2021)** held that constitutional courts can grant bail even in UAPA cases if the trial is unduly delayed — asserting that **liberty cannot be indefinitely sacrificed**.

### [Modern Example 3] — Komagata Maru — Official Apology
The **Komagata Maru incident (1914)**, where 370 Sikh and Punjabi Muslim immigrants were turned back from Canada and 22 were killed at Budge Budge, remained a painful historical wound. **Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau issued a formal apology in the House of Commons in May 2016**, acknowledging that the Canadian government's actions were wrong and racist. This was a landmark moment — connecting 1914's revolutionary history to 21st-century reconciliation politics.

### [Modern Example 4] — Rashbehari Bose, the INA, and Azad Hind
**Rashbehari Bose**, who escaped to Japan in 1915 after the Ghadr plot failed, later became instrumental in founding the **Indian National Army (INA)**. The **INA's legacy** was powerfully reaffirmed when the **Modi government** in **2018** lit up the **INA Memorial at Red Fort** on the 75th anniversary of the formation of the **Azad Hind Government (October 21, 1943)** — officially recognising the contribution of the revolutionary nationalist tradition that began with the Ghadr era.

### [Modern Example 5] — Sedition Law and Revolutionary Legacy
The same **IPC Section 124A (Sedition)** used against **Tilak's Kesari** articles (which inspired the Bengal revolutionaries) was **put in abeyance by the Supreme Court in May 2022** in *S.G. Vombatkere vs Union of India*. The new **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023** has replaced it with **Section 152**, which critics argue retains similar powers under a new name — showing how the tension between state security and individual liberty that defined the 1907–1917 era has never truly been resolved.
