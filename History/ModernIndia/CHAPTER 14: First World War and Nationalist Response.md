## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 core ideas of this chapter.**

1. The **First World War (1914–19)** produced three distinct nationalist responses — Moderate support, Extremist conditional support, and Revolutionary armed resistance — reflecting deep divisions within the Indian nationalist movement.
2. The **Home Rule League Movement** (two separate leagues by **Tilak** and **Annie Besant**) marked a crucial shift from elite politics to **mass-based political mobilisation** — a bridge between the Swadeshi era and the Gandhian era.
3. The **Lucknow Session of the INC (1916)** achieved two historic breakthroughs: the **readmission of Extremists** into the Congress and the **Lucknow Pact** between the Congress and the Muslim League.
4. The **Lucknow Pact** was both a milestone and a minefield — it created Hindu-Muslim political unity but also gave the Congress's stamp of approval to **separate electorates**, inadvertently advancing the **two-nation theory**.
5. **Montagu's August Declaration of 1917** was the first time the British government officially acknowledged **self-government** as a goal — but with no timeline and British control over the pace, making it deeply unsatisfying to Indian nationalists.

## 🔷 PART 1: India and the First World War — Three Kinds of Nationalist Response

### The War's Context

In the **First World War (1914–19)**, Britain allied with **France, Russia, USA, Italy, and Japan** against **Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Turkey**.

This period saw the **maturing of Indian nationalism**. Indians did not respond to the war in a single voice — there were three distinct responses:

---

### The Three Responses

| Group | Response | Reason |
|-------|----------|--------|
| **Moderates** | Supported Britain in the war as a matter of **duty** | Believed in the imperial connection as beneficial |
| **Extremists** (including Tilak, released June 1914) | Supported the war effort | **Mistakenly believed** Britain would repay India's loyalty with self-government |
| **Revolutionaries** | Decided to use the war as an opportunity to **wage war on British rule** | Saw the war as the perfect moment to liberate India by force |

### A Critical Blind Spot

The Indian supporters of British war efforts — both Moderates and Extremists — **failed to see** that the imperialist powers were fighting the war primarily **to safeguard their own colonies and markets**. Their goodwill was never going to translate into Indian freedom.

### How Revolutionaries Saw the War as Opportunity

For revolutionaries striving for **immediate complete independence**, the war appeared as a **heaven-sent opportunity** because:
- The war was **draining India of British troops** — at one point, the number of white soldiers in India fell to only **15,000**
- There was a real **possibility of financial and military help from Germany and Turkey** — Britain's enemies
- Revolutionary activity was carried out through:
  - The **Ghadr Party** in North America
  - The **Berlin Committee** in Europe
  - Scattered **mutinies by Indian soldiers** (such as the one in Singapore)

*(Details of these revolutionary activities have been covered in Chapter 13)*

## 🔷 PART 2: The Home Rule League Movement

### What Was It?

The **Home Rule Movement** was India's response to the First World War — less dramatic than the Ghadr adventure, but ultimately **more effective and politically significant**.

Prominent leaders — **Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Annie Besant, G.S. Khaparde, Sir S. Subramania Iyer, Joseph Baptista, and Muhammad Ali Jinnah** among others — came together with a clear plan:

- Create a **national alliance** working throughout the year (unlike the Congress which only met annually)
- Main objective: **Demand self-government or home rule** for all of India within the British Commonwealth
- The model: **Irish Home Rule League**

In the end, **two separate Home Rule Leagues** were launched — one by **Tilak** and one by **Annie Besant** — both aiming to begin a **new trend of aggressive politics**.

---

### 📍 Factors That Led to the Home Rule Movement

The formation of the Home Rule Movement was driven by seven key factors:

1. A section of nationalists felt that **popular pressure** was needed to extract concessions from the government — petitions alone had failed
2. The **Moderates were disillusioned** with the Morley-Minto Reforms of 1909, which had given the appearance of reform without genuine power
3. **Wartime miseries** — high taxation and rising prices — had made the people ready to participate in any aggressive protest movement
4. The war, fought among the major imperialist powers and accompanied by vicious propaganda against each other, had **exposed the myth of white superiority**
5. **Tilak was ready to lead** after his release in **June 1914**, and had made conciliatory gestures:
   - Reassured the government of his loyalty
   - Told the Moderates he wanted **reform of administration, not overthrow of the government** (like Irish Home Rulers)
   - Admitted that acts of violence had **retarded the pace of political progress** in India
   - Urged all Indians to assist the British government in its crisis
6. **Annie Besant** — the Irish theosophist (member of a spiritual-philosophical movement) based in India since **1896** — had decided to expand her activities to build a home rule movement on the lines of **Irish Home Rule Leagues**
7. The **Russian Revolution of 1917** proved to be an added boost for the Home Rule campaign — showing that even the mightiest empires could be challenged

---

### 📍 Why Two Separate Leagues?

Both Tilak and Besant realised they needed the sanction of the Moderate-dominated Congress for maximum impact. After failing to achieve **Moderate-Extremist rapprochement (reconciliation)** at the **1914 Congress session**, they decided to act independently.

**Annie Besant's campaign (early 1915):**
- Demanded self-government for India after the war — on the lines of **white colonies**
- Campaigned through her newspapers **New India** and **Commonweal**, and through public meetings and conferences

**At the annual Congress session of 1915:**
- Extremists were readmitted to the Congress — a partial success
- Besant failed to get the Congress to formally approve the Home Rule Leagues scheme
- But the Congress committed to **educative propaganda** and revival of **local-level Congress committees**
- Besant gave the Congress a deadline — if it didn't act, she would set up her own league. It didn't, so she did.

**Why two leagues instead of one?**
As Annie Besant explained: some of Tilak's supporters were not comfortable with her, and some of her supporters were not comfortable with Tilak. To avoid friction, they set up separate leagues — but **coordinated their efforts** by dividing geographic areas and cooperating where possible.

---

---

| **Other key workers** | **B.W. Wadia** and **C.P. Ramaswamy Aiyar** |
| **Organisation style** | Loosely organised (compared to Tilak's league) |

---

### 📍 The Home Rule League Programme — How Did They Work?

The League campaign aimed to carry the **message of home rule as self-government** to the common man. It attracted **hitherto 'politically backward' regions** like **Gujarat and Sindh** — a significant geographic expansion of nationalist politics.

**Methods used:**
- Promoting **political education** and discussion through public meetings
- Organising **libraries and reading rooms** containing books on national politics
- Holding **conferences**
- Organising **political education classes for students**
- Carrying out propaganda through **newspapers, pamphlets, posters, illustrated postcards, plays, and religious songs**
- **Collecting funds** for the movement
- Organising **social work**
- Participating in **local government activities**

---

### 📍 Who Joined the Movement?

The Home Rule agitation was eventually joined by a long list of major leaders:

**Motilal Nehru, Jawaharlal Nehru, Bhulabhai Desai, Chittaranjan Das, K.M. Munshi, B. Chakravarti, Saifuddin Kitchlew, Madan Mohan Malaviya, Muhammad Ali Jinnah, Tej Bahadur Sapru, and Lala Lajpat Rai**

- Some of these leaders became **heads of local branches** of Annie Besant's League
- **Muhammad Ali Jinnah** led the **Bombay division**
- Many **disillusioned Moderate Congressmen** and some members of **Gokhale's Servants of India Society** also joined

**Who did NOT join:**
- **Anglo-Indians**
- **Most Muslims**
- **Non-Brahmins from the South**

Their concern: That Home Rule would simply mean **rule of the Hindu majority** — and specifically of the **upper castes (high caste Hindus)**.

---

### 📍 Government's Repressive Response

The government came down hard on the movement — especially in **Madras**:
- Students were **prohibited from attending political meetings**
- A case was filed against **Tilak** — but was **rescinded (overturned) by the High Court**
- Tilak was **barred from entering Punjab and Delhi**

**The turning point:** In **June 1917**, **Annie Besant** and her associates **B.P. Wadia** and **George Arundale** were arrested.

**The national reaction:**
- **Nationwide protests** erupted
- In a dramatic gesture, **Sir S. Subramania Aiyar renounced his knighthood** in protest
- **Tilak** advocated a programme of **passive resistance**

The repression backfired spectacularly. As **Secretary of State Montagu** wryly commented:
> *"Shiva ...cut his wife into fifty-two pieces only to discover that he had fifty-two wives. This is what happens to the Government of India when it interns Mrs Besant."*

**Annie Besant was released in September 1917.**

---

### 📍 Why Did the Home Rule Agitation Fade Out by 1919?

Despite its initial success, the movement had petered out by **1919**. Seven reasons:

1. **Lack of effective organisation** — the movement was never tightly structured
2. **Communal riots in 1917–18** created an atmosphere of suspicion and division
3. Moderates who had joined after Besant's arrest were **pacified by Montagu's statement of August 1917** (which promised self-government as a long-term goal) and by Besant's release
4. **Talk of passive resistance** by Extremists from September 1918 onwards kept the Moderates away from active participation
5. The **Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (announced July 1918)** further **divided nationalist ranks**; Annie Besant herself was in two minds — she **vacillated (went back and forth)** about whether to accept the reforms or oppose them
6. **Tilak had to go abroad** in **September 1918** to fight a **libel case** against **Valentine Chirol**, whose book ***Indian Unrest*** had portrayed Tilak as responsible for agitational politics in India. With Besant unable to give direction and Tilak abroad, the movement became **leaderless**
7. **Gandhi's fresh approach** to the freedom struggle was **slowly capturing the people's imagination** — the mass movement he was building pushed the Home Rule movement to the sidelines

> **Postscript:** In **1920**, Gandhi accepted the presidentship of the **All India Home Rule League** and renamed it the **Swarajya Sabha**. Within a year, it merged into the **Indian National Congress**.

---

### 📍 Positive Gains of the Home Rule Movement

Despite its short life, the movement left lasting contributions:

The Home Rule Movement was a **transition** — between the deliberative, inactive Congress politics of the earlier era and the mass-involving **Gandhian phase** that was to follow.

1. ✅ It **permanently shifted the emphasis** from the educated elite to the **masses** — deflecting the movement from the narrow Moderate path
2. ✅ It created an **organisational link between the town and the countryside** — crucial when the national movement entered its true mass phase
3. ✅ It created a **generation of ardent nationalists**
4. ✅ It **prepared the masses** for the style of Gandhian politics that was to come
5. ✅ The **Montagu August 1917 declaration** and the **Montford reforms** were directly **influenced** by the pressure of the Home Rule agitation
6. ✅ The efforts of Tilak and Annie Besant towards **Moderate-Extremist reunion at Lucknow (1916)** **revived the Congress** as an effective instrument of Indian nationalism
7. ✅ It **lent a new dimension and urgency** to the national movement

## 🔷 PART 3: The Lucknow Session of the Indian National Congress (1916)

The **Lucknow session of 1916** was one of the most consequential Congress sessions in the entire independence struggle. Two historic events happened simultaneously.

---

### Event 1: Readmission of Extremists to Congress

The session was presided over by a **Moderate — Ambika Charan Majumdar** — and it finally readmitted the **Extremists led by Tilak** back into the Congress fold (after the **Surat Split of 1907**).

**Why was reunion possible now? Four reasons:**

1. **Old controversies** of the Surat era had become **meaningless** — the political context had completely changed
2. Both sides realised the **split had led to political inactivity** — neither group had achieved much alone
3. **Annie Besant and Tilak** had made **vigorous efforts** for reunion; Tilak had allayed Moderate fears by declaring he supported reform of administration, not overthrow of the government, and by denouncing acts of violence
4. The **death of Pherozeshah Mehta** — who had led Moderate opposition to the Extremists — **removed the biggest personal obstacle** to reunion

> *"After nearly ten years of painful separation and wanderings through the wilderness of misunderstanding and mazes of unpleasant controversies...both wings of Indian Nationalist Party have come to realise the fact that united they stand, but divided they fall."*
> — **A.C. Majumdar**, President, Lucknow Session, INC, 1916

---

### Event 2: The Lucknow Pact — Congress Meets the Muslim League

The second historic event was the **coming together of the Muslim League and the Congress** — and their joint presentation of common demands to the government.

#### Why Had the Muslim League's Attitude Changed?

The Muslim League had originally been a pro-British, anti-Congress organisation. But by 1916, it was being dominated by **younger, militant nationalists** who were turning **increasingly anti-imperialist**. Why?

1. **Britain's refusal to help Turkey** (ruled by the Khalifa — the religio-political leader of all Muslims) in its wars in the **Balkans (1912–13)** and with **Italy (during 1911)** had **angered Muslims** worldwide
2. The **annulment of the partition of Bengal in 1911** had annoyed Muslim sections who had supported the partition, believing it gave them a Muslim-majority East Bengal
3. The **British refusal** to set up a university at **Aligarh** with powers to **affiliate colleges across India** had alienated educated Muslims
4. Younger League members were **outgrowing the limited political outlook** of the **Aligarh school** and turning to bolder nationalist politics. The **Calcutta session of the Muslim League (1912)** had committed the League to *"working with other groups for a system of self-government suited to India, provided it did not conflict with its basic objective of protection of interests of Indian Muslims"*
5. **Government repression during WWI** had angered young Muslims: **Maulana Azad's Al Hilal** and **Mohammad Ali's Comrade** were suppressed; leaders like the **Ali brothers, Maulana Azad**, and **Hasrat Mohani** were interned. This generated strong **anti-imperialist sentiments** among the **'Young Party'** of the League

---

### The Terms of the Lucknow Pact

**What the Muslim League agreed to:**
- Present **joint constitutional demands** with the Congress to the government

**What the Congress accepted:**
- The Muslim League's position on **separate electorates** (which would continue until any one community demanded joint electorates)
- A **fixed proportion of seats** for Muslims in the legislatures at the all-India and provincial levels

**The joint demands presented to the government:**

- Government should **declare it would confer self-government** on Indians at **an early date**
- Representative assemblies at **central and provincial levels** should be **further expanded** — with an **elected majority** and **more powers**
- The **term of the legislative council** should be **five years**
- The **salary of the Secretary of State for India** should be paid by the **British treasury** — not drawn from **Indian funds** (a major economic justice demand)
- **Half the members** of the Viceroy's and provincial governors' **executive councils** should be **Indians**

---

### Critical Assessment of the Lucknow Pact

**The structural flaw:**
- Though **half the executive was to be elected** by the legislature, the **executive as a whole was NOT responsible to the legislature**
- The legislature could not remove the elected half of the executive
- Important matters like the **budget** depended on legislative approval — making a **constitutional deadlock** (complete governmental gridlock) almost inevitable
- In essence, the Congress was demanding parliamentary forms without parliamentary responsibility — a contradiction

**The bigger picture:**
- The Lucknow Pact demands were essentially just a **significantly expanded version of the Morley-Minto reforms** — not a radical departure
- The effort to present a united Congress-League front was **far-sighted** — it generated enormous popular enthusiasm and even pushed the government towards Montagu's 1917 declaration
- But the **acceptance of separate electorates** by the Congress meant the two organisations came together **as separate political entities** — not as one unified national voice
- This was a **major landmark in the evolution of the two-nation theory** by the Muslim League
- While leaders came together, **efforts to unite the masses** of both communities were **not made**

However, the Congress's controversial acceptance of separate electorates also showed a **serious desire to allay minority fears** of being dominated by the Hindu majority.

## 🔷 PART 4: Montagu's August Declaration of 1917

### What Was It?

The **Secretary of State for India, Edwin Samuel Montagu**, made a landmark statement on **August 20, 1917** in the **British House of Commons** — known as the **August Declaration of 1917**.

The statement said:
> *"The government policy is of an increasing participation of Indians in every branch of administration and gradual development of self-governing institutions with a view to the progressive realisation of responsible government in India as an integral part of the British Empire."*

---

### Why Was This Significant?

**Before this statement:** Nationalist demands for self-government could be — and were — branded as **seditious (anti-state activity)**.

**After this statement:**
- Attainment of **self-government for Indians became an official government policy** — a 180-degree shift from Morley's 1909 statement that reforms were not intended to give self-government to India
- The term **"responsible government"** implied that rulers would be **answerable to elected representatives** — not just to the imperial government in London

**However, the reality was clear:** The British had **no intention of handing power to predominantly elected legislatures** with an Indian majority.

To work around this contradiction, the concept of **'dyarchy'** (a system of dual government — one set of subjects controlled by elected Indians, another retained by British-appointed officials) was to be evolved — introduced eventually through the **Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms**.

---

### Indian Objections to the Statement

Indian leaders had two clear objections:

1. **No specific time frame was given** — "gradual development" could mean anything from 5 years to 500 years
2. **The government alone was to decide** the nature and timing of advance towards responsible government — Indians resented that the British would unilaterally decide what was good or bad for India

## 🌍 Modern Context

### [Modern Example 1] — Home Rule to Home Affairs: The Decentralisation Parallel
The **Home Rule League's demand for self-government** and its emphasis on **linguistic states and vernacular education** finds its modern expression in India's federal structure. The **States Reorganisation Act (1956)** created linguistic states — exactly what Tilak's Home Rule League had demanded in 1916. More recently, debates around **cooperative federalism** under the **GST Council (established 2017)** and the **Finance Commission (15th Finance Commission, 2021–26)** reflect the same underlying tension between central authority and regional/local self-governance that the Home Rule Movement fought over.

### [Modern Example 2] — Separate Electorates: A Warning from History
The **Lucknow Pact's acceptance of separate electorates** is widely seen as a step toward the **Partition of India (1947)**. The Indian Constitution (via **Articles 325 and 326**) explicitly **abolished separate electorates** to prevent such divisive politics. However, debates about **proportional representation for minorities**, **reservations in local bodies**, and **delimitation of constituencies** continue to echo these 1916 debates. The **Supreme Court in Abhiram Singh vs. C.D. Commachen (2017)** ruled that candidates **cannot seek votes in the name of religion, caste, or community** — directly addressing the kind of identity-based politics that separate electorates once institutionalised.

### [Modern Example 3] — Montagu's Declaration and the Right to Self-Determination
**Montagu's August 1917 declaration** — the first British acknowledgment of self-government as a goal — was vague, non-committal, and kept entirely on British terms. This echoes modern debates about **autonomy promises** made to various groups within India. The **Bodoland Territorial Council Agreement (2020)**, the **Naga Peace Accord process**, and demands from various states for greater fiscal autonomy all reflect the same dynamic: a powerful centre making promises of self-governance while retaining ultimate control. The **Indian Constitution's Article 3** (which allows Parliament to change state boundaries without the state legislature's consent) reflects this inherited tension.

### [Modern Example 4] — Annie Besant and Women in Political Leadership
**Annie Besant** — an Irish woman who became president of the Indian National Congress in **1917** (the year of her arrest and release) — remains one of the most remarkable figures in Indian political history. She was only the **second woman** (after Sarojini Naidu in 1925) to serve as INC President. In 2023, India's **G20 Presidency** under Prime Minister Modi made **women-led development (Nari Shakti)** a central theme, reflecting a century of evolution from the days when Besant's gender made her presence in politics extraordinary.

### [Modern Example 5] — The Dyarchy Concept and Modern Concurrent List
The **dyarchy** concept — introduced as a result of the Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (which followed the 1917 Declaration) — divided subjects into two lists: those controlled by elected Indians and those retained by the British. This structural idea lives on in India's **Constitution (Seventh Schedule)** which divides powers between the **Union List, State List, and Concurrent List** — a sophisticated evolution of the same fundamental question: who governs what? Recent controversies over **Governor vs. State Government conflicts** in Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and West Bengal (2021–2024) show this question is as alive today as in 1917.
