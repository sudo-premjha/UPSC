## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways to Remember Before You Read:**
>
> 1. **From Dominion Status to Purna Swaraj:** The **Lahore Congress Session (December 1929)** marked a watershed — Congress officially adopted **complete independence** as its goal and fixed **January 26, 1930** as the first Independence Day.
> 2. **Salt as a Symbol:** Gandhi's **Dandi March (March 12 – April 6, 1930)** transformed a simple tax violation into a nationwide mass movement — salt connected the ideal of swaraj to the lived reality of the rural poor.
> 3. **Gandhi-Irwin Pact (March 5, 1931):** A landmark agreement that placed the Congress on an **equal footing with the British government** — the first time Britain negotiated with Indian leaders as equals.
> 4. **Communal Award and Poona Pact (1932):** The British attempt to give **separate electorates to the depressed classes** triggered Gandhi's fast-unto-death, leading to the **Poona Pact** — which increased reserved seats but abandoned separate electorates. Ambedkar contested this outcome throughout his life.
> 5. **Gandhi vs. Ambedkar — A Complex Relationship:** Two of India's greatest leaders agreed on the *evil* of untouchability but differed profoundly on its *causes, remedies, and the role of the caste system* — a debate whose echoes resonate in Indian politics today.

# CHAPTER 19: Civil Disobedience Movement and Round Table Conferences

---

## 🔍 Part 1: The Run-Up to Civil Disobedience Movement

### Calcutta Session of Congress (December 1928)

The **Nehru Report** was approved at the Calcutta session of the Congress in **December 1928**. However, it was not a smooth endorsement.

**The Conflict:**
- **Younger leaders** — **Jawaharlal Nehru, Subhas Chandra Bose**, and **Satyamurthy** — were deeply dissatisfied with **dominion status** (self-governing status within the British Empire) as Congress's goal.
- They demanded **purna swaraj** (complete, total independence) instead.

**The Compromise:**
- **Older leaders** like **Gandhi** and **Motilal Nehru** wanted to retain the demand for dominion status, as consensus over it had been built with great difficulty.
- They initially suggested a **two-year grace period** for the government to accept dominion status.
- Under pressure from the younger elements, this was **reduced to one year**.

**The Decision:**
If the government did not accept a constitution based on dominion status **by the end of 1929**, Congress would:
- Demand **complete independence**, AND
- Launch a **Civil Disobedience Movement** to attain it.

---

### Political Activity During 1929 — Building the Momentum

**Gandhi's Role:**
Gandhi travelled incessantly during 1929, preparing the country for direct political action:
- Telling the youth to **prepare for the fiery ordeal**.
- Helping to organise **constructive work in villages**.
- **Redressing specific grievances** (on lines of the Bardoli agitation of 1928).

**Congress Working Committee (CWC) Actions:**
- Organised a **Foreign Cloth Boycott Committee** to propagate an aggressive programme of boycotting and publicly burning foreign cloth.
- Gandhi **initiated the campaign in March 1929 in Calcutta** and was arrested.
- This was followed by **bonfires of foreign cloth** all over the country.

**Other Events That Kept the Temperature High:**

| Event | Date |
|---|---|
| **Meerut Conspiracy Case** | March 1929 |
| **Bomb explosion in Central Legislative Assembly** by Bhagat Singh and B.K. Dutt | April 1929 |
| **Minority Labour government** under Ramsay MacDonald came to power in England | May 1929 |
| **Wedgewood Benn** became Secretary of State for India | 1929 |

---

### Irwin's Declaration (October 31, 1929)

Before the Simon Commission report came out, **Lord Irwin** made a significant declaration. It was a combined effort of:
- The **Labour government** (traditionally more sympathetic to Indian aspirations than the Conservatives), AND
- A **Conservative Viceroy**.

**Purpose:** To "restore faith in the ultimate purpose of British policy."

**The Declaration** was made as an official communique in the *Indian Gazette* on **October 31, 1929**. It stated:

> *"...it is implicit in the Declaration of 1917 that the natural issue of India's constitutional progress as they contemplated is the attainment of Dominion status."*

**BUT — Critical Limitation:**
- There was **no time scale** mentioned.
- The dominion status promised would **not be available for a long time**.
- In reality, there was **nothing new or revolutionary** in the declaration.
- Irwin also promised a **Round Table Conference** after the Simon Commission submitted its report.

---

### Delhi Manifesto (November 2, 1929)

A conference of prominent national leaders responded to Irwin's Declaration with a **'Delhi Manifesto'**, setting out conditions for attending the Round Table Conference:

1. The purpose of the Round Table Conference should be **not to determine whether or when dominion status was to be reached**, but to **formulate a constitution to implement dominion status** immediately (acting like a constituent assembly — a body that drafts a constitution).
2. The **Congress should have majority representation** at the conference.
3. There should be a **general amnesty for political prisoners** and a policy of conciliation.

**Gandhi, Motilal Nehru and other leaders met Irwin in December 1929** (after the Viceroy had narrowly escaped a bomb explosion targeting his train) and asked him to confirm that the conference would draft a dominion status constitution.

**Irwin's response:** That was **not** the purpose of the conference. **He rejected the Delhi Manifesto demands.**

The stage for confrontation was now set.

## 🏛️ Part 2: Lahore Congress Session — The Declaration of War

### Why Nehru Was Chosen as President

**Jawaharlal Nehru** was nominated as president for the **Lahore session (December 1929)** — primarily due to **Gandhi's backing**.

> ⚠️ **Important Fact:** **15 out of 18 Provincial Congress Committees had *opposed* Nehru** for the presidentship!

**Two reasons why Nehru was chosen despite this opposition:**
1. The **appropriateness of the occasion** — Congress was about to adopt complete independence as its goal, and Nehru had done more than anyone to popularise that concept.
2. To **acknowledge the upsurge of youth** which had made the anti-Simon Commission campaign a huge success.

**From Nehru's Presidential Address:**

> *"We have now an open conspiracy to free this country from foreign rule and you, comrades, and all our countrymen and countrywomen are invited to join it."*

He also clarified what liberation truly meant:

> *"I must frankly confess that I am a socialist and a republican, and am no believer in kings and princes, or in the order which produces the modern kings of industry..."*

On the methods of struggle:

> *"Any great movement for liberation today must necessarily be a mass movement, and mass movements must essentially be peaceful, except in times of organised revolt..."*

---

### Major Decisions at the Lahore Session

- ✅ The **Round Table Conference was to be boycotted**.
- ✅ **Complete independence (purna swaraj) was declared** as the aim of Congress.
- ✅ The **CWC was authorised** to launch a programme of civil disobedience, including **non-payment of taxes**.
- ✅ All **members of legislatures were asked to resign their seats**.
- ✅ **January 26, 1930 was fixed as the first Independence (Swarajya) Day**, to be celebrated everywhere.

---

### December 31, 1929 — The Midnight Flag Hoisting

At **midnight on the banks of River Ravi**, the **newly adopted tricolour flag of freedom** was hoisted by **Jawaharlal Nehru** amidst slogans of **Inquilab Zindabad**.

---

### January 26, 1930 — The Independence Pledge

Public meetings were organised **all over the country** in villages and towns. The independence pledge (supposedly drafted by Gandhi) was read out in local languages and the national flag was hoisted.

**The Pledge Made These Key Points:**
- ✊ It is the **inalienable right** of Indians to have freedom.
- ✊ The British Government has **deprived India of freedom and exploited us economically, politically, culturally, and spiritually**.
- ✊ India must sever the British connection and attain **purna swaraj**.
- ✊ **Economic ruin** through high revenue, destruction of village industries, and manipulated customs/currency/exchange rates.
- ✊ **No real political powers** given — rights of free association denied, administrative talent suppressed.
- ✊ **Culturally**, the system of education has torn us from our moorings.
- ✊ **Spiritually**, compulsory disarmament has made us unmanly.
- ✊ We hold it **a crime against man and God** to submit any longer to British rule.
- ✊ We will **withdraw voluntary association from the British government** and prepare for civil disobedience through **non-payment of taxes**.
- ✊ We will carry out Congress instructions to establish purna swaraj.

> 📌 **Historical Note:** January 26 was chosen as Independence Day in 1930 to commemorate this pledge. When India became a Republic in 1950, **January 26 was chosen as Republic Day** to honour this historical significance.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Lahore Session and Purna Swaraj

### Republic Day's Connection to This Chapter
- `[Modern Example]` India celebrates **Republic Day on January 26** every year — directly because of the **first Independence Day observed on January 26, 1930** at the Lahore Congress's direction. The **75th Republic Day (January 26, 2024)** was celebrated as part of **Amrit Mahotsav** (India's 75 years of Independence). Every citizen saluting the flag on Republic Day is unknowingly continuing a ritual that began with this very chapter's events.

## 🧂 Part 3: Gandhi's Eleven Demands and the Salt Satyagraha

### Gandhi's 11 Demands (Ultimatum: January 31, 1930)

To carry forward the Lahore Congress mandate, Gandhi presented **11 demands** to the government with an ultimatum of **January 31, 1930** to accept or reject them.

**Issues of General Interest:**
1. Reduce expenditure on army and civil services by **50 per cent**.
2. Introduce **total prohibition** (ban on alcohol).
3. Carry out reforms in the **Criminal Investigation Department (CID)**.
4. Change the **Arms Act** — allowing popular control of firearms licences.
5. **Release political prisoners**.
6. Accept the **Postal Reservation Bill**.

**Specific Bourgeois Demands:**

7. Reduce the **rupee-sterling exchange ratio to 1s 4d** (protecting Indian trade interests).
8. Introduce **textile protection** (protecting Indian textile industry).
9. Reserve **coastal shipping for Indians**.

**Specific Peasant Demands:**

10. Reduce **land revenue by 50 per cent**.
11. **Abolish the salt tax** and the government's salt monopoly.

With **no positive response** from the government, the **CWC invested Gandhi with full powers** to launch the Civil Disobedience Movement at a time and place of his choice.

By **February-end**, Gandhi had decided to make **salt the central formula** for the movement.

---

### Why Salt Was Chosen

Gandhi explained it powerfully:

> *"There is no other article like salt, outside water, by taxing which the government can reach the starving millions, the sick, the maimed and the utterly helpless... it is the most inhuman poll tax the ingenuity of man can devise."*

**Why Salt Was the Perfect Symbol:**

- 🧂 Salt **linked the ideal of swaraj** to a **most concrete and universal grievance of the rural poor** — with **no socially divisive implications** (unlike a no-rent campaign which would divide landlords from tenants).
- 🧂 Salt afforded a small but **psychologically important income** — like khadi — for the poor through self-help.
- 🧂 Like khadi, it offered the **urban population the opportunity for symbolic identification** with mass suffering.

---

### 🚶 The Dandi March (March 12 – April 6, 1930)

**On March 2, 1930**, Gandhi informed the Viceroy of his plan.

**The Plan:**
- Gandhi, along with **78 members of Sabarmati Ashram**, would march from his headquarters in **Ahmedabad** through the villages of Gujarat.
- Distance: **240 miles**.
- Destination: The coast at **Dandi** — where the salt law would be violated by **collecting salt from the beach**.

Even before the march began, **thousands thronged to the ashram**.

**Gandhi's Directions for Future Action (for when he was arrested):**
- ✅ Wherever possible, **civil disobedience of the salt law** should be started.
- ✅ **Foreign liquor and cloth shops** can be picketed.
- ✅ We can **refuse to pay taxes** if we have the requisite strength.
- ✅ **Lawyers can give up practice**.
- ✅ Public can **boycott law courts** by refraining from litigation.
- ✅ **Government servants can resign** from their posts.
- ✅ All these subject to one condition — **truth and non-violence** must be faithfully adhered to.
- ✅ **Local leaders should be obeyed** after Gandhi's arrest.

**The March:**
- **Began March 12** — marking the formal launch of the Civil Disobedience Movement.
- **Gandhi broke the salt law** by picking up a lump of salt at **Dandi on April 6**.
- This was a symbol of the Indian people's resolve **not to live under British-made laws**.
- Gandhi openly asked people to **make salt from sea water in their homes**.
- The march was **extensively covered by newspapers** — both Indian and international.
- In Gujarat, **300 village officials resigned** in answer to Gandhi's appeal.

## 🌊 Part 4: Spread of the Civil Disobedience Movement Across India

### Salt Law Disobedience Spreads Nationally

Once Gandhi performed the ritual at Dandi, defiance of salt laws started all over the country.

**Key Events in the Spread:**
- **Nehru's arrest in April 1930** for salt law defiance → huge demonstrations in **Madras, Calcutta**, and **Karachi**.
- **Gandhi's arrest on May 4, 1930** (when he announced he would lead a raid on **Dharasana Salt Works**) → massive protests in **Bombay, Delhi, Calcutta**, and most fiercely in **Sholapur**.

**After Gandhi's Arrest, the CWC Sanctioned:**
- Non-payment of revenue in **ryotwari areas** (areas where farmers paid tax directly to the government).
- **No-chowkidari-tax campaign** in zamindari areas (areas with landlords).
- **Violation of forest laws** in the Central Provinces.

---

### Region-by-Region Survey of the Movement

#### 🔴 Tamil Nadu
- **C. Rajagopalachari** (Rajaji) organised a march from **Thiruchirapalli (Trichinapoly)** to **Vedaranniyam on the Tanjore (Thanjavur) coast** in April 1930 to break the salt law.
- Widespread **picketing of foreign cloth shops** followed.
- Strong **anti-liquor campaign** in interior regions of Coimbatore, Madura, Virdhanagar, etc.
- Despite Rajaji's efforts to keep the movement non-violent, violent eruptions occurred.
- Police force was used to break the Choolai Mills strike.
- **Unemployed weavers attacked liquor shops** and police pickets at Gudiyattam.
- **Peasants rioted** at Bodinayakanur in Madura due to falling prices.

#### 🔴 Malabar
- **K. Kelappan** (a Nair Congress leader famed for the Vaikom Satyagraha) organised salt marches.
- **P. Krishna Pillai** (future founder of the Kerala Communist movement) heroically defended the national flag during a police lathi-charge on **Calicut beach in November 1930**.

#### 🔴 Andhra Region
- District salt marches organised in **east and west Godavari, Krishna**, and **Guntur**.
- **Sibirams** (military-style camps) set up as headquarters of the Salt Satyagraha.
- Merchants contributed to Congress funds.
- Dominant caste **Kamma and Raju cultivators** defied repressive measures.
- However, mass support was **less than in the Non-Cooperation Movement (1921–22)**.

#### 🔴 Orissa
- Under **Gopalbandhu Choudhuri** (a Gandhian leader), salt satyagraha was effective in coastal regions of **Balasore, Cuttack**, and **Puri** districts.

#### 🔴 Assam
- Civil disobedience **failed to regain the heights of 1921–22** due to divisive issues:
  - Growing conflicts between **Assamese and Bengalis**.
  - **Hindu-Muslim tensions**.
  - Tensions from the **inflow of Muslim peasants from densely populated east Bengal**.
- However, a successful **student strike against the Cunningham Circular** (which banned students' participation in politics) was seen in **May 1930**.
- In **December 1930**, **Chandraprabha Saikiani** incited aboriginal Kachari villages to break forest laws — though this was later denied by the Assam Congress leadership.

#### 🔴 Bengal
- The Bengal Congress was divided into two factions led by **Subhas Chandra Bose** and **J.M. Sengupta**, and was involved in Calcutta Corporation elections — leading to **alienation of Calcutta bhadralok leaders** from the rural masses.
- **Communal riots** in Dacca (now Dhaka) and Kishoreganj; **little Muslim participation** in the movement.
- Despite this, Bengal provided the **largest number of arrests** and the **highest amount of violence**.
- **Midnapur, Arambagh**, and several rural pockets saw powerful movements around salt satyagraha and chaukidari tax.
- **Surya Sen's Chittagong revolt group** raided two armouries and declared a **provisional revolutionary government**.

#### 🔴 Bihar
- **Champaran and Saran** were the first two districts to start salt satyagraha.
- In landlocked Bihar, making salt on a large scale was not practicable — it was mostly a **symbolic gesture**.
- In Patna, **Nakhas Pond** was chosen to make salt under **Ambika Kant Sinha**.
- A very powerful **no-chaukidari tax agitation** replaced salt satyagraha (due to physical constraints in making salt).
- By November 1930, **foreign cloth and liquor sales dramatically declined** and **administration collapsed** in several parts like the **Barhee region of Munger**.
- The **tribal belt of Chhotanagpur (now Jharkhand)** saw lower-class militancy.
- **Bonga Majhi and Somra Majhi**, influenced by Gandhism, led a movement in **Hazaribagh** combining socio-religious reform — followers asked to give up meat, liquor, and use khadi.
- Interestingly, **Santhals reportedly took up illegal distillation of liquor under the banner of Gandhi!**
- Most big zamindars remained loyal to the government; small landlords and better-off tenants participated — but increased lower-class militancy sometimes dampened their enthusiasm.

#### 🔴 Peshawar (NWFP)
- **Khan Abdul Gaffar Khan** (also called **Badshah Khan** and **Frontier Gandhi**) had politically mobilised the Pathans through educational and social reform work.
- He had started the first **Pushto political monthly — *Pukhtoon***.
- He organised the **'Khudai Khidmatgars'** (Servants of God) — popularly known as the **'Red-Shirts'** — pledged to the freedom struggle and **non-violence**.
- On **April 23, 1930**, the arrest of Congress leaders in NWFP led to **mass demonstrations in Peshawar** — the city was virtually in the hands of crowds for **more than a week** till order was restored on May 4.
- A reign of terror and **martial law** followed.
- Here, a section of **Garhwal Rifles soldiers refused to fire on an unarmed crowd** — a remarkable act of defiance.
- This upsurge in a province with **92 per cent Muslim population** left the British government deeply nervous.

#### 🔴 Sholapur
- This industrial town of southern Maharashtra saw the **fiercest response** to Gandhi's arrest.
- **Textile workers went on strike from May 7** and, along with other residents, burnt **liquor shops, railway stations, police stations, municipal buildings, law courts** — symbols of government authority.
- Activists established a **virtual parallel government**, which could only be dislodged by **martial law after May 16**.

#### 🔴 Dharasana (May 21, 1930)
- **Sarojini Naidu, Imam Sahib**, and **Manilal (Gandhi's son)** led a raid on the **Dharasana Salt Works**.
- The unarmed, peaceful crowd was met with a **brutal lathi-charge** → **2 dead, 320 injured**.

> **Sarojini Naidu's words before the raid:**
> *"Gandhiji's body is in jail but his soul is with you. India's prestige is now in your hands. You must not use any violence under any circumstances. You will be beaten but you must not resist, you must not even raise a hand to ward off blows."*

> **Web Miller (American journalist) reporting on Dharasana:**
> *"Although everyone knew that within a few minutes he would be beaten down, and perhaps killed, I could detect no signs of wavering or fear. They marched steadily with heads up…."*

- This new form of salt satyagraha was adopted in **Wadala (Bombay), Karnataka (Sanikatta Salt Works), Andhra, Midnapore, Balasore, Puri**, and **Cuttack**.

#### 🔴 Gujarat
- Impact felt in **Anand, Borsad, Nadiad** (Kheda district), **Bardoli** (Surat district), and **Jambusar** (Bharuch district).
- A determined **no-tax movement** — including **refusal to pay land revenue**.
- Villagers crossed into neighbouring **princely states like Baroda** with their families and belongings and **camped in the open for months** to evade police repression.
- Police retaliated by **destroying property and confiscating land**.

#### 🔴 Maharashtra, Karnataka, Central Provinces
- **Defiance of forest laws** — grazing/timber restrictions violated and **illegal forest produce sold publicly**.

#### 🔴 United Provinces
- A **no-revenue campaign** was organised — zamindars were called upon to refuse to pay revenue to the government.
- A **no-rent campaign** called on tenants to resist zamindars.
- Since most zamindars were loyalists, the campaign became virtually a **no-rent campaign**.
- Activity picked up speed in **October 1930**, especially in **Agra and Rai Bareilly**.

#### 🔴 Manipur and Nagaland — Rani Gaidinliu
- These areas took a brave part in the movement.
- At the **young age of 13**, **Rani Gaidinliu** — a Naga spiritual leader who followed her cousin **Haipou Jadonang** (born in present-day Manipur) — raised the banner of revolt against foreign rule.
- She declared: *"We are free people, the white men should not rule over us."*
- She urged people **not to pay taxes or work for the British**.
- **Haipou Jadonang was caught and hanged on charges of treason in 1931**.
- A manhunt was launched for Rani Gaidinliu — she outwitted the British till **October 1932** when she was finally captured.
- She was **sentenced to life imprisonment**.
- She was finally released by order of the **Interim Government of India set up in 1946** from Tura jail.

---

### Forms of Mass Mobilisation

The movement was carried forward through creative means:
- **Prabhat pheries** (morning processions with patriotic songs)
- **Vanar senas** (youth squads — literally "monkey armies")
- **Manjari senas** (women's squads)
- **Secret patrikas** (underground pamphlets/newsletters)
- **Magic lantern shows** (early form of visual propaganda)

---

### Impact of the Agitation

1. **Imports of foreign cloth** and other items **fell**.
2. Government suffered a **loss of income** from liquor, excise, and land revenue.
3. **Elections to Legislative Assembly were largely boycotted**.

## 👥 Part 5: Who Participated — Extent of Mass Participation

### Women
- Gandhi **specially asked women to play a leading part** in the movement.
- Women became a familiar sight **picketing outside liquor shops, opium dens, and shops selling foreign cloth**.
- For Indian women, this movement was the most **liberating experience** and can truly be said to have **marked their entry into the public sphere**.

### Students and Youth
- Along with women, **students and youth played the most prominent part** in the boycott of foreign cloth and liquor.

### Muslims
- Muslim participation was **nowhere near the 1920–22 level** because of:
  - Appeals by Muslim leaders to stay away.
  - Active government encouragement to communal dissension.
- Still, some areas saw overwhelming participation:
  - **NWFP** — overwhelming Muslim participation.
  - Significant middle-class Muslim participation in **Senhatta, Tripura, Gaibandha, Bagura**, and **Noakhali**.
  - In **Dacca** — Muslim leaders, shopkeepers, lower class people, and upper class women were active.
  - **Muslim weaving community** in Bihar, Delhi, and Lucknow were also effectively mobilised.

### Merchants and Petty Traders
- Very enthusiastic supporters.
- **Traders' associations and commercial bodies** actively implemented the boycott — especially in **Tamil Nadu** and **Punjab**.

### Tribals
- Active participants in **Central Provinces, Maharashtra**, and **Karnataka**.

### Workers and Peasants
- Workers participated in **Bombay, Calcutta, Madras, Sholapur**, etc.
- Peasants were active in **United Provinces, Bihar**, and **Gujarat**.

## 🤝 Part 6: Government Response and the Gandhi-Irwin Pact

### Government's Dilemma Throughout 1930

The government's attitude was **ambivalent** — puzzled and perplexed throughout 1930. It faced a classic dilemma:
- **If force was applied** → Congress cried "repression!"
- **If little action taken** → Congress cried "victory!"

Either way, the government suffered **erosion of power**.

**But When Repression Began:**
- Ordinances banning civil liberties were freely used.
- Press was **gagged**.
- Provincial governments given freedom to **ban civil disobedience organisations**.
- The **CWC was not declared illegal till June**.
- Lathi charges and firing on crowds → several killed/wounded; thousands imprisoned.
- The **Simon Commission Report** (which had no mention of dominion status and was regressive) further upset even moderate political opinion.

### Moves Towards a Truce

- **July 1930:** Viceroy **Lord Irwin** suggested a round table conference and reiterated dominion status.
- He accepted a suggestion that **Tej Bahadur Sapru** and **M.R. Jayakar** explore peace between Congress and the government.
- **August 1930:** **Motilal and Jawaharlal Nehru** were taken to **Yerawada Jail** to meet Gandhi.

**The Nehrus and Gandhi's Three Non-Negotiable Demands:**
1. Right of **secession from Britain**.
2. Complete national government with **control over defence and finance**.
3. An **independent tribunal** to settle Britain's financial claims.

Talks broke down at this point.

---

### 🤝 Gandhi-Irwin Pact (March 5, 1931)

**January 25, 1931:** Gandhi and all CWC members were **released unconditionally**.

The CWC authorised Gandhi to initiate discussions with the Viceroy. The discussions resulted in the **Delhi Pact** — also known as the **Gandhi-Irwin Pact** — **signed in Delhi on March 5, 1931**.

> This pact **placed the Congress on an equal footing with the government** — a historic first.

**What Irwin (Government) Agreed To:**
1. Immediate **release of all political prisoners** not convicted of violence.
2. **Remission of all fines** not yet collected.
3. **Return of all lands** not yet sold to third parties.
4. **Lenient treatment** for government servants who had resigned.
5. Right to **make salt in coastal villages** for personal consumption (not for sale).
6. Right to **peaceful and non-aggressive picketing**.
7. **Withdrawal of emergency ordinances**.

**What Irwin Refused:**
1. Public enquiry into **police excesses**.
2. **Commutation of Bhagat Singh and his comrades' death sentence** to life imprisonment.

**What Gandhi (Congress) Agreed To:**
1. **Suspend the Civil Disobedience Movement**.
2. **Participate in the next Round Table Conference** on the constitutional question around three pillars:
   - **Federation**
   - **Indian responsibility**
   - **Reservations and safeguards** necessary in India's interests (defence, external affairs, minorities, financial credit, discharge of obligations).

---

## ⚖️ Part 7: Evaluation of the Civil Disobedience Movement

### Was the Gandhi-Irwin Pact a Retreat?

Gandhi's decision to suspend the Civil Disobedience Movement was **NOT a retreat**, because:

1. **Mass movements are necessarily short-lived** — they cannot be sustained indefinitely.
2. **Capacity of the masses to make sacrifices**, unlike that of full-time activists, is **limited**.
3. There were **signs of exhaustion after September 1930** — especially among shopkeepers and merchants who had participated so enthusiastically.

**Mixed Reactions:**
- **Youth were disappointed** — they had participated enthusiastically and wanted the world to end with a bang, not a whimper.
- **Peasants of Gujarat were disappointed** — their lands were not restored immediately (they were restored only during the Congress ministry's rule in the province later).
- **Many were jubilant** — the government had been made to regard the movement as significant, treat their leader as an equal, and sign a pact with him.
- **Political prisoners received a hero's welcome** on release.

---

### Civil Disobedience vs. Non-Cooperation Movement — Key Differences

| Aspect | Non-Cooperation Movement | Civil Disobedience Movement |
|---|---|---|
| **Stated Objective** | Remedying two specific wrongs + vaguely worded swaraj | **Complete independence** |
| **Methods** | Non-cooperation (boycotting, non-participation) | **Violation of law** from the very beginning |
| **Intelligentsia Participation** | High (lawyers gave up practice, students left colleges) | **Declined** in such forms |
| **Muslim Participation** | Very high | **Much lower** |
| **Labour Upsurge** | Coincided with major labour movements | **No major labour upsurge** |
| **Peasant/Business Participation** | Significant | **Massive — compensated for other declines** |
| **Imprisonments** | Significant | **About three times more** |
| **Congress Organisation** | Relatively weaker | **Organisationally stronger** |

## 🏛️ Part 8: Karachi Congress Session (March 1931)

A special session of the Congress was held at **Karachi** to endorse the Gandhi-Irwin Pact.

**Context:** Just **six days before the session** (which was held on **March 29**), **Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev**, and **Rajguru** were executed.

Throughout Gandhi's route to Karachi, he was greeted with **black flag demonstrations by the Punjab Naujawan Bharat Sabha**, protesting his failure to secure commutation of the death sentences.

### Resolutions Passed at Karachi

- ✅ While **disapproving of and dissociating itself from political violence**, the Congress **admired the 'bravery' and 'sacrifice' of the three martyrs**.
- ✅ The **Delhi Pact (Gandhi-Irwin Pact) was endorsed**.
- ✅ The goal of **purna swaraj was reiterated**.
- ✅ Two historic resolutions were adopted — making this session particularly memorable:

**Resolution on Fundamental Rights guaranteed:**
- Free speech and **free press**
- Right to **form associations**
- Right to **assemble**
- **Universal adult franchise**
- **Equal legal rights** irrespective of caste, creed, and sex
- **Neutrality of state** in religious matters
- **Free and compulsory primary education**
- **Protection to culture, language, script** of minorities and linguistic groups

**Resolution on National Economic Programme included:**
- Substantial **reduction in rent and revenue** for landholders and peasants
- **Exemption from rent** for uneconomic holdings
- **Relief from agricultural indebtedness**
- **Control of usury** (moneylending at excessive interest rates)
- Better conditions of work including:
  - A **living wage**
  - **Limited hours of work**
  - **Protection of women workers** in industry
- **Right for workers and peasants to form unions**
- **State ownership and control** of key industries, mines, and means of transport

> This was the first time the Congress spelt out what swaraj would mean for the masses: *"in order to end exploitation of masses, political freedom must include economic freedom of starving millions."*

> The **Karachi Resolution** was to remain, in essence, the **basic political and economic programme of the Congress** in later years.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Karachi Resolution's Enduring Legacy

`[Modern Example]`

### The Karachi Resolution as the DNA of India's Constitution
- The **Karachi Resolution's** demands for free speech, free press, universal franchise, equal rights irrespective of caste/creed/sex, neutrality of state in religion, and free primary education are almost directly mirrored in **Part III (Fundamental Rights)** and **Part IV (Directive Principles of State Policy)** of the **Constitution of India**.
- `[Modern Example]` The demand for **"living wage and limited hours of work"** from 1931 finds its modern expression in the ongoing debates about the **Labour Codes (2019–2020)** — specifically the **Code on Wages (2019)** which sets a national floor wage. The Supreme Court in **2023** directed the Centre to fix a national minimum wage, echoing what the Congress demanded at Karachi in 1931.
- `[Modern Example]` **Free and compulsory primary education** — guaranteed by the Karachi Resolution in 1931 — was finally made a **Fundamental Right** through **Article 21A** via the **86th Constitutional Amendment, 2002**, and operationalised through the **Right to Education (RTE) Act, 2009**.

4. The prospect of a **unilateral British Communal Award** if Indians failed to agree.

## 🔄 Part 10: Civil Disobedience Resumed (1932–1934)

**December 29, 1931:** On the failure of the Second RTC, the **CWC decided to resume the Civil Disobedience Movement**.

### Activity During the Truce Period (March–December 1931)

- **United Provinces:** Congress led a movement for **rent reduction and against summary evictions**.
- **NWFP:** Severe repression against the **Khudai Khidmatgars** and peasants agitating against brutal tax-collection.
- **Bengal:** Draconian ordinances and **mass detentions** in the name of fighting terrorism.
- **September 1931:** A **firing incident on political prisoners in Hijli Jail**.

### Changed British Attitude After Second RTC

Higher British officials drew their own lessons from the Delhi Pact — they felt it had raised Congress's prestige and undermined British authority. Three principles drove their new policy:

1. **Gandhi would not be permitted** to build up the tempo for a mass movement again.
2. The **goodwill of Congress was not required** — but the confidence of loyalists, functionaries, and government supporters was essential.
3. The **national movement would not be allowed to consolidate** in rural areas.

**January 4, 1932:** After Viceroy Willingdon refused to meet Gandhi (December 31), **Gandhi was arrested**.

### Government's "Civil Martial Law"

A series of repressive ordinances created a **virtual martial law under civilian control**:
- Congress organisations at all levels **banned**.
- **Mass arrests** of activists, leaders, and sympathisers.
- **Properties confiscated**; Gandhi ashrams occupied.
- **Repression particularly harsh on women**.
- Press gagged; **nationalist literature banned**.

### Popular Response

Despite being unprepared, the response was massive:
- In the **first four months alone**, about **80,000 satyagrahis** (mostly urban and rural poor) were jailed.
- Other forms of protest included:
  - Picketing of liquor and foreign cloth shops
  - Illegal gatherings and non-violent demonstrations
  - Celebrations of national days
  - Symbolic flag hoistings
  - Non-payment of chowkidara tax
  - Salt satyagraha and forest law violations
  - **Installation of a secret radio transmitter near Bombay**
- This phase also coincided with upsurges in two princely states — **Kashmir** and **Alwar**.

### Why the Movement Couldn't Be Sustained

1. Gandhi and other leaders had **no time to build up the tempo**.
2. The masses were **not prepared**.

**April 1934:** Gandhi decided to **withdraw the Civil Disobedience Movement**.

> Though people had been cowed down by superior force, they had **not lost political faith in the Congress** — they had won **freedom in their hearts**.

## ⚖️ Part 11: Communal Award and Poona Pact

### The Communal Award (August 16, 1932)

The **Communal Award** was announced by **British Prime Minister Ramsay MacDonald** on **August 16, 1932**.

It was based on the findings of the **Indian Franchise Committee** (also called the **Lothian Committee**).

**Main Provisions:**

- **Muslims, Europeans, Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians, depressed classes, women**, and even the **Marathas** were to get **separate electorates** (where each community votes only for its own candidates).
- Separate electorates for the **depressed classes** — for a period of **20 years**.
- In provincial legislatures, seats to be distributed on **communal basis**.
- Existing seats in provincial legislatures to be **doubled**.
- Muslims, wherever in minority, to be granted a **weightage** (extra representation).
- Except in NWFP, **3 per cent seats reserved for women** in all provinces.
- **Depressed classes to be declared a minority**.
- Depressed classes to get a **'double vote'**:
  - One through **separate electorates**
  - One through **general electorates**
- Seats allocated for **labourers, landlords, traders, and industrialists**.
- In Bombay province, **7 seats allocated for the Marathas**.

**Nationalist Reaction:**
- Seen as another manifestation of the British policy of **divide and rule**.
- Congress strongly disagreed but decided **neither to accept nor to reject** it — not wanting to change it without consent of the minorities.

### Background: Ambedkar's Position

- Dr. Ambedkar had previously (before Simon Commission) argued that the depressed classes should be treated as a **distinct, independent minority**.
- The **Bengal Depressed Classes Association** had lobbied for separate electorates with seats reserved in proportion to their population.
- But the Simon Commission had **rejected** separate electorates for depressed classes (retaining only the concept of reserved seats).
- At the **Second Round Table Conference**, Ambedkar again raised the issue of separate electorates.
- Earlier at the conference, Ambedkar had attempted to compromise with Gandhi on reserved seats in a common electorate — but **Gandhi, who had declared himself the sole representative of India's oppressed masses, rejected Ambedkar's proposal**.
- Gandhi also **attempted to strike a deal with Muslims** — promising to support their demands if they voted against separate electorates for the depressed classes.
- Despite these efforts, no consensus was reached — leading to Ramsay MacDonald's mediation and the Communal Award.

---

### Gandhi's Response to the Communal Award

Gandhi saw the Communal Award as an **attack on Indian unity and nationalism**. He argued:

- It was harmful to both **Hinduism** and to the **depressed classes**.
- It provided **no answer** to the socially degraded position of the depressed classes.
- Treating depressed classes as a **separate political entity** would undermine the abolition of untouchability.
- Separate electorates would ensure that **the untouchables remained untouchables in perpetuity**.

**Gandhi's Alternative Demand:**
- Depressed classes should be elected through **joint electorates** (and if possible, wider ones with universal franchise).
- **No objection to a larger number of reserved seats**.

**To press his demands, Gandhi went on an indefinite fast on September 20, 1932.**

---

### The Poona Pact (September 24, 1932)

Leaders of various persuasions — **B.R. Ambedkar, M.C. Rajah, Madan Mohan Malaviya** — came together to hammer out a compromise.

**Signed by B.R. Ambedkar** on behalf of the depressed classes on **September 24, 1932**.

**Key Terms:**
- **Abandoned separate electorates** for the depressed classes.
- Reserved seats for the depressed classes were **increased**:
  - In **provincial legislatures**: from **71 to 147 seats**
  - In the **Central Legislature**: to **18 per cent of the total**
- The Poona Pact was **accepted by the government as an amendment to the Communal Award**.

---

### Impact of Poona Pact on Dalits — A Critical Analysis

Despite giving certain political rights, the Poona Pact could not achieve the desired goal of emancipation of the depressed class. Key criticisms:

- ❌ Made depressed classes **political tools** that could be used by majoritarian caste Hindu organisations.
- ❌ Made the depressed classes **leaderless** — true representatives were unable to win against stooges chosen and supported by caste Hindu organisations.
- ❌ Led the depressed classes to **submit to the status quo** — politically, ideologically, and culturally — without developing independent, genuine leadership to fight the Brahminical order.
- ❌ **Subordinated the depressed classes** into the Hindu social order by denying them a separate and distinct existence.
- ❌ **Obstructed the path to an ideal society** based on equality, liberty, fraternity, and justice.
- ❌ By denying Dalits recognition as a **separate and distinct element in national life**, it **pre-empted rights and safeguards** for Dalits in the Constitution of independent India.

### Joint Electorates and Their Impact

The **Working Committee of the All India Scheduled Caste Federation** alleged that in elections held under the **Government of India Act, 1935**, the system of joint electorates:
- **Deprived scheduled castes** of the right to send true and effective representatives to legislatures.
- Gave the **Hindu majority the virtual right to nominate** scheduled caste members who were prepared to be tools of the Hindu majority.

The federation therefore demanded:
- **Restoration of separate electorates**
- **Nullification of the joint electorate and reserved seats system**

> **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar continued to denounce the Poona Pact right until 1947**, even after signing it.

## 🕊️ Part 12: Gandhi's Harijan Campaign

Determined to undo the British divide-and-rule policy, Gandhi launched a **comprehensive campaign against untouchability**.

**Timeline of Key Actions:**

| Date | Action |
|---|---|
| **September 1932** (while in jail) | Set up the **All India Anti-Untouchability League** |
| **January 1933** | Started the weekly **Harijan** |
| **August 1933** | Released from jail; shifted to **Satyagraha Ashram in Wardha** (had vowed not to return to Sabarmati until swaraj was won) |
| **November 1933 – July 1934** | Conducted a **Harijan tour of the country** — covering **20,000 km** |
| During the tour | Collected money for **Harijan Sevak Sangh** and propagated removal of untouchability in all forms |
| **May 8 and August 16, 1934** | Undertook **two fasts** to emphasise the seriousness of the issue |

**Gandhi's Core Messages During the Campaign:**
- A **damning indictment** of Hindu society for the oppression practised on Harijans.
- Called for **total eradication of untouchability** — symbolised by his plea to **throw open temples to the untouchables**.
- Stressed the need for caste Hindus to do **'penance'** for the miseries inflicted on Harijans.
- *"Hinduism dies if untouchability lives, untouchability has to die if Hinduism is to live."*
- His campaign was based on **principles of humanism and reason** — the Shastras do not sanction untouchability, and if they do, they should be **ignored as they are against human dignity**.

**Reactions:**
- **Orthodox and reactionary elements** attacked Gandhi throughout his campaign — disrupted meetings, held black flag demonstrations, accused him of attacking Hinduism. They also offered **support to the government against the Congress and Civil Disobedience Movement**.
- The government **defeated the Temple Entry Bill in August 1934**, obliging these orthodox elements.
- Orthodox Hindu opinion in Bengal was against the Poona Pact's acceptance of permanent caste Hindu minority status.

**Gandhi's Distinctions:**

Gandhi was careful to distinguish between:
- **Abolition of untouchability** vs. **abolition of the caste system** — he differed from Ambedkar here.
- He was **not against varnashram** (the system of social divisions) per se — he felt it was the distinctions of high and low *within* it that produced untouchability.
- He was **not in favour of mixing** the issue of untouchability with inter-caste marriages and inter-dining.
- He was **opposed to using compulsion** against orthodox Hindus ('sanatanis') — they were to be won over by **persuasion, reason, and appeals to their hearts**.

**Impact:**
- The campaign gradually **carried the message of nationalism to Harijans** (who were also agricultural labourers in most parts of the country).
- This led to their **increasing participation** in the national and peasant movements.

## 🔍 Part 13: Ideological Differences and Similarities Between Gandhi and Ambedkar

> *"It would thus appear that Ambedkar and Gandhi had common allergy for social evil and imperial injustice. But a fundamental difference, more apparent than real, demarcated the two minds."*
> — **Justice Krishna Iyer**

This section is one of the most intellectually rich in the chapter — crucial for UPSC Mains analysis.

### Striking Similarities in Symbolism

| Gandhi | Ambedkar |
|---|---|
| **Burning of foreign cloth** (symbol of colonial bondage) | **Burning of Manusmriti** (symbol of Brahminical oppression) |
| **A pinch of salt from the ocean** (act of political catharsis) | **A drop of water from the Mahad tank** (act of social philosophy) |

Both acts represented far more than sentiment — they were statements of philosophy and liberation.

---

### Key Differences Between Gandhi and Ambedkar

**1. How Freedom is Achieved:**
- **Gandhi:** Freedom is **never bestowed — it must be wrested** from authority by the people who desire it.
- **Ambedkar:** Expected freedom to be **bestowed by imperial rulers** through constitutional/legal mechanisms.

**2. Democracy and Parliamentary System:**
- **Ambedkar:** Advocated the **parliamentary system** for independent India; believed in **mass democracy** as it could pressure the government for advancement of the oppressed.
- **Gandhi:** Had very **little respect** for the parliamentary system — believed democracy tends to convert into **mass democracy with a propensity for domination by leaders**.

**3. Ideology and Rigidity:**
- **Ambedkar:** Had certain **rigid political and social principles**.
- **Gandhi:** Had **no rigidities of ideology** except the uncompromising notion of non-violence. Tried to put forward simple practical alternatives to liberalism, communism, and fascism.
- **Ambedkar:** Had a natural inclination for **liberal ideology** and desired **institutional frameworks and structures**.

**4. Indian Unity vs. Indian Disunity:**
- **Gandhi's politics:** Highlighted the aspect of **Indian unity** — argued India was always a nation before imperial rule, which broke its cultural unity (*Hind Swaraj*).
- **Ambedkar's politics:** Tended to highlight the aspect of **Indian disunity** — believed Indian unity was a by-product of the **legal system introduced by the imperial state**.

**5. Village vs. Urban India:**
- **Gandhi:** For Gandhi, *"Gramraj"* (village self-rule) was *"Ramraj"* (ideal governance) and real independence.
- **Ambedkar:** Believed the **status-quoist nature of Indian villages denied equality, fraternity, and liberty** — as casteism and untouchability were most dominant in rural areas. *"Gramraj"* would continue social hierarchy. He stated there was **nothing to be proud of the Indian village system**.

**6. On Caste and Untouchability:**
- **Gandhi:** Untouchability was a product of **distinctions of high and low** — not the caste system itself. Caste and varna are different; caste is perversive degeneration. If varnashram were purged of these distinctions, each caste would be complementary to the others.
- **Ambedkar:** Advocated **annihilation of the caste system** to remove untouchability. The caste system and untouchability are **manifestations of Hindu religious scriptures** — the Vedas and scriptures don't lend themselves to unified understanding and reflect strong contradictions.

**7. On Hinduism:**
- **Gandhi:** Caste system in Hinduism has **nothing to do with religious precepts** and spirituality. Endorsed freedom of religion but **never approved separation of politics and religion**.
- **Ambedkar:** In early life, wanted to reform Hinduism — but later **left Hinduism**, denouncing it as an entity that couldn't be reformed. Denounced the Vedas and Hindu scriptures. Believed in **freedom of religion, free citizenship, and separation of State and religion**.

**8. On Mechanisation:**
- **Gandhi:** Apprehensive about the **dehumanising impact of mechanisation** — held it responsible for creating and sustaining exploitative socio-economic orders.
- **Ambedkar:** Attributed the evil effect of machinery to **wrong social organisations** that gave sanctity to private property and personal gain. Believed machinery and modern civilisation were **of benefit to all** — "the slogan of a democratic society must be machinery and more machinery, civilisation and more civilisation."

**9. Sovereign Power of State:**
- **Both agreed:** Believed in **limited sovereign power of the State**. Gandhi believed least governance is the best governance; Ambedkar held that people should be the ultimate sovereign.

**10. Violence and Non-Violence:**
- **Gandhi:** An **avowed opponent of violence of any kind** — never made a distinction between violence as means vs. end.
- **Ambedkar:** Held **absolute non-violence as an end** but **relative violence as a means** (when the ends are just, means can be justified).

**11. Methods of Political Struggle:**
- **Gandhi:** To disobey the law to make the law more just — expressed through non-cooperation, hartal, satyagraha, and civil disobedience. **Little use of legal/constitutional modes** — looked to morality and conscience to remedy evil.
- **Ambedkar:** More inclined towards **observance of law and constitutionality** in the political process. Wanted to solve untouchability through **laws and constitutional methods**.

**12. Definition of Untouchability:**
- **Gandhi:** Untouchability was **one of the many problems** confronted by Indian society — treated it as a **moral stigma** to be erased by acts of atonement.
- **Ambedkar:** Untouchability was the **major problem that captured his sole attention** — made an exhaustive **historical study** of it.

**13. Communication and Mobilisation:**
- **Gandhi:** Spoke in **plain local vernacular** — accessible to the common people.
- **Ambedkar:** Spoke in **English** — more aligned with educated, institutional audiences.

**14. The "Harijan" Naming Controversy:**
- When Gandhi named the depressed classes **'Harijan'**, Ambedkar denounced it as a **clever scheme**.
- When the Depressed Classes League was renamed **Harijan Sevak Sangh**, Ambedkar **left the organisation**, claiming Gandhi's removal of untouchability was only a **platform, not a sincere programme**.

### Where They Agreed

- **Both denounced** anything that diminished the role of religion in individual or social life.
- **Both supported** social transformation through **democratic and peaceful means** — neither sought violent overthrow.
- **Both believed** in the **idea of proper education** to make individuals desire change, reform, and integration.
- **Religion as an agent of social change** was accepted by both leaders.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Communal Award, Poona Pact, and Gandhi-Ambedkar Legacy

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. Sub-Classification Within SC Reservations — A Direct Continuation
- `[Modern Example]` The **Supreme Court's landmark 7-judge Constitution Bench judgment in *State of Punjab vs. Davinder Singh* (August 2024)** held that states can sub-classify within the SC/ST categories for reservations — allowing states to prioritise the most disadvantaged among the Scheduled Castes.
- This directly continues the Gandhi-Ambedkar debate: Ambedkar always argued that the most marginalised within the depressed classes needed the most protection — a principle finally recognised in this 2024 judgment.

### 2. Ambedkar's Constitution vs. Gandhi's Vision
- `[Modern Example]` The **Constitution of India (Ambedkar's legacy)** enshrines **Articles 330–342** (reservation in legislatures for SCs/STs), **Article 17** (abolition of untouchability), and **Article 46** (DPSP for promotion of educational and economic interests of SCs/STs).
- The ongoing debates in 2023–24 about extending reservations, the **caste census demand**, and the **Economically Weaker Sections (EWS) reservation** (upheld by the Supreme Court in 2022 in *Janhit Abhiyan vs. Union of India*) are all downstream consequences of the debates that began with the Poona Pact.

### 3. Rani Gaidinliu's Legacy
- `[Modern Example]` **Rani Gaidinliu** — the young Naga leader who fought the British at age 13 — was awarded the **Padma Bhushan** in 1982. The **Rani Gaidinliu Tribal Freedom Fighters Museum** was inaugurated in 2021 in Manipur as part of the Azadi Ka Amrit Mahotsav. The **Rani Gaidinliu Zeliangrong University** in Manipur was established in 2019, honouring her legacy.

### 4. Khan Abdul Gaffar Khan — Frontier Gandhi
- `[Modern Example]` The legacy of **Khan Abdul Gaffar Khan (Frontier Gandhi)** and his non-violent **Khudai Khidmatgars** in the NWFP (now in Pakistan) resonates in contemporary discussions about non-violence as a political tool.
- In 2022, the Government of India posthumously honoured Khan Abdul Gaffar Khan's memory during various Amrit Mahotsav commemorations as one of the great unsung heroes of the freedom movement.

### 5. Salt Tax to GST — Taxation and the Common Person
- The salt tax that triggered one of history's greatest mass movements has a modern analogue in debates about **taxation on essential goods**.
- `[Modern Example]` The GST Council's decisions on taxing essential food items (pre-packaged food items were brought under 5% GST in July 2022) sparked national debate — echoing Gandhi's argument that taxing essentials is "the most inhuman poll tax the ingenuity of man can devise." The GST Council subsequently reversed or modified several such decisions following public outcry.
