## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 core ideas of this chapter.**

1. **Militant Nationalism** rose due to 9 key factors — from British repression to international events that shattered the myth of European superiority.
2. The **Partition of Bengal (1905)** was the immediate trigger for the **Swadeshi and Boycott Movement** — one of the first mass movements in Indian history.
3. The movement introduced powerful new tools: **boycott, samitis, national education, swadeshi enterprises, and passive resistance** — all later used by Gandhi.
4. The **Surat Split (1907)** divided the Congress into Moderates and Extremists, weakening the movement at a critical juncture.
5. The **Morley-Minto Reforms (1909)** introduced **separate electorates for Muslims** — a poisonous seed that contributed to the eventual Partition of India in 1947.

## 🔷 PART 1: Why Did Militant Nationalism Grow?

A **radical, militant** (aggressive, action-oriented) trend in nationalist politics started emerging in the **1890s** and took a concrete shape by **1905**. A **revolutionary wing** also developed alongside it.

There were **9 major reasons** for this growth:

---

### 1️⃣ Recognition of the True (Exploitative) Nature of British Rule

Politically conscious Indians realised the British were **not conceding any important demands**. They got disillusioned and started looking for more effective action.

The feeling grew: **only an Indian government could lead India to progress.**

The economic miseries of the 1890s added fuel to the fire:
- **Severe famines** killed **90 lakh persons between 1896 and 1900**
- **Bubonic plague** (a deadly infectious disease) affected large areas of the Deccan
- There were **large-scale riots** in the Deccan

Nationalists also noticed that the government was **taking away existing rights**, not giving new ones. Here's the timeline of repression:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1892 | Indian Councils Act — criticised as it failed to satisfy nationalists |
| 1897 | Natu brothers deported without trial; **Tilak** and others imprisoned for sedition |
| 1898 | **IPC Section 156A** added to strengthen the already repressive **IPC Section 124A** |
| 1899 | Number of Indian members in **Calcutta Corporation** was reduced |
| 1904 | **Official Secrets Act** curbed freedom of press |
| 1904 | **Indian Universities Act** gave greater government control over universities; government called universities "factories producing political revolutionaries" |

---

### 2️⃣ Growth of Confidence and Self-Respect

A growing **faith in self-effort** emerged. Leaders like **Tilak, Aurobindo Ghosh**, and **Bipin Chandra Pal** urged nationalists to rely on the character and capacities of the Indian people.

The idea gained ground: **the masses were capable of making immense sacrifices to win freedom** — they just needed to be mobilised.

---

### 3️⃣ Growth of Education

Education had a **dual effect**:
- It **increased awareness** among the masses
- The **rise in unemployment and underemployment** among the educated drew attention to the poverty and underdevelopment caused by colonial rule

This added to the **simmering discontent** among radical nationalists.

---

### 4️⃣ International Influences

Several global events **demolished the myth of European invincibility** (the false belief that Europeans/whites could never be defeated):

- **Japan's rise after 1868** as an industrial power showed that an **Asian country could progress without outside help**
- **1896:** The Italian army was defeated by **Ethiopians** (Abyssinians)
- **1899–1902:** **Boer Wars** — the British faced major reverses
- **1905:** **Japan defeated Russia** — a Western power beaten by an Asian one!

Indians were also inspired by nationalist movements worldwide — in **Ireland, Russia, Egypt, Turkey, Persia, and China**.

> *"What one Asiatic has done, others can do... if Japan can drub Russia, India can drub England with equal ease."*
> — **Karachi Chronicle, June 18, 1905**

---

### 5️⃣ Reaction to Increasing Westernisation

The new leadership felt that **excessive westernisation** was a colonial design to **submerge Indian national identity** in the British Empire.

Their intellectual and moral inspiration was deeply Indian. Key thinkers who shaped this:
- **Swami Vivekananda** — weakness is sin; self-reliance is strength
- **Bankim Chandra Chatterjee** — painted India's past in glorious colours
- **Swami Dayananda Saraswati** — political message: *"India for the Indians"*

These thinkers **exploded the myth of western superiority** by pointing to the richness of Indian civilisation.

---

### 6️⃣ Dissatisfaction with Achievements of the Moderates

The **younger elements within the Congress** were fed up with 15–20 years of Moderate politics. They mocked the Moderate methods — petitions, prayers, and protests — as the **"Three P's"** and called it **"political mendicancy"** (begging for rights like a beggar).

---

### 7️⃣ Reactionary Policies of Curzon

**Lord Curzon's seven-year rule** in India created a sharp reaction. He:
- Refused to recognise India as a nation
- Insulted Indian nationalists by calling their activities **"letting off of gas"**
- Made derogatory remarks about Indian character

His key repressive measures included:
- **Official Secrets Act**
- **Indian Universities Act**
- **Calcutta Corporation Act**
- **Partition of Bengal (1905)** — the most explosive of all

---

### 8️⃣ Existence of a Militant School of Thought

By the dawn of the 20th century, a **band of militant nationalist thinkers** had emerged:

| Region | Leaders |
|--------|---------|
| **Bengal** | Raj Narain Bose, Ashwini Kumar Dutta, **Aurobindo Ghosh**, **Bipin Chandra Pal** |
| **Maharashtra** | Vishnu Shastri Chiplunkar, **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** |
| **Punjab** | **Lala Lajpat Rai** |

**Tilak** was the most outstanding representative. The core beliefs of this school:
- Hatred for foreign rule — Indians must work out their own salvation
- **Swaraj** (self-rule) to be the goal of the national movement
- **Direct political action** required — not just petitions
- Belief in the **capacity of the masses** to challenge authority
- **Personal sacrifice** is required from every true nationalist

---

### 9️⃣ Emergence of a Trained Leadership

The new militant leaders could **channel the immense political energy** of the masses. This energy found its release in the **Swadeshi Movement** — which grew directly from the agitation against the Partition of Bengal.

---

## 🔷 PART 2: The Swadeshi and Boycott Movement

### 📍 Background: Partition of Bengal

The British government's decision to partition Bengal was **made public in December 1903**.

**The Plan:**
- **Bengal** (Western Bengal + Bihar + Orissa) with Calcutta as capital
- **Eastern Bengal and Assam** with Dacca as capital

**Official reason given:** Bengal, with a population of **78 million** (about a quarter of British India's population), was too big to administer. It was also said that Assam's development would be helped.

**Real motive (as seen by nationalists):** To **weaken Bengal**, the nerve centre of Indian nationalism.

**How partition would divide the Bengalis:**

1. **On language:** Bengalis would become a minority in Bengal itself
   - New Bengal would have **17 million Bengalis** but **37 million Hindi and Oriya speakers**

2. **On religion:** Creating a Hindu-Muslim divide
   - Western half: **Hindu majority** (42 million out of 54 million)
   - Eastern half: **Muslim majority** (18 million out of 31 million)

Curzon even tried to **woo Muslims** by arguing that Dacca would become the capital of a new Muslim-majority province. This showed the government was **using Muslim communalism** (religion-based politics) to counter the Congress.

> *"Bengal united is a power. Bengal divided will pull in several different ways... One of our main objects is to split up and thereby to weaken a solid body of opponents to our rule."*
> — **Risley**, Home Secretary to the Government of India, 1904

---

### 📍 Phase 1: Anti-Partition Campaign Under Moderates (1903–05)

**Leaders:** Surendranath Banerjea, K.K. Mitra, Prithwishchandra Ray

**Methods used:**
- Petitions to the government
- Public meetings
- Memoranda (written representations)
- Propaganda through pamphlets and newspapers — **Hitabadi, Sanjibani, Bengalee**

**Goal:** To use educated public opinion in India and England to prevent the partition.

**Result:** The government **ignored** all public opinion. It **formally announced partition in July 1905**.

---

### 📍 Key Dates of the Movement

- **August 7, 1905:** The **Boycott Resolution** was passed in a massive meeting at **Calcutta Townhall** — this was the **formal proclamation of the Swadeshi Movement**. Leaders then spread out across Bengal to propagate boycott of Manchester cloth and Liverpool salt.

- **October 16, 1905:** The day partition came into force. Observed as a **day of mourning** across Bengal:
  - People **fasted** and **bathed in the Ganga**
  - Walked barefoot in processions singing **Bande Mataram** (which spontaneously became the theme song)
  - **Rabindranath Tagore** composed ***'Amar Sonar Bangla'*** (later adopted as the **national anthem of Bangladesh**)
  - Tagore and **Ramendrasundar Trivedi** appealed to observe **Rakshabandhan** on this day — people tied rakhis as a symbol of Bengali unity

- At a major meeting on this day, **Surendranath Banerjea and Ananda Mohan Bose** addressed perhaps the **largest gathering under the nationalist banner** till then. **Rs 50,000** was raised for the movement within hours.

---

### 📍 Spread Across India

The movement quickly spread beyond Bengal:

| Region | Leader(s) |
|--------|-----------|
| Poona & Bombay | **Tilak** |
| Punjab | **Lala Lajpat Rai** and **Ajit Singh** |
| Delhi | **Syed Haider Raza** |
| Madras | **Chidambaram Pillai** |

---

### 📍 The Congress Position

The **Indian National Congress session of 1905** under **Gokhale** resolved to:
1. Condemn the partition of Bengal and Curzon's reactionary policies
2. Support the anti-partition and Swadeshi Movement

The **Extremists** (Tilak, Lajpat Rai, Bipin Chandra Pal, Aurobindo) wanted to:
- Take the movement **outside Bengal** to the whole country
- Go beyond boycott of goods to a **full-fledged political mass struggle** with the goal of **swaraj**

The **Moderates** who dominated the Congress were not willing to go that far.

**Big step at the Calcutta session (1906)** under **Dadabhai Naoroji:** Congress declared its goal as **"self-government or swaraj like the United Kingdom or the colonies of Australia or Canada"** — the word **swaraj** was used for the first time in Congress.

---

### 📍 Phase 2: The Movement Under Extremist Leadership (Post-1905)

After 1905, the Extremists acquired dominant influence over the Swadeshi Movement in Bengal. Why?

1. The Moderate-led movement had **failed to yield results**
2. The **divisive tactics** of governments of both Bengals had embittered nationalists
3. The government had adopted **suppressive measures**, including:
   - Corporal punishment (physical beating) for students
   - **Ban on public singing of Vande Mataram**
   - Restrictions on public meetings
   - Prosecution and long imprisonment of swadeshi workers
   - Arrests and deportation of leaders
   - Suppression of press freedom

---

## 🔷 PART 3: The Extremist Programme — New Forms of Struggle

Inspired by Dadabhai Naoroji's declaration at the Calcutta session (1906), the Extremists gave a call for **passive resistance** (non-violent non-cooperation) in addition to swadeshi and boycott.

Aurobindo's purpose: To **"make the administration under present conditions impossible"** by an organised refusal to do anything that helped British commerce or officialdom.

The militant nationalists put **India's independence from foreign rule at the centre of politics**.

> *"Political freedom is the lifebreath of a nation."*
> — **Aurobindo Ghosh**

---

### 🛠️ New Forms of Struggle Introduced

#### 1. Boycott of Foreign Goods
- Public burning of foreign cloth
- Boycott of foreign-made salt or sugar
- Priests refused to ritualise marriages involving foreign goods
- Washermen refused to wash foreign clothes
- ✅ **Met with great success at the popular level**

#### 2. Public Meetings and Processions
- Emerged as major tools of **mass mobilisation** and popular expression

#### 3. Corps of Volunteers / Samitis
- **Swadesh Bandhab Samiti** of **Ashwini Kumar Dutta** (in Barisal) — very popular
- In **Tirunelveli, Tamil Nadu:** **V.O. Chidambaram Pillai**, **Subramania Siva**, and lawyers formed the **Swadeshi Sangam**

These samitis:
- Gave magic lantern lectures and sang swadeshi songs
- Provided physical and moral training
- Did social work during famines and epidemics
- Set up schools and trained people in swadeshi crafts
- Ran arbitration courts

#### 4. Use of Traditional Festivals and Melas
- **Tilak's Ganapati and Shivaji festivals** became vehicles for swadeshi propaganda in western India and Bengal
- Traditional folk theatre forms were used in Bengal to spread the message

#### 5. Self-Reliance (Atma Shakti)
- Re-assertion of national dignity, honour, and confidence
- Social and economic regeneration of villages
- Campaigns against: **caste oppression, early marriage, dowry system, alcohol consumption**

#### 6. Programme of Swadeshi / National Education

The British government tried to suppress student participation — threatening to stop grants, affiliations, and scholarships to institutions dominated by nationalists. This triggered the founding of **national schools**.

Key milestones:
- **Raja Subodh Mullick** contributed **Rs 1,00,000** towards a national university in Bengal
- **August 15, 1906:** **National Council of Education** was set up
- **Bengal National College** and **Bengal Technical Institute** were established
  - First Principal of Bengal National College: **Aurobindo Ghosh**
  - First President: **Rashbehari Ghosh**
- Inspired by **Tagore's school at Shantiniketan** (set up in 1901)
- Funds were raised to send students to **Japan** for advanced learning
- **Satishchandra Mukherjee's** role was crucial:
  - His newspaper **Dawn** (since 1897) and **Dawn Society** (1902) propagated self-help
  - He founded the **Bhagabat Chatuspathi** in 1895
  - He pioneered the national education movement and became principal of Bengal National College after Aurobindo resigned
- Education was to be in the **vernacular medium** (mother tongue)

#### 7. Swadeshi / Indigenous Enterprises

The swadeshi spirit led to the establishment of:
- Swadeshi textile mills
- Soap and match factories
- Tanneries, banks, insurance companies, shops

These were based **more on patriotic zeal than on business acumen**.

Notable examples:
- **Sakharam Ganesh Deuskar** published **Desher Katha** (1904), which popularised Dadabhai Naoroji and Ranade's ideas. It was **banned by the colonial government in 1910** after selling over **15,000 copies** and inspired many swadeshi street plays and folk songs.
- **V.O. Chidambaram Pillai** launched the **Swadeshi Steam Navigation Company** at Tuticorin, directly challenging the British Indian Steam Navigation Company
- **Rabindranath's Swadeshi Bhandar** (1897)
- **Jogeshchandra Chaudhuri's Indian Stores** (1901)
- **Sarala Debi's Lakshmir Bhandar** (1903)
- **Bengal Chemicals factory** established by **Prafullachandra Ray** in 1893
- Attempts to manufacture **porcelain** in 1901 (as noted by Sumit Sarkar)

#### 8. Impact in the Cultural Sphere

The movement was a cultural renaissance:

**Literature & Music:**
- Songs by **Rabindranath Tagore, Rajnikant Sen, Dwijendralal Ray, Mukunda Das, Syed Abu Muhammad**
- **Tagore's *Amar Sonar Bangla*** — later became the **national anthem of Bangladesh**
- In Tamil Nadu, **Subramania Bharati** wrote ***Sudesha Geetham***

**Painting:**
- **Abanindranath Tagore** broke the dominance of Victorian naturalism; took inspiration from **Ajanta, Mughal, and Rajput** paintings
- **Nandalal Bose** was the first recipient of a scholarship by the **Indian Society of Oriental Art** (founded 1907)

**Science:**
- **Jagdish Chandra Bose** and **Prafullachandra Roy** pioneered original research praised worldwide

## 🔷 PART 4: Who Participated in the Movement?

### Students
- Came out in large numbers to propagate swadeshi and organise **picketing** of shops selling foreign goods
- Active in **Bengal, Maharashtra (especially Poona)**, and parts of the South — **Guntur, Madras, Salem**
- Government retaliated by threatening disaffiliation, stopping grants, disqualifying students from government jobs, and imposing fines, expulsions, and arrests

### Women
- Urban middle-class women — **traditionally home-centred** — took active part in **processions and picketing**
- From this point onwards, women would play a **significant role** in the national movement

### Muslims
- **Some Muslims participated:** Barrister Abdul Rasul, Liaqat Hussain, Guznavi, **Maulana Azad** (who even joined a revolutionary terrorist group)
- **Most upper and middle-class Muslims stayed away** or, led by **Nawab Salimullah of Dacca**, supported the partition
- The government propped up the **All-India Muslim League** on **December 30, 1905** as an anti-Congress front
- The movement's use of **Hindu festivals and goddesses** also tended to exclude Muslims

### Labour / Workers
- In **September 1905:** Over 250 Bengali clerks of the **Burn Company, Howrah**, walked out
- In **July 1906:** A strike of workers in the **East Indian Railway** led to formation of a **Railwaymen's Union**
- **1906–1908:** Frequent strikes in jute mills, at times affecting **18 out of 18 mills**
- **Subramania Siva and Chidambaram Pillai** led strikes in Tuticorin and Tirunelveli
- In **Rawalpindi:** Arsenal and railway workers struck under **Lala Lajpat Rai and Ajit Singh**
- By **summer of 1908**, labour unrest subsided under strict government action

---

## 🔷 PART 5: Annulment of the Partition

The partition of Bengal was **annulled in 1911**, mainly to curb the **menace of revolutionary terrorism**.

- The annulment was a **rude shock to the Muslim political elite**
- To offer a consolation to Muslims, the **capital was shifted to Delhi** (associated with Muslim glory), but Muslims were not fully pleased
- **Bihar and Orissa** were taken out of Bengal
- **Assam** was made a separate province

---

## 🔷 PART 6: Why Did the Swadeshi Movement Fizzle Out?

By **1908**, the open phase of the movement was almost over. Reasons:

- **Severe government repression**
- **Lack of effective organisation** or party structure — it threw up great techniques (non-cooperation, passive resistance, social reform) but couldn't give them a disciplined focus
- **Movement became leaderless** — most leaders arrested or deported by 1908; **Aurobindo Ghosh and Bipin Chandra Pal** retired from active politics
- **Internal squabbles** among leaders, magnified by the Surat Split (1907)
- The movement **aroused the people but didn't know how to tap the newly released energy**
- Largely **confined to upper and middle classes** and zamindars — failed to reach the masses, especially the **peasantry**
- **Non-cooperation and passive resistance remained mere ideas** — never fully implemented
- It is difficult to **sustain a mass-based movement at a high pitch for too long**

---

## 🔷 PART 7: Was the Swadeshi Movement a Failure or a Turning Point?

Despite its decline, the movement was a **turning point in modern Indian history** — a true **"leap forward"**:

✅ Hitherto untouched sections — **students, women, workers, zamindars, lower middle classes** — participated for the first time

✅ All **major trends of the later national movement emerged** here: conservative moderation, political extremism, revolutionary activities, incipient socialism, passive resistance, non-cooperation

✅ The richness was not just political — it extended to **art, literature, science, and industry**

✅ People learned to **take bold political positions** and participate in new forms of political work

✅ The swadeshi campaign **undermined the hegemony** (dominance) of colonial ideas and institutions

✅ The future (Gandhian) struggle would draw **heavily from the experience** gained here

## 🔷 PART 8: Moderates vs. Extremists — A Comparison

| Feature | **Moderates** | **Extremists** |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| **Social Base** | Zamindars and upper middle classes | Educated middle and lower middle classes |
| **Ideological Inspiration** | Western liberal thought and European history | Indian history, cultural heritage, Hindu traditional symbols |
| **On British Rule** | Believed in England's providential mission in India | Rejected 'providential mission theory' as an illusion |
| **On British Connection** | Believed connections with Britain were in India's interest | Believed it would perpetuate exploitation |
| **On the Crown** | Professed loyalty to the British Crown | Believed the Crown was unworthy of Indian loyalty |
| **On the Masses** | Masses not yet ready for participation | Had immense faith in the capacity of masses |
| **Goal** | Constitutional reforms and share in services | **Swaraj** as the only solution |
| **Methods** | Constitutional methods only | Did not hesitate to use extra-constitutional methods like boycott and passive resistance |
| **Patriotism** | Patriots; not a comprador (collaborating) class | Patriots who made sacrifices for the country |

---

## 🔷 PART 9: Why Did Moderate Methods Give Way to Extremist Modes?

The Moderates had **outlived their utility** by the time of the Swadeshi Movement. Their politics of **petitions and speeches had become obsolete** because:

- They failed to keep pace with time
- They failed to work among the masses, so their ideas never took root at the grassroots
- Their propaganda never reached the masses
- They were not the leaders of the Swadeshi campaign — the Extremists were

**However, the Extremists also had serious flaws:**

- Their ideology lacked consistency
- Leaders had **different interpretations of their goal**: For Tilak, swaraj meant some form of self-government; for Aurobindo, it meant **complete independence**
- They were **politically progressive but socially reactionary** (backward-looking in social matters)

**Social Reactionary tendencies of Extremists:**
- **Tilak** opposed the **Age of Consent Bill** (which would raise the marriageable age for girls from 10 to 12 years)
- His **Ganapati and Shivaji festivals**, his support to **anti-cow killing campaigns** portrayed him as a **Hindu nationalist**
- **B.C. Pal and Aurobindo** spoke of a **Hindu nation and Hindu interests**
- This **alienated many Muslims** from the movement
- Their tactics promoted an **unhealthy relationship between politics and religion** — with bitter consequences in later years

## 🔷 PART 10: The Surat Split (1907)

### Background: The Build-Up

**Benaras Session (December 1905)** under Gokhale:
- Extremists wanted to extend Boycott and Swadeshi Movement nationally and include boycott of government service, law courts, legislative councils, etc.
- Moderates wanted to restrict the movement to Bengal and oppose boycott of councils
- A mild compromise resolution was passed, averting a split temporarily

**Calcutta Session (December 1906)** under Dadabhai Naoroji:
- Extremists wanted **Tilak or Lajpat Rai** as president; Moderates proposed **Dadabhai Naoroji** — widely respected, so he was elected
- As a concession, goal was defined as **'swarajya or self-government'** like UK or colonies of Australia and Canada
- Resolution supporting swadeshi, boycott, and national education was passed
- **The word 'swaraj' was used for the first time** — but its meaning was deliberately left vague

### The Split Happens

- **Extremists** wanted the 1907 session at **Nagpur** with **Tilak or Lajpat Rai** as president and reiteration of swadeshi, boycott, and national education resolutions
- **Moderates** wanted the session at **Surat** — this was strategic: since a leader from the host province cannot be session president, and Surat was in Tilak's home province of Bombay, Tilak would be excluded
- Moderates proposed **Rashbehari Ghosh** as president and sought to **drop the resolutions** on swadeshi, boycott, and national education
- Both sides adopted **rigid positions** — no room for compromise

**Result:** The Congress split at **Surat, December 1907**. The Congress was now dominated by the Moderates, who reiterated commitment to self-government within the British Empire and to constitutional methods only.

> *"The mischief of the trial and condemnation of Tilak would be greater than if you left him alone."*
> — **John Morley**, Secretary of State for India, in a letter to Sydenham, Governor of Bombay

---

## 🔷 PART 11: Government Repression After the Split

Between **1907 and 1911**, the government brought into force **five new repressive laws**:

| Law | Year |
|-----|------|
| **Seditious Meetings Act** | 1907 |
| **Indian Newspapers (Incitement to Offences) Act** | 1908 |
| **Criminal Law Amendment Act** | 1908 |
| **Explosive Substances Act** | 1908 |
| **Indian Press Act** | 1910 |

### Trial of Tilak (1909)

Tilak was tried for **sedition** for what he had written in **1908** in his newspaper **Kesari** about a bomb thrown by Bengal revolutionaries in **Muzaffarpur**, which resulted in the **death of two innocent European women**.

Tilak's argument in Kesari: *"Rulers who exercise unrestricted power must always remember that there is also a limit to the patience of humanity... if they resort to Russian methods, then Indians too will be compelled to imitate Russian methods."*

In another article, he argued that **the real means of stopping the bombs was to grant 'Swarajya' to the people**.

**Verdict:** Guilty. Sentenced to **6 years' transportation** (exile) and a fine of **Rs 1,000**. Sent to **Mandalay (Burma) jail**.

Tilak's iconic statement in court:
> *"In spite of the verdict of the jury, I maintain that I am innocent. There are higher powers that rule the destiny of men and nations and it may be the will of providence that the cause which I represent may prosper more by my suffering than my remaining free."*

📌 In **1956**, Chief Justice of Bombay High Court **M.C. Chagla** unveiled a **marble plaque** with these words engraved outside **courtroom 46** of Bombay High Court — where Tilak was tried.

**Aftermath:**
- **Aurobindo and B.C. Pal** retired from active politics
- **Lajpat Rai** left for abroad
- The Extremists could not organise an effective alternative party
- The Moderates were left with **no popular base or support**
- After **1908**, the national movement declined for a period
- In **1914**, Tilak was released and picked up the threads of the movement

## 🔷 PART 12: The Government Strategy

The British government had always been hostile to the Congress. When the Swadeshi Movement emerged, the government adopted a **three-pronged strategy** often called **Repression-Conciliation-Suppression** or the **"Carrot and Stick"** policy (as described by Secretary of State **John Morley** as "rallying them"):

1. **Stage 1 — Mild Repression:** Repress the Extremists mildly — mainly to frighten the Moderates
2. **Stage 2 — Conciliation of Moderates:** Placate the Moderates with some concessions; drop hints that more reforms would come if they distanced from Extremists
3. **Stage 3 — Full Suppression of Extremists:** With Moderates on its side, suppress the Extremists with full force; then ignore the Moderates

> **Neither side understood the strategy:**
> - Moderates didn't realise council reforms were meant **to isolate Extremists**, not reward Moderates
> - Extremists didn't realise Moderates could act as their **front line of defence** against repression
> - **Neither side realised** that only a **broad-based nationalist movement** could succeed against a strong imperialist power

The **Surat Split** showed that the **carrot and stick policy had brought rich dividends to the British**.

## 🔷 PART 13: Morley-Minto Reforms of 1909

### Background

**October 1906:** A group of Muslim elites called the **Simla Deputation**, led by the **Agha Khan**, met **Lord Minto** (Viceroy) and demanded:
- **Separate electorates** for Muslims
- **Representation in excess** of their numerical strength, in view of the "value of their contribution to the defence of the empire"

The same group took over the **Muslim League**, which had been initially floated by **Nawab Salimullah of Dacca** along with **Nawabs Mohsin-ul-Mulk and Waqar-ul-Mulk** in **December 1906**.

**Muslim League's purpose:** Preach loyalty to the empire and keep Muslim intelligentsia away from the Congress.

Meanwhile, **Gopal Krishna Gokhale** went to England to meet Secretary of State **John Morley** to demand a **self-governing system** similar to that in other British colonies.

---

### The Reforms (Indian Councils Act of 1909)

The **Viceroy Lord Minto** and **Secretary of State John Morley** agreed that some reforms were needed to placate the Moderates and the Muslims. The result was the **Morley-Minto Reforms** = **Indian Councils Act of 1909**.

Key provisions:

- ✅ **Elective principle recognised** for the non-official membership of councils — Indians could now participate in elections to legislative councils (though on the basis of class and community)
- ⚠️ **Separate electorates for Muslims** for election to the central council — **introduced for the first time — described as the most detrimental step for India**
- ✅ **Number of elected members increased** in the Imperial Legislative Council and Provincial Legislative Councils; non-official majority introduced in provincial councils (but some non-officials were nominated, so overall elected majority was still absent)
- 📊 **Imperial Legislative Council composition** (per Sumit Sarkar): Of 69 total members — 37 were officials; of the 32 non-officials, 5 were nominated; of the 27 elected non-officials — **8 seats reserved for Muslims** (separate electorate), **4 for British capitalists**, **2 for landlords**, **13 under general electorate**
- 🗳️ **Elections were indirect** — local bodies elected an electoral college → which elected provincial legislature members → who elected central legislature members ("infiltration of legislators through a number of sieves")
- Besides separate electorates, Muslims were given **representation in excess of their population strength**; income qualification for Muslim voters was **kept lower than for Hindus**
- 📋 **Powers of legislatures enlarged:** Could now pass resolutions, ask questions and supplementaries, vote on **separate items of the budget** (though the budget as a whole could not be voted upon)
- 👤 **One Indian to be appointed to the Viceroy's Executive Council** — **Satyendra Sinha** was the first Indian appointed in 1909

---

### Evaluation of the Reforms

Lord Morley himself said the reforms were **not a step towards parliamentary or responsible government**:

> *"If it could be said that this chapter of reforms led directly or indirectly to the establishment of a parliamentary system in India, I, for one, would have nothing at all to do with it."*

**Critical Assessment:**

- The 'constitutional' reforms were aimed at **dividing nationalist ranks** by confusing the Moderates
- The **separate electorates** were a tool to check the **growth of unity** among Indians
- The government aimed at **rallying Moderates and Muslims** against the rising tide of nationalism
- Separate electorates appeased only a **small section of the Muslim elite**, not the entire community
- System of election was **too indirect**
- Parliamentary forms were introduced but **no responsibility was conceded** — leading to irresponsible criticism sometimes
- Only leaders like **Gokhale** used the opportunity constructively — demanding universal primary education, attacking repressive policies, drawing attention to plight of indentured labour and Indian workers in South Africa

> *"Political barrier was created round them, isolating them from the rest of India and reversing the unifying and amalgamating process which had been going on for centuries... like some canker which corrupted the entire system."*
> — **Jawaharlal Nehru** on separate electorates

**Bottom line:** The people had demanded **self-government**; what they were given was **'benevolent despotism'** (kind-sounding, but still absolute foreign rule). The reforms gave a **shadow rather than substance** of representation.

## 🌍 Modern Context

### [Modern Example 1] — Make in India as the New Swadeshi
The spirit of the **Swadeshi Movement** finds its modern avatar in the **Make in India initiative (2014–present)**. Just as swadeshi activists promoted indigenous enterprises over foreign goods, Make in India aims to promote domestic manufacturing and reduce import dependence. The **Atmanirbhar Bharat (Self-Reliant India) package (2020)**, announced during the COVID-19 crisis, is explicitly rooted in the same philosophy of **Atma Shakti (self-reliance)** that the Extremists championed in 1905.

### [Modern Example 2] — Boycott of Chinese Goods (2020)
Following the **Galwan Valley clash (June 2020)** between India and China, there was a massive public call to boycott Chinese goods — a direct echo of the 1905 boycott of Manchester cloth and Liverpool salt. The government also banned **59 Chinese mobile apps** including TikTok and later **118 more apps**, using the modern equivalent of the Swadeshi spirit to assert national dignity and economic sovereignty.

### [Modern Example 3] — Separate Electorates' Legacy
The **Morley-Minto separate electorates of 1909** are considered one of the foundational causes of the eventual **Partition of India in 1947**. The modern Indian Constitution (via **Article 325 and Article 326**) explicitly **abolished separate electorates** and established a **common electoral roll** for all citizens, directly rejecting the Minto legacy. The **Supreme Court** has consistently upheld this principle — most recently in cases involving political reservations.

### [Modern Example 4] — National Education Policy (NEP 2020)
The Bengal Swadeshi movement's demand for **national education in the vernacular medium** resonates powerfully with the **National Education Policy 2020**, which mandates **mother-tongue based education** up to at least Class 5 and promotes Indian knowledge systems alongside science and technology — echoing the goals of the **National Council of Education (1906)**.

### [Modern Example 5] — Sedition Law Debate
The **IPC Section 124A (Sedition Law)** — the same law used against **Tilak in 1897** and **1908** — was challenged in the Supreme Court in **2022**. The Supreme Court in **S.G. Vombatkere vs Union of India (May 2022)** put the application of the sedition law in **abeyance (suspended)** and asked the government to reconsider it. The government has since proposed replacing it in the new **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023**, though critics argue the replacement provisions are equally or more repressive.
