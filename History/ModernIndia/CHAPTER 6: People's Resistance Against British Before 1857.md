

# Chapter: People's Resistance Against British Before 1857

---

## 📋 Chapter at a Glance

> 1. **The 1857 Revolt was NOT the first resistance** — decades of uprisings by peasants, tribals, artisans, soldiers, and deposed rulers preceded it.
> 2. **People's resistance took four broad forms**: Civil Rebellions, Tribal Uprisings, Peasant Movements, and Sepoy Mutinies.
> 3. **Root causes were common**: Colonial land revenue policies, destruction of indigenous industries, exploitation by moneylenders, encroachment on tribal lands, and the alien character of British rule.
> 4. **Tribal revolts were the most frequent, militant, and violent** of all movements — driven by land alienation, forest restrictions, and intrusion of outsiders.
> 5. **All these uprisings had weaknesses**: They were localised, lacked modern weapons, had backward-looking leadership, and failed to offer a unified national alternative — but they built a **legacy of resistance** that fuelled future movements.

---

## Introduction

Most of us think of the **1857 Revolt** as the first major show of resentment against the British, represented by the rule of the **East India Company**. However, there were **many incidents before 1857** that showed a building resentment against alien rule. This resentment showed itself in several bouts of resistance by different groups across different regions of India.

---

## People's Resistance: Meaning

In the context of resistance against the British, the word **'people'** covers several sections of Indian society affected by alien rule:

- **Peasants**
- **Artisans**
- **Tribals**
- **Ruling classes** (active or dispossessed/removed from power)
- **Military personnel** (those under the Company as well as demobilised soldiers of ex-rulers)
- **Religious leaders** (Hindu and Muslim)

These groups fought for the protection of their interests — **sometimes separately, sometimes together**.

### Examples of Early Urban Movements

| Year | Place | Cause |
|------|-------|-------|
| **1810** | **Benares** | Against a **house tax** imposed by the colonial government |
| **1814** | **Surat** | Against the **salt duty** |
| **1816** | **Bareilly** | Against **police tax and municipal taxes** |

In these urban movements, people from **lower strata** (artisans, petty shopkeepers, urban poor) fought **alongside** the prosperous urban gentry.

While each section had **different grievances**, they all converged on a **common objective — to end British rule**.

### Forms of Resistance

According to **Bipan Chandra**, people's resistance took **three broad forms**:
1. **Civil Rebellions**
2. **Tribal Uprisings**
3. **Peasant Movements**

A fourth form — **Military Revolts** (involving Indians in the Company's forces) — is also considered to make the study more comprehensive.

---

## Genesis of People's Resistance

In **pre-colonial India**, protests against rulers were not uncommon. High land revenue demands, corrupt practices, and the harsh attitude of officials were common instigating factors.

However, the establishment of **colonial rule** had a far more **annihilative** (completely destructive) effect on Indians:

- There was **no one to hear their grievances** or address their problems.
- The Company was merely interested in **extracting revenue**.
- Colonial law and judiciary **safeguarded the interests** of the government and its collaborators — the **landlords, merchants, and moneylenders**.

Left with no options, people chose to **take up arms and defend themselves**.

The **tribal people** faced similar conditions, but the **encroachment by outsiders into their independent tribal polity** (self-governing tribal systems) made them even more aggrieved and violent.

---

## Causative Factors for People's Uprisings

The major factors responsible for people's resentment and uprisings against the Company rule:

- **Colonial land revenue settlements**, heavy burden of new taxes, **eviction of peasants** from their lands, and **encroachments on tribal lands**.
- **Exploitation in rural society** coupled with the growth of **intermediary revenue collectors**, tenants, and **moneylenders**.
- **Expansion of revenue administration over tribal lands**, leading to the loss of tribal people's hold over agricultural and forest land.
- **Promotion of British manufactured goods**, heavy duties on Indian industries (especially export duties), leading to the **devastation of Indian handloom and handicraft industries**.
- **Destruction of indigenous industry** leading to **migration of workers from industry to agriculture**, increasing pressure on land.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The exploitation of peasants by intermediaries and moneylenders that drove colonial-era uprisings finds echoes in modern India's agrarian distress. The **Farmers' Protests of 2020–21** against the three farm laws were driven by similar fears of intermediary exploitation and loss of land rights. The government's **PM-KISAN Scheme (2019)** — providing ₹6,000 annually to small farmers — and the **Debt Relief Schemes** by various state governments attempt to address the same root issues of rural indebtedness. The **Forest Rights Act, 2006** (Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers Act) was a landmark attempt to correct the historical injustice of tribal land alienation, a cause that drove dozens of tribal revolts under colonial rule. In **2023**, the Supreme Court in cases related to FRA implementation continued to affirm the importance of recognising tribal land rights.

## Civil Uprisings

### What Does 'Civil' Mean Here?

The word **'civil'** here covers everything not related to defence/military. These uprisings were generally led by:

- **Deposed native rulers** or their descendants
- **Former zamindars and landlords**
- **Poligars** (in South India, holders of territory or **palayam** — a group of villages granted to them by rulers, mainly the **Nayakas**, in return for military service and tribute)
- **Ex-retainers and officials** of conquered kingdoms
- **Religious leaders**

**Mass support** came from rack-rented peasants (peasants paying extremely high rent), unemployed artisans, and demobilised soldiers. However, at the **centre** of these uprisings were **erstwhile power-wielding classes**.

### Major Causes of Civil Uprisings

- **Rapid changes** in economy, administration, and land revenue system under Company rule that went against the people.
- **Zamindars and poligars** who had lost control over their land and revenues had **personal scores to settle** with the new rulers.
- The **ego of traditional zamindars and poligars** was hurt by being sidelined by government officials and a new class of **merchants and moneylenders**.
- The **ruin of Indian handicraft industries** due to colonial policies impoverished millions of artisans. Their misery was worsened by the **disappearance of their traditional patrons** — princes, chieftains, and zamindars.
- The **priestly classes** (preachers, priests, pundits, maulvis) instigated hatred against alien rule because they had been **dependent on the traditional landed and bureaucratic elite**. The fall of zamindars directly affected them.
- The **foreign character** of the British rulers, who always remained **alien to this land**, and their **contemptuous treatment** of native people hurt the pride of Indians.

### General Characteristics of Civil Uprisings

- These uprisings represented **common conditions**, though separated in time and place.
- The **semi-feudal leaders** were **backward-looking and traditional** in outlook.
- Their basic objective was to **restore earlier forms of rule** and social relations.
- These uprisings were the result of **local causes and grievances** and were also **localised in their consequences**.

## Important Civil Uprisings

### Sanyasi Revolt (1763–1800)

- The disastrous **famine of 1770** and the harsh economic order of the British compelled a group of **sanyasis in Eastern India** to fight the British yoke.
- Originally **peasants** (some even evicted from land), these sanyasis were joined by **dispossessed small zamindars**, **disbanded soldiers**, and the **rural poor**.
- They **raided Company factories and treasuries** and fought the Company's forces.
- Only after prolonged action could **Warren Hastings** subdue the sanyasis.
- **Equal participation of Hindus and Muslims** characterised the uprisings, sometimes referred to as the **Fakir Rebellion**.
- **Important leaders**: **Majnum Shah** (or Majnu Shah), **Chirag Ali**, **Musa Shah**, **Bhawani Pathak**, and **Debi Chaudhurani**.
- **Debi Chaudhurani's** participation recognises the **women's role** in early resistances against the British.
- **Anandamath**, a semi-historical novel by **Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay**, is based on the Sanyasi Revolt.
- Bankim Chandra also wrote a novel, **Devi Chaudhurani**, as he saw the importance of women taking up the struggle against alien rule.

### Revolt in Midnapore and Dhalbhum (1766–74)

- The English took hold of **Midnapore in 1760**. At that time, about **3,000 zamindars and talukdars** had cordial relations with their ryots (cultivators).
- This harmonious scenario changed after the **new land revenue system** was introduced by the English in **1772**.
- According to British governor **Vansittart**, the zamindars of Midnapore **sided with the ryots** in conflicts between ryots and English revenue-collecting officials.
- Zamindars of **Dhalbhum, Manbhum, Raipur, Panchet, Jhatibuni, Karnagarh, and Bagri** — living in the vast tract of **Jungle Mahals** of west and north-west Midnapore — were ultimately **dispossessed of their zamindaries by the 1800s**.
- **Important leaders**: **Damodar Singh** and **Jagannath Dhal**.

### Revolt of Moamarias (1769–99)

- The revolt of the **Moamarias** in **1769** was a potent challenge to the **Ahom kings of Assam**.
- The Moamarias were **low-caste peasants** who followed the teachings of **Aniruddhadeva (1553–1624)**. Their rise was similar to other low-caste groups in North India.
- Their revolts **weakened the Ahoms** and opened doors for others to attack the region.
- In **1792**, the **King of Darrang (Krishnanarayan)**, assisted by his band of **burkandazes** (demobilised soldiers of Muslim armies and zamindars), revolted.
- To crush these revolts, the Ahom ruler had to **request British help**.
- The Moamarias made **Bhatiapar** their headquarters. **Rangpur and Jorhat** were the most affected regions.
- Although the Ahom kingdom survived the rebellion, the weakened kingdom fell to a **Burmese invasion** and finally came under British rule.

### Civil Uprisings in Gorakhpur, Basti, and Bahraich (1781)

- **Warren Hastings**, to meet **war expenses** against the Marathas and Mysore, planned to earn money by involving English officers as **izaradars** (revenue farmers) in **Awadh**.
- He involved **Major Alexander Hannay** as an izaradar in **1778**.
- Hannay secured the izara of **Gorakhpur and Bahraich** for **22 lakh rupees** for one year — a secret experiment by the Company to see how much surplus money was accessible.
- Hannay's **oppression and excessive revenue demands** caused panic in a region that had been flourishing under the Nawab.
- **Zamindars and cultivators** rose against the unbearable exactions in **1781**.
- Within weeks, **all of Hannay's subordinates were either killed or besieged** by zamindari guerilla forces.
- Although the rebellion was suppressed, **Hannay was dismissed** and his izara forcibly removed.

### Revolt of Raja of Vizianagaram (1794)

- In **1758**, a treaty was made between the English and **Ananda Gajapatiraju**, the ruler of Vizianagaram, to jointly oust the French from the **Northern Circars**.
- The mission was successful, but the English **went back on their word** to honour the treaty terms.
- The Company demanded a tribute of **three lakh rupees** from **Vizayaramaraju**, the Raja of Vizianagaram, and asked him to **disband his troops**.
- The raja, supported by his subjects, **rose up in revolt**.
- The English captured the raja in **1793** and ordered him to go into exile with a pension. **The raja refused**.
- The raja **died in a battle at Padmanabham** (in modern **Visakhapatnam district, Andhra Pradesh**) in **1794**.
- Vizianagaram came under Company rule. The Company later offered the estate to the deceased raja's son and reduced the demand.

### Revolt of Dhundia in Bednur (1799–1800)

- After the **conquest of Mysore in 1799**, the English confronted many native leaders.
- **Dhundia Wagh**, a local Maratha leader converted to Islam by **Tipu Sultan** and imprisoned for his misadventures, got released with the **fall of Seringapatam**.
- Dhundia organised an **anti-British force** and carved out a small territory.
- A defeat by the English in **August 1799** forced him to take refuge in Maratha territory, where he instigated disappointed princes to fight the English.
- In **September 1800**, he was **killed** while fighting British forces under **Wellesley**.
- Though he failed, **Dhundia became a venerated leader of the masses**.

### Resistance of Kerala Varma Pazhassi Raja (1797; 1800–05)

- **Kerala Varma Pazhassi Raja**, popularly known as **Kerala Simham** (Lion of Kerala) or **'Pyche Raja'**, was the de facto head of **Kottayam (Cotiote)** in the **Malabar region**.
- Apart from resisting **Hyder Ali** and **Tipu Sultan**, Kerala Varma fought the British between **1793 and 1805**.
- The **Third Anglo-Mysore War (1790–92)** extended English paramountcy over Kottayam, violating an earlier **agreement of 1790** that recognised Kottayam's independence.
- The English appointed **Vira Varma**, the uncle of Pazhassi Raja, as the Raja of Kottayam.
- To meet the Company's revenue target, the new raja levied **exorbitant tax rates** on peasants.
- This led to **mass resistance** under Pazhassi Raja's leadership in **1793**.
- Pazhassi Raja used **guerilla warfare**, and a **peace treaty** was made in **1797**.
- A conflict over **Wayanad** in **1800** started an insurgent warfare.
- Pazhassi Raja organised a large force of **Nairs**, supplemented by **Mappilas** and **Pathans** (demobilised soldiers of Tipu who became unemployed after Tipu's death).
- In **November 1805**, the Kerala Simham **died in a gunfight at Mavila Todu** near the present-day Kerala-Karnataka border.

### Civil Rebellion in Awadh (1799)

- **Wazir Ali Khan**, the fourth Nawab of Awadh, ascended the throne with British help in **September 1797**.
- His relations with the British soured, and he was replaced by his uncle, **Saadat Ali Khan II**.
- Wazir Ali Khan was granted a pension in **Benares**.
- In **January 1799**, he killed **George Frederick Cherry**, a British resident who had invited him to lunch.
- His guards killed **two other Europeans** and attacked the **Magistrate of Benares**.
- The incident became famous as the **Massacre of Benares**.
- Wazir Ali assembled an army of several thousand men, defeated by **General Erskine**.
- He fled to **Butwal** and was granted asylum by the **ruler of Jaipur**.
- **Arthur Wellesley** requested the Raja of Jaipur to extradite him.
- After surrender in **December 1799**, he was placed in confinement at **Fort William, Calcutta**.
- The condition of extradition was that he would **neither be hanged nor put in fetters** (chains).

### Uprisings in Ganjam and Gumsur (1800, 1835–37)

- In the **Northern Circars**, Ganjam and adjoining regions rose in revolt.
- **Strikara Bhanj**, a zamindar of **Gumsur** in Ganjam district, refused to pay revenues in **1797** and openly rebelled in **1800**.
- **Snodgrass**, an oppressive and corrupt collector, was replaced to suppress the insurrection.
- Strikara was joined by **Jlani Deo of Vizianagar (Poddakimedi)** and **Jagannath Deo of Pratapgiri (Chinakimedi)**.
- In **1804**, Jagannath Deo was captured and sent to **Masulipatnam**.
- The English had to assign certain districts to Strikara Bhanj.
- In **1807–08**, **Dhananjaya Bhanj** (son of Strikara) forced his father to leave the estate and rebelled, but surrendered in **June 1815**.
- Strikara managed the estate between **1819 and 1830** but, failing to liquidate arrears, retired in favour of his son Dhananjay.
- Unable to pay enormous arrears, **Dhananjay Bhanj rose in rebellion** for the second time when British forces occupied **Gumsur and Kolaida** in **November 1835**.
- Dhananjay died in **December 1835** and followers continued the resistance.
- The government appointed **Russell** with full discretionary powers.
- The struggle lasted till **February 1837**, when **Doora Bisayi**, a formidable leader, was arrested.
- The zamindari of Gumsur was **forfeited**.

### Uprisings in Palamau (1800–02)

- The political situation of **Palamau** was complicated by crises of agrarian landlordism and the feudal system.
- In **1800**, **Bhukhan Singh**, a **Chero chief**, rose in rebellion.
- **Colonel Jones** camped for two years in Palamau and Sarguja to suppress the rebellion.
- Bhukhan Singh died in **1802**, and the insurrection calmed down.

### Poligars' Revolt (1795–1805)

The **poligars** (or **palayakkarargal**) of South India gave a stiff resistance to the British between **1795 and 1805**.

**Main centres**: **Tinneveli** (or Thirunelveli), **Ramanathapuram**, **Sivaganga**, **Sivagiri**, **Madurai**, and **North Arcot**.

**Background:**
- The problem started in **1781** when the **Nawab of Arcot** gave management and control of **Tinneveli and the Carnatic Provinces** to the East India Company.
- This caused resentment among the poligars who had long considered themselves as **independent sovereign authorities** within their territories.

**First Phase (1795–1799):**
- The first revolt was over **taxation** but had a larger political dimension — the English treated the poligars as enemies.
- **Kattabomman Nayakan**, the poligar of **Panjalankurichi**, led the insurrection between **1795 and 1799**.
- After a fierce battle in which Company forces were defeated by **Veerapandiya Kattabomman**, a **price was put on his head**.
- With reinforcements, the Company defeated Kattabomman, who fled to **Pudukottai forests**.
- **Ettappan**, the Raja of Pudukottai, betrayed Kattabomman by entering into an agreement with the British.
- **Kattabomman was hanged** in a conspicuous place.
- **Subramania Pillai** was also hanged and **Soundara Pandian** was brutally killed.
- The palayam of Panjalankurichi and estates of **five other poligars** were confiscated.

**Second Phase (1801–1805):**
- More violent than the first. In **February 1801**, poligars imprisoned in **Palamcotta** fort escaped.
- Rebels took control of many forts and even captured **Tuticorin**.
- **Oomathurai** (brother of Kattabomman), fleeing to **Sivaganga in Ramnad**, joined the rebellion of the **'Marudus'** led by **Marathu Pandian** — suppressed in **October 1801**.
- The fort of Panjalankurichi was **razed to the ground** and its name was **expunged from all district documents**.
- The nawab surrendered **civil and military administration** of all Carnatic territories to the Company **in perpetuity**.

**North Arcot Phase (1803–1805):**
- Poligars of North Arcot rebelled when deprived of their right to collect **kaval fees**.
- **Kaval** (or 'watch') was an **ancient institution of Tamil Nadu** — a hereditary village police office with specified rights and responsibilities.
- The region was lawless, particularly in the palayams of **Chittur** and **Chandragiri**.
- The poligar of **Yedaragunta** was joined by the dispossessed poligar of **Charagallu**.
- By **February 1805**, the rebels were suppressed.
- Some chiefs were ordered to reside in **Madras**; others received an allowance of **18 per cent upon revenues** of their estates.
- The poligar rebellion spread over a **vast area of South India**.
- According to **A. Shunmugaiah**, the proclamations by the rebels indicate they believed in a **mass movement against alien rule**, seeking independence.

### Uprisings in Haryana Region (1803–1810)

- The present-day **Haryana** region was taken over by the British from **Scindia** by the **Treaty of Surji-Arjungaon in 1803**.
- There was strong opposition from **Sikh chiefs** of **Ambala, Karnal, and Thanesar**.
- In western Haryana, opposition was organised by the **Muslim Bhatti Rajputs** under **Zabita Khan of Sirsa and Rania** and **Khan Bahadur Khan of Fatehabad**.
- After many failed efforts, a large contingent under **Col. Adams** attacked **Fatehabad, Sirsa, and Rania** in **November 1809**.
- The people of **Rohtak, Bhiwani**, and eastern **Hisar** refused to accept Company-chosen rulers.
- **Lord Minto**, the Governor General, advised military action.
- The British Resident at Delhi, **Archibald Seton**, sent a force under **Gardiner** in **March 1810** to subdue the rebels, mainly **Jats and Ranghars**.
- The people of **Bhiwani** checked the British advance briefly, but heavy artillery breached the town walls. **Bhiwani was captured** after a bloody battle.
- The fort of **Hansi** was converted into a **military cantonment**.

### Diwan Velu Thampi's Revolt (1808–09)

- The East India Company's harsh conditions on **Travancore** after the **subsidiary alliance** under **Wellesley in 1805** caused deep resentment.
- The ruler could not pay the subsidy and fell in **arrears**.
- The British resident was **meddling in internal affairs**.
- The Company's high-handedness compelled **Prime Minister (or Dalawa) Velu Thampi** to rise against the Company, assisted by the **Nair troops**.
- Velu Thampi addressed a gathering in **Kundara**, openly calling for taking up arms against the British. This became known as the **Kundara Proclamation**.
- A large-scale rebellion followed, requiring a **large military operation** to restore peace.
- The **Maharaja of Travancore** had not wholly supported the rebellion and **defected to the Company's side**.
- **Velu Thampi killed himself** to avoid capture. The rebellion petered out.

### Disturbances in Bundelkhand (1808–12)

- **Bundelkhand**, conquered during the **Second Anglo-Maratha Wars (1803–05)**, was placed within the **Presidency of Bengal**.
- The **Bundela chiefs** resisted from their forts, numbering **nearly 150**.
- **Lakshaman Dawa**, the **killadar** (fort commander) of **Ajaygarh Fort**, was permitted to retain it temporarily for two years ending in **1808** but wanted to continue. He surrendered in **February 1809** and was taken to Calcutta.
- **Darya Singh**, killadar of **Kalanjar**, was suppressed in **January 1812**.
- The most serious threat came from **Gopal Singh**, a famous military adventurer with a dispute against his British-supported uncle.
- For **four years**, Gopal Singh eluded all British vigilance and military tactics.
- To stop these disturbances, the British adopted a policy of binding hereditary chieftains through **Ikarnamahs** (contractual obligations).

### Parlakimedi Outbreak (1813–34)

- **Parlakimedi**, in the western border of **Ganjam district** (now in **Odisha**), saw resistance from zamindars and rajas.
- When the Company acquired Ganjam, **Narayan Deo** was the raja.
- **Colonel Peach** defeated Narayan Deo in **1768** and made **Gajapathi Deo** (son of Narayan) a zamindar.
- Narayan Deo, supported by his son and brothers, revolted again.
- The Presidency of **Madras** appointed **George Russell** as commissioner in **1832** with full powers.
- Russell pacified the region by **1834**.

### Kutch or Cutch Rebellion (1816–32)

- A treaty in **1816** between the British and **Maharaja Bharamal II** of Kutch vested power in the throne.
- A power struggle between the maharaja and chieftains saw **British interference**.
- In **1819**, Raja Bharmal II raised **Arab and African troops** to remove the British. Chieftains ranged on his side.
- The British **defeated and deposed** Bharmal in favour of his **infant son**.
- A **British resident** governed as the de facto ruler with a **regency council**.
- Administrative innovations and **excessive land assessment** caused deep resentment.
- News of **British reverses in the Burma War** emboldened chiefs to demand **restoration of Bharamal II**.
- After failed military operations, the Company was compelled to follow a **conciliatory policy**.

### Rising at Bareilly (1816)

- The immediate cause was the imposition of the **police tax**, which aroused burning indignation.
- The issue became **religious** when **Mufti Muhammad Aiwaz**, a venerated old man, petitioned the magistrate in **March 1816**.
- The situation worsened when police, while collecting tax, **injured a woman**.
- This led to a bloody scuffle between the Mufti's followers and the police.
- Within two days, several armed Muslims from **Pilibhit, Shahjahanpur, and Rampur** rose in rebellion for the defence of the faith.
- In **April 1816**, insurgents **murdered the son of Leycester** (judge of provincial court of Bareilly).
- The uprising was suppressed with heavy military deployment — **more than 300 rebels killed**, and even more wounded and imprisoned.
- The upsurge was the product more of **discontent than actual grievance** — rooted in the very nature of alien administration.

### Upsurge in Hathras (1817)

- **Dayaram**, a talukdar of several villages in **Aligarh district**, had a strong base in the fort of **Hathras**.
- The fort was considered among the strongest in India — a **'second Bharatpur'** — with walls of great height and thickness, defended by a deep ditch and artillery.
- The English had settled the Hathras estate with Dayaram as a farmer.
- Due to **progressively increasing high revenues**, Dayaram constantly failed to pay arrears and committed acts of hostility by harbouring government fugitives.
- The Company attacked Hathras in **February 1817** with a large army.
- Dayaram fought bravely for **more than 15 days** and escaped, but ultimately submitted and settled with a **pension**.
- **Bhagwant Singh**, Raja of Mursan, another noted rebel, submitted to the government.

### Paika Rebellion (1817)

- The **Paiks** of Odisha were the traditional **landed militia** ('foot soldiers' literally) who enjoyed **rent-free land tenures** for military service and policing functions on a **hereditary basis**.
- The Company's **conquest of Odisha in 1803** and the **dethronement of the Raja of Khurda** greatly reduced the Paiks' power and prestige.
- Further causes of resentment:
  - **Extortionist land revenue policy**
  - **Rise in salt prices** due to taxes
  - **Abolition of cowrie currency**
  - **Requirement to pay taxes in silver**
- **Bakshi Jagabandhu Bidyadhar** had been the military chief of the Raja of Khurda's forces. In **1814**, his ancestral estate of **Killa Rorang** was taken by the Company, reducing him to penury (extreme poverty).
- The spark was lit by the arrival of **Khonds from Gumsur** into Khurda territory in **March 1817**.
- With support of **Mukunda Deva** (last Raja of Khurda) and other zamindars, **Bakshi Jagabandhu** led a sundry army of Paikas, forcing Company forces to retreat temporarily.
- The rebellion came to be known as the **Paika Bidroh** (rebellion).
- Initial success set the whole province of **Odisha** in arms against the British.
- Jagabandhu, declared an outlaw, was sheltered by the **Raja of Nayagarh**.
- **Dinabandhu Santra** and his group surrendered in **November 1818**.
- Jagabandhu evaded British vigilance. **None of the people betrayed their leaders** despite rewards offered.
- Though Khurda was back under Company control by **mid-1817**, rebels resorted to **guerilla tactics**.
- The rebellion was **brutally repressed by 1818**. Priests at the **Puri temple** who sheltered Jagabandhu were caught and **hanged**.
- In **1825**, Jagabandhu surrendered under negotiated terms. (Some sources say he was captured and died in captivity in **1829**.)

**Achievements of the Paika Rebellion:**
- Large **remissions of arrears**
- **Reductions in assessments**
- **Suspension** of the sale of estates of defaulters
- A **new settlement on fixed tenures**
- Other features of **liberal governance**

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The **Paika Rebellion of 1817** received official recognition when the **Government of India** in **2017** commemorated its **bicentenary** and acknowledged it as one of the earliest armed rebellions against British rule. The **Government of Odisha** has been pushing to include the Paika Rebellion in **NCERT textbooks** as the "First War of Indian Independence," preceding the 1857 Revolt by 40 years.

### Waghera Rising (1818–20)

- Resentment against alien rule, coupled with the exactions of the **Gaekwad of Baroda** (supported by the British), compelled the **Waghera chiefs of Okha Mandal** to take up arms.
- The Wagheras carried out inroads into British territory during **1818–19**.
- A **peace treaty** was signed in **November 1820**.

### Ahom Revolt (1828)

- The British had pledged to **withdraw from Assam** after the **First Burma War (1824–26)**.
- Instead, the British attempted to **incorporate Ahom territories** in the Company's dominion.
- This sparked a rebellion in **1828** under **Gomdhar Konwar** (an Ahom prince), alongside **Dhanjay Borgohain** and **Jairam Khargharia Phukan**.
- Assembling near **Jorhat**, the rebels formally made Gomdhar Konwar the king.
- The Company followed a **conciliatory policy**, handing over **Upper Assam** to **Maharaja Purandar Singh Narendra**. Part of the kingdom was restored to the Assamese king.

### Surat Salt Agitations (1840s)

- A strong anti-British sentiment resulted in **attacks on Europeans** by the local Surat population in **1844** over the government's step to raise the **salt duty from 50 paise to one rupee**.
- Faced with a popular movement, the government **withdrew the additional salt levy**.
- Again in **1848**, the government was forced to **withdraw its measure** to introduce **Bengal Standard Weights and Measures** when people resorted to **boycott and passive resistance**.

### Kolhapur and Savantvadi Revolts

- The **Gadkaris** were a **hereditary military class** garrisoned in Maratha forts.
- These garrisons were **disbanded** during administrative reorganisation in **Kolhapur state after 1844**.
- Facing unemployment, the Gadkaris revolted and occupied the **Samangarh** and **Bhudargarh** forts.
- In **Savantvadi** areas, people had already revolted in **1830, 1836, and 1838** (the last because the British deposed their ruler).
- The British introduced many laws to bring the region under control.

### Wahabi Movement

- The Wahabi Movement was essentially an **Islamic revivalist movement** (a movement to return to the pure, original form of Islam).
- Founded by **Syed Ahmed of Rai Bareilly**, inspired by the teachings of **Abdul Wahab (1703–87)** of Saudi Arabia and **Shah Waliullah of Delhi**.
- Syed Ahmed condemned **Western influence on Islam** and advocated a return to **pure Islam** and society as it was in the Arabia of the **Prophet's time**.
- Syed Ahmed was acclaimed as the desired leader (**Imam**).
- A **countrywide organisation** with an elaborate **secret code** was set up under spiritual vice-regents (**Khalifas**).
- **Sithana** in the north-western tribal belt was chosen as the base.
- In India, the important centre was at **Patna**, with missions in **Hyderabad, Madras, Bengal, United Provinces, and Bombay**.
- Since **Dar al-Harb** (territory of war or chaos) was to be converted into **Dar al-Islam** (the land of Islam), a **jihad** was declared against the **Sikh kingdom of Punjab**.
- After the defeat of the Sikh ruler and Punjab's incorporation into the Company in **1849**, the **English dominion became the sole target**.
- The Wahabis played an important role in spreading **anti-British sentiments**.
- A series of British military operations in the **1860s** on the Sithana base and court cases of sedition weakened the movement, though sporadic encounters continued into the **1880s and 1890s**.

### Kuka Movement

- The Kuka Movement was founded in **1840** by **Bhagat Jawahar Mal** (also called **Sian Saheb**) in **western Punjab**.
- A major leader after him was **Baba Ram Singh**, who founded the **Namdhari Sikh sect**.
- After the British took Punjab, the movement transformed from a **religious purification campaign** to a **political campaign**.

**Basic tenets:**
- **Abolition of caste** and similar discriminations among Sikhs
- Discouraging consumption of **meat, alcohol, and drugs**
- Permission for **intermarriages** and **widow remarriage**
- Encouraging **women to step out of seclusion**

**Political objectives:**
- Remove the British and **restore Sikh rule** over Punjab
- Advocated wearing **hand-woven clothes** (Swadeshi)
- **Boycott of English laws, education, and products**

> **Key Point**: The concepts of **Swadeshi** and **non-cooperation** were propagated by the Kukas **much before** they became part of the Indian national movement in the early 20th century.

- As the movement gained popularity, the British took steps to crush it between **1863 and 1872**.
- In **1872**, **Ram Singh was deported to Rangoon**.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The **Namdhari Sikh sect** founded by Baba Ram Singh continues to be active today and is recognised for its early advocacy of Swadeshi and social reform. The **Kuka Movement** is often cited as a precursor to Gandhi's Non-Cooperation and Swadeshi movements. In **2022**, the Punjab government announced initiatives to preserve the heritage of the Namdhari movement and memorialise its leaders.

## Peasant Movements with Religious Overtones

Peasant uprisings were protests against:
- **Evictions**
- **Increase in rents** of land
- **Moneylenders' greedy ways**

Their aim was **occupancy rights for peasants** among other things. They were revolts of the **peasants themselves**, though often led by local leaders.

### Narkelberia Uprising

- **Mir Nithar Ali (1782–1831)** or **Titu Mir** inspired the **Muslim tenants in West Bengal** to rise against landlords (mainly Hindu) who imposed a **beard-tax** on the **Faraizis** and British indigo planters.
- Often considered the **first armed peasant uprising** against the British.
- The revolt soon took on a **religious hue**.
- The revolt later **merged into the Wahabi Movement**.

### The Pagal Panthis

- The **Pagal Panthi**, a semi-religious group mainly constituting the **Hajong** and **Garo** tribes of **Mymensingh district** (earlier in Bengal), was founded by **Karam Shah**.
- The tribal peasants organised under Karam Shah's son, **Tipu**, to fight the oppression of zamindars.
- From **1825 to 1835**, the Pagal Panthis **refused to pay rent above a certain limit** and attacked the houses of zamindars.
- The government introduced an **equitable arrangement** to protect these peasants, but the movement was **violently suppressed**.

### Faraizi Revolt

- The Faraizis were followers of a **Muslim sect** founded by **Haji Shariatullah** of **Faridpur in Eastern Bengal**.
- They advocated radical **religious, social, and political changes**.
- Shariatullah and his son **Muhsinuddin Ahmad**, popularly known as **Dudu Miyan (1819–62)**, organised followers to **expel the English from Bengal**.
- The sect also supported the **cause of tenants against zamindars**.
- The Faraizi disturbances continued from **1838 to 1857**.
- Most Faraizis **joined the Wahabi ranks**.

### Moplah Uprisings

- **Hike in revenue demand** and **reduction of field size**, coupled with **oppression of officials**, resulted in widespread peasant unrest among the **Moplahs of Malabar**.
- **Twenty-two rebellions** took place between **1836 and 1854**. None proved successful.
- The **second Moplah uprising** occurred after the Moplahs came to be organised by the **Congress** and **Khilafat supporters** during the **Non-Cooperation Movement**.
- But **Hindu-Muslim differences** distanced the Congress and the Moplahs. By **1921**, the Moplahs had been subdued.

### Peasants' Role in the 1857 Revolt

- Peasant participation was active only in some areas, mainly **western Uttar Pradesh**.
- Peasants united with **local feudal leaders** to fight against foreign rule.
- After the revolt, the plight of the peasants **worsened** as the British Government decided to gain the support of the **landed classes** while **ignoring the peasants**.
- In **Awadh**, land was restored to the **taluqdars**, who were given revenue and other powers. Peasants could not avail of the provisions of the **1859 Bengal Rent Act**.
- As punishment for their participation in the 1857 revolt, peasants had to pay an **additional cess** in some regions.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The historical pattern of peasant exploitation by intermediaries continues to inform Indian agrarian policy. The **Swaminathan Commission Report** (recommending MSP at C2+50%) has been a central demand of modern farmer movements. The **PM-KISAN scheme** (launched 2019) directly transfers ₹6,000/year to farmer families, bypassing intermediaries — addressing the same structural problem of middlemen that plagued colonial-era peasants. The **e-NAM (National Agriculture Market)** launched in 2016 also seeks to eliminate exploitative intermediaries in agricultural trade.

## Tribal Revolts

**Tribal movements under British rule were the most frequent, militant, and violent of all movements.**

### Different Causes for Mainland and North-Eastern Tribal Revolts

Tribal movements can be better analysed if categorised into:
1. **Mainland tribal revolts**
2. **Frontier tribal revolts** (concentrated mainly in the **north-eastern part** of India)

#### Causes of Mainland Tribal Revolts:

- **Land settlements** affected the **joint ownership tradition** among tribals and disrupted their social fabric.
- As settled agriculture expanded, tribals **lost their land**, and there was an **influx of non-tribals**.
- **Shifting cultivation** in forests was curbed, adding to tribal problems.
- The government set up **reserved forests** and restricted **timber use and grazing** — due to increasing Company demand for timber for **shipping and railways**.
- Exploitation by **police, traders, and moneylenders** (most of them 'outsiders') aggravated suffering.
- Some **general laws** were abhorred for their intrusive nature, as tribals had their own customs and traditions.
- With colonial expansion, **Christian missionaries** interfered with traditional customs and were resented as representatives of alien rule.

#### How North-Eastern Frontier Tribal Revolts Were Different:

- Tribes sharing **tribal and cultural links with countries across the border** did not concern themselves much with the nationalist struggle. Their revolts were often in favour of **political autonomy** within the Indian Union or **complete independence**.
- These movements were **not forest-based or agrarian revolts** as these tribals generally controlled land and forest area.
- The British entered north-eastern areas **much later** than non-frontier tribal areas.
- Frontier tribal revolts **continued for a longer time** than non-frontier movements.
- **De-sanskritisation movements** also spread among frontier tribals. The **Meiteis** organised a movement during **Churchand Maharaja's rule (1891–1941)** to denounce malpractices of neo-Vaishnavite Brahmins.
- **Sanskritisation movements were almost totally absent** in the north-east in the colonial period.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The distinct character of north-eastern tribal movements continues to shape India's governance framework. The **Sixth Schedule** of the Constitution provides autonomous district councils for tribal areas in Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram. The **AFSPA (Armed Forces Special Powers Act)**, still applicable in parts of the north-east, has roots in the colonial-era approach to frontier areas. In **December 2022**, the government partially withdrew AFSPA from parts of **Nagaland, Assam, and Manipur**, responding to long-standing demands. The **Forest Rights Act, 2006** continues to be a critical tool for protecting mainland tribal land rights; as of **2023**, over **50 lakh claims** have been filed under FRA across India.

### Characteristics of Tribal Revolts

Despite being separated in time and space, tribal uprisings shared common characteristics:

- **Tribal identity or ethnic ties** lay behind their solidarity. Not all 'outsiders' were seen as enemies — the poor who lived by manual labour and had a supportive role in the village were left alone. Violence was directed towards **moneylenders and traders** seen as extensions of the colonial government.
- A common cause was resentment against **laws imposed by the 'foreign government'** that tried to destroy the tribals' traditional socio-economic framework.
- **Erosion of tribal rights over land and forest** was at the root of many movements. As land became **private property** and **market forces** dominated, there was large-scale **alienation of land**. This worsened after **roads and railways** linked tribal areas.
- Many uprisings were led by **messiah-like figures** who encouraged revolt and promised to end suffering by 'outsiders'.
- The tribal uprisings were **doomed from the beginning** — they fought with **outdated arms** against modern weapons and techniques.

## Important Tribal Movements of the Mainland

> **Note:** Most mainland tribal movements were concentrated in **central India**, the **west-central region**, and the **south**. The tribals of **Chotanagpur** and **Santhal Pargana** (in present-day **Jharkhand**) have a long history of resistance against British administration and its non-tribal collaborators.

### The Pahariyas

- The hill folk around the **Rajmahal hills** were known as the **Pahariyas**.
- They subsisted on **forest produce** and practised **shifting cultivation**.
- They considered the entire region as **their land** and were hostile to outsiders.
- Before the British came, they maintained independence due to **geographical isolation**.
- In years of scarcity, the Pahariyas **raided the plains** — these raids also symbolised **asserting power** over settled communities.
- **Zamindars on the plains** paid regular tribute to the Pahariyas to buy peace; **traders** paid hill chiefs for permission to use the passes.
- This brittle peace disintegrated in the **last decades of the 18th century** when the British encouraged **forest clearance** and expanded settled agriculture.
- Reduced forests and pastures **increased conflict** between hill folk and settled cultivators.
- In the **1770s**, the British resorted to **brutal attacks** aimed at hunting them down and killing them.
- The **Pahariyas rebellion of 1778** led by **Raja Jagganath** is notable.
- In the **1780s**, the British initiated **pacification** — Paharia chiefs received annual allowances in return for good conduct.
- Not all Pahariyas accepted this. Some **withdrew deep into the mountains**, continuing their war against the **diku** (outsiders).

### The Revolt led by Tilka Manjhi

- The British faced much opposition in **Santhal Pargana** under the leadership of **Tilka Manjhi** (or Majhi), a **Santhal** whose real name is said to have been **Jabra Paharia**.
- Tilka was against the British policy of **divide and rule**.
- He is reputed to have moved in the hills around **Sultanganj**, attacking **East India Company boats** on the Ganga and **looting the British treasury**, sharing the loot with the poor.
- The **guerilla warfare** he organised had many **Santhal women** participating.
- In **1778**, Tilka is said to have taken the **Ramgarh Camp** from the British after forming a joint front with the **Paharia Sardars**.
- In **1784**, Tilka led his followers to attack **Bhagalpur** and is said to have shot the British magistrate of Rajmahal, **Augustus Cleveland**.
- The British made a concerted attack, finally capturing Manjhi and **hanging him in 1785**.

> **Key Point:** Manjhi is considered the **first Adivasi leader** to take up arms against the British.

**Main causes:**
- Exploitation, extortion, and harassment by revenue collectors, police officers, and landlords' agents
- The **drought of 1770** led to starvation and consequent protests, which the British met with more oppression

### Jungle Mahal Revolt or the Chuar Uprising

**Background:**
- **Jungle (or Jungal) Mahals** was the name for an indefinite administrative unit between **Chota Nagpur** and the **plains of Bengal**.
- Later identified as parganas in **Birbhum, Bankura, and Midnapore**. (Due to continued disturbances, it was split amongst neighbouring districts in **1833**.)
- According to **J.C. Jha**, the **Chuars** were the **Bhumij tribals** belonging to the **Mundari main stock**.
- After moving from the Chotanagpur plateau, they settled in large numbers in **Midnapur, Bankura, and Purulia** districts of Bengal.
- They were basically **farmers and hunters**; some worked under local zamindars.
- They held lands under a kind of **feudal tenure** but were always ready to switch from farming to hunting at the bidding of jungle chiefs or zamindars.
- These jungle zamindars hired **paiks** (village guards/police) from among the Chuars. Head paiks were known as **sardars**.

**Phases of the Chuar Uprisings:**

**First Phase (1768):**
- Broke out as a reaction to the **increase in revenue** of jungle zamindars.
- In **1767**, the British instructed the resident of Midnapur to **demolish their mud forts**, offending the zamindars.
- In **1768**, **Jagannath Singh**, the zamindar of **Ghatsila** (or king of Dhalbhum), rose with thousands of Chuar followers. The Company capitulated.

**Phase of 1771:**
- Chuar sardars **Shyam Ganjan of Dhadka**, **Subla Singh of Kaliapal**, and **Dubraj** rose in rebellion. Suppressed.

**Most Significant Phase (1798):**
- Under **Durjan (or Durjol) Singh** in **1798**.
- The **Permanent Settlement** and police regulations in rural Bengal made the system of hiring local paiks **redundant** as they were replaced by professional police.
- Aggrieved paiks and ordinary Chuars joined jungle zamindars.
- Durjan Singh was the zamindar of **Raipur**, dispossessed due to Bengal Regulations.
- In **May 1798**, his followers (a body of **1,500 Chuars**) engaged in violent activities to halt the **auction of Raipur estate**.
- The revolt was **brutally suppressed** by the British in **1799**.

**Other leaders:** **Madhab Singh** (brother of raja of Barabhum), **Raja Mohan Singh** (zamindar of Juriah), and **Lachman Singh of Dulma**.

All participants were associated with the land — zamindars, paiks, and ordinary Chuars. It was a **clash of ways of life**: people living in a closed agrarian community were exposed to a new colonial situation that upset their way of life.

> **Note:** The term 'Chuar' is considered **derogatory** by some historians who call this the **Revolt of the Jungle Mahal** instead.

### Tamar Revolt

- The tribals of **Tamar** (in Chotanagpur) rose in revolt in **1798** under **Bholanath Sahay** (some sources say Bholanath Singh).
- The uprising was in reaction to the **faulty and alien systems** imposed by the British.
- The **Munda tribals** and their chiefs joined forces with Bholanath.
- The revolt expressed anger against the **diku** (outsiders).
- Firmly suppressed by an expedition led by **Lt. Cooper**.
- There were **several more revolts** by the tribals of Tamar.

### Kol Mutiny (1831)

- The **Kols**, along with other tribes, inhabited **Chhotanagpur** — covering **Ranchi, Singhbhum, Hazaribagh, Palamau**, and western parts of **Manbhum**.
- The trouble in **1831** started with large-scale **transfers of land** from Kol headmen to outsiders like Hindu, Sikh, and Muslim farmers and moneylenders who were **oppressive** and demanded heavy taxes.
- British judicial and revenue policies badly affected the **traditional social conditions** of the Kols.
- In **1831**, under **Buddho Bhagat**, the Kol rebels **killed or burnt about a thousand outsiders**.
- Only after **large-scale military operations** could order be restored.

### Ho and Munda Uprisings (1820–37)

- The **Raja of Parahat** organised his **Ho tribals** to revolt against the occupation of **Singhbhum** (now in Jharkhand).
- The revolt continued till **1827** when the Ho tribals were forced to submit.
- In **1831**, they organised again, joined by the **Mundas of Chotanagpur**, to protest against:
  - The newly introduced **farming revenue policy**
  - The **entry of Bengalis** into their region
- The revolt was extinguished in **1832**, but **Ho operations continued till 1837**.
- The Mundas were not to remain quiet for long.

### Santhal Rebellions (1833; 1855–56)

**Background — The Santhals' Migration:**
- The **Santhals** moved into the **Rajmahal area** in the late **1770s and early 1780s** from **Cuttack, Dhalbhum, Manbhum, Hazaribagh, and Midnapore**.
- They showed willingness to give up **nomadic life** and lead a **settled life** — clearing forests, ploughing land, and growing crops.
- This suited British interests — the Company wanted more revenue and crops for export.
- The British persuaded Santhals to settle in the **foothills of Rajmahal**.

**The Santhal-Paharia Conflict:**
- A feud between the Santhals and Pahariyas continued for a long time.
- Called a **battle between the hoe and the plough**: the hoe symbolising the Pahariyas (shifting cultivation) and the plough standing for the Santhals (settled agriculture).

**Formation of Damin-i-koh (1832–33):**
- The British formed the **Damin-i-koh** (a Persian term meaning the **skirts — or outside edges — of the hills**) as a compromise.
- A portion of land at the foothills was declared Santhal territory for settled agriculture.
- The **Pahariyas** were practically forced to retreat into higher hill tracts.

**Growing Exploitation:**
- Over time, the Santhals gradually **lost their cultivated land**.
- The **Permanent Settlement Act of 1793** proved disastrous — taxes were heavy and money had to be borrowed.
- **Diku moneylenders** charged very high interest rates and, when debts went unpaid, **took possession of the land**.
- Zamindars slowly took over the **Damin tracts**.

**The Hul — A Movement for Liberation (1855–56):**
- By the **1850s**, the Santhals felt the need to rebel against zamindars and moneylenders.
- The rebellion soon turned into a **movement against the British colonial state**.
- The Santhals called the rebellion **'hul'** — meaning a **movement for liberation**.
- Under **Sidhu** and **Kanhu Murmu** (two brothers), the Santhals:
  - Proclaimed an **end to Company rule**
  - Declared the area between **Bhagalpur and Rajmahal** as **autonomous**
- Emissaries with **sal branches** (a form of secret communication) were sent to gather support.
- **Phulo** and **Jhano Murmu**, the **sisters** of Sidhu and Kanhu, participated and are said to have entered the enemy camp under cover and killed several soldiers before dying themselves.
- The British suppressed the rebellion with a heavy hand by **1856** — burning villages, destroying them with elephants, and killing **thousands of Santhals**.
- **Sidhu and Kanhu were killed**.

**Aftermath:**
- The **Santhal Pargana** was created out of the districts of **Bhagalpur** and **Birbhum**.
- The pargana was to have **special laws** within it.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The **Santhal Pargana Tenancy Act, 1949** (building on colonial-era protections) continues to restrict land transfers from tribals to non-tribals in the Santhal Pargana region. The legacy of the Santhal Rebellion is commemorated annually on **June 30 as Hul Diwas** in Jharkhand. The creation of the **state of Jharkhand in 2000** was partly a fulfilment of the tribal aspiration for autonomy that movements like the Santhal rebellion and Birsa Munda's Ulgulan represented. **Sidhu and Kanhu Murmu** are celebrated as national heroes, and their statues stand at prominent places in Jharkhand. President **Droupadi Murmu** (elected 2022), the first tribal President of India, hails from the Santhal community, marking a historic milestone in tribal representation at the highest level of governance.

### Khond Uprisings (1837–56)

- From **1837 to 1856**, the **Khonds** of hilly tracts extending from **Odisha** to the **Srikakulam** and **Visakhapatnam** districts of **Andhra Pradesh** revolted against Company rule.
- **Chakra Bisoi**, a young raja, led the Khonds, who were joined by the **Ghumsar, Kalahandi**, and other tribals.
- They opposed:
  - The **suppression of human sacrifice**
  - **New taxes**
  - The **entry of zamindars** into their areas
- With Chakra Bisoi's **disappearance**, the uprising ended.
- A later Khond rebellion in **1914** in the Orissa region was triggered by the hope of ending foreign rule and gaining autonomous government.

### Koya Revolts

- The **Koyas** of the eastern **Godavari** track (modern Andhra), joined by **Khonda Sara chiefs**, rebelled in **1803, 1840, 1845, 1858, 1861, and 1862**.
- They rose again in **1879–80** under **Tomma Sora**.
- Their complaints: oppression by **police and moneylenders**, **new regulations**, and denial of **customary rights over forest areas**.
- After Tomma Sora's death, another rebellion was organised in **1886** by **Raja Anantayyar**.

### Bhil Revolts

- The **Bhils** lived in the **Western Ghats** and controlled mountain passes between the north and the Deccan.
- They revolted against Company rule in **1817–19** due to **famine, economic distress, and misgovernment**.
- The British used both **force and conciliation** to control the uprising.
- The Bhils revolted again in **1825, 1831, and 1846**.
- Later, a reformer, **Govind Guru**, helped the Bhils of **south Rajasthan** (Banswara, Sunth states) to organise themselves to fight for a **Bhil Raj** by **1913**.

### Koli Risings

- The **Kolis** in the neighbourhood of Bhils rose in rebellion in **1829, 1839**, and again during **1844–48**.
- They resented the **imposition of Company's rule** which brought **large-scale unemployment** and the **dismantling of their forts**.

### Ramosi Risings

- The **Ramosis**, hill tribes of the **Western Ghats**, had not reconciled to British rule and the British pattern of administration. They resented the **policy of annexation**.
- After the annexation of Maratha territories, the Ramosis, who had been employed by the **Maratha administration**, lost their livelihood.
- They rose under **Chittur Singh** in **1822** and plundered the country around **Satara**.
- Eruptions in **1825–26** under **Umaji Naik of Poona** and his supporter **Bapu Trimbakji Sawant**; disturbances continued till **1829**.
- Disturbances again in **1839** over the deposition and banishment of **Raja Pratap Singh of Satara**, and in **1840–41**.
- A **superior British force** restored order.
- The British generally followed a **pacifist policy** towards the Ramosis and even **recruited some into the hill police**.

## Tribal Movements of the North-East

### Khasi Uprising

- After occupying the hilly region between **Garo and Jaintia Hills**, the Company wanted to build a **road linking the Brahmaputra Valley with Sylhet**.
- A large number of outsiders — **Englishmen, Bengalis, and labourers from the plains** — were brought in.
- The **Khasis, Garos, Khamptis, and Singphos** organised under **Tirath Singh** to drive away the strangers.
- The uprising became a **popular revolt against British rule**.
- By **1833**, the superior English military force suppressed the revolt.

### Singphos Rebellion

- The rebellion of the **Singphos** in **Assam** in early **1830** was immediately quelled, but they continued to organise revolts.
- An uprising in **1839** saw the **death of the British political agent**.
- Chief **Nirang Phidu** led an uprising in **1843**, involving an attack on the British garrison and the death of many soldiers.

**Smaller movements:** The **Mishmis** (1836); the **Khampti rebellion** in Assam (1839–42); and the **Lushais' revolt** in 1842 and 1844 (attacking villages in Manipur).

### North-East Frontier Tribal Movements at a Glance

**Before 1857:**

| Movement | Period | Details |
|----------|--------|---------|
| **Ahoms' Revolt** | 1828–33 | Against non-fulfilment of British pledges after Burmese War |
| **Khasis' Revolt** | 1830s | Led by **Tirath Singh**; against occupation of hilly region |
| **Singphos' Rebellion** | 1830s | Led to murder of British political agent in 1839 |

**After 1857:**

## Some Tribal Movements After 1857

> Though this chapter focuses on uprisings before 1857, some tribal revolts continued after 1857 and deserve mention.

### Bokta Rising, Sardari Larai, or Mukti Larai Movement

- **Chotanagpur** was the scene of this movement between **1858 and 1895**.
- Aimed at regaining the tribals' age-old **right over land** by pushing out landlords.
- Earlier phases: tribal tenants rose against landlords because of **increased rent, eviction, and harassment**.
- In the **1890s**, the Sardar movement turned against **all Europeans** suspected of colluding with landlords.
- British rule was perceived as the root of tribal problems.
- When constitutional methods failed, tribals took to **violence** using traditional weapons (bows and arrows).
- The struggle lacked **organisation** and a good leader.

### Birsa Munda Revolt

- In the **1890s**, **Birsa Munda** emerged at the head of a movement of the **Munda tribes** of **Singhbhum and Ranchi districts** of Chotanagpur.
- The **Ulgulan** ('Great Tumult'), aimed at gaining independence and establishing a **Munda Raj**.

**Why did the Mundas revolt?**
- British policies introduced the **zamindari system** with new classes of landlords and tenants.
- Tribals were forced to **pay rent** to landlords; failure meant **eviction**.
- Paying rent meant increasing dependence on **usurious moneylenders** and ultimately **debt**.
- Landlords **encroached tribal land**, pushed tribals into **begar** (forced labour), and used brute force.

**The Revolt:**
- Objective: attain **religious and political independence**.
- Birsa's arrest and conviction to **two years of imprisonment** made rebels more determined.
- The revolt broke out in **December 1899** directed against the **dikus** — Christian missionaries, landlords, contractors, police, and government officials.
- The British response was **ruthless suppression**.
- Birsa was **arrested in 1900** and **died of illness**.
- Many rebels were arrested, imprisoned, and sentenced to death.

**Aftermath:**
- Authorities felt tribal interests needed safeguarding.
- The government **abolished compulsory begar** and passed the **Tenancy Act of 1903**, recognising the Mundas' **Khuntkatti system**.
- The **Chotanagpur Tenancy Act** was passed in **1908**.
- Birsa assumed **legendary fame**, and his movement inspired future tribal movements.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` **Birsa Munda** remains one of India's most celebrated tribal icons. His birth anniversary, **November 15**, is observed as **Janjatiya Gaurav Divas** (Tribal Pride Day), declared by the Government of India in **2021**. The **Birsa Munda Tribal University** has been established in Jharkhand. The **PESA Act (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas Act), 1996**, extends the principle of tribal self-governance that Birsa fought for. The **PM-JANMAN scheme (2023)** — Pradhan Mantri Janjati Adivasi Nyaya Maha Abhiyan — with a budget of ₹24,000 crore specifically targets Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs), addressing the same marginalisation issues that fuelled colonial-era tribal revolts.

### Tana Bhagat Movement

- In **April 1914**, a young man, **Jatra Oraon** of Ranchi, proclaimed that he had been divinely ordained to become a king and his followers would be the **Tana Bhagats**.
- Tana Bhagats were called upon to **discard earthly possessions** (agricultural implements) and reject practices such as **spirit worship and sacrifice**.
- They sought to **reorder the Oraon society**.
- In the official history of **Jharkhand**, the movement was initiated by **Jatra Oraon** in **1914** and later led by **Sibu Oraon** in **1919**.
- It was part of **agrarian discontent** against begar and illegal rent increases by zamindars and intermediary tenure-holders.
- They opposed **moneylenders** as well as **missionaries**.
- The Tana Bhagats conducted a **satyagraha** even **before Gandhi's** satyagraha movement.
- They differed from movements led by **Sido and Kanhu Murmu** or **Birsa Munda** in being **primarily religious and non-violent**.
- In **1921**, during the **Non-Cooperation Movement**, the Tana Bhagats were drawn into the organisational fold of the **Congress**.

### Devi Movement

- Initially a **social movement** among tribals of **South Gujarat** in **1922–23**.
- Initiated by the belief that **Devi Salabai** was commanding tribals to abstain from eating flesh and drinking liquor and to be clean.
- By **December 1922**, the movement spread over the region inhabited by tribals as well as **Surat city**.
- The movement soon **targeted exploitative classes** — landlords, moneylenders, and the liquor trade.
- Towards the end of **1922**, it became part of the **Non-Cooperation Movement**.

## Sepoy Mutinies

A number of **sporadic military uprisings** took place before the Great Revolt of **1857** in different parts of the country.

### Causes

Rising discontent of the sepoys against British rule due to:

1. **Discrimination in payment and promotions**
2. **Mistreatment of sepoys** by British officials
3. **Refusal of foreign service allowance** while fighting in remote regions
4. **Religious objections** of high-caste Hindu sepoys to **Lord Canning's General Service Enlistment Act (1856)** ordering all recruits to be ready for service **both within and outside India**

Further, the sepoys shared all the discontent and grievances — **social, religious, and economic** — that afflicted the civilian population.

Over the years, upper caste sepoys found their **religious beliefs in conflict** with service conditions:
- In **1806**, the replacement of the **turban by a leather cockade** caused a mutiny at **Vellore**.
- In **1844**, there was a mutinous outbreak of the **Bengal army sepoys** for being sent to distant **Sind**.
- In **1824**, sepoys at **Barrackpore** rose in revolt when asked to go to **Burma** because **crossing the sea meant loss of caste**.

## Weaknesses of People's Uprisings

- These uprisings drew a **large number of participants** but were **localised** and occurred at **different times** in different regions.
- They mostly arose out of **local grievances**.
- The leadership was **semi-feudal**, backward-looking, traditional in outlook, and their resistance **did not offer alternatives** to the existing social set-up.
- If many revolts seemed similar in wanting to oust alien rule, it was **not because of a 'national' impulse** or common effort, but because they were protesting against **common conditions**.
- These rebellions were **centuries-old in form** and ideological/cultural content.
- Those who were not so obstinate were **pacified through concessions** by the authorities.
- The **methods and arms** used were practically **obsolete** compared to the weapons, strategy, and **deception and chicanery** employed by their opponents.

🌍 **Modern Context**

`[Modern Example]` The weaknesses of pre-1857 uprisings — localised nature, lack of national consciousness, absence of a unifying ideology — were precisely what the **Indian National Congress** (founded 1885) and later the **Gandhian movement** sought to overcome by creating a pan-India framework of resistance. The evolution from fragmented local uprisings to a unified national movement is one of the most important transitions in Indian history. Today, India's constitutional framework — with provisions like the **Fifth Schedule** (for Scheduled Areas), **Sixth Schedule** (for tribal areas in the NE), and laws like **PESA Act (1996)** and **Forest Rights Act (2006)** — attempts to institutionally address the very grievances that drove colonial-era resistance.
