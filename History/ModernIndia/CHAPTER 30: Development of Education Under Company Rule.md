# 📚 Chapter 30: Development of Education Under Company Rule

---

## 🗂️ Chapter at a Glance (Read This First!)

> **5 Big Takeaways from this Chapter:**
>
> 1. **The East India Company ignored education for 60 years** — its interest was profit, not public welfare.
> 2. **Macaulay's Minute (1835)** was a turning point — it imposed English as the medium and created the "downward filtration theory."
> 3. **Wood's Despatch (1854)** was the *real* foundation of modern education in India — the "Magna Carta of English Education."
> 4. **Every major education commission** (Hunter, Sadler, Hartog) identified the same problems: neglect of primary education, neglect of women's education, and poor quality.
> 5. **British education policy was never truly philanthropic** — it served colonial administrative and commercial needs.

## PART 1: Education Under Company Rule (Pre-1858)

---

### 1.1 The Company's Initial Indifference

For the **first 60 years** of its rule in India, the **East India Company** — a trading and profit-making body — took absolutely **no interest** in promoting education.

A few individuals, however, made notable early efforts:

- 🕌 **Calcutta Madrasah (1781):** Established by **Warren Hastings** for the study of **Muslim law and related subjects.**
- 🛕 **Sanskrit College, Benaras (1791):** Established by **Jonathan Duncan** (the resident at Benaras) for the study of **Hindu law and philosophy.**
- 🏛️ **Fort William College (1800):** Set up by **Wellesley** to train Company civil servants in **Indian languages and customs.** It was closed in **1802.**

> **Why were the Madrasah and Sanskrit College started?**
> They served a practical colonial purpose — to produce qualified Indians who could help administer law in Company courts, and to provide knowledge of classical/vernacular languages for correspondence with Indian states. It was utility, not genuine interest in Indian learning.

---

### 1.2 Pressure for Modern Education

Two groups started pushing the government toward **modern, secular, Western education:**

- **Enlightened Indians** (like Raja Rammohan Roy) believed Western education was the remedy for India's social, economic, and political problems.
- **Christian Missionaries** (especially the **Serampore missionaries**) believed modern education would weaken Indians' faith in their own religions and open the door to Christianity.

---

### 1.3 A Humble Beginning: Charter Act of 1813

The **Charter Act of 1813** was the first legislative step in education. It included:

- The principle of **encouraging learned Indians** and **promoting modern sciences.**
- A direction to the Company to spend **₹1 lakh (one lakh rupees) annually** on education.

> ⚠️ **However**, even this small amount was **not released until 1823** — a delay of 10 years! The reason was a fierce debate about *what kind* of education to fund.

**Meanwhile**, educated Bengalis — influenced by **Raja Rammohan Roy** — set up **Calcutta College in 1817**, which taught English education in Western humanities and sciences. The government also established **three Sanskrit colleges** at Calcutta, Delhi, and Agra.

---

### 1.4 The Orientalist-Anglicist Controversy

Inside the **General Committee on Public Instruction**, a major intellectual battle was fought:

| Side | What They Argued |
|---|---|
| **Anglicists** | All government money should go to **modern/Western studies only.** |
| **Orientalists** | Western sciences should be taught, but **traditional Indian learning** should be the focus. |
| **Sub-divide among Anglicists** | One faction wanted **English** as the medium; the other wanted **Indian vernacular languages.** |

> This confusion between English and vernacular languages — both as *medium of instruction* and as *subjects of study* — caused enormous policy paralysis.

---

### 1.5 Lord Macaulay's Minute (1835) ⚡

**Lord Macaulay's Minute** decisively ended the debate — **in favour of the Anglicists.**

**Key decisions:**

- Limited government resources to be used **exclusively** for Western sciences and literature.
- **Medium of instruction: English only.**
- Government opened a **few English schools and colleges** instead of a large number of elementary schools → **mass education was neglected.**

**Macaulay's controversial view:** *"Indian learning was inferior to European learning"* — he considered this true for physical and social sciences at that time.

---

#### 🎯 The "Downward Filtration Theory" (Educate the Top, Hope it Trickles Down)

The British designed a deliberate strategy:

> Educate a **small section of upper and middle classes** to create a class that is *"Indian in blood and colour but English in tastes, in opinions, in morals and in intellect"* — who would act as **interpreters** between the government and the masses, and would spread Western knowledge downward through vernacular languages.

**Did it actually work?**
Modern *ideas* did filter down to the masses — but NOT in the way the British wanted. They spread through:
- Political parties
- The press
- Pamphlets and public platforms

Modern education **did** help nationalists access literature on physical and social sciences, sharpening their capacity for **social analysis**. But the overall content and structure of education served **colonial interests**, not Indian ones.

---

### 1.6 Efforts of James Thomson (1843–53)

**James Thomson**, Lieutenant-Governor of **NW Provinces (1843–53)**, developed a practical scheme:

- **Village education** through the medium of **vernacular languages.**
- Subjects like **mensuration (measurement)** and **agricultural sciences** were taught.
- **Purpose:** To train personnel for the newly created **Revenue and Public Works Department.**

> This was a rare, ground-level effort at practical education for the common people.

---

### 1.7 Wood's Despatch (1854) — The Magna Carta of English Education 📜

In **1854**, **Charles Wood** prepared a detailed despatch on education for India. It is called the **"Magna Carta of English Education in India"** — the **first comprehensive plan** for spreading education in India.

**Its 6 Key Provisions:**

1. ✅ **Government's Responsibility:** The government of India must take responsibility for **mass education** — this *formally* rejected the downward filtration theory (at least on paper).
2. 🏗️ **Educational Hierarchy:** A structured ladder was proposed:
   - *Bottom:* Vernacular primary schools in villages
   - *Middle:* Anglo-Vernacular High Schools + affiliated colleges at district level
   - *Top:* **Affiliating universities** in presidency towns of **Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras**
3. 📖 **Medium of Instruction:** **English** for higher education; **vernaculars** at school level.
4. 👩‍🏫 **Special emphasis on:** Female education, vocational education, and teachers' training.
5. ⛪ **Secular Education:** Education in government institutions must be **secular** (non-religious).
6. 💰 **Grants-in-Aid:** A system of grants-in-aid to encourage **private enterprise** in education.

---

### 1.8 Developments Following Wood's Despatch

- **1857:** Universities established at **Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras;** education departments set up in all provinces.
- **Bethune School (1849):** Founded by **J.E.D. Bethune** (President of the Council of Education) at Calcutta — the **first major fruit of the women's education movement** of the 1840s–50s. Girls' schools were brought under the government's grants-in-aid and inspection system.
- **Agriculture Institute at Pusa (Bihar)** and **Engineering Institute at Roorkee** were established.
- **European headmasters and principals** dominated institutions for five decades; missionary enterprises and, gradually, private Indian efforts also grew.

## PART 2: Education After the Crown Takeover (Post-1858)

---

### 2.1 Hunter Education Commission (1882–83)

**The Problem:** Earlier schemes had neglected **primary and secondary education.** When education was transferred to provinces in **1870**, things got worse — provinces already had limited funds.

In **1882**, the government appointed a commission under **W.W. Hunter** to review progress since Wood's Despatch (1854).

**Key Recommendations:**

- **Primary Education:**
  - State must give **special care** to extend and improve primary education.
  - Primary education to be in **vernacular languages.**
  - Control of primary education to be **transferred to newly set up district and municipal boards.**

- **Secondary Education:**
  - Should have **two divisions:**
    - *Literary* — leading to university
    - *Vocational* — for commercial careers
  - Attention drawn to **inadequate facilities for female education** outside presidency towns.

**Result:** The next two decades saw rapid growth in secondary and collegiate education. New universities came up:
- **Punjab University (1882)**
- **Allahabad University (1887)**

---

### 2.2 Indian Universities Act, 1904

**Context:** At the dawn of the 20th century, political unrest was growing.

- **Official view:** Quality had deteriorated under private management; educational institutions were producing "political revolutionaries."
- **Nationalist view:** The government wasn't doing enough to eradicate illiteracy.

In **1902**, the **Raleigh Commission** was set up to examine university conditions. Based on its recommendations, the **Indian Universities Act, 1904** was passed.

**Key Provisions of the Act:**

- (i) Universities to give more attention to **study and research.**
- (ii) Number of university fellows and their tenure **reduced;** most fellows to be **nominated by the government.**
- (iii) Government got powers to **veto universities' senate regulations** and even amend or pass regulations on its own.
- (iv) Stricter conditions for **affiliation of private colleges.**
- (v) **₹5 lakh per annum for 5 years** sanctioned for improvement of higher education.

> **Lord Curzon** justified greater control in the name of "quality and efficiency." But nationalists saw it as an attempt to **discipline educated Indians into loyalty** to the government.
>
> **Gokhale** famously called it a **"retrograde measure."**

---

### 2.3 Government Resolution on Education Policy — 1913

**Background:**

- **1906:** The progressive state of **Baroda** introduced **compulsory primary education** throughout its territories.
- **1910:** **Gokhale** raised a Resolution in the **Imperial Legislative Council** calling for:
  - Compulsory primary education where at least **35% of 6-to-7-year-old boys** were already in school.
  - A **separate department of education** under the central government.
  - A **secretary for education** to monitor progress and prepare budget reports.
  - State governments and local authorities to decide cost of education.

The government promised to consider the idea. Gokhale withdrew the Resolution. The government did create an education department and appointed a secretary — but **ignored the main demand** for free and compulsory primary education.

- **March 1911:** Gokhale introduced a **bill** for free and compulsory primary education in a phased manner. It was **rejected by a select committee.**

**The 1913 Resolution said:**

- Government **refused** to take responsibility for compulsory education.
- Accepted the **policy of removing illiteracy** and urged provinces to provide free elementary education for the poor.
- Private efforts to be encouraged.
- One **university in each province** to be established; teaching activities of universities to be encouraged.
- For women's education: curriculum of **practical utility** for girls; more **women teachers and inspectors** to be appointed; examinations for girls not to be overemphasized.

---

### 2.4 Sadler University Commission (1917–19)

In **1917**, the **Calcutta University Commission** was appointed, commonly called the **Sadler Commission** after its chairman, **Michael Sadler.**

Though set up to study Calcutta University specifically, its recommendations applied more or less to **all universities in India.**

**Key Observations and Recommendations:**

1. **12-Year School Course:** Students should enter university after an **intermediate stage** (not after matric), for a **3-year degree course.** This would:
   - (a) Better prepare students for university.
   - (b) Reduce the burden of below-standard students in universities.
   - (c) Provide collegiate education to those not going to university.
   - A **separate Board of Secondary and Intermediate Education** to be created.

2. **Less rigidity** in framing university regulations.

3. Universities should function as **centralised, unitary, residential-teaching autonomous bodies** — not just as affiliating bodies for scattered colleges.

4. **Extension of:** Female education, applied scientific and technological education, teachers' training (including professional and vocational colleges).

**Impact of the Sadler Commission:**

From **1916 to 1921**, seven new universities came up:
**Mysore, Patna, Benaras, Aligarh, Dacca, Lucknow, and Osmania.**

Soon after: **Delhi, Agra, and Annamalai (Madras).**

**Other positive changes:**
- Honours courses introduced → increased academic activities.
- Indian languages became subjects of study.
- Facilities for **higher studies and research** created.
- Post of **Professor** created in universities.
- **Departments of Education** opened in Calcutta and Dacca universities.
- **Academic Council** created for curriculum, examination, and research.
- **1925:** An **Inter-University Board** was set up to coordinate among Indian universities.
- For the **first time**, students' welfare became important — a **Board of Students' Welfare** formed in each university.

> **Criticism:** Some said the recommendations were "too advanced" for the time and unsuitable for existing conditions in India.

---

### 2.5 Education Under Dyarchy (Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms)

Under the **Montagu-Chelmsford reforms:**

- Education was **shifted to provincial ministries** (transferred subject).
- The central government **stopped taking direct interest** in educational matters.
- Government grants, which had been liberally given since 1902, were **now stopped.**

**Result:** Financial difficulties prevented substantial expansion. Education still grew, mainly due to **philanthropic (charity-based) efforts.**

---

### 2.6 Hartog Committee (1929)

The **Hartog Committee** was set up as an auxiliary committee of the **Simon Commission**, under **Phillip Hartog**, to examine education standards.

#### 🔴 Primary Education — Major Problems Found:

**Wastage** *(students dropping out mid-way, making the investment pointless)*:
Caused by:
- Poverty
- Ignorance
- Caste barriers
- Religious conservatism
- Seasonal diseases
- Agricultural failure

**Stagnation** *(children being held back in the same class for more than one year, leading to dropout)*:
Caused by:
- Most schools run by a **single teacher**
- Teachers not well qualified
- Lack of **women teachers**
- Schools often **short-lived**
- Lack of **school inspection**

**Hartog's Recommendations for Primary Education:**

- Government to be responsible for **control and inspection** of primary schools.
- Primary education to be for **four years.**
- School timings and curriculum to suit the **local environment.**
- **Refresher courses and training** for primary teachers.
- More frequent and efficient **school inspection.**
- Primary schools to serve as **community centres** for adult education, medical relief, and recreation.

#### 🔴 Secondary Education — Problems & Solutions:

- **Highly examination-oriented;** too many failures in matriculation exam.
- Recommended: **Diversified curriculum;** alternative courses for students based on aptitude.
- Inclusion of **industrial and commercial subjects.**

#### 🔴 Higher Education:

- Low standards; **overcrowding** in universities; libraries poorly equipped.
- Recommended: Affiliated universities alongside unitary and residential ones; better libraries; **honours courses;** merit-based admission.

#### 🔴 Women's Education:

- Boys' and girls' education to get **equal importance.**
- More **primary schools for girls.**
- Curriculum for girls to include **hygiene, home science, and music** at secondary level.
- Focus on **training women for teaching.**

---

### 2.7 Wardha Scheme of Basic Education (1937) — Nai Talim 🌿

The **Indian National Congress** organised an **All India Education Conference** in **October 1937 in Wardha.**

Based on resolutions passed there, the **Zakir Hussain Committee** formulated a national scheme for basic education — popularly called **"Nai Talim"** (New Education).

**The Philosophical Foundation — Gandhi's Vision:**

Gandhiji wrote a series of articles in the weekly **Harijan** articulating his views on education:

> *"I am fully convinced that the present system of education is not only wasteful but positively harmful... English has created a permanent bar between the highly educated few and the uneducated many."*

Gandhi believed Western education had:
- Created a **gulf** between the educated few and the masses.
- Made the **educated elite ineffective** in connecting with ordinary Indians.

**Core Principle: "Learning Through Activity"**

**Key Provisions of the Wardha/Nai Talim Scheme:**

- (i) **Free and compulsory education** for all from **ages 7 to 14.**
- (ii) Inclusion of a **basic handicraft** in the syllabus — education through productive activity (spinning, weaving, carpentry, agriculture, pottery, leather work, home science for girls, etc.)
- (iii) First **seven years of schooling** to be a free and compulsory nationwide system, taught through the **mother tongue.**
- (iv) **Hindi** to be taught in areas where it was not the mother tongue.
- (v) Schools to establish **contact with the surrounding community** through service.
- (vi) **Social and scientific implications** of a craft to be studied.
- (vii) Curriculum to include: **Mathematics, General Science, Social Studies, Painting, Music, and Physical Education.**
- (viii) **No religious or moral education** included.

> This was not just an education method — it was a **vision for a new society**: independent, non-violent, child-centred, and cooperative.

**Why did it fail to fully develop?**
- The **Second World War** began.
- **Congress ministries resigned** in October 1939.

---

### 2.8 Sargent Plan of Education (1944)

**Sir John Sargent**, Educational Advisor to the Government of India, was asked to draw up a plan for post-World War II Indian education. His **memorandum was submitted to the Central Advisory Board of Education in 1944.**

**Aim:** Achieve **universal literacy in India within 40 years** of the plan's introduction.

**Key Recommendations:**

| Level | What was Proposed |
|---|---|
| **Pre-primary (ages 3–6)** | Free pre-primary education; women teachers; aim = social experience for children. |
| **Junior Basic (ages 6–11)** | Universal, compulsory, free education; "learning through activity." |
| **Senior Basic (ages 11–14)** | Universal, compulsory, free; emphasis on a basic craft suited to local needs; physical training and organised games. |
| **High School (ages 11–17, selected students)** | 6-year course for students with exceptional aptitude; two streams: *Academic* (Arts & Pure Sciences) and *Technical/Vocational* (Applied Sciences, Industrial/Commercial); mother tongue as medium, English as compulsory second language; fees charged but scholarships available for the poor. |
| **University** | 3-year degree after higher secondary; intermediate course to be abolished; merit-based admissions; improved teacher service conditions; an **all-India body on the lines of the University Grants Committee of England** to coordinate universities. |

**Other Recommendations:**
- Adult illiteracy to be liquidated in **20 years.**
- Adult education to include **general and technical/vocational** content, not just literacy.
- **Medical check-ups** for school students.
- **Special education** for physically handicapped and mentally retarded children.
- Setting up of **employment bureaus.**

> **Criticism of the Sargent Plan:** Many Indian educationists felt:
> - 40 years for universal primary education was **too long.**
> - The cost was **too high (exorbitant).**
> - Aiming for England's education standards in India was **utopian and unsuitable** for Indian conditions.
>
> In hindsight, the plan was realistic and had several good points — but it was *ahead of its times.*

## PART 3: Development of Vernacular Education

---

| Year/Period | Development |
|---|---|
| **Early 19th Century** | Vernacular education in poor state; dependent on wealthy zamindars' charity. |
| **1835, 1836, 1838** | **William Adam's Reports** on vernacular education in Bengal and Bihar — identified major defects in the system. |
| **1843–53** | **James Thomson** (Lt. Governor, NW Provinces) opened model government schools in each tehsildari and normal schools for teachers' training. |
| **1853** | **Lord Dalhousie's Minute** — strongly favoured vernacular education. |
| **1854** | **Wood's Despatch** — provided for improvement of standards, government supervision, and normal schools to train teachers. This gave a major boost. |
| **1854–71** | Government paid some attention; number of vernacular schools increased **more than five-fold.** |
| **1882** | **Hunter Commission** — State should make special efforts for vernacular mass education. |
| **1904** | Education policy put **special emphasis** on vernacular education and **increased grants.** |
| **1929** | **Hartog Committee** — painted a **gloomy picture** of primary education. |
| **1937** | Vernacular schools received encouragement from **Congress ministries.** |

> **Critical turning point:** In **1844**, it was declared that applicants for **government employment** must possess knowledge of **English.** This single decision dealt a devastating blow to traditional Indian learning and vernacular education.

## PART 4: Development of Technical Education

---

| Year | Institution |
|---|---|
| **1835** | Medical College established in **Calcutta.** |
| **1847** | **Engineering College at Roorkee** set up. |
| **1856** | **Calcutta College of Engineering** established. |
| **1858** | Overseers' School at Poona raised to **Poona College of Engineering**, affiliated to Bombay University. |
| **1858** | **Guindy College of Engineering** affiliated to Madras University. |

> **Lord Curzon's Contribution:** Curzon broadened the basis of professional courses — **medicine, agriculture, engineering, veterinary sciences.** He established an **Agriculture College at Pusa**, which became the parent institution for similar colleges in other provinces.

> **Critical Fact:** By **1857**, there were only **three medical colleges** (Calcutta, Bombay, Madras) and **only one good engineering college** at Roorkee — which was **open only to Europeans and Eurasians.** Indians were excluded.

## PART 5: Critical Evaluation of British Education Policy

---

The British education policy was **never truly philanthropic.** Here is a balanced critical assessment:

### 🔴 Why Did the Government Promote Education? (Real Motives)

- **Pressure from enlightened Indians, missionaries, and humanitarian officials** forced the government's hand.
- **Need for cheap, educated Indians** to man subordinate posts in administration and British commercial enterprises — hence the emphasis on English as the medium.
- **Hope that educated Indians** would help **expand the market for British manufactures** in India.
- **Expectation** that Western education would **reconcile Indians to British rule** — especially as it glorified British conquerors and their administration.

> In short: **The British used modern education to strengthen their political authority in India** — not to genuinely uplift Indians.

### 🔴 The Negative Consequences:

1. **Decline of Traditional Indian Learning:** Especially after **1844**, when knowledge of English became mandatory for government jobs.

2. **Widespread Illiteracy:** Mass education was neglected.
   - **1911:** Illiteracy rate = **84%**
   - **1921:** Illiteracy rate = **92%**
   - This created a wide **linguistic and cultural gulf** between the educated few and the masses.

3. **Education Became a Class Monopoly:** Since education had to be paid for, it became accessible mainly to **upper and richer classes** and **city dwellers.**

4. **Near-Total Neglect of Women's Education:** Because:
   - (i) The government did not want to **arouse the anger of orthodox sections** of society.
   - (ii) Women's education had **no immediate utility** for colonial rule.

5. **Scientific and Technical Education Neglected:** Only three medical colleges and one engineering college (open only to Europeans/Eurasians) by 1857.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Examples — Added for Contemporary Relevance]`

---

### Connecting "Downward Filtration Theory" Failure → Modern Literacy Efforts

The failure of the British "trickle-down" education model is why independent India had to build from scratch.

- **[Modern Example]** India's **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020** directly addresses the colonial legacy by:
  - Mandating **mother tongue/regional language as the medium of instruction** up to Grade 5 (and preferably Grade 8) — directly reversing Macaulay's 1835 English-only policy.
  - Emphasising **vocational education from Grade 6** — echoing the spirit of Gandhi's Nai Talim/Wardha Scheme.
  - Proposing a **5+3+3+4 curricular structure** — a rethinking of the colonial-era school structure first debated by the Sadler Commission.

---

### Connecting Wood's Despatch → Modern University Grants System

- **[Modern Example]** The Sargent Plan (1944) recommended *"an all-India organisation on the lines of the University Grants Committee of England."* This vision was realised when India established the **University Grants Commission (UGC) in 1956** under the UGC Act.
- In **2023**, the UGC issued new draft regulations for universities, including significant changes to degree requirements and credit systems aligned with NEP 2020.

---

### Connecting Neglect of Primary Education → Current Initiatives

The same problems identified by the Hartog Committee (1929) — wastage, stagnation, poor quality — persisted for decades.

- **[Modern Example]** The **Right to Education (RTE) Act, 2009** (Article 21A of the Constitution) — making free and compulsory education a **Fundamental Right** for children aged **6 to 14** — is the direct fulfilment of what Gokhale demanded in his **1910 Resolution and 1911 Bill**, more than 100 years earlier.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Annual Status of Education Report (ASER) 2023** found that while enrolment has improved, **learning outcomes remain poor** — the same quality vs. quantity problem the Hartog Committee warned about in 1929.

---

### Connecting Women's Education Neglect → Present Data

The British deliberately neglected women's education. Today, India is working to reverse this:

- **[Modern Example]** **Beti Bachao, Beti Padhao (2015)** scheme directly targets female education and gender gap in enrolment, particularly in states like Haryana, Rajasthan, and UP.
- **[Modern Example]** According to **UDISE+ 2022-23 data**, the **Gender Parity Index (GPI)** at the secondary level has improved significantly — female enrolment at secondary level now exceeds male enrolment in several states — a remarkable reversal from the colonial era.

---

### Connecting Technical Education Neglect → Make in India / Skill India

- **[Modern Example]** The colonial neglect of technical education is one reason India struggled with industrial development post-Independence. Today, **Skill India Mission (2015)** and the establishment of **new IITs and IIMs** are direct policy responses to this historical deficit.
- **[Modern Example]** The **National Institutional Ranking Framework (NIRF) 2024** ranks Indian institutions to promote quality improvement in higher education — addressing the "low standards" problem that committees from Hunter (1882) to Hartog (1929) repeatedly flagged.
