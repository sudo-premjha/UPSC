# 📖 Chapter 36: The Evolution of Nationalist Foreign Policy

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Big Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. India's foreign policy did **not begin in 1947** — it was shaped over **70+ years of nationalist thinking** under colonial rule, evolving through phases of anti-imperialism, pan-Asianism, socialism, and anti-fascism.
> 2. At independence, India was already a member of **51 international organisations** and signatory to **600+ treaties** — it entered the world stage with diplomatic experience, not as a newcomer.
> 3. **Panchsheel (1954)** — the Five Principles of Peaceful Co-existence — was the philosophical cornerstone of India's engagement with the world, though its actual origin involved a fascinating three-way negotiation between Nehru, Zhou Enlai, and diplomat T.N. Kaul.
> 4. **Non-Alignment** was NOT neutrality — it was an *active*, *positive* policy of refusing to join either the US-led or Soviet-led military blocs while engaging constructively with all nations.
> 5. The **five founding leaders of NAM** — Nehru (India), Tito (Yugoslavia), Nasser (Egypt), Nkrumah (Ghana), Sukarno (Indonesia) — represented the largest collective assertion of post-colonial sovereignty in history.

## PART 1: INDIA'S DIPLOMATIC HEAD START

---

### 1.1 India Was Already "In the World" Before 1947

A common misconception is that India's international engagement began at independence. The reality is strikingly different.

**By independence, India was:**
- A member of **51 international organisations**
- A signatory to **600+ treaties**

**Historical diplomatic milestones:**

| Year/Period | Event |
|-------------|-------|
| **Post-WWI** | India signed the **Versailles Treaty** — as a result of contributing **more than a million soldiers** to WWI |
| **1920s** | India was a **founding member** of: the **League of Nations**, the **International Labour Organisation (ILO)**, and the **International Court of Justice** |
| **1921-22** | India participated in the **Washington Conference on Naval Armaments** |
| **From 1920** | An **Indian High Commissioner** was stationed in London |
| **Pre-WWI** | Indian nationals were already **staffing some diplomatic posts** |

> 💡 **Result:** Indians formed the **largest and most influential non-Western contingent** in the United Nations and allied agencies very soon after independence — this was no accident. It was built over decades.

---

### 1.2 How Nationalist Foreign Policy Evolved

**The starting point:** Western influence on Indian nationalist intellectuals created a **growing interest in and contact** with dominant international events and currents.

**The key realisation:** Nationalist thinkers gradually understood that **colonialism and imperialism had an international character** — they were not just local Indian problems but part of a global system.

This realisation gave birth to a **nationalist foreign policy perspective** — which evolved through four distinct phases.

---

## PART 2: PHASE 1 — FROM 1880 TO FIRST WORLD WAR: ANTI-IMPERIALISM AND PAN-ASIAN FEELING

---

### 2.1 Opposing British Imperial Adventures

After **1878**, the British undertook several **expansionist expeditions** — all of which were **opposed by Indian nationalists**:

- **Second Afghan War (1878-80)**
- **1882**: England dispatched troops to suppress the **nationalist uprising by Colonel Arabi in Egypt**
- **Annexation of Burma (1885)**
- **Invasion of Tibet under Curzon (1903)**
- **Several annexations during the 1890s** in the north-west to stop the Russian advance

The nationalists supported the **tribal resistance** to these British adventures.

**Congress President C. Sankaran Nair (1897)** articulated the alternative vision:
> *"Our true policy is a peaceful policy."*

---

### 2.2 Two Emerging Themes: 1880-1914

#### Theme 1: Solidarity with Other Colonies

Nationalists expressed **solidarity with other nations fighting for freedom**, including:

- Russia, Ireland, Egypt, Turkey, Ethiopia, Sudan, Burma, and Afghanistan

> 💡 **Key Insight:** Indian nationalists understood early that their struggle was **part of a global anti-colonial wave** — not an isolated local uprising.

#### Theme 2: Pan-Asian Feeling *(feeling of shared identity among Asian nations)*

This sentiment was expressed through:

- **Condemnation of the annexation of Burma (1885)**
- **Inspiration from Japan** as an example of industrial development by a non-Western nation
- **Condemnation of Japan's participation** in the international suppression of the **I-Ho-Tuan uprising (1895)**
- **Condemnation of imperialist efforts to divide China**
- The **defeat of Czarist Russia by Japan** — which **exploded the myth of European superiority** — was celebrated
- **Congress support for Burma's freedom**

---

## PART 3: PHASE 2 — WORLD WAR I (1914-1918)

---

### 3.1 Initial Support — Then Disillusionment

**During the War:**
- Nationalists **supported the British Indian Government** — believing Britain would apply the same **principles of democracy** for which they claimed to be fighting

**After the War:**
- Congress **insisted on being represented at the Peace Conference**
- **1920**: Congress urged people **not to join the army** to fight in the West
- **1925**: Congress **condemned the dispatch of the Indian Army** to suppress the **Chinese nationalist army under Sun Yat-Sen**

> ⚠️ The gap between Britain's democratic rhetoric and its colonial actions deepened nationalist disillusionment — and sharpened the demand for self-rule.

---

## PART 4: PHASE 3 — 1920s AND 1930s: IDENTIFYING WITH SOCIALISTS

---

### 4.1 Key International Contacts

Indian nationalist leaders actively built connections with socialist and progressive movements worldwide:

| Leader | International Connection |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Nehru** | In Europe in **1926-27**; made contact with socialists and leftist leaders |
| **Dadabhai Naoroji** | Attended the **Hague session of the International Socialist Congress**; close friend of **H.M. Hyndman** (famous socialist) |
| **Lajpat Rai** | Made contacts with **American socialists** during his US visit (**1914-1918**) |
| **Gandhi** | Had close relations with **Tolstoy** and **Rolland Romain** |

---

### 4.2 The Brussels Conference (1927) — A Pivotal Moment

**1927**: Nehru attended the **Congress of Oppressed Nationalists at Brussels** on behalf of the Indian National Congress.

**What was this conference?**
- Organised by political exiles and revolutionaries from **Asia, Africa, and Latin America**
- All were suffering under political and economic imperialism

**Nehru's role:** One of the **honorary presidents**, alongside luminaries including:
- **Einstein**
- **Madam Sun Yat-Sen**
- **Rolland Romain**
- **George Lansbury**

**What Nehru understood at Brussels:**
- The **international character of US imperialism**
- Nehru was also **nominated to the executive council of the League Against Imperialism**

**Congress's institutional response:**
- Decided to **open a foreign department** to stay in touch with other peoples' movements

---

### 4.3 Nehru's Visit to Soviet Union (1927)

- Nehru visited the **Soviet Union in 1927**
- Was **very impressed** by the achievements of the "infant socialist state"
- Came to see **Russia as a bulwark** *(a protective force)* **against imperialism**

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: India and Socialism

> `[Modern Example]` The socialist inspiration of 1920s-30s nationalist foreign policy finds its constitutional expression in the word "**socialist**" inserted into the **Preamble of the Constitution** by the **42nd Amendment, 1976**. In **Waman Rao vs. Union of India (1981)**, the Supreme Court upheld this insertion. More recently, in **2024**, petitions challenged the word "socialist" in the Preamble — the **Supreme Court (November 2024)** dismissed these petitions, reaffirming that "socialist" as used in the Indian Constitution does not mean Soviet-style state socialism but a commitment to social and economic justice. This connects directly to the Nehruvian synthesis of socialism and liberal democracy developed in the 1920s-30s.

---

## PART 5: PHASE 4 — AFTER 1936: ANTI-FASCISM

---

### 5.1 Fascism as Capitalism's Extreme Form

The **1930s** saw the rise of **Fascism** *(extreme authoritarian nationalism, anti-democratic)* in Europe.

**Indian nationalist understanding:**
- Imperialism and Fascism were seen as **two organs of capitalism** — different expressions of the same exploitative system

**Practical solidarity:**
- Nationalists lent support to **anti-fascist struggles** in:
  - **Ethiopia**
  - **Spain**
  - **China**
  - **Czechoslovakia**

---

### 5.2 Key Positions Taken

**1939 — Tripuri Session of Congress:**
- Congress **dissociated itself from British policy** which was seen as supporting fascism in Europe

**1939 — Japanese attack on China:**
- **Condemned by nationalists**
- Congress **sent a medical mission under Dr Atal to China** — a remarkable act of international solidarity

**On the Palestine Issue:**
Congress took a nuanced, principled position:
- **Supported Palestinians**
- **Expressed sympathy with Jews**
- BUT urged that **Palestinians not be displaced**
- Called for the issue to be settled by **direct dealing between Jews and Arabs** — without Western intervention
- **Opposed the partition of Palestine**

> 💡 This 1930s Congress position on Palestine is remarkably similar to the position India maintains today — supporting a two-state solution and Palestinian rights while maintaining relations with Israel.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: India and Palestine

> `[Modern Example]` India's consistent support for Palestinian rights — rooted in the 1930s Congress position — continues today. India voted **in favour of the UN General Assembly resolution (October 2023)** calling for a humanitarian ceasefire in Gaza, even as it simultaneously maintained its strategic relationship with Israel. India abstained on some earlier votes but the overall trajectory reflects the same principled position established in the 1930s: supporting Palestinian rights without endorsing violence, and calling for a negotiated two-state solution. India's **recognition of Palestine as a state (1988)** and its current status as a **non-permanent member of the UN Security Council (2021-22)** reflect this long-standing foreign policy tradition.

## PART 6: NEHRU AS THE ARCHITECT OF INDEPENDENT INDIA'S FOREIGN POLICY

---

### 6.1 Nehru's Core Vision

Nehru is widely called the **architect of independent India's foreign policy**.

**His foundational address to the Constituent Assembly on December 4, 1947:**

> *"...the art of conducting the foreign affairs of a country lies in finding out what is most advantageous to the country. We may talk about peace and freedom and earnestly mean what we say. But in the ultimate analysis, a government functions for the good of the country it governs, and no government dare do anything which in the short or long run is manifestly to the disadvantage of the country."*

**Two things Nehru realised India needed:**

1. **Direct contact and cooperation** with other nations to enhance world peace and freedom
2. **An independent identity** — not becoming a satellite *(dependent follower)* of any other nation, however powerful

---

### 6.2 The Strategic Logic of Non-Alignment

**Nehru's challenge:** Evolve a policy that could help India **compete on the world stage** with modern states — which required:

- A **drastic socio-economic and technological transformation** of the country
- Achieving this transformation **without becoming dependent** on any particular country or group of countries to the extent of losing independence of thought or policy

**What India needed:**
- **Peaceful relations with all nations** → to concentrate on developmental efforts
- **Good enough relations** to get necessary help for development → without compromising freedom

> **Conclusion:** In these circumstances, **non-alignment was both prudent AND pragmatic**.

## PART 7: PANCHSHEEL — THE FIVE PRINCIPLES OF PEACEFUL CO-EXISTENCE

---

### 7.1 The Formal Birth of Panchsheel

> **Date:** April 29, 1954
> **Occasion:** Agreement on Trade and Intercourse between the **Tibet region of China** and **India**

The **preamble** of this agreement stated that both governments resolved to enter it on the basis of **five principles** — collectively known as **Panchsheel**:

| # | Principle |
|---|-----------|
| **(i)** | **Mutual respect** for each other's territorial integrity and sovereignty |
| **(ii)** | **Mutual non-aggression** |
| **(iii)** | **Mutual non-interference** |
| **(iv)** | **Equality and mutual benefit** |
| **(v)** | **Peaceful co-existence** |

---

### 7.2 Panchsheel Goes Global

**June 1954:** Chinese Premier **Zhou Enlai** visited India. He and **Nehru** issued a **joint statement** elaborating their vision of Panchsheel as:
- The framework for **India-China relations**
- The basis for relations with **all other countries**

Their joint hope:
> Panchsheel *"will also help in creating an area of peace which as circumstances permit can be enlarged thus lessening the chances of war and strengthening the cause of peace all over the world."*

---

### 7.3 The Real Story Behind Panchsheel — Who Gets the Credit?

> 📜 *(Based on an article by V.V. Paranjpe, formerly Chinese language expert to the Government of India, in The Hindustan Times, June 2004)*

**The popular belief:** Panchsheel was Nehru's contribution to the world.

**The more accurate story:** It was a three-way process:

**Step 1 — Zhou Enlai propounds:**
- On **December 31, 1953**, while receiving the Indian delegation for Tibetan trade talks, **Zhou Enlai** enunciated them as *"five principles governing China's relations with foreign countries"*

**Step 2 — T.N. Kaul picks them up:**
- **T.N. Kaul** (joint secretary in the External Affairs Ministry / Director General for Asian Affairs in Delhi) was deeply impressed
- He conveyed the significance of these principles to **Nehru**, with whom he enjoyed a close rapport
- Nehru agreed
- Kaul **took the initiative to include them at the very outset of his draft text** of the agreement — in **January 1954**

**Step 3 — A compromise is reached:**
- The Chinese foreign office's **initial response was negative** (Zhou wasn't in China at the time)
- When **Zhou returned to Peking (Beijing)**, he found a **via-media** *(middle path/compromise)*:
  - The five principles would NOT appear prominently in the main text
  - But they COULD appear in the **preamble**
  - India accepted this compromise

**Step 4 — Nehru propagates:**
- Two months later, when Zhou visited Delhi, **Nehru and Kaul emphasised these principles** in the joint statement of **June 18, 1954**
- "**China's hesitant formulation caught worldwide attention because of Indian sponsorship**"

> 💡 **Perfect Summary:** *"Zhou propounded the principles, but Kaul picked them up and Nehru propagated them."*

> **A note on language:** The word **'sheel'** in classical language does not mean 'principle' but **'character'**. The term comes from **Indonesian usage**, which may have been influenced by the **Buddhist use of the word 'sheel'**.

---

### 7.4 Panchsheel's Journey into Global Law

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **April 1955** | Panchsheel incorporated into the **Ten Principles of International Peace and Cooperation** at the **Bandung Conference** of **29 Afro-Asian countries** |
| **December 11, 1957** | A resolution on peaceful co-existence presented by **India, Yugoslavia, and Sweden** was **unanimously adopted by the UN General Assembly** |
| **1961** | The **Conference of Non-Aligned Nations in Belgrade** accepted Panchsheel as the **basic principles at the centre of the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM)** |

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Panchsheel Today

> `[Modern Example]` The **70th Anniversary of Panchsheel** was marked in **June 2024**. India-China relations have been severely tested since the **Galwan Valley clash (June 2020)**, in which soldiers from both sides died — the first deadly border conflict between the two countries in 45 years. This clash directly violated Panchsheel's principles of mutual non-aggression and mutual respect for territorial integrity. The subsequent **disengagement at Depsang and Demchok (October 2024)** shows the painstaking process of rebuilding trust. PM Modi's meeting with Chinese President Xi Jinping at **Kazan (BRICS Summit, October 2024)** signalled a cautious return to diplomatic engagement — all framed around the same Panchsheel language that Nehru and Zhou coined in 1954.

## PART 8: NON-ALIGNMENT — THE DEFINING POLICY

---

### 8.1 The Post-WWII World India Faced

The world of 1947 was radically different from the pre-war world. Understanding this context is essential to understanding why non-alignment made sense.

**What changed:**

- **UK, France, Germany, Japan** — the pre-war major powers — lay subdued; their empires shrinking fast
- **USA** — had followed an **isolationist policy** *(staying out of world affairs)* before the war — now became **dramatically active** globally
- **USSR** — had acquired **unprecedented influence** in Eastern Europe + gained recognition as a superpower for crushing German might on the **Eastern Front** (where most German military casualties occurred)
- **1945**: USA demonstrated **nuclear weapon capability**
- **1949**: USSR conducted its **own nuclear test**
- **China** was aligned with the Soviet Union until the **mid-1950s**

**The Cold War:**
- The entire developed world divided into **two opposing nuclear-armed blocs**
- **USA and USSR** as **superpowers** *(states with global military/economic dominance)*
- **Pre-war balance of power diplomacy** *(multiple states balancing each other's power)* disappeared
- The **Third World** became a **surrogate field** *(substitute battleground)* for superpower competition
- **Decolonisation** was proceeding rapidly — new independent nations emerging in Asia and Africa

**India's unique position:**
- The **largest country** with the ability to **manoeuvre between the two blocs**

---

### 8.2 Why India Could NOT Simply Choose a Bloc

**Why NOT align with the West?**
- Economically attractive
- BUT would have created a **dependent relationship** — seen by most newly independent nations as **obstructive to self-reliant development**
- At this time, the **Soviet Union did not possess the economic or military support capability** to influence newly decolonised countries — it was the West trying to incorporate them into its strategic grouping

**Why NOT align with the Communist bloc?**
- India had **socialist leanings** — but could NOT visualise a **Chinese-type restructuring** of society and economy
- India was **basically attuned to a liberal democratic political vision**

**Conclusion:**
> **Political non-alignment was therefore prudent as well as pragmatic.**

---

### 8.3 The Five Criteria of Non-Alignment

The **Preparatory Committee of the first non-aligned conference** laid down these five criteria — a country must meet these to be truly non-aligned:

1. Follow an **independent policy** based on peaceful co-existence and non-alignment
2. Have **consistently supported national freedom movements** in other countries
3. **NOT be a member** of multilateral military alliances concluded in the context of superpower conflicts
4. If it has **conceded military bases**, these concessions should NOT have been made in the context of superpower conflicts
5. If it is a member of a **bilateral or regional defence arrangement**, this should NOT be in the context of superpower politics

---

### 8.4 The First NAM Summit (Belgrade, 1961)

**The first Non-Aligned Movement (NAM) summit:**
- Held in **Belgrade, 1961**
- Attended by **36 Mediterranean and Afro-Asian powers**

**Five Pioneering Founding Leaders of NAM:**

| Leader | Country |
|--------|---------|
| **President Tito** (original name: Josip Broz) | Yugoslavia |
| **President Gamal Abdel Nasser** | Egypt |
| **President Kwame Nkrumah** | Ghana |
| **President Sukarno** | Indonesia |
| **Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru** | India |

**Nehru's definition of non-alignment at Belgrade:**

> *"We call ourselves the conference of non-aligned countries. Now the word non-aligned may be differently interpreted but basically it was used and coined almost with the meaning: non-aligned with greater power blocs of the world. Non-aligned has a negative meaning but if you give it a positive connotation it means nations which object to this lining up for war purpose, military blocs, military alliances and the like. Therefore, we keep away from this and we want to throw our weight, such as it is, in favour of peace."*

---

### 8.5 Non-Alignment vs. Neutrality — A Critical Distinction

This is one of the **most frequently tested distinctions** in UPSC:

| Aspect | **Non-Alignment** | **Neutrality** |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Nature** | Active and positive | Passive and inactive |
| **When applicable** | Both in times of **war AND peace** | Basically in times of **war** only |
| **Opinions on issues** | Has **positive and constructive opinions** on international issues | Has **no opinions** — passive by definition |
| **Stance** | Refuses to join military blocs while engaging with all | Stays completely out of all conflicts |
| **India's stand** | **Non-aligned** — firmly asserted | India has never claimed to be neutral |

> ⚠️ **Critical Point:** Non-alignment is NOT the same as neutrality. A neutral state is *passive*. A non-aligned state is *actively engaged* in promoting peace — it just refuses to be a satellite of either superpower.

---

### 8.6 What Non-Alignment Aims to Achieve

Non-alignment as India's foreign policy principle attempts to:

- **Promote international peace**
- **Promote disarmament**
- **Protect territorial independence**
- **Democratise international relations** by ending imperialism and hegemony *(dominance of one powerful country over others)*
- **Establish a just and equal world order**

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: NAM and Non-Alignment Today

> `[Modern Example]` The **19th NAM Summit** was held in **Kampala, Uganda (January 2024)** — India participated, reaffirming its non-aligned credentials. However, the concept of non-alignment has evolved significantly in the 21st century. India's **"Strategic Autonomy"** policy — maintaining independent positions while engaging with the **Quad (USA, Japan, Australia, India)**, **SCO (with China and Russia)**, **BRICS**, and **I2U2 (India, Israel, UAE, USA)** simultaneously — is seen as **"multi-alignment" or "Neo-Non-Alignment"** by scholars. The **Russia-Ukraine War (2022-present)** has been the ultimate test: India has abstained on multiple UN resolutions condemning Russia while continuing to buy Russian oil and maintaining its strategic partnerships with the West. This is a direct continuation of the Nehruvian tradition of protecting national interest through strategic independence.
