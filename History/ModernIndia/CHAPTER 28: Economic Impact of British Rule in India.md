# 📘 Chapter 28: Economic Impact of British Rule in India

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Big Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. **British rule was unique** — unlike earlier invaders, the British *structurally transformed* India's economy to serve British interests.
> 2. **India's global economic share fell from ~23% to ~3%** between the 18th century and Independence.
> 3. **Deindustrialisation** destroyed Indian artisans, handicrafts, and industries — not by accident, but by deliberate policy.
> 4. **Economic Drain** — India's wealth was systematically transferred to Britain without any return benefit.
> 5. **Nationalist economists** like **Dadabhai Naoroji** exposed this exploitation, making poverty a *national political issue*.

---

## 🔍 Introduction: What Made British Rule Different?

Earlier invaders — the Mughals, the Khiljis, the Tughlaqs — conquered India. They may have looted wealth or imposed taxes. But **they did not change the fundamental structure of India's economy**, and they did not drain wealth *out of the country* as a permanent tribute.

The **British were fundamentally different.** They:
- Transformed India's economy into a **colonial economy** (an economy whose structure and functioning is shaped by the interests of a foreign power, not the local population).
- Systematically transferred India's wealth to Britain.

### 📊 The Numbers Tell the Story:
| Period | India's Share of World Economy |
|---|---|
| Early 18th Century | ~**23%** |
| At Independence (1947) | ~**3%** |

This catastrophic decline is the story of this chapter.

---

## 🏭 Part 1: Deindustrialisation — The Ruin of Artisans and Handicraftsmen

**Deindustrialisation** (the process of destroying existing industries without building new ones) was one of the most devastating consequences of British rule.

### 1.1 One-Way Free Trade: The Rigged Game

After the **Charter Act of 1813**, India's markets were forced open to British goods. This allowed **one-way free trade** (trade that benefits only one side).

Here is how the system was rigged against India:

- ✅ **Cheap, machine-made British goods** flooded the Indian market freely.
- ❌ **Indian products**, especially textiles, faced **tariffs of nearly 80%** in European markets — making Indian cloth too expensive to compete.
- After **1820**, European markets were **virtually closed** to Indian exports.
- The **newly introduced railway network** helped British products reach even the remotest Indian villages.
- **Result:** India went from being a **net exporter** (selling more than it buys) to a **net importer** (buying more than it sells).

> 💬 *"The misery hardly finds a parallel in the history of commerce; the bones of cotton weavers are bleaching the plains of north India."*
> — **William Bentinck**

> 💬 *"The armour of the isolated self-sufficient village was pierced by the steel rail, and its life blood ebbed away."*
> — **D.H. Buchanan**

---

### 1.2 No Steps Towards Modern Industrialisation

In countries like **Britain, Germany, and the USA**, when old industries died, new modern industries replaced them. Workers moved from handlooms to factories.

**In India, this did NOT happen.**

- Old livelihoods were destroyed, but **no industrial revolution replaced them**.
- This happened precisely when **Europe was experiencing an intensified Industrial Revolution**.
- Indian artisans were already suffering because **princes and nobles** (their traditional patrons) had adopted **western tastes** and stopped buying Indian-made goods.

---

### 1.3 Ruralisation — Cities Declined, Villages Became Overcrowded

Another ugly consequence of deindustrialisation was **ruralisation** — the movement of people *back* to villages from cities.

- Many artisans, facing poverty and repressive policies (in Bengal, artisans were **paid low wages** and **forced to sell at low prices**), abandoned their crafts.
- They moved to villages and **took up agriculture**.
- This created **increased pressure on land**.
- An **overburdened agriculture sector** became a major cause of poverty.

> 💬 *India's share of world manufacturing exports fell from **27% to 2%** under British rule.*
> — **Shashi Tharoor**, *An Era of Darkness*

---

## 🌾 Part 2: Impoverishment of the Peasantry

The Indian peasant suffered under a **triple burden**: the Government, the Zamindar, and the Moneylender.

### How the System Crushed the Peasant:

- **The Government** enforced the **Permanent Settlement** system, focused only on maximising revenue collection. Little was spent on improving land productivity.
- **The Zamindar**, given increased powers, resorted to:
  - Summary evictions (throwing tenants out without notice)
  - Demanding illegal dues
  - Forcing unpaid labour (**'begar'**)
  - No incentive to invest in agriculture improvement
- **The Moneylender** (often also the village grain-merchant):
  - Lent money to peasants who had to borrow to pay zamindars
  - Forced farmers to sell produce at **low prices** to clear debts
  - Manipulated the **judiciary and law** in his favour

**The Result:** The peasant became **landless**. His suffering multiplied during times of famine and scarcity.

> This triple burden was true not just for zamindari areas, but also for areas under **Ryotwari** and **Mahalwari** systems.

---

## 🏘️ Part 3: Emergence of Intermediaries & Absentee Landlordism

The old zamindar class was replaced by a new, exploitative one.

### Key Facts:
- By **1815**, **half the total land in Bengal** had passed into new hands — merchants, moneylenders, and urban moneyed classes.
- These **new zamindars** had no emotional or traditional ties to the villages.
- They resorted to **land-grabbing** and **sub-infeudation** (creating many layers of middlemen between the actual cultivator and the land owner).
- More intermediaries = more people to pay = heavier burden on the peasant.
- High demand for land pushed **land prices up**, increasing peasant liabilities.
- The new zamindars' interests lay in **perpetuating British rule** and **opposing the national movement**.

---

## 📉 Part 4: Stagnation and Deterioration of Agriculture

Indian agriculture entered a long period of stagnation because:

- The **cultivator** had no money and no incentive to invest.
- The **zamindar** had no roots in the village.
- The **government** spent little on agricultural education, technical knowledge, or rural welfare.
- **Fragmentation of land** due to sub-infeudation made it impossible to introduce modern farming technology.
- Result: **Perpetually low productivity**.

---

## 💀 Part 5: Famine and Poverty

- Famines became a **regular feature** of life in India under British rule.
- These were **not just natural disasters** — they were a direct result of the **poverty unleashed by colonial exploitation**.
- Between **1850 and 1900**, about **2.8 crore (28 million) people died in famines**.

---

## 💰 Part 6: Commercialisation of Indian Agriculture

In the **latter half of the 19th century**, Indian agriculture shifted from being a way of life to a business enterprise — but not for the benefit of Indian farmers.

### What Changed?
- Certain crops were now grown **not for village consumption** but for **national and international markets**.

### Commercial Crops That Were Promoted:
- Cotton, Jute, Groundnut, Oilseeds, Sugarcane, Tobacco
- Condiments, Spices, Fruits, Vegetables
- **Plantation crops** (highest level of commercialisation): Tea, Coffee, Rubber, Indigo — mostly **European-owned**

### Factors That Encouraged Commercialisation:
- Spread of the **money economy**
- Replacement of custom by **competition and contract**
- Emergence of a **unified national market**
- Growth of **internal trade**
- Improvement in **railways and roads**
- Entry of **British finance capital**

### Why Commercialisation Hurt Indian Farmers:

The peasant was living at **subsistence level** (barely surviving) — there was no surplus to invest in commercial crops.

**A clear example:**
- The **American Civil War (1860s)** disrupted cotton supply globally, pushing up cotton prices.
- Indian farmers grew more cotton.
- But **most profits went to intermediaries**, not farmers.
- When prices **crashed in 1866**, the farmers bore the full loss.
- This led to **heavy indebtedness, famine, and the Deccan agrarian riots in the 1870s**.

---

## 🏗️ Part 7: Destruction of Industry & Late Development of Modern Industry

### 7.1 Deliberate Destruction of Existing Industries

> 💬 *"Deindustrialisation was a deliberate British policy, not an accident."* — **Shashi Tharoor**

**Textiles:** The British stopped paying for Indian textiles in **British pounds**. Instead, they paid from **Bengal's own revenue** — at very low rates — doubly impoverishing both the weavers and the peasants.

**Ship-Building:** India had thriving ship-building industries at:
- **Western coast:** Surat and Malabar
- **Eastern coast:** Bengal and Masulipatnam

The British systematically destroyed this:
- British ships contracted by the Company got a **monopoly over trade routes**.
- Indian merchant ships were made to pay **heavy duties** even on coastal routes.
- **1813 Law:** Ships below **350 tonnes** were prohibited from sailing between India and Britain — putting most Bengal-built ships out of commission.
- **1814 Law:** Indian-built ships were **refused recognition as 'British-registered vessels'** — cutting them off from trade with America and Europe.

**Steel:** The British did not allow the Indian steel industry to grow.
- Industries like **Tatas** were forced to produce only a **higher standard of steel** for British use.
- This excluded them from the larger domestic market that demanded lower-quality (cheaper) steel.
- Restrictions on Indian steel imports further hampered growth.

---

### 7.2 Late and Lopsided Development of Modern Industry

Modern industry came to India **very late** and under **unfavourable conditions**.

### First Modern Industries:
| Industry | Year | Location | Founder |
|---|---|---|---|
| First Cotton Textile Mill | **1853** | Bombay | **Cowasjee Nanabhoy** |
| First Jute Mill | **1855** | Rishra, Bengal | (British interests) |

### Why Foreign Capital Rushed to India:
- High profits
- Cheap labour
- Cheap and readily available raw materials
- Ready domestic and neighbouring market
- Diminishing investment avenues in Britain
- Full support from colonial administration

### Indian-Owned Industries:
- **19th century:** Cotton textiles, Jute
- **20th century:** Sugar, Cement

### Challenges Faced by Indian Industries:
- Credit problems
- **No tariff protection** from the government
- Unequal competition from foreign companies backed by strong financial and technical infrastructure
- **Stiff opposition from British capitalist interests**

### Structural Problems Created:
- **Lopsided pattern** — core industries (heavy industry, power generation) were neglected
- **Regional disparities** — some regions favoured over others, hampering nation-building
- **Lack of technical manpower** — no investment in technical education
- However, this phase did see the rise of an **industrial capitalist class** and a **working class** in India

## 🔬 Part 8: The Economic Drain — India's Wealth Transferred to Britain

### What is the 'Economic Drain'?

**Economic Drain** refers to the portion of India's national income that was:
- **NOT available** for consumption by India's own people
- **Transferred to Britain** for political reasons
- With **no adequate economic or material return** to India

> This theory was formally put forward by **Dadabhai Naoroji** in his landmark book ***Poverty and UnBritish Rule in India***.

### Major Components of the Drain:
- Salaries and **pensions of British civil and military officials** (sent back to Britain)
- **Interest on loans** taken by the Indian Government from abroad
- **Profits on foreign investments** in India
- Purchases of **British stores** for civil and military departments
- Payments for **shipping, banking, and insurance** services (which also stunted Indian enterprise in these sectors)

### Scale of the Drain (Nationalist Estimates):
The drain at that time was:
- More than the **total land revenue** collected, OR
- Half the **total government revenue**, OR
- One-third of **total savings**
- In modern terms: equivalent to **8% of India's national product**

### Effect on India:
- **Checked and retarded capital formation** in India
- Accelerated **growth of the British economy**
- The surplus from British economy then re-entered India as **finance capital** — creating a vicious cycle of further drain
- Had immense negative effect on **income and employment** within India

> 💬 *"There can be no denial that there was a substantial outflow which lasted for 190 years. If these funds had been invested in India, they could have made a significant contribution to raising income levels."* — **Angus Maddison**

> 💬 *"Our system acts very much like a sponge, drawing up all the good things from the banks of the Ganges, and squeezing them down on the banks of the Thames."* — **John Sullivan**, President, Board of Revenue, Madras

## 📣 Part 9: Nationalist Critique of the Colonial Economy

### The Early Phase (Before 1860s):
Early 19th-century Indian intellectuals **supported British rule**, believing it would modernise India using technology and capitalist organisation.

### The Turning Point (After 1860s):
Disillusionment set in. Nationalist economists began to **systematically analyse** how British rule was making India poor.

### Key Nationalist Economists:
| Name | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Dadabhai Naoroji** ('Grand Old Man of India') | *Poverty and UnBritish Rule in India* — the Drain Theory |
| **Justice Mahadev Govind Ranade** | Economic analysis and critique |
| **Romesh Chandra Dutt** | *The Economic History of India* |
| **Gopal Krishna Gokhale** | Economic and political critique |
| **G. Subramania Iyer** | Nationalist economic journalism |
| **Prithwishchandra Ray** | Economic analysis |

### Core Arguments of Nationalist Critique:

**1. India is Poor Because of British Policy:**
- India's poverty was **man-made**, therefore **explainable and removable**.
- Poverty was not fate — it was a **national problem of raising productive capacity**.
- This made poverty a **national issue**, rallying all sections of society together.

**2. Industrialisation is the Answer — But Indian Capital, Not Foreign:**
- Development = Industrialisation.
- **Foreign capital** replaces and suppresses Indian capital — it does not encourage it.
- Foreign capital also causes **political subjugation** by creating vested interests that need British protection to survive.

**3. Railways Helped Britain, Not India:**
- Railways were not coordinated with India's **industrial needs**.
- They ushered in a **commercial revolution**, not an **industrial one**.
- Net effect: enabled foreign goods to outsell Indian products.
- As **G.V. Joshi** remarked: *"Expenditure on railways should be seen as an Indian subsidy to British industries."*

**4. One-Way Free Trade and Unfair Tariffs:**
- Handicrafts were exposed to **premature, unequal, and unfair competition**.
- Tariff policy was guided entirely by **British capitalist interests**.
- Taxes **overburdened the poor** while sparing British capitalists and bureaucrats.

**Demands of the Nationalists:**
- Reduction of **land revenue**
- Abolition of the **salt tax**
- Imposition of **income tax**
- **Excise duties** on consumer goods consumed by the rich middle classes
- Complete severance of India's **economic subservience to Britain**
- Development of an **independent economy based on modern industries**

### Political Impact of Nationalist Economic Critique:
- Undermined the British claim that foreign rule was "for India's benefit."
- Exposed the **myth of the moral foundations** of British rule.
- Was a key **stimulant for intellectual unrest** and the **spread of national consciousness** during the **Moderate Phase of the freedom struggle (1875–1905)**.
- By the **first decade of the 20th century**, nationalists moved from demanding a share in power to demanding complete **self-rule** — led prominently by **Dadabhai Naoroji**.

## 🔄 Part 10: Three Stages of British Colonialism in India

**Marxist historians**, especially **Rajni Palme Dutt**, identified **three overlapping stages** of British imperialist rule.

> **Important Note:** Each stage *grew out of* the previous one. Old forms of exploitation never fully stopped — they got *integrated* into new patterns.

---

### 🔴 Stage 1: Period of Merchant Capital (Mercantilism)
**Also called:** Period of Monopoly Trade and Direct Appropriation / Period of East India Company's Domination
**Period:** ~**1757–1813**

**Two Basic Objectives:**
1. Acquire a **monopoly of trade** with India (against other European merchants AND Indian merchants).
2. **Directly appropriate governmental revenues** by controlling State power.

**What Did NOT Change:**
- Administration, judicial system, transport, communication
- Methods of agricultural or industrial production
- Education, culture, social organisation
- Traditional Indian civilisation, religion, caste system, family structure (seen as *no obstacle* to exploitation)

**What DID Change:**
- Military organisation and technology (to consolidate control)
- Administration at the top of revenue collection (to make it more efficient)

**Key Features:**
- **Large-scale drain of wealth** = **2–3% of Britain's national income** at the time
- This wealth **financed Britain's Industrial Revolution**
- No large-scale import of British manufactured goods into India yet
- Indian textile exports actually **increased** in this phase
- But Indian weavers were ruined by the **Company's monopoly** — forced to produce under uneconomic conditions

---

### 🟠 Stage 2: Colonialism of Free Trade
**Period:** **Charter Act of 1813 to 1860s**

After the East India Company became the ruler of most of India, **British industrial capitalists** challenged the Company. They argued that India should now serve *their* interests:
- India as a **market** for British manufactured goods (especially textiles)
- India as a **source of raw materials** (cotton, foodgrains)

**Dominant Features of This Stage:**

1. India's colonial economy was **integrated with the British and world capitalist economy** through free trade — import duties were removed or drastically reduced.
2. **Free entry** was granted to British capitalists for tea, coffee, indigo plantations, trade, transport, mining, and modern industries. The British Indian Government **actively helped** such capitalists.
3. The **Permanent Settlement** and **Ryotwari system** were introduced to transform agrarian structure into a capitalist one.
4. Administration was made more comprehensive to **penetrate villages** — enabling British goods to reach interior India and agricultural products to be extracted from it.
5. **Personal law** was largely left untouched (not affecting colonial economy). But **criminal law, contract law, and legal procedures** were overhauled to promote capitalist commercial relations.
6. **Modern education** was introduced — primarily to provide cheap manpower for expanded administration, and also to create a culture of **loyalty to rulers**.
7. **Tax burden on peasants rose sharply** due to economic transformation and expensive administration.
8. India absorbed **10–12% of British exports** and **~20% of Britain's textile exports**. After 1850, railway stores were imported on a large scale.
9. The **Indian Army** was used for British colonial expansion in **Asia and Africa**.

---

### 🔵 Stage 3: Era of Foreign Investments & International Competition for Colonies
**Period:** From **~1860s** onwards

**Global Changes That Triggered This Stage:**
1. **Britain's industrial supremacy was challenged** by Europe, the USA, and Japan.
2. **Pace of industrialisation accelerated** globally (petroleum for internal combustion engines; electricity for industry).
3. The **world market became more unified** due to revolution in international transport.

**Britain's Response — Tightening Control Over India:**
- **Liberal imperialist policies** were replaced by **reactionary imperialist policies** (visible in the viceroyalties of **Lytton, Dufferin, Lansdowne, and Curzon**).
- Aim: keep out rivals AND attract British capital to India, providing it security.
- **Large British capital invested in:** Railways, government loans, trade, plantations, coal mining, jute mills, shipping, banking.

**Ideological Shift:**
- The idea of preparing Indians for self-government **vanished** (only revived after 1918 due to pressure from the Indian national movement).
- New justification for British rule: **permanent 'trusteeship'** over Indians.
- Indians were declared **"permanently immature"** — a "child people" needing British control.
- Geography, climate, race, history, religion, culture, and social organisation were all cited to declare Indians **unfit for self-government or democracy**.
- This became the ideological justification for permanent colonial rule — **"The White Man's Burden."**

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *These sections connect historical concepts to present-day events and policies.*

### On Deindustrialisation & Industrial Policy:
- The legacy of colonial deindustrialisation is why India launched the **'Make in India' initiative (2014)** — to rebuild domestic manufacturing. India's **manufacturing share of GDP** remains around **17%**, far below China's ~28%, partly reflecting the long shadow of colonial destruction.
- The **Production Linked Incentive (PLI) Scheme (2020–21)**, covering 14 sectors, directly addresses the colonial-era problem of India being only a raw material exporter. [Modern Example]

### On Economic Drain & Global Wealth:
- Economist **Utsa Patnaik** (2018, Columbia University Press) estimated that Britain drained approximately **$45 trillion** from India between 1765 and 1938 — giving modern quantitative weight to Dadabhai Naoroji's Drain Theory. [Modern Example]
- The debate on **colonial reparations** has been reignited globally. In 2021, the **UK Parliament** saw renewed discussion on Britain's colonial wealth extraction, directly citing Patnaik's work. [Modern Example]

### On Peasant Poverty & Land Rights:
- The colonial legacy of zamindari exploitation is why **land reforms** remained a major post-independence agenda. The **PM-KISAN scheme (2019)** — providing ₹6,000/year direct income support to farmers — is a direct modern response to the centuries-old problem of peasant indebtedness. [Modern Example]
- The **Farmers' Protests (2020–21)** against the three Farm Laws echoed the same colonial-era fear of farmers losing control over their produce prices to powerful market intermediaries. [Modern Example]

### On Regional Disparities:
- The colonial pattern of favouring certain regions (Bengal, Bombay, Madras Presidencies) created regional disparities that persist. The **Aspirational Districts Programme** and **PM Gati Shakti (2021)** are modern attempts to address uneven development. [Modern Example]

### On the Famine Legacy:
- Nobel laureate **Amartya Sen's** work (drawing on Bengal Famine of 1943) showed famines are rarely just about food shortage — they are about **entitlement failure**, echoing the nationalist critique that colonial famines were caused by poverty, not just scarcity. India's **National Food Security Act (2013)** and **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PMGKAY, 2020–2023)** directly address this historical food insecurity. [Modern Example]

## 📋 Summary of Key Concepts

### Economic Impacts of British Rule:
| Impact | Key Effect |
|---|---|
| Deindustrialisation | Ruin of artisans, handicraftsmen |
| Ruralisation | Decline of cities, pressure on land |
| Impoverishment of Peasantry | Triple burden: Govt + Zamindar + Moneylender |
| New Land Relations | Absentee landlordism, sub-infeudation |
| Agricultural Stagnation | Low productivity, no investment |
| Commercialisation | Forced cash crop farming, market vulnerability |
| Industrial Destruction | Ship-building, textiles, steel crushed |
| Late Industrialisation | Lopsided, foreign-controlled |
| Economic Drain | Wealth transferred to Britain |
| Famine & Poverty | 2.8 crore deaths (1850–1900) |

### Nationalist Response:
| Theme | Core Argument |
|---|---|
| Cause of Poverty | Man-made, due to colonial policy |
| Solution | Industrialisation via Indian capital |
| Railways | Benefited Britain, not India |
| Free Trade | Unfair and one-sided |
| Political Goal | Complete economic independence |
