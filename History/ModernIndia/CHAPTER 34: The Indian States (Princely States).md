# 📚 Chapter 34: The Indian States (Princely States)

---

## 🗂️ Chapter at a Glance (Read This First!)

> **5 Big Takeaways from this Chapter:**
>
> 1. There were **562 princely states** covering **7,12,508 sq miles** — ranging from tiny Bilbari (27 people!) to massive Hyderabad (as large as Italy).
> 2. British relations with these states evolved through **6 distinct phases** — from subordination to equal federation (which never worked) to final integration.
> 3. **Subsidiary Alliance + Doctrine of Lapse** = the two key tools Britain used to swallow these states.
> 4. **Three states caused the biggest headaches** during integration: **Junagarh, Hyderabad, and Kashmir.**
> 5. **Sardar Patel + V.P. Menon** were the masterminds of integrating 562 states into the Indian Union — one of history's greatest political achievements.

## PART 1: Background — What Were the Indian States?

---

### 1.1 The Scale and Diversity

The **princely states** (also called **Indian states**) were a patchwork of kingdoms that existed alongside British-controlled territory.

**Key Facts at a Glance:**

| Feature | Data |
|---|---|
| Total number of states | **562** |
| Total area covered | **7,12,508 square miles** |
| Smallest example | **Bilbari** — population of just **27 persons** |
| Largest example | **Hyderabad** — as large as Italy; population of **14 million** |

### 1.2 How Were These States Located?

The **East India Company** strategically grabbed the most valuable territories:
- Important **coastal tracts**
- Valleys of **great navigable rivers**
- Tracts **rich in agriculture** and densely populated

What was **left for the Indian states?** Generally, the *"inaccessible and less fertile tracts of the Indian peninsula."*

> In short, the British took the cream — the states got what was left over.

> The **making of Indian states** was shaped by the same historical circumstances that drove the growth of the East India Company's power in India.

---

## PART 2: The Six Phases of British-State Relations

---

### Phase I: The Company's Struggle for Equality from a Position of Subordination (1740–1765)

This was the **early phase** when the Company was still fighting to establish itself as a political power — not yet dominant.

**Key milestones:**

- **1751:** Anglo-French rivalry began with the arrival of **Dupleix.** The East India Company asserted its political identity with the **capture of Arcot (1751).**
- **1757:** The **Battle of Plassey** gave the Company political power second only to the Bengal Nawabs.
- **1765:** With the acquisition of the **Diwani** *(revenue collection rights)* **of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa**, the Company became a **significant political power.**

---

### Phase II: Policy of Ring Fence (1765–1813)

**"Ring Fence"** *(creating protective buffer zones around British territory)* — this policy aimed to **defend Company frontiers** by creating protective zones around them.

**How it worked:**

- **Warren Hastings** fought wars against the **Marathas and Mysore** to push threats away from Company borders.
- The main threats were from:
  - The **Marathas**
  - **Afghan invaders**
- The Company undertook to **organise Awadh's defence** to safeguard Bengal's security — making Awadh a buffer state.

**Wellesley's Subsidiary Alliance** *(states pay for British troops, lose sovereignty)* was an **extension** of this Ring Fence policy — its goal was to **reduce states to dependence** on British India.

**Major powers that accepted Subsidiary Alliance:**
- **Hyderabad**
- **Awadh**
- **The Marathas**

> **Result:** British supremacy was established across much of the subcontinent.

---

### Phase III: Policy of Subordinate Isolation (1813–1857)

In this phase, the **imperial idea grew** and the **theory of paramountcy** *(British claim to supreme authority over all states)* began to develop.

**What changed for Indian states:**

- States were expected to act in **subordinate cooperation** with the British government.
- States **surrendered all forms of external sovereignty** (foreign policy, treaties, war) but **retained sovereignty in internal administration.**
- **British Residents** — who were earlier diplomatic agents of a foreign power — were now transformed into **executive and controlling officers of a superior government.** A massive shift in their role.

**Key Policy Developments:**

- **1833 Charter Act:** Ended the Company's commercial functions; retained political functions. Introduced the practice of **requiring prior approval for all matters of succession** (who inherits a throne).
- **1834:** The **Board of Directors** issued guidelines to **annex states wherever and whenever possible.**

**Dalhousie's Annexations:** This annexation policy reached its peak under **Dalhousie**, who usurped **eight states**, including major ones like:
- **Satara**
- **Nagpur**

---

### Phase IV: Policy of Subordinate Union (1857–1935)

**1858** was the turning point — the **Crown directly took over** from the Company.

**Why did the policy of annexation stop?**

- The Indian states had shown **loyalty during the 1857 revolt.**
- The British saw their potential as **"breakwaters"** *(shock absorbers)* in future political storms.
- **New policy:** Punish or depose a ruler, but **do NOT annex** the state.

**New Rules of the Game After 1858:**

- The **fiction of Mughal emperor's authority** ended completely.
- **All matters of succession** now required **Crown's sanction.**
- A ruler inherited his throne (**gaddi**) not as a matter of right, but as a **gift from the paramount power.**
- The Queen adopted the title **'Kaiser-i-Hind'** *(Queen Empress of India)* — ending the fiction that Indian states were equal, independent, sovereign states.
- **Paramount supremacy** of the Crown implied and required the **subordination of all states.**

**When could the British interfere in a state's internal affairs?**

The British government exercised the right to interfere in states' internal affairs under four justifications:
- Partly in the **interest of the princes themselves**
- Partly in the **interest of people's welfare**
- Partly to **secure proper conditions for British subjects and foreigners**
- Partly in the **interest of the whole of India**

**Modern technology helped British control:** Railways, roads, telegraph, canals, post offices, press, and public opinion all helped the British extend their reach into states.

**International Affairs — Complete British Control:**

The Government of India had **complete and undisputed control** in international affairs:
- It could declare **war, peace, or neutrality** on behalf of states.

> The **Butler Commission (1927)** stated: *"For the purpose of international relations, state territory is in the same position as British territory and state subjects in the same position as British subjects."*

---

#### 🔍 Curzon's Approach (within Phase IV)

**Lord Curzon** had a unique and intrusive interpretation of British-state relations:

- He stretched old treaty interpretations to argue that **princes were servants of the people** who were supposed to work **side-by-side with the Governor General.**
- He adopted a policy of **patronage and 'intrusive surveillance'** *(close, interfering supervision).*
- He believed the relationship was **neither feudal nor federal** — but a unique type, grown from different historical conditions, gradually conforming to a single pattern.
- The new trend: **reduce all states to a single type** — uniformly dependent on the British government and integrated into the Indian political system.

---

#### 🔍 Post-1905 Developments and Chamber of Princes

After 1905, with rising political unrest and nationalist movements:

- A policy of **cordial cooperation** with states began — to use them as a counterweight against progressive and revolutionary developments.
- Based on **Montford Reforms (1921)** recommendations, a **Chamber of Princes (Narendra Mandal)** was set up.

**What was the Chamber of Princes?**
- A **consultative and advisory body** only.
- It had **no say** in the internal affairs of individual states.
- It had **no powers** to discuss matters concerning existing rights and freedoms of states.

**Classification of states for the Chamber:**

| Category | Number of States |
|---|---|
| Directly represented | **109** |
| Represented through representatives | **127** |
| Recognised as feudal holdings or jagirs | Remaining states |

---

#### 🔍 Butler Committee (1927) — within Phase IV

The question of exactly **how much sovereignty and paramountcy** the British had was still undefined. The **Butler Committee (1927)** was set up to examine the nature of this relationship.

**Key Recommendations:**

1. **Paramountcy must remain supreme** and must fulfil its obligations, adapting itself according to the changing needs of the time and progressive development of states.
2. **States should NOT be handed over to an Indian Government** (responsible to an Indian legislature) **without the consent of the states themselves.**

> **Result:** Paramountcy was deliberately left **vague and undefined** — described colourfully as a *"hydra-headed creature"* (a monster with many heads, impossible to pin down) that fed on usage, Crown's prerogative, and the princes' implied consent. This vagueness actually served British interests.

---

### Phase V: Policy of Equal Federation (1935–1947) — A Non-Starter

The **Government of India Act, 1935** proposed an **all-India federation** that would include the princely states.

**What was Proposed:**

| Legislative Body | Total Seats | Seats for Princes |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal Assembly** | 375 | **125** |
| **Council of States** | 160 | **104** |

**Condition for it to come into force:** Ratification by states representing **more than half** of the total population and entitled to **more than half** of the seats in the Council of States.

> **This scheme NEVER came into existence.** With the outbreak of **World War II in September 1939**, it was **dropped altogether.**

---

### Phase VI: Integration and Merger (1947 onwards)

---

#### 6.1 The Road to Integration

After World War II, multiple attempts were made to break the political deadlock between the Congress, the Muslim League, and the British:

- **Cripps Mission (1942)**
- **Wavell Plan (1945)**
- **Cabinet Mission (1946)**
- **Attlee's Statement (February 1947)**

**Cripps' crucial statement:** The British government did **not contemplate** transferring the paramountcy of the Crown to any other party in India.

**The states' own ambitions:** The states tried various schemes to:
- Form a **union of their own.**
- Position themselves as **sovereign** or as a **"third force"** in Indian politics.

**What the June 3 Plan and Attlee's Statement actually said:**
- States were **free to join either of the two dominions** (India or Pakistan).
- **Mountbatten refused** to give sovereign status to any state.

---

#### 6.2 The Role of Sardar Patel and V.P. Menon

**Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel** (in charge of the States' Ministry in the interim cabinet), assisted by **V.P. Menon** (Secretary in the Ministry), executed one of the greatest political feats in history.

**Their Strategy:**

- They **appealed to the patriotic feeling** of rulers to join the Indian dominion.
- The initial ask was limited to just **three areas:**
  - **Defence**
  - **Communication**
  - **External Affairs**
- These were the **exact three areas** that had always been under Crown paramountcy — areas over which states had **no real control anyway.**

> **Result:** By **August 15, 1947**, **136 states had joined the Indian Union.** However, others remained **precariously outside.**

## PART 3: The Three Problem States — Plebiscite and Army Action

---

### 3.1 Junagarh

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Ruler** | Muslim Nawab |
| **Nawab's wish** | Join **Pakistan** |
| **Population** | Hindu majority |
| **People's wish** | Join **India** |
| **Resolution** | A **plebiscite** (direct vote by people) was held → decided in **favour of India** |

> The Nawab's repressive attitude against his own people forced the plebiscite. The voice of the people prevailed.

---

### 3.2 Hyderabad

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Ruler** | The **Nizam** |
| **Nizam's wish** | **Sovereign status** — independent of both India and Pakistan |
| **Initial agreement** | **Standstill Agreement** signed with India in **November 1947** |

**What happened next:**

- Indian troops withdrew.
- The **Nizam's police and stormtroopers (Razakkars)** *(a violent paramilitary force)* took over.
- The Nizam wanted an **outlet to the sea through Goa.**
- **Violence escalated** and **foreign arms** were being supplied to the Nizam.

**Resolution:**
- Indian troops moved back in **1948** — officially described as a **"police action to restore law and order."**
- Hyderabad **acceded to India in November 1949.**

---

### 3.3 Kashmir — The Unfinished Chapter 🔴

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Ruler** | **Hindu prince** (Maharaja Hari Singh) |
| **Population** | **Muslim majority** |
| **Prince's wish** | **Sovereign status** — join neither India nor Pakistan |

**The Chain of Events:**

1. The prince **procrastinated** (delayed making a decision).
2. The newly established **Pakistan sent its forces** behind a front of **tribal militia**, moving towards **Srinagar.**
3. The prince was now forced to sign the **Instrument of Accession (October 1947)** with the Indian Union.
4. The accession was **endorsed by popular leader Sheikh Abdullah.**
5. **Indian troops were despatched** to defend the state against Pakistani raiders.

**The UN Involvement:**

- India complained to the **UN Security Council** about raids from Pakistan.
- India **offered a plebiscite** to settle the status of the state.
- A **ceasefire** was eventually declared.
- **Result:** **84,000 sq km** of Kashmir territory remained **under Pakistani occupation.**

**Constitutional Recognition:**

- The **special status of Jammu and Kashmir** was recognised under **Article 370** of the Indian Constitution.
- This implied a **limited jurisdiction of the Indian Union** over J&K compared to other states.

## PART 4: Gradual Administrative Integration

---

After accession, the government faced a **two-fold problem:**

1. Transforming states into **viable administrative units** (practical governance)
2. **Absorbing them** into the constitutional framework

**Three methods were used to solve this:**

### Method 1: Merging Smaller States into Contiguous Provinces (Listed in Part A)

- **216 smaller states** were incorporated into adjacent provinces.
- Examples:
  - **39 states of Orissa and Chhattisgarh** → merged into **Central Provinces and Orissa**
  - **Gujarat states** → incorporated into **Bombay**

### Method 2: Centrally Administered States (Listed in Part C)

- **61 states** were made **centrally administered** for strategic or special reasons.
- Examples: **Himachal Pradesh, Vindhya Pradesh, Manipur, Tripura, Bhopal**

### Method 3: Creating Five Unions (Listed in Part B)

Five new **unions of states** were created:
1. **United States of Kathiawar**
2. **United States of Matsya**
3. **Patiala and East Punjab States Union**
4. **Rajasthan**
5. **United States of Travancore-Cochin** (later became **Kerala**)

**The Deepening of Integration:**

- Initially, states had only acceded on **Defence, Communication, and External Affairs.**
- Later, they recognised the need for **closer association.**
- The **five unions and Mysore** accepted Indian jurisdiction over **Union and Concurrent subjects** (except taxation), subject to the differences under **Article 238** and supervisory power of the Union for **10 years.**

**The Final Step — Seventh Amendment (1956):**

- The **Seventh Amendment (1956)** abolished **Part-B states as a separate class.**
- It merged **Parts A and B** into **one single class.**
- All special provisions relating to Part-B states were **deleted.**

> **Result:** The Indian states became part of **one uniform political set-up** — the integration was complete.

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Examples — Added for Contemporary Relevance]`

---

### Connecting Article 370 → Abrogation in 2019

The chapter explains that **Article 370** gave **Jammu and Kashmir** a special, limited-jurisdiction status due to the unique circumstances of its accession.

- **[Modern Example]** On **August 5, 2019**, the Government of India **abrogated Article 370**, removing the special status of Jammu and Kashmir. The state was simultaneously **bifurcated into two Union Territories:**
  - **Jammu & Kashmir** (with a legislature)
  - **Ladakh** (without a legislature)
- **[Modern Example]** The **Supreme Court of India**, in **December 2023**, upheld the abrogation of Article 370 in the case ***Dr. Shah Faesal & Ors. vs Union of India***, ruling that the President had the power to abrogate it. The Court also directed that **statehood be restored to J&K** at the earliest.

> This is a direct, living continuation of the Kashmir issue that began with the **Instrument of Accession in October 1947.**

---

### Connecting Sardar Patel's Integration → Modern Recognition

- **[Modern Example]** **Sardar Patel's birthday, October 31**, is celebrated as the **Rashtriya Ekta Diwas (National Unity Day)** — a recognition of his role in integrating 562 princely states. The tradition was started in **2014** and continues every year with a **Run for Unity** across India.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Statue of Unity** (world's tallest statue at **182 metres**), inaugurated in **October 2018** in Gujarat, was built as a tribute to Sardar Patel — a modern monument to the man who completed India's territorial integration.

---

### Connecting Hyderabad's Police Action → Debates on State Power

- **[Modern Example]** The **declassification of the Sundarlal Committee Report** (which investigated the aftermath of the 1948 Hyderabad police action) has been a subject of historical and political debate in India. The original report, submitted to PM Nehru in 1948, documented significant civilian casualties but was suppressed for decades. Historians and scholars continue to demand its full public release, connecting it to questions of **accountability and historical memory.**

---

### Connecting Chamber of Princes → Inter-State Council

The **Chamber of Princes (1921)** was a consultative body to coordinate between the Centre and the states (princely states then). Today, a similar spirit of cooperative federalism *(states and centre working together)* is institutionalised through:

- **[Modern Example]** The **Inter-State Council** (constituted under Article 263) continues to function as a platform for Centre-State and Inter-State consultations. Its reconstitution and meetings in recent years reflect the ongoing relevance of coordinating diverse political units — the same challenge that the Chamber of Princes tried to address in the colonial era.
