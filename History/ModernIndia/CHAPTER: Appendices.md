## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Big Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. The **Swadeshi Movement** was NOT just a Bengali phenomenon — it had leaders from Punjab, Madras, Maharashtra, UP, and beyond, spanning poets, lawyers, zamindars, and revolutionaries.
> 2. The **Non-Cooperation Movement** was India's first truly **mass national movement** — it drew in peasants, tribal communities, communists, lawyers, novelists, and even industrialists.
> 3. The **Civil Disobedience Movement** saw remarkable **women's participation** — from Sarojini Naidu to teenage assassins Santi and Suniti Chaudhari — marking a permanent shift in the nature of Indian nationalism.
> 4. The **Quit India Movement** produced India's most radical underground resistance — parallel governments, radio stations, and armed rebellions — alongside the **INA's armed struggle** under Subhas Chandra Bose.
> 5. The **Governors-General and Viceroys list** is the single most useful **chronological spine** for Modern Indian History — every major event from 1773 to 1947 can be anchored to a specific administrator's tenure.

## SECTION A: THE SWADESHI MOVEMENT

---

### A.1 Key Leaders and Their Roles

---

#### 🔵 Lokmanya Bal Gangadhar Tilak

- Spread the message of **swadeshi to Poona and Bombay**
- Organised **Ganapati and Shivaji festivals** to arouse patriotic feelings
- Stressed that **swadeshi, boycott, and national education** all aimed at the attainment of **swaraj**
- Opened **cooperative stores**
- Headed the **Swadeshi Wastu Pracharini Sabha**

---

#### 🔵 Lala Lajpat Rai

- Took the movement to **Punjab and parts of northern India**
- Assisted by **Ajit Singh**
- His articles in **Kayastha Samachar** endorsed **technical education and industrial self-sufficiency**

---

#### 🔵 Syed Haider Raza

- Popularised the Swadeshi Movement in **Delhi**

---

#### 🔵 Chidambaram Pillai — The Maritime Swadeshi Hero

This leader deserves special attention for his remarkable story:

- Joined politics following the **partition of Bengal**
- Spread the Swadeshi Movement to **Madras**
- Organised the **strike at the Tuticorin Coral Mill** to protest working conditions
- Founded the **Swadeshi Steam Navigation Company** in **Tuticorin** on the east coast of Madras Province
  - Purpose: To **break the trade monopoly** of the British India Steam Navigation Company
- Bought **SS Lavo and SS Galia** from France and started regular services between **Tuticorin and Colombo (Sri Lanka)**
- His ships flew flags emblazoned with **'Vande Mataram'**
- **1908**: Sentenced to **two life terms for sedition**
- Was reportedly **put to hard labour and even tortured** — NOT treated as a political prisoner
- Sentence reduced by **Madras High Court**; released in **1912**
- But his **lawyer's licence had been revoked** — he could not practice law; opened a shop for livelihood

**As a scholar:**
- Wrote his **autobiography in Tamil verse**
- Wrote a commentary on the **Thirukural**
- Compiled the ancient works of Tamil grammar, **Tholkappiam**

---

#### 🔵 Bipin Chandra Pal

- Of the **Extremist clan**
- Played a major role in **popularising the movement, especially in urban areas**
- Was the editor of **New India**

---

#### 🔵 Liaquat Hussain (Patna)

- Suggested **boycott** and organised the **East Indian Railway strike in 1906**
- Wrote **fiery articles in Urdu** to rouse nationalist sentiments in Muslims
- Supported by Muslim swadeshi agitators: **Ghaznavi, Rasul, Din Mohammed, Dedar Bux, Moniruzzaman, Ismail Hussain, Siraji, Abdul Hussain, and Abdul Gaffar**

---

#### 🔵 Shyamsundar Chakrabarti

- A swadeshi political leader who helped **organise strikes**
- One of the **'Pabna Group'** of Bengali revolutionaries
- Was sub-editor of the revolutionary journal **Sandhya**
- Later joined **Bande Mataram** as assistant to editor **Aurobindo Ghosh**, eventually becoming its **editor**
- **Deported to Burma in 1908** for political activities
- Much later, became a supporter of **non-violent methods** of the Indian National Congress

---

#### 🔵 Ramendra Sunder Trivedi

- Called for observance of **arandhan** *(keeping the hearth unlit — a form of mourning)* as protest on the day the partition was put into effect

---

#### 🔵 Rabindranath Tagore

- Composed **several songs** to inspire the freedom struggle
- **Revived Bengali folk music** to rouse national pride
- Set up **swadeshi stores**
- Called for observance of **raksha bandhan** (tying threads on each other's wrists as a sign of brotherhood)

---

#### 🔵 Aurobindo Ghosh

- Favoured **extending the movement to the rest of India**
- Appointed as **principal of Bengal National College** (founded **1906**) — to encourage patriotic thinking and education rooted in Indian conditions and culture
- Was the **editor of Bande Mataram** — through editorials encouraged strikes, national education, etc.
- Assisted by **Jatindranath Bannerji** and **Barindrakumar Ghosh** (who managed the Anushilan Samiti)

---

#### 🔵 Sakharam Ganesh Deuskar — The Intellectual Backbone

- Originally from **Maharashtra** but domiciled in **Bengal**
- Close associate of **Aurobindo Ghosh**; a good writer in **Bengali**
- Best known for his book **Desher Katha** — described British exploitation of India

**What Desher Katha covered:**
- The state of the country and the good and evil of British rule
- The **mental degradation** of Indians who had been "hypnotised" by the British/West
- The **ruin of peasants and artisans** of Bengal
- The **decline of indigenous industries**

**Influences on Deuskar:** Writings of **Naoroji and Dutt** on India's economic conditions under British rule

**Impact of the book:**
- Proved **immensely popular** and served as inspiration for the Swadeshi Movement
- Government **banned it in 1910** and confiscated all available copies — but by then, it had been read by innumerable people

**Other contributions:**
- Reputed to have **introduced the Shivaji festival in Bengal**
- The **earliest modern political use of the word 'swaraj'** is considered to have been by Deuskar in his **pamphlet on Shivaji (1902)**

---

#### 🔵 Sarala Devi Ghoshal (Chaudarani) — A Pioneer in Every Sense

**Family background:**
- Daughter of **Janakinath Ghoshal** (one of the **founding members of the Indian National Congress**)
- **Niece of Rabindranath Tagore**

**Academic achievement:**
- Pursued higher education at **Bethune College under Calcutta University**
- Became the **first woman** to achieve the highest marks in that university
- Received the **'Padmaboti Swarnapodok'** (the gold medal)

**Early career:**
- Worked as assistant superintendent at the **Maharani Girls School in Mysore**
- Returned to Calcutta and edited the Bengali magazine **Bharati Patrika**

**Revolutionary activities:**
- Wrote **Ahitagnika** — to generate awareness about the freedom struggle among school children
- Launched an **underground revolutionary group** — believed aggression and violence were the only way to get freedom
- Considered to have **contributed to the music of Bande Mataram** and written several other revolutionary songs

**Swadeshi work:**
- Among the **first to promote swadeshi products**
- Opened a shop called **Lakhir Bhandar** in the **Bowbazar neighbourhood of Calcutta** — sold ONLY swadeshi products

**Women's organisation:**
- Founded the **Bharat Stree Mahamandal** — the All India Women's Organisation *(India's first national women's organisation)*
- It was a **semi-revolutionary group** with presence in: **Allahabad, Lahore, Amritsar, Delhi, Karachi, Hyderabad, Kanpur, and Calcutta**

**Later life:**
- Married **Rambhaja Dutta Chowdhury** (a noted freedom fighter from Punjab); moved to Lahore
- Continued revolutionary work in Punjab; opened several associations and organisations for women, including a **home for widows**
- Associated with the newspaper **Hindusthan** as editor and publisher
- Wrote her biography: **Jiboner Jhora Pata** ("the Scattered Leaves of My Life")

### 🌍 Modern Context: Swadeshi Movement

> `[Modern Example]` The spirit of swadeshi was formally revived through the **"Vocal for Local"** and **"Atmanirbhar Bharat"** campaigns launched in **2020** following the COVID-19 pandemic. PM Modi's call to buy Indian-made products echoes Chidambaram Pillai's Swadeshi Steam Navigation Company and Sarala Devi's Lakhir Bhandar. The **Production Linked Incentive (PLI) Scheme** across 14 sectors (with an outlay of ~₹1.97 lakh crore) is the modern institutional equivalent of what Acharya P.C. Roy did by founding Bengal Chemicals. The **Punjab National Bank**, founded by **Lala Harkishan Lal** during the Swadeshi Movement, continues to function as one of India's largest public sector banks — a living legacy of swadeshi economic nationalism.

## SECTION B: THE NON-COOPERATION MOVEMENT (1920-22)

---

### B.1 Gandhi — The Driving Force

- Issued a **manifesto in March 1920** announcing his doctrine of **non-violent Non-Cooperation Movement**
- Urged people to adopt **swadeshi principles** including hand spinning, weaving, and removal of untouchability
- **Addressed lakhs of people** during his nationwide tour in **1921**
- **Suspended the movement** after the violence at **Chauri Chaura in UP in February 1922**

---

| Leader | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|
| **C.R. Das** | Moved the main resolution on non-cooperation at the **Nagpur session (1920)**; boycotted law courts; gave up a lucrative practice. His three key supporters: **Birendranath Samsal** (Midnapore), **J.M. Sengupta** (Chittagong), **Subhash Bose** (Calcutta) — all helped unite Hindus and Muslims |

### B.7 The Anti-Non-Cooperation Group

- **Purushottamdas Thakurdas, Jamnadas Dwarkadas, Cowasji Jehangir, Phroze Sethna, and Setalvad** — all from the industrialist section — launched an **Anti-Non-Cooperation Association in 1920**

### B.8 The Future Prime Ministers Who Started Here

- **Purushottamdas Tandon, Ganesh Shankar Vidyarthi, Govind Ballabh Pant, and Lal Bahadur Shastri** began their political careers in **1920-21** with the onset of the Non-Cooperation Movement

---

### B.9 Premchand — The Novelist Who Resigned

- Well-known novelist **Premchand** resigned his post in a **Gorakhpur government school** in **February 1921** and started contributing to the journal **Aaj**
- His novels **Premasharam, Rangbhumi etc.** reflect **Gandhian principles** and endorse non-cooperation as an effective weapon for freedom

---

### B.10 The Chauri Chaura Trigger

- **Bhagwan Ahir**, an army pensioner in a Gorakhpur village, was **beaten up by the British police**
- This incident flared up nationalist sentiments in the village
- Led to the **killing of 22 policemen in Chauri Chaura** by peasants
- Gandhi used this as justification for withdrawing the movement

### 🌍 Modern Context: Non-Cooperation Movement Legacy

> `[Modern Example]` The tradition of lawyers giving up their practice for a cause — demonstrated by Motilal Nehru, Vallabhbhai Patel, and C. Rajagopalachari — found a modern echo when hundreds of lawyers boycotted courts during various political protests. More significantly, the **peasant movement tradition** pioneered by Baba Ramchandra and Jawaharlal Nehru through kisan sabhas culminated in the **2020-21 Farm Laws Protests** — the largest sustained farmer agitation in post-independence India, involving millions from Punjab, Haryana, and UP (the same regions where Non-Cooperation had its deepest roots). The **repeal of three Farm Laws (November 2021)** showed that mass non-cooperation remains a potent tool of democratic resistance.

## SECTION C: THE CIVIL DISOBEDIENCE MOVEMENT (1930-34)

---

### C.1 Gandhi — The Salt March

- **Formally launched** the Civil Disobedience Movement on **April 6, 1930**
- Picked a handful of salt after completing the historic **Dandi March** from **Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi** — thus breaking the salt law
- Was the major force behind the movement and inspired grassroots participation

---

### C.2 Regional Leaders of the Salt Satyagraha

| Leader | Region/Action |
|--------|--------------|
| **C. Rajagopalachari** | Led a **salt march from Trichinopoly to Vedaranniyam** on the Tanjore coast (Tamil Nadu); arrested **April 30, 1930** |
| **K. Kelappan** | Nair Congress leader; launched the **Vaikom Satyagraha**; marched from **Calicut to Payanneer** in defiance of salt laws |
| **Jawaharlal Nehru** | Actively involved; arrested **April 17, 1930** for defiance of salt law; formulated a **radical agrarian programme**; suggested formation of the **Constituent Assembly** as prime political slogan |
| **P. Krishna Pillai** | Defended the national flag; resisted **lathicharge on Calicut beach (November 11, 1930)**; later founded the **Kerala Communist Movement** |
| **Khan Abdul Gaffar Khan** | Formed the **Khudai Khidmatgars** *(Servants of God)* — known as **Red Shirts** — a clan of non-violent revolutionaries; played an active role |
| **Sarojini Naidu** | First Indian woman president of Congress; involved in a march towards **Dharsana Salt Works** (a government salt depot); other participants included **Imam Saheb** (Gandhi's South African comrade) and **Manilal** (Gandhi's son) |
| **Gopabandhu Chaudhuri** | Popularised the movement in **Orissa**; led salt satyagraha in **Balasore, Cuttack, and Puri districts** |

---

### C.3 Revolutionary Activities

| Leader | Action |
|--------|--------|
| **Surya Sen's Chittagong Revolt Group** | Carried out a **raid on two armouries**; declared establishment of a **provisional government**; issued a manifesto in the name of **Indian Republican Army**; called on Indians to revolt |
| **Santi and Suniti Chaudhari** | **Assassinated the district magistrate of Tippera, Stevens** — marked the **entry of women in the revolutionary movement** |
| **Hemachandra Kanungo** | (Continued from Swadeshi; part of the broader revolutionary network) |

---

### C.4 After Gandhi's Arrest — Abbas Tayabji

- **Abbas Tayabji**, a leader of nationalist Muslims in Bombay, **took Gandhi's place** in the movement after the latter's arrest
- He too was subsequently arrested by the government

---

### C.5 Industrialists — Support and Withdrawal

**Phase 1: Initial Support**

- **G.D. Birla**: Donated between **one lakh and five lakh rupees**
- **Jamnalal Bajaj**: Served as **AICC treasurer for several years**; represented Gandhian leadership in Bombay
- **Homi Modi**: In his presidential speech to Bombay Mill-owners' Association (March 1931), noted that while the Swadeshi Movement helped Indian industry, **frequent strikes had dislocated trade and industry**
- **Walchand Hirachand, Lalji Naranji, Purushottamdas Thakurdas, Lala Sri Ram**: Supported the movement in its first phase
- **Naranji and Thakurdas** (who had been indifferent in 1921) now demanded **Indian control over finance, currency, fiscal policy, and railways**
- **Ambalal Sarabhai and Kasturbhai Lakhai**: Gave cooperation to Motilal Nehru in removing barriers between Congress and Bombay mill-owners

**Phase 2: Sharp Decline (from September 1930)**

- Prominent businessmen had **differences of opinion with the Congress**
- Support from industrialists and traders sharply declined

### C.7 The Ideological Divergence — Left vs. Right

| Group | Leaders | Position |

### 🌍 Modern Context: The Colonial Administrative Legacy

> `[Modern Example]` The **Durand Line** drawn under Lord Lansdowne (1893) remains the most consequential colonial administrative boundary in the world today — unrecognised by Afghanistan, it is the source of perpetual Pakistan-Afghanistan tensions and was the background to the **Taliban takeover of Afghanistan (August 2021)**. The **Radcliffe Line** drawn under Lord Mountbatten (1947) continues to shape India-Pakistan relations; the **Kartarpur Corridor (opened 2019)** — allowing Sikh pilgrims to visit Gurdwara Darbar Sahib in Pakistan — represents a rare moment of humanitarian cooperation across this colonial-era boundary. The **Ancient Monuments Preservation Act (1904)** under Curzon was replaced by the **Ancient Monuments and Archaeological Sites and Remains Act (1958)**, which continues to govern India's 3,691 centrally protected monuments — including the Taj Mahal, whose conservation disputes remain active in the **Supreme Court** to this day.

## 🕌 Section 2: Socio-Religious Reform Movements (Late 18th to Mid-20th Century)

> **How to Study This:** Group movements by religion (Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, Parsi) and by region. Focus on founders, key beliefs, and publications — all are frequently asked in Prelims.

---

### 🔶 Hindu Reform Movements

#### 1. Swaminarayan Sampradaya — Gujarat (Early 19th Century)
- **Founder:** **Swami Sahajanand** (original name: Gyanashyama; 1781–1830)
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Belief in a theistic god (a single personal God)
  - Protest against the **epicurean practices** (excessive pleasure-seeking practices) of Vaishnavism
  - Prescribed a strict moral code of conduct

---

#### 2. Brahmo Samaj — Calcutta (Earlier Atmiya Sabha; Late 18th–Early 19th Century)
- **Key People:**
  - **Raja Rammohan Roy** (1772–1833) — founder
  - **Debendranath Tagore** — later formed **Adi Brahmo Samaj**
  - **Keshub Chandra Sen** — associated with **Brahmo Samaj of India** (secessionists from this group formed **Sadharan Brahmo Samaj**)
- **Key Beliefs and Objectives:**
  - Propagated **monotheism** (belief in one God)
  - Opposed: incarnation, meditation, sacrifices, priesthood, idolatry, superstition, **sati**
  - Sought reforms in Hindu society
- **Publications:**
  - *Sambad Kaumudi* (1821) — by Rammohan Roy
  - *Mirat-ul-Akbar* — by Rammohan Roy
  - *Tattva Bodhini Patrika* — by Debendranath Tagore
  - *Indian Mirror* — by Keshub Chandra Sen
  - *Tattva Kaumudi, The Indian Messenger, The Sanjibari, the Navyabharat, Prabasi* — by Sadharan Brahmo Samaj

---

#### 3. Young Bengal Movement — 1826–31
- **Founder:** **Henry Louis Vivian Derozio**
- **Key People:** Rasik Krishna Mullick, Tarachand Chuckervati, Krishna Mohan Banerjee
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Opposed social vices
  - Believed in **truth, freedom, and reason**
  - Brought out the journal ***Jnanavesan***
  - Established the **Society for the Acquisition of General Knowledge**
  - Derozio also edited: *Hesperus*, *The Calcutta Library Gazette*; associated with *India Gazette*

---

#### 4. Dharma Sabha — Calcutta, 1830
- **Founder:** **Radhakanta Deb** (1794–1876)
- **Nature:**
  - Emerged to **counter the Brahmo Samaj**
  - Aimed at **protection of Hindu orthodoxy**
  - **Condemned** radical and liberal reforms
  - Paradoxically, also **helped spread Western education**

---

#### 5. Paramhans Mandali — 1849
- **Key Objective:** Emphasised **unity of godhead**; stood against **caste rules**

---

#### 6. Prarthana Samaj — Bombay, 1867
- **Key People:** **Atmaram Pandurang** (founder), **Govind Ranade** (chief mentor), **R.G. Bhandarkar**
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Worship and social reform through **monotheism**
  - Upliftment of women
  - Abolition of caste discrimination and religious orthodoxy

---

#### 7. Arya Samaj — Bombay, 1875
- **Founder:** **Dayanand Saraswati** (originally Mula Shankar)
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Asserted Hindu faith; worked within a **revivalist framework** (reviving past Hindu traditions)
  - Denounced: rites, Brahmins' supremacy, idolatry, superstitions
  - Established **Dayanand Anglo-Vedic (DAV) schools**

---

#### 8. Deccan Education Society — Pune, 1884
- **Founders:** **M.G. Ranade, V.G. Chibdonkar, G.G. Agarkar**
- **Contribution:** Education and culture in western India; established **Ferguson College, Pune (1885)**

---

#### 9. Seva Sadan — Bombay, 1885
- **Founder:** **Behramji M. Malabari**
- **Objectives:** Against **child marriages** and **forced widowhood**; to help socially exploited women

---

#### 10. Indian National Social Conference — Bombay, 1887
- **Founders:** **M.G. Ranade, Raghunath Rao**
- **Objective:** Social reform

---

#### 11. Bharat Dharma Mahamandala — Benaras, 1902
- **Key People:** **Madan Mohan Malaviya, Deen Dayal Sharma**
- **Nature:** Orthodox Hindus' organisation; **opposed Arya Samaj's teachings**

---

#### 12. Servants of India Society — Bombay, 1905
- **Founder:** **Gopal Krishna Gokhale**
- **Objectives:** Famine relief; improving tribals' condition

---

#### 13. Poona Seva Sadan — 1909
- **Founders:** **G.K. Devadhar and Ramabai Ranade**
- **Objectives:** Economic uplift; employment for women

---

#### 14. Nishkam Karma Math — Pune, 1910
- **Founder:** **Dhondo Keshav Karve**
- **Objectives:** Educational progress of women; improving widows' condition; founded a **women's university in Pune** (now in Bombay)

---

#### 15. Bharat Stri Mandal — Calcutta, 1910
- **Founder:** **Saralabala Devi Chaudhurani**
- **Objective:** Women's education and emancipation

---

#### 16. Social Service League — 1911
- **Founder:** **Narayan Malhar Joshi**
- **Objective:** Improving the condition of the common masses; opened schools, libraries

---

#### 17. Seva Samiti — Allahabad, 1914
- **Founder:** **Hridyanath Kunzru**
- **Objective:** Improving the status of suffering classes through social service and education

---

#### 18. Ramakrishna Mission — Bengal, 1897
- **Founder:** **Vivekananda** (originally Narendranath Dutta; 1863–1902)
- **Guru:** **Ramakrishna Paramhansa** (1834–86)
- **Centres:** Belur and Mayavati
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Revived Hinduism based on ancient religious texts (Vedanta, etc.)
  - Against: caste restrictions, oppression, superstition in Hinduism
  - Aimed to uplift women and overhaul the education system

---

#### 19. The Indian Women's Association — Madras, 1917
- **Founder:** **Annie Besant**
- **Objective:** Upliftment of Indian women; held **Annual All India Women's Conferences**

---

#### 20. Deva Samaj — Lahore, 1887
- **Founder:** **Shiva Narain Agnihotri**
- **Nature:** Religious ideas closer to **Brahmo Samaj**; favoured a social code of conduct that was against bribe-taking, gambling, alcohol consumption, and non-vegetarian food

---

### 🔷 The Theosophical Society — Founded in New York; Headquarters Shifted to Adyar, near Madras, 1875

- **Founders:** **Madam H.P. Blavatsky** (1831–91, Russian) and **Col. H.S. Olcott** (1832–1907, American)
- **Notable President:** **Annie Besant**
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Drew inspiration from the **Upanishads** and philosophy of **Vedanta**
  - Aimed at **religious revival** and **social reform**

### 🟢 Muslim Reform Movements

#### 1. Wahabi Movement — Founded 1820; Headquarters at Sittana (NWFP) from 1850; Suppressed by British by ~1870
- **Spread:** Rohilkhand, Kabul, NWFP, Bengal, Central Provinces
- **Founder:** **Syed Ahmed of Rai Bareilly**
- **Key People:** Vilayat Ali, Shah Muhammad Hussain, Farhat Hussain, Inayat Ali (all from Patna)
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Popularised teachings of **Waliullah**
  - **Opposed the British** and fought against the Sikhs
  - Stressed the role of **individual conscience in religion**

---

#### 2. Taayuuni Movement — Founded in Dacca, 1839
- **Founder:** **Karamat Ali Jaunpuri**
- **Basis:** Religious teachings of **Shah Waliullah**; opposed the **Faraizi movement**

---

#### 3. The Deoband School of Islamic Theology — Deoband, Saharanpur, UP, 1866
- **Founders:** **Muhammad Qasim Nanautavi** (1832–80) and **Rashid Ahmad Gangohi**
- **Key People:** **Maulana Abul Kalam Azad, Mahmud-ul-Hasan, Shibli Numani**
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Revivalist movement (reviving traditional Islamic scholarship)
  - Liberal interpretation of Islam
  - For moral and religious upliftment
  - Did **not** take to Western influences in education
  - Opposed **Syed Ahmed Khan's** views to some extent
  - Welcomed the formation of the **Indian National Congress**

---

#### 4. Aligarh Movement — 1875 (Aligarh School grew into Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College in 1877, later **Aligarh Muslim University**)
- **Founder:** **Syed Ahmed Khan** (1817–98)
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Religious reform through the **principle of enquiry**
  - Favoured **scientific and rational outlook**
  - Recognised Western education
  - Aimed at **social reform**
- **Publications:**
  - Founded a **Scientific Society (1864)**
  - ***Tahzib-al-akhlaq*** (1870) — Urdu journal

---

#### 5. Ahmadiya Movement — Qadiani, Punjab, 1889
- **Founder:** **Mirza Ghulam Ahmad** (1839–1908)
- **Key Beliefs:**
  - Defended Islam from attacks by Christian missionaries and Hindu revivalists
  - Belief in a **universal religion**
  - Ghulam Ahmad proclaimed himself as a **Messiah** and as an **incarnation of Lord Krishna**

- **Objective:** Debated popular science and social questions

### 🌍 Modern Context — Reform Movements

The **Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Bill, 2021** — raising the minimum marriage age for women to 18 (uniform with men) — is the legislative continuation of the work started by **Behramji Malabari's Seva Sadan (1885)** against child marriages. `[Modern Example]`

> `[Modern Example]` The **Waqf (Amendment) Act, 2024** — reforming management of Muslim religious property — reignited debates about the relationship between religious autonomy and state intervention, mirroring the tensions faced by the **Aligarh Movement** and **Deoband School** in the 19th century. `[Modern Example]`

> `[Modern Example]` The **Ramakrishna Mission's** educational and social welfare network continues to operate hundreds of schools, colleges, and hospitals across India. In **2023**, it was recognised by the UN for its rural development programmes — a direct continuation of Vivekananda's 1897 vision. `[Modern Example]`

### 🌍 Modern Context — Caste Movements

> `[Modern Example]` The legacy of Ambedkar's **Depressed Classes Movement** is directly reflected in the **Constitution's reservation provisions** (Articles 15, 16, 46). The **Supreme Court's 2024 ruling** in *Pankaj Kumar Bharti vs. State of Jharkhand* reaffirmed the **sub-classification of SC/ST reservations** — a debate rooted in the very questions Ambedkar raised in 1924. `[Modern Example]`

> `[Modern Example]` The **Justice Party Movement's** demand for reservations, which led to the **1930 Government Order** in Madras, is the direct ancestor of the **OBC reservation system**. The **2021 Supreme Court ruling** upholding **27% OBC reservation in NEET-PG** continues this century-old journey. `[Modern Example]`

> `[Modern Example]` **Periyar's Self-Respect Movement** of 1925 continues to influence Tamil politics. The **Dravidar Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK)**, which won the **2021 Tamil Nadu Assembly elections**, directly traces its ideology to Periyar's movement. `[Modern Example]`

### 🌍 Modern Context — Peasant Movements

> `[Modern Example]` The **Farmers' Protests (2020–21)** against the three Farm Laws drew direct parallels with the Bardoli Satyagraha of 1928 — both were organised, non-violent resistances against policies seen as threatening farmers' land and price security. The Farm Laws were eventually **repealed in November 2021**. `[Modern Example]`

> `[Modern Example]` The **PM-KISAN Scheme (2019)** — providing ₹6,000/year to farmers — and the **Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY)** directly address the root causes that sparked movements like the **Kheda Satyagraha** (failure of government to provide relief during crop failures). `[Modern Example]`

### 🌍 Modern Context — Press

> `[Modern Example]` The **Bengal Gazette (1780)** began a tradition of press freedom that India now enshrines through **Article 19(1)(a)**. The **Press and Registration of Periodicals Act, 2023** replaced the colonial-era **Press and Registration of Books Act, 1867** — finally modernising the legal framework governing India's press after 156 years. `[Modern Example]`

> `[Modern Example]` *The Hindu*, founded in **1878**, and *Hindustan Times*, founded in **1920**, remain among India's most influential newspapers today — living institutional legacies of the nationalist press. `[Modern Example]`

> `[Modern Example]` Tilak's use of *Kesari* to spread nationalist consciousness parallels the modern debate over **digital media and social media regulation**. The **IT (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021** — which brought online news portals under a regulatory framework — sparked the same questions about **press freedom vs. state control** that Tilak faced in 1897 and 1908. `[Modern Example]`
