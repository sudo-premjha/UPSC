> **5 Key Takeaways to Remember Before You Read:**
>
> 1. **Attlee's Bombshell (February 20, 1947):** Britain set a firm deadline of **June 30, 1948** for leaving India — the first time a clear exit date was announced — shocking all parties into action but also hinting clearly at partition.
> 2. **Mountbatten Plan (June 3, 1947):** The freedom-with-partition formula — provinces of Punjab and Bengal would vote on partition; two dominions (India and Pakistan) would be created; power transferred on **August 15, 1947** — more than a year ahead of the original deadline.
> 3. **The Indian Independence Act (July 18, 1947):** Passed by the British Parliament on **July 5, 1947**, got Royal Assent on **July 18**, and implemented on **August 15, 1947** — creating two independent dominions of India and Pakistan.
> 4. **Integration of States — Patel's Masterwork:** Under **Vallabhbhai Patel**, all princely states except **Kashmir, Hyderabad**, and **Junagarh** signed instruments of accession by **August 15, 1947** — the most rapid political unification in modern history.
> 5. **Partition Was Not Sudden:** It was the culmination of a **step-by-step process of concessions** to the League's demand for Pakistan — from the Cripps Mission (1942) to the 3rd June Plan (1947) — combined with the Congress's long-term failure to fully integrate Muslim masses into the nationalist movement.

# CHAPTER 25: Independence with Partition

---

## 🔍 Part 1: Attlee's Statement of February 20, 1947

### The Context

By early 1947, the situation in India was spiralling out of control:
- The **Congress-League coalition in the Interim Government** was paralysed by the League's obstructionist tactics.
- **Communal riots** were spreading across the country.
- The constitutional deadlock seemed permanent.

British Prime Minister **Clement Attlee**, sensing the trouble all around, made a historic announcement on **February 20, 1947** — the British House of Commons declared Britain's intention to **leave the Indian subcontinent**.

---

### Main Points of Attlee's Statement

- ✅ A **deadline of June 30, 1948** was fixed for transfer of power — **even if Indian politicians had not agreed** on the constitution by that time.
- ✅ The British would relinquish power either to **some form of central government** OR, in some areas, to the **existing provincial governments** — if the constituent assembly was not fully representative (i.e., if the Muslim majority provinces did not join).
- ✅ British powers and obligations towards **princely states** would **lapse with transfer of power** — but these would NOT be transferred to any successor government in British India.
- ✅ **Mountbatten** would replace **Wavell** as the Viceroy.

> ⚠️ The statement contained **clear hints of partition** and even **Balkanisation** (fragmentation into many small states) — and was, in essence, a reversion of the Cripps Offer.

---

### Why Did Britain Fix a Date for Withdrawal?

Four reasons:
1. A fixed date would **shock the parties into an agreement** on the main question.
2. The government was keen to **avert the developing constitutional crisis**.
3. The government hoped to **convince Indians of British sincerity**.
4. Wavell's assessment could no longer be denied — an **irreversible decline of the government's authority** had taken place.

---

### Congress Stand on Attlee's Statement

- The provision for **transfer of power to more than one centre** was acceptable to Congress — it meant the existing assembly could go ahead and frame a constitution for the areas represented by it, offering a way out of the deadlock.
- But illusory hopes of a settlement were **soon shattered** — the statement proved to be a prelude to the final showdown.
- The League felt **emboldened** and launched a **civil disobedience movement** to overthrow the coalition government in Punjab.

---

## ⚡ Part 2: The Road to Partition — How the "Unthinkable" Became Inevitable

### The Shift in Thinking

The **communal riots** and the **unworkability of the Congress-League coalition** compelled many in early 1947 to think about the previously unthinkable idea of **partition**.

**The Demand from Within India:**
- The most insistent demand for partition now came from **Hindu and Sikh communal groups in Bengal and Punjab** — alarmed at the prospect of compulsory grouping which might put them in Pakistan.
- The **Hindu Mahasabha in Bengal** was already assessing the feasibility of a **separate Hindu province in West Bengal**.

**Key Statements of Early 1947:**

> **March 10, 1947 — Nehru:** *"The Cabinet Mission's was the best solution if carried out; the only real alternative was the partition of Punjab and Bengal."*

> **April 1947 — Congress President Kripalani to the Viceroy:** *"...rather than have a battle, we shall let them have their Pakistan provided you allow Bengal and Punjab to be partitioned in a fair manner."*

---

## 🏛️ Part 3: Mountbatten as Viceroy

### Why Mountbatten Was Different

**Lord Mountbatten** proved more firm and quick in taking decisions than his predecessors because:
- He was **informally given more powers** to decide things on the spot.
- He had the advantage of the **firm British decision** to quit at the earliest.

**His Mandate:**
- To explore the options of **unity and division till October 1947**.
- Then advise the British government on the **form of transfer of power**.

**What He Found:**
- The **broad contours of the scenario were already discernible** even before he arrived in India.
- The **Cabinet Mission Plan was a dead horse**.
- **Jinnah was obstinate** about not settling for anything less than a sovereign state.

**Mountbatten's Choice:**
- A serious attempt at unity would involve **supporting forces wanting a unified India** and countering those who opposed it.
- **Mountbatten preferred to woo both sides** — which ultimately meant accepting partition.

---

### The Balkan Plan — Quickly Abandoned

Between **March and May 1947**, Mountbatten initially formulated an alternative plan that would have led to **Balkanisation** (fragmentation into many pieces):

- Transfer of power to **separate provinces** (or to a confederation, if formed before transfer).
- Punjab and Bengal given the option to **vote for partition of their provinces**.
- The various units thus formed — along with princely states (rendered independent by lapse of paramountcy) — would have the **option of joining India, Pakistan, or remaining separate**.

> This plan was **quickly abandoned** after **Nehru reacted violently** to it.

---

## 📋 Part 4: The Mountbatten Plan (June 3, 1947)

### The Key Innovation

The freedom-with-partition formula was already widely accepted before Mountbatten arrived. The major innovation — **actually suggested by V.P. Menon** — was:

- **Immediate transfer of power** on the basis of grant of **dominion status** (self-governing status within the British Commonwealth, with a right of secession).
- This **obviated the need to wait** for an agreement in the constituent assembly on a new political structure.

---

### Main Points of the Mountbatten Plan

- ✅ **Punjab and Bengal Legislative Assemblies** would meet in **two groups** (Hindus and Muslims) to vote for partition. If a **simple majority of either group** voted for partition, these provinces would be partitioned.
- ✅ In case of partition, **two dominions and two constituent assemblies** would be created.
- ✅ **Sindh** would take its own decision.
- ✅ **Referendums** in **NWFP and Sylhet district of Bengal** would decide the fate of these areas.
- ✅ Since Congress had conceded a **unified India** (in principle), all their other points would be met:
  - (i) **Independence for princely states ruled out** — they would join either India or Pakistan.
  - (ii) **Independence for Bengal ruled out**.
  - (iii) **Accession of Hyderabad to Pakistan ruled out** (Mountbatten supported the Congress on this).
  - (iv) **Freedom to come on August 15, 1947**.
  - (v) A **boundary commission** to be set up if partition was to be effected.

> **Mountbatten's formula:** *Divide India but retain maximum unity.*
>
> The League's demand was conceded to the extent that **Pakistan would be created**.
> The Congress' position on unity was taken into account to make **Pakistan as small as possible**.

---

### Why Congress Accepted Dominion Status (Despite the Lahore Congress Spirit)

The Congress was willing to accept dominion status despite it being against the spirit of the Lahore Congress (1929) because:

1. It would ensure a **peaceful and quick transfer of power**.
2. It was more important for Congress to **assume authority to check the explosive situation**.
3. It would allow for **much-needed continuity** in the bureaucracy and the army.

**For Britain:** Dominion status offered a chance to **keep India in the Commonwealth** — even if temporarily — considering India's economic strength, defence potential, and trade/investment value.

---

### Why August 15, 1947 Was Chosen as the Date

Two reasons:
1. Britain wanted to **secure Congress' agreement** to the dominion status quickly.
2. The British could **escape the responsibility for the communal situation** by leaving at the earliest.

---

### Implementation of the Plan

- Legislative assemblies of **Bengal and Punjab** decided in favour of partition of these two provinces:
  - **East Bengal** and **West Punjab** → joined Pakistan.
  - **West Bengal** and **East Punjab** → remained with the Indian Union.
- The **referendum in Sylhet** resulted in the incorporation of that district in **East Bengal** (Pakistan).
- Two **boundary commissions** (one for each province) were constituted — under **Radcliffe** — to demarcate new boundaries.
- The **referendum in NWFP** decided in favour of **Pakistan** — the Provincial Congress refrained from the referendum.
- **Baluchistan and Sindh** threw in their lot with **Pakistan**.

---

## 📜 Part 5: The Indian Independence Act (1947)

- **July 5, 1947:** The **British Parliament passed the Indian Independence Act** — based on the Mountbatten Plan.
- **July 18, 1947:** The Act received **Royal Assent**.
- **August 15, 1947:** The Act was implemented.

### Key Provisions of the Act

- ✅ Creation of **two independent dominions of India and Pakistan** with effect from **August 15, 1947**.
- ✅ Each dominion was to have a **Governor General** responsible for the effective operation of the Act.
- ✅ The constituent assembly of each new dominion was to **exercise the powers of the legislature** of that dominion.
- ✅ The existing **Central Legislative Assembly and the Council of States** were to be **automatically dissolved**.
- ✅ For the transitional period (till a new constitution was adopted by each dominion), governments were to be carried on in accordance with the **Government of India Act, 1935**.
- ✅ As per provisions: **Pakistan became independent on August 14** while **India got its freedom on August 15, 1947**.
- ✅ **M.A. Jinnah** became the **first Governor General of Pakistan**.
- ✅ India decided to request **Lord Mountbatten** to continue as the **Governor General of India**.

---

### Problems Caused by Early Withdrawal

The **breakneck speed** of events under Mountbatten caused serious anomalies and **totally failed to prevent the Punjab massacre** because:

- ❌ There were **no transitional institutional structures** within which partition problems could be tackled.
- ❌ Mountbatten had hoped to be the **common Governor General of India and Pakistan** (providing the necessary link) — but Jinnah wanted the position for himself in Pakistan.
- ❌ There was a **delay in announcing the Boundary Commission Award** (under Radcliffe) — though the award was ready by **August 12, 1947**, Mountbatten decided to make it public **after August 15** so that **the British could escape all responsibility** for disturbances.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Independence and Partition

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. Partition's Living Legacy — Refugees and Cross-Border Relations
- `[Modern Example]` The **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA, 2019)** — providing expedited citizenship to **Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian** persecuted minorities from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan — is a direct policy consequence of the Partition of 1947. The Act acknowledges that the minority communities left behind in Pakistan and Bangladesh faced systematic persecution — a problem that was created by the hasty partition. The **Supreme Court upheld the CAA's constitutionality in 2024**, affirming that India has a special obligation to Partition's victims.

### 2. The Radcliffe Award's Unhealed Wounds
- The Radcliffe Boundary Commission's award — made public only after August 15 to shield the British from responsibility — left borders that have been disputed and bloody ever since.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Line of Control (LoC) in Kashmir** and the **India-Pakistan border in Punjab** — both direct products of the Radcliffe Award — continue to be sites of tension. The **Kartarpur Corridor (opened November 2019)** — allowing Indian Sikhs visa-free access to Gurdwara Darbar Sahib in Pakistan — is a rare example of both nations bridging Partition's divide. The **Indus Waters Treaty (1960)**, currently under review and suspended by India in 2023 following the Pahalgam attack, is another product of the Partition-era border that continues to shape India-Pakistan relations.

### 3. Integration of Princely States — The Patel Model
- `[Modern Example]` Vallabhbhai Patel's **integration of 562 princely states** within a year of independence remains one of history's greatest political achievements. In recognition of this, the **Statue of Unity** — the world's tallest statue at 182 metres, inaugurated on **October 31, 2018** (Patel's birth anniversary) at **Kevadiya, Gujarat** — was built as a tribute. October 31 is now observed as **National Unity Day (Rashtriya Ekta Diwas)** — a direct celebration of the integration Patel achieved in 1947–48.

### 4. August 15 — India's Independence Day
- `[Modern Example]` India celebrated its **78th Independence Day on August 15, 2024** as part of the continuing **Azadi Ka Amrit Mahotsav** (which ran from 2021 to 2023, celebrating 75 years of independence). The Prime Minister's address from the Red Fort on August 15 — an unbroken tradition since 1947 — is itself a living ritual that connects every generation of Indians to the founding moment this chapter describes.

## 🏰 Part 6: Integration of Princely States

### The Challenge

During **1946–47**, there was a new upsurge of the **State People's Movement** — demanding political rights and elective representation in the Constituent Assembly.

**Nehru's Role:**
- Presided over the **All India State People's Conference** sessions in **Udaipur (1945)** and **Gwalior (April 1947)**.
- Declared that states refusing to join the Constituent Assembly would be **treated as hostile**.

**In July 1947**, **Vallabhbhai Patel** took charge of the new **States Department**.

---

### Phase I — The Instrument of Accession

By **August 15, 1947**, all states **except Kashmir, Hyderabad**, and **Junagarh** had signed an **instrument of accession** with the Indian government, acknowledging central authority over:
- **Defence**
- **External affairs**
- **Communication**

**Why Princes Agreed Easily:**
1. They were effectively *"surrendering"* only what they **never actually had** — these three functions had always been part of British paramountcy.
2. There was **no change in the internal political structure** of their states.

---

### Phase II — Integration and Constitutional Change

The second phase involved a much more difficult process of **'integration'** of states with neighbouring provinces or into new units:

**New Units Created:**
- Kathiawar Union
- Vindhya and Madhya Pradesh
- Rajasthan
- Himachal Pradesh

Some states retained their old boundaries for several years (Hyderabad, Mysore, Travancore-Cochin) while undergoing internal constitutional changes.

**The Bait:**
- A **generous privy purse** (annual payment to the erstwhile royal families).
- Some princes were made **governors and rajpramukhs** in free India.

**The Achievement:** The entire Phase II was accomplished **within a year** of independence.

> *"This rapid political unification of the country after independence was Patel's greatest achievement."*

---

## ❓ Part 7: Why Was Partition Inevitable? — The Congress Accepted the Unacceptable

### The Deeper Reasons

**1. Long-term failure to integrate Muslim masses into the national movement:**
- The Congress had a two-fold task:
  - (i) **Structuring diverse classes, communities, groups, and regions into a nation**.
  - (ii) **Securing independence for this nation**.
- While the Congress succeeded in building sufficient national consciousness to **pressure the British to quit India**, it **failed to complete the task of welding the nation** — especially in integrating Muslims into it.

**2. Only immediate transfer of power could forestall spreading violence:**
- Only an **immediate transfer of power** could forestall the spread of 'direct action' and communal violence.

**3. The virtual collapse of the Interim Government:**
- This made the notion of **Pakistan appear unavoidable**.

**4. Partition ruled out independence for princely states:**
- The partition plan ruled out independence for the princely states — which could have been a **greater danger to Indian unity** as it would have meant **Balkanisation** of the country.

**5. Partition was the final act of a step-by-step concession process:**

The Congress had been making step-by-step concessions to the League's demand for a separate Muslim state:

| Stage | Concession |
|---|---|
| **Cripps Mission (1942)** | Autonomy of Muslim majority provinces was accepted |
| **Gandhi-Jinnah talks (1944)** | Gandhi accepted the **right of self-determination** of Muslim-majority provinces |
| **After Cabinet Mission Plan (1946)** | Congress conceded the possibility of Muslim majority provinces setting up a **separate constituent assembly** |
| **December 1946** | Congress accepted, **without demur**, that grouping was compulsory |
| **March 1947** | CWC resolution stated that Punjab (and by implication, Bengal) must be **partitioned if the country was divided** |
| **3rd June Plan** | Congress **accepted partition** |

**6. The Congress quietly accepted compulsory grouping:**
- While loudly asserting the sovereignty of the Constituent Assembly, the Congress had already accepted the compulsory grouping and accepted partition most of all because **it could not stop the communal riots**.

---

### The Wishful Thinking of Congress Leadership

Despite accepting partition, the Congress leadership — especially Nehru — showed a remarkable **lack of appreciation of the dynamics of communal feeling**. Nehru stated at various times:

> *"Once the British left, Hindu-Muslim differences would be patched up and a free, united India would be built up."*

> *"Partition is only temporary."*

> *"Partition would be peaceful — once Pakistan was conceded, what was there to fight for?"*

All three predictions proved disastrously wrong.

---

### What the Key Leaders Said About Accepting Partition

> *"The truth is that we were tired men, and we were getting on in years too. Few of us could stand the prospect of going to prison again — and if we had stood out for a united India as we wished it, prison obviously awaited us. We saw the fires burning in the Punjab and heard every day of the killings. The plan for partition offered a way out and we took it."* — **Jawaharlal Nehru** (as quoted by Wali Khan)

> *"I felt that if we did not accept partition, India would be split into many bits and would be completely ruined. My experience of office for one year convinced me that the way we have been proceeding would lead us to disaster. We would not have had one Pakistan but several. We would have had Pakistan cells in every office."* — **Sardar Patel**

> *"The acceptance was only in a resolution of the AICC of the Congress and on the register of the Muslim League. The people of India had not accepted partition with free and open minds. Some had accepted it out of sheer anger and resentment and others out of a sense of despair."* — **Maulana Azad**

> *"I alone with the help of my Secretary and my typewriter won Pakistan for the Muslims."* — **M.A. Jinnah**

---

### British Role — Not Simply "Divide and Rule"

> *"The British were neither the foes of the Hindus nor friends of the Muslims. They set up Pakistan not as a gesture of friendship towards the Muslims, but under the compulsions of their international policies. It was not so much that Britain pursued a policy of divide and rule as that the process of devolving power by stages in a politically and socially desperate country was inherently divisive."* — **R.J. Moore**

---

### Gandhi's Helplessness

Gandhi felt helpless because there had been a **communalisation of the people**. He had no option but to accept partition because **the people wanted it**.

> How could there be a movement to fight communalism **involving a communalised people?**

He asked Congressmen, however, **not to accept it in their hearts**.

---

### The Nature of 1940s Communalism — Different from Earlier Decades

- The communalism of the **1920s and 1930s** was different from that of the **1940s**.
- The 1940s saw an **all-out effort for an assertive 'Muslim nation'**.
- The Congress leadership **underestimated the potential** of this type of communalism.

---

### R.C. Mazumdar's Assessment — A Revisionist View

> *"There is, however, no basis for the claim that the Civil Disobedience Movement directly led to independence. The campaigns of Gandhi... came to an ignoble end about fourteen years before India achieved independence... The INA trial... made it quite plain to the British, already exhausted by the war, that they could no longer depend upon the loyalty of the sepoys for maintaining their authority in India. This had probably the greatest influence upon their final decision to quit India."* — **R.C. Mazumdar**

This view highlights that **the cumulative effect of all struggles** — Gandhi's civil disobedience, revolutionary activities, Bose's INA, and the battles fought by Hitler and Japan indirectly against Britain — together brought about independence.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Partition's Legacy and Reconciliation

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The Partition Museum — Remembering the Tragedy
- `[Modern Example]` The **Partition Museum at Town Hall, Amritsar** — inaugurated in **2017** on the 70th anniversary of Partition — is the world's first museum dedicated to the Partition of 1947. It houses oral histories, artefacts, and testimonies of Partition survivors. As of 2023, with most survivors of Partition now in their 90s or beyond, the museum's digital archiving project has become urgently important — preserving the last living memories of the greatest forced migration in human history (an estimated **10–20 million displaced, 200,000–2 million killed**).

### 2. Partition Horrors Recognised Officially
- `[Modern Example]` The Government of India declared **August 14 as Partition Horrors Remembrance Day (Vibhajan Vibhishika Smriti Diwas)** starting from **2021** — to honour the memory of victims of Partition and promote social harmony and unity. This was announced by PM Modi on **August 14, 2021** — the 74th anniversary of Pakistan's independence.

### 3. Integration of Princely States — The Unfinished Business
- The integration of princely states was largely completed by Patel, but the three exceptions — Kashmir, Hyderabad, and Junagarh — required special treatment.
- `[Modern Example]` **Kashmir** remains the most consequential unresolved issue from Partition, with the **abrogation of Article 370 (August 5, 2019)** being the most significant recent development — converting the **state of Jammu & Kashmir into two Union Territories** (J&K and Ladakh). The **Supreme Court upheld the abrogation in December 2023** in *Dr. Shah Faesal vs. Union of India* — closing, at least legally, one chapter of the unfinished integration that began in 1947.

### 4. The Dominion Status Decision — Strategic Wisdom
- `[Modern Example]` India's decision to accept dominion status (remaining in the Commonwealth) has had lasting benefits. India **left the Commonwealth realm in 1950** when it became a Republic — but remained a member of the **Commonwealth of Nations**. This has provided India with significant soft power and diplomatic networks. The **Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting (CHOGM)** continues to be a forum where India exercises influence — a direct strategic benefit of the decision Nehru made in 1947 to accept dominion status.
