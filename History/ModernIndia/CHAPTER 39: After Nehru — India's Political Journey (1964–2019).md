# 📚 Chapter 39: After Nehru — India's Political Journey (1964–2019)

---

## 🗂️ Chapter at a Glance (Read This First!)

> **5 Big Takeaways from this Chapter:**
>
> 1. India defied all predictions after Nehru's death — **smooth democratic transitions** proved that Indian democracy was deeply rooted, not personality-dependent.
> 2. The **Emergency (1975–77)** was the darkest chapter in post-Independence India — a constitutional crisis caused by both **Indira Gandhi's authoritarianism** AND **JP's destabilising mass movement.**
> 3. The **1991 Economic Reforms** under **Narasimha Rao and Manmohan Singh** were India's most transformative policy shift — from socialism to liberalisation, privatisation, globalisation.
> 4. From **1989 onwards**, India entered a prolonged era of **coalition politics** — no single party ruled alone for decades, reshaping how governance worked.
> 5. The **2014 and 2019 elections** marked a decisive shift — the **BJP under Narendra Modi** won back-to-back majorities, the first non-Congress party to do so.

## PART 1: The Lal Bahadur Shastri Years (June 1964 – January 1966)

---

### 1.1 Smooth Succession After Nehru

When **Jawaharlal Nehru died in May 1964**, many feared India would collapse into chaos. Instead, a smooth succession took place.

- **Gulzarilal Nanda** was appointed **interim prime minister** pending elections.
- A group within the Congress formed in **1963**, known as the **Syndicate** (including Congress president **K. Kamaraj**), guided the succession.
- The contest was between **Morarji Desai** (senior, experienced, conservative, leaning "right") and **Lal Bahadur Shastri** (mild, soft-spoken, flexible, incorruptible).
- **Shastri was chosen** — he had wider support across the party; Desai decided not to contest.

---

### 1.2 Early Life of Lal Bahadur Shastri

- Born on **October 2, 1904** in **Uttar Pradesh** (then United Provinces).
- Firmly opposed to the caste system — dropped his surname **Srivastava.**
- **'Shastri'** was a title he earned on completing graduation at **Kashi Vidyapeeth, Varanasi, in 1928.**
- When he married **Lalita Devi (1928)**, he refused dowry — only accepted **five yards of khadi and a spinning wheel** on his father-in-law's insistence.
- Inspired by Gandhi, he joined the freedom movement. Arrested during the **Non-Cooperation Movement (1921)** but released as he was a minor.
- Participated in the **Salt Satyagraha**, **Individual Satyagraha**, and the **Quit India Movement (1942).**
- Used time in prison to study social reformers and Western philosophers.
- Became **secretary of the local Congress unit**, later **president of the Allahabad Congress Committee.**
- Elected to the **Legislative Assembly of the United Provinces in 1937.**

---

### 1.3 Political Journey After Independence

- Became minister in **Uttar Pradesh** in **Govind Ballabh Pant's cabinet** — held Police and Transport portfolios.
- Notable initiatives:
  - Appointed **women as bus conductors.**
  - Directed police to use **water jets instead of lathis** for dispersing mobs.
- Made **General Secretary of the AICC** (with Nehru as President) in **1951;** same year nominated to **Rajya Sabha.**
- Served as **Railways Minister** — resigned in **1956** taking moral responsibility for a serious rail accident.
- Later appointed **Home Minister (1961)** — gained reputation as a **skilful mediator.**
- Formed the **Committee on Prevention of Corruption** headed by **K. Santhanam** — on whose recommendations the **Central Vigilance Commission** was established.
- Also served as **Minister for Transport and Communications; Minister for Commerce and Industry;** and **Minister without Portfolio** during Nehru's illness.

---

### 1.4 Shastri as Prime Minister

- **Sworn in as second Prime Minister of India on June 9, 1964.**
- Initially kept **External Affairs and Atomic Energy** portfolios; later transferred External Affairs to **Swaran Singh.**

**His vision in his first broadcast:**
> *"Our way is straight and clear — the building up of a socialist democracy at home with freedom and prosperity for all, and the maintenance of world peace and friendship with all nations."*

**On secularism:**
> *"The unique thing about our country is that we have Hindus, Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, Parsis... But we do not bring all this into politics. This is the difference between India and Pakistan."*

**Key governance decisions:**
- Retained most of Nehru's cabinet, but also inducted **Indira Gandhi** (Nehru's daughter) as **Information and Broadcasting Minister.**
- Was the **first prime minister to appoint a Secretary to the PM** — drew upon **L.K. Jha**, a senior ICS officer. This laid the foundation of what later became the **Prime Minister's Office (PMO).**

---

### 1.5 Major Challenges

Shastri's short tenure faced three major crises:

1. **Anti-Hindi agitation in Madras state** — violent protests against Hindi being imposed as the only national language. Shastri assured that English would continue as long as non-Hindi speaking states wanted. The army was called in to quell riots.

2. **Widespread food shortages** — India faced serious agricultural crisis.

3. **Second war with Pakistan (1965).**

---

### 1.6 Shastri as an Economic Reformer (Ahead of His Time)

Few people realise that **Shastri initiated major economic reforms** — very different from Nehru's approach.

- Tried to **decentralise governance** by moving decision-making from the Planning Commission to economic ministries.
- Attempted to **reduce dominance of the Planning Commission** by setting up a national planning council.
- In **1965**, announced in Parliament a **reconsideration of government controls** — relaxed regulations in steel and cement sectors.
- His economic team included: **L.K. Jha, I.G. Patel, Dharma Vira, S. Bhoothalingam** — all tilted towards modernising agriculture and allowing more private sector freedom.
- The **decision to devalue the rupee** was taken in principle by **Shastri** — though implemented by Indira Gandhi.

> According to **P.N. Dhar**, if Shastri had lived longer, he might have pursued *"an agenda of economic reform of the kind that was taken up only twenty-five years later"* (under **Narasimha Rao**).

---

### 1.7 The Green Revolution and White Revolution — Seeds Sown by Shastri

**India faced a food crisis.** Shastri brought in **C. Subramaniam** as Food and Agriculture Minister.

**The Green Revolution (threefold thrust — technological, economic, organisational):**

- The **Indian Council of Agricultural Research** was reorganised; for the first time a scientist, **Dr B.P. Pal**, was appointed as its head.
- **M.S. Swaminathan** of the Indian Agricultural Research Institute made the government aware of new **high-yielding wheat varieties** developed by **Norman Borlaug's team in Mexico.**
- Shastri approved the **import of 250 tonnes of wheat seeds in 1965** — despite opposition from within Congress.
- **November 1965:** **C.S. Subramaniam and US Agriculture Secretary Orville Freeman** signed an accord in **Rome** — India committed to end food grain imports by **1971** by investing in agriculture, irrigation, research, seeds, and fertilisers.
- **January 1965:** The **Agricultural Prices Commission (APC)** and the **Food Corporation of India (FCI)** came into being.
- The **National Seeds Corporation** and the **Central Warehousing Corporation** were also set up around this time.

**The White Revolution:**
- On a visit to **Anand, Gujarat in October 1964**, Shastri was impressed by the **Amul milk cooperative.**
- He wished **Verghese Kurien** (General Manager of Kaira District Co-operative Milk Producers' Union Ltd, or Amul) would replicate cooperatives nationwide.
- As a result, the **National Dairy Development Board (NDDB)** was established at **Anand in 1965.**

---

### 1.8 The "Skip a Meal" Initiative

During the **1965 war with Pakistan**, US President **Lyndon B. Johnson** threatened to stop wheat supply under the **PL-480 agreement** if India didn't stop the war.

Shastri's response: **"We may go hungry, but not bow before the US."**

- Motivated Indians to **voluntarily give up one meal a week** to share food.
- Before asking the public, he got **his own family** to try the system first.
- The public response was huge — **restaurants even closed on Monday evenings.**

---

### 1.9 New Institutions and Projects Under Shastri

- **November 1964:** Central Institute of Technology Campus at **Tharamani, Chennai.**
- **March 1965:** Andhra Pradesh Agricultural University at **Hyderabad** (renamed Acharya N.G. Ranga Agricultural University in 1996; separated into two after Telangana formation, with the Telangana university named **Professor Jayashankar Agricultural University in July 2014**).
- **National Institute of Technology, Allahabad.**
- **Jawahar Dock** of the Chennai Port Trust inaugurated; construction of **Tuticorin Port** began in November 1964.
- **1964:** Foundation stone of the **Upper Krishna Project** (Alamatti Dam) laid. (The Almatti Dam was later named after Lal Bahadur Shastri.)
- **1965:** Shastri inaugurated the **plutonium reprocessing plant at Trombay.**
- Following **Dr Homi Bhabha's** suggestion, the nuclear explosive design group **SNEPP (Study of Nuclear Explosions for Peaceful Purposes)** was set up.

---

### 1.10 Foreign Relations Under Shastri

- Maintained **non-alignment** but was aware of threats from Chinese-Pakistani military ties post the **1962 war.**
- Decided India should build **closer ties with the Soviet Union.**
- **Modernised the Indian armed forces.**
- **Bandarnaike-Shastri Accord (1964):** Signed with **Sirimavo Bandaranaike** of Ceylon (Sri Lanka) — settled repatriation of **6,00,000 Indian Tamils** to India and Ceylonese citizenship for **3,75,000 Tamils.** (The accord lapsed after Shastri's death.)
- Made an official visit to **Rangoon (December 1965)** to restore cordial relations with **General Ne Win's Burma** after the strained relations following Burma's 1962 coup and the repatriation of Indian families in 1964.
- In **1965**, authorised the **Atomic Energy Commission** to work on achieving a **nuclear test** — faced strong opposition but did not waver.

---

### 1.11 The Indo-Pak War of 1965

**Background:**
- **General Mohammad Ayub Khan** had seized power in Pakistan through a military coup in **1958**, displacing **President Iskander Mirza.**
- The US was giving Pakistan significant military support.
- Ayub Khan tried to exploit India's demoralisation after the **1962 China war.**

**Sequence of Events:**
- **April 1965:** Pakistan tested India in **Sindh.** Initial clashes in the **Rann of Kachchh** seemed to favour Pakistan; a British-brokered ceasefire followed.
- **August 1965:** Pakistan launched **'Operation Grandslam'** in Kashmir — aimed at seizing the only significant overland route to Kashmir; Pakistan's foreign minister **Zulfikar Ali Bhutto** encouraged Ayub Khan.
- Well-trained Pakistani infiltrators were sent into the valley.
- Shastri **ordered the Indian Army to cross the ceasefire line** and close the passes used by infiltrators.
- By **early September**, the **Second Indo-Pakistan War** had begun.
- In **September**, India launched a **three-pronged attack** with tanks aiming at **Lahore and Sialkot** — Lahore came within Indian fire range.
- The **Indian Air Force participated in war for the first time** since Independence.
- **Defence Minister Y.B. Chavan** proved outstanding.
- **September 23:** The UN brokered a **ceasefire** — accepted by both sides.

---

### 1.12 The Tashkent Declaration (January 10, 1966)

A peace conference was held at **Tashkent** (capital of Uzbekistan, then a Soviet republic), sponsored by Soviet President **Alexei Kosygin.**

**Signatories:** Pakistan President **Ayub Khan** and India PM **Lal Bahadur Shastri** — signed on **January 10, 1966.**

**Key Points of the Tashkent Declaration:**

1. Both sides to exert all efforts for good neighbourly relations as per the **UN Charter;** to refrain from force and settle disputes peacefully. **Kashmir** was discussed with each side stating its position.
2. All armed personnel of both countries to be withdrawn to positions held **prior to August 5, 1965** not later than **February 25, 1966;** ceasefire terms on the ceasefire line to be observed.
3. Relations to be based on **non-interference** in each other's internal affairs.
4. Both sides to **discourage hostile propaganda** and encourage propaganda that promotes friendly relations.
5. **High Commissioners** of both countries to return to their posts; both to observe the **Vienna Convention of 1961** on Diplomatic Intercourse.
6. Measures to be considered for restoration of **economic and trade relations, communications, and cultural exchanges.**
7. Both leaders to instruct authorities to carry out **repatriation of prisoners of war.**
8. Both sides to continue discussion on **refugees and evictions/illegal immigrations** and create conditions preventing exodus of people; property and assets taken over to be discussed.
9. Both sides to **continue meetings** at highest and other levels; recognised the need to set up **joint Indian-Pakistani bodies** to recommend further steps.

**Criticism:**
- Critics felt the agreement should have included a **no-war pact.**
- No provision that Pakistan would give up **guerrilla aggression** in Kashmir.
- Pakistan received the agreement even more angrily — riots and demonstrations followed.
- **Zulfikar Bhutto** distanced himself from Ayub Khan and later broke away to form his own party.

---

### 1.13 Shastri's Death

- On **January 11, 1966** (the morning after signing the Tashkent Declaration), **Lal Bahadur Shastri died of a heart attack.**
- Controversy exists — rumours of poisoning circulated, with suspicion directed at the **CIA** (as the West was wary of India's nuclear aspirations and its impact on South Asian balance of power; also suspected in the death of **Homi Bhabha** in an air crash).
- **RTIs seeking relevant information have been turned down** by the government — the controversy remains unresolved.

## PART 2: Indira Gandhi — First Phase (January 1966 – March 1977)

---

### 2.1 Succession

- After Shastri's unexpected death, **Gulzarilal Nanda** was again interim PM.
- The **Syndicate supported Indira Gandhi** (Nehru's daughter) — many senior Congressmen thought she could be easily manipulated. **Time proved them wrong.**

### 2.2 Early Life of Indira Gandhi

- Born **November 19, 1917** in **Allahabad** to **Jawaharlal Nehru and Kamala Nehru.**
- At age 12, formed the **Vanar Sena** — a children's group that addressed envelopes, made flags, put up notices, and carried messages for freedom fighters.
- Attended multiple institutions: **Ecole de Bex (Switzerland), Rabindranath Tagore's Visva-Bharati at Santiniketan, Somerville College, Oxford.**
- **1936:** Joined the **Indian National Congress.**
- **1938:** Became member of the **India League** in England, where she met **Feroze Jehangir Ghandy** (a Parsi, later changed to Gandhi), also a member of the India League.
- Returned to India in **1941,** married Feroze Gandhi in **1942.**
- Was imprisoned in **Naini Central Jail** during the **Quit India Movement.**
- Had two sons: **Rajiv** and **Sanjay.**
- **1955:** Joined Congress Working Committee.
- **1959:** Elected **President of the Congress party.**
- After Nehru's death (1964), became **Rajya Sabha member** and was inducted into cabinet by Shastri as **Information and Broadcasting Minister.**
- **Feroze Gandhi:** Exposed corruption involving major insurance companies and Finance Minister **T.T. Krishnamachari** in Nehru's cabinet. Died in **September 1960.**

---

### 2.3 Indira Gandhi Becomes Prime Minister

- On **January 19, 1966**, **Indira Gandhi became the third Prime Minister of India — the first woman to hold the post.**

---

### 2.4 Congress Split and the Path to Dominance

**1967 General Elections:**
- Economic problems — stagnation, food crisis, rising prices, unemployment — caused disenchantment.
- Indira Gandhi won from **Rae Bareilly.**
- Congress won but with only a **thin majority**; **Morarji Desai** became Deputy PM and Finance Minister.
- Congress lost power or failed to get majority in **several states.**

**Growing Ideological Divide:**
- Indira Gandhi favoured **radical economic reform** — not approved by conservative senior members.
- Her **10-point programme** included: social control of banks, government takeover of foreign trade, nationalisation of general insurance, ceilings on property, public distribution of food grains, land reforms, subsidised housing for rural poor, etc.
- She was in favour of **abolishing privy purses** to former princely state rulers.
- **Nationalised 14 major private banks in July 1969** through an ordinance — without consulting Finance Minister **Morarji Desai**, who was forced to resign.
- (Later passed as the **Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act.**)

**Presidential Election Crisis (1969):**
- After President **Zakir Hussain** died in May 1969:
  - Official Congress candidate: **Neelam Sanjiva Reddy** (Syndicate member).
  - Indira Gandhi supported **V.V. Giri** (independent candidate, ex-Vice President) fearing Syndicate would use Reddy to unseat her.
  - **V.V. Giri won** and became President in **August 1969.**
- In **November**, Congress president **S. Nijalingappa** expelled Indira Gandhi for indiscipline.
- Indira created **Congress (R)** (R = Requisitionists); the other group became **Congress (O)** (O = Organisation) led by **Ram Subhag Singh.**
- Retained power at Centre with issue-based support from **DMK, Akali Dal, and two communist parties.**

---

### 2.5 The 1971 Elections: Indira Triumphant

- Indira Gandhi recommended **dissolution of Lok Sabha in December 1970** and called early elections.
- **Congress (R) slogan:** ***"Garibi Hatao"* (Remove Poverty)**
- **Opposition (Grand Alliance — Swatantra, Congress-O, SSP) slogan:** ***"Indira Hatao"* (Remove Indira)**
- **Congress (R) won a good majority** — voters showed maturity, preferring national issues.
- **India's victory over Pakistan in December 1971** and the creation of **Bangladesh** further boosted her image.
- In **March 1972 state elections**, Congress (R) came to power in many states.

---

### 2.6 The Challenges of 1973–74

By **1973**, enthusiasm for Indira Gandhi was fading:

- **High inflation** from wartime expenses, drought, and the **oil crisis of 1973.**
- Food grain production declined due to poor monsoons.
- **Unemployment grew.**
- **Major strikes** — including the **all-India railway strike, May 1974.**
- Alienation of:
  - **Rich peasants** — resented land reforms.
  - **Industrialists** — resented nationalisation and socialist policies.
  - **Middle classes** — hit by price rise and spreading corruption.
- **Sanjay Gandhi** given the contract and exclusive production licence for producing a small fuel-efficient Indian car despite little experience — widely seen as **nepotism.**

---

### 2.7 The JP Movement

**Nav-Nirman Movement in Gujarat (December 1973 – March 1974):**
- Protests against rising prices of essential commodities.
- Serious enough for the Centre to dissolve the assembly. Chief Minister **Chimanbhai Patel** resigned; President's Rule promulgated in Gujarat.

**The Bihar Movement:**
- Began as student protests, taken up by **Jayaprakash Narayan (JP)** on the larger issue of corruption.
- JP called for **'Total Revolution'** — demanded Congress government in Bihar resign, assembly be dissolved.
- Demanded people **gherao** the legislature and offices, **refuse to pay taxes** — obstruct government functioning.
- JP tried to expand the protest nationally to bring down Indira Gandhi's government.
- Movement **lost momentum by end of 1974.**
- Indira Gandhi challenged JP to contest elections in **1976;** JP accepted.

---

### 2.8 The Allahabad High Court Verdict and Emergency

**June 12, 1975:** Justice Sinha of the **Allahabad High Court** delivered a verdict in an election petition filed by **Raj Narain** (Indira Gandhi's 1971 opponent from Rae Bareilly):

- Declared Indira Gandhi's **1971 election to Lok Sabha invalid** on grounds of:
  - Electoral malpractice
  - Excessive election expenditure
  - Using government machinery and officials for party purposes
- She could no longer be PM and could not try for election for **six years.**
- An appeal was made to the Supreme Court.

**Justice V.R. Krishna Iyer (vacation judge) on June 24** granted a **conditional stay** — Indira Gandhi could remain in post, speak in Parliament but could not vote.

**Sequence Leading to Emergency:**
- Congress suffered reverses in **Gujarat assembly elections.**
- JP movement revived with greater zeal; demand for Indira's resignation grew.
- Announcement of **civil disobedience from June 29.**
- Indira Gandhi recommended to President **Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed** that a State of Emergency be declared.
- President declared a **State of Emergency due to internal disorder** under **Article 352(1)** on **June 25–26, 1975.**
- (The cabinet endorsed the decision only **after the event.)**

> **Who was to blame?** British sociologist **Joe Elder** observed: *"JP erred in launching a mass movement without a cadre of disciplined, nonviolent volunteers... On the other hand, the Prime Minister clearly over-reacted in imposing the Emergency."*
>
> **P.N. Dhar** said: *"Both JP and Indira Gandhi, between whom the politics of India was then polarized, failed democracy and betrayed their lack of faith in the rule of law."*

---

### 2.9 State of Emergency (1975–77)

**Immediate Actions:**

- **President's Rule** imposed in **Gujarat** and **Tamil Nadu** — the two states not under Congress.
- **Indefinite postponement** of all pending assembly elections.
- Opposition leaders arrested under **Maintenance of Internal Security Act (MISA):**
  - **Jayaprakash Narayan, Atal Behari Vajpayee, L.K. Advani, Morarji Desai, Charan Singh, Asoka Mehta,** Congress dissident **Chandra Shekhar,** and journalists including **Ajit Bhattacharjea and Kuldip Nayar.**
- Organisations banned: **Anand Marg, RSS, Jamaati-Islami, CPI(ML).**
- **Fundamental rights suspended;** civil liberties curtailed.
- **42nd Amendment** was passed — see below.
- **Press censorship** imposed — The **Indian Express** and **The Statesman** tried their best to resist.
- **Sanjay Gandhi** (Indira's son) wielded unofficial power, led:
  - **Demolition of crowded housing settlements.**
  - **Forced sterilisation** drives — caused mass misery.

**Initial Support for Emergency:**
- Middle classes initially welcomed it — tired of protests, wanted discipline and price control.
- Law and order improved initially.
- **MISA** was used against smugglers, hoarders, black marketeers.
- **Communist Party of India** supported it to "thwart right reactionary forces."
- **Acharya Vinobha Bhave** called it a time for discipline.
- **Mother Teresa** supported it for bringing calm.
- Journalist **Khushwant Singh** initially supported it (later changed his view).
- Congress president **Dev Kant Baruah's** sycophantic praise: *"India is Indira, and Indira is India."*

**Growing Discontent by Mid-1976:**
- Economic improvement could not be sustained.
- Welfare programmes moved too slowly.
- Police and bureaucracy abused power.
- Fear and insecurity, with no avenue for grievance redress.
- **Sanjay Gandhi's** demolition and sterilisation drives created enormous anger.
- **The 1976 AICC session in Guwahati** institutionalised sycophancy and prepared the way for **dynastic politics.** Marked Sanjay Gandhi's debut in politics.

---

### 2.10 The 42nd Constitutional Amendment — "A Mini Constitution"

The **Forty-Second Constitutional Amendment Act (1976)** brought sweeping changes:

- Added **40 articles and a new chapter** to the Constitution.
- Amended the **Preamble** — India now called **'sovereign, socialist secular democratic republic'** (originally just 'sovereign democratic republic'); 'unity of the nation' changed to **'unity and integrity of the nation.'**
- Placed amendments **outside the purview of courts;** Parliament's power to amend the Constitution declared unlimited.
- **Curtailed powers of High Courts and Supreme Court** in judicial review and writ jurisdiction.
- New **directive principles** added; directive principles given primacy over fundamental rights.
- New chapter on **fundamental duties** added.
- **Term of Lok Sabha extended from 5 years to 6 years.**
- Froze number of Lok Sabha constituencies and state assembly seats at **1971 census level** till first census after 2000.
- Laws to deal with **anti-national activities** declared to take precedence over fundamental rights.
- **Quorum requirement** in Parliament and state legislatures discarded.
- Emergency could now be declared for **part of India's territory.**
- Duration of President's Rule without Parliament's approval extended from **six months to one year.**
- Centre given power to **deploy armed forces** in a state in case of serious law and order situation.

---

### 2.11 The 1977 Elections — Democracy Fights Back

**January 1977:** Indira Gandhi unexpectedly announced elections; political prisoners released, press censorship lifted.

**Opposition unites:**
- **Jan Sangh, Congress (O), Bharatiya Lok Dal** (Charan Singh), and **Socialist Party** merged to form the **Janata Party.**
- **February 1977:** **Jagjivan Ram, Nandini Satpathy,** and **Hemvati Nandan Bahuguna** left Congress to form **Congress for Democracy (CFD).**
- **CFD, Akali Dal, DMK, and CPM** allied with Janata Party.
- After elections, CFD merged with Janata Party.

**Election Results (March 1977):**
- Janata alliance won **330 out of 542 seats** — a massive mandate.
- Congress was trounced, especially in the North.
- **Both Indira Gandhi and Sanjay Gandhi lost their seats.**
- Congress did well in the South (where Emergency excesses were fewer and pro-poor programmes better implemented).
- **Emergency ended on March 21, 1977.**

> The elections proved that **Indian democracy was deeply embedded** — even the illiterate poor understood the value of civil rights. A non-Congress government came to power at the Centre for the first time since Independence.

## PART 3: Key Policy Developments Under Indira Gandhi's First Phase

---

### 3.1 Nationalisation of Banks and Other Sectors

- **July 1969:** Nationalised **14 major private banks** through an ordinance — later passed as the **Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act.**
- Banks were to cater to not just large industries but also agriculturists, small industries, and entrepreneurs.
- Results: Huge expansion of banking sector; branches in rural areas; increased household savings.
- **Differential Interest Rates Scheme (1972):** Public sector banks to provide loans at **lower than normal rates** to weaker sections.
- After re-election in 1971, nationalised **coal, steel, copper, refining, cotton textiles, and insurance.**
- **1973:** Nationalised **oil companies** after foreign-owned ones refused to supply fuel to Indian forces during 1971 war.
- **1980 (second term):** Another **six banks** nationalised.

### 3.2 Abolition of Privy Purses

- Former princely rulers had been granted **'privy purses'** under **Article 291** — fixed, tax-free amounts ranging from **Rs 5,000 to Rs 26 lakh per annum.**
- **1969 attempt:** Constitutional Amendment Bill passed in Lok Sabha, but **failed by one vote** in Rajya Sabha.
- **1971:** The **Twenty-sixth Amendment** to the Constitution abolished privy purses.
- New **Article 363-A** was inserted in the Constitution.

### 3.3 MRTP Act (1969)

- The **Monopolies and Restrictive Trade Practices (MRTP) Act** was passed after abolishing the **managing agency system.**
- The **MRTP Commissioner** was to check concentration of economic power in a few leading business families.

### 3.4 Steps for Equity and Poverty Reduction

- **Equal pay for equal work** for men and women enshrined in the Constitution.
- Rapid enforcement of **land ceilings** (agricultural and urban).
- Legislation for redistribution of land to marginal farmers.
- Programme for distribution of food grains at low cost to economically vulnerable sections.
- Crash programme for rural employment creation.
- Programmes for building houses for landless labourers.
- Abolition of **bonded labour** (at least on paper); moratorium on debts of the poor.
- Nationalised banks had to maintain **priority sector lending.**

### 3.5 Language Policy

- **Official Languages Act amended in 1967** to guarantee that English could continue as official language until a resolution to end it was passed by every state's legislature that had not adopted Hindi, and by each house of Parliament.
- This established a policy of **official bilingualism** — effectively ended anti-Hindi protests.

### 3.6 Centralisation of Power — The PMO

- The **Prime Minister's Secretariat** (later called **PMO** under Morarji Desai) expanded greatly under Indira Gandhi.
- **P.N. Haksar** became the first **Principal Secretary to a Prime Minister.**
- Key advisers: **T.N. Kaul** (diplomat), **D.P. Dhar** (politician-turned diplomat), **P.N. Dhar** (economist-turned bureaucrat), **R.N. Kao** (security analyst).
- The **Research and Analysis Wing (RAW)** was established in **1968** on the CIA model, with **Kao as its head.**
- Haksar, with a leftward orientation, was the principal influence in Indira Gandhi's socialist path.

### 3.7 Clipping the Judiciary's Wings

| Amendment | Year | What It Did |
|---|---|---|
| **24th Amendment** | 1971 | Authorised Parliament to amend any provision of the Constitution (overcame Golaknath case restriction) |
| **25th Amendment** | 1972 | Directive principles could not be challenged in court on grounds of inconsistency with certain fundamental rights |
| **A.N. Ray appointment** | 1973 | Senior-most SC judge tradition set aside; politically motivated appointment of Justice A.N. Ray as CJI (three senior judges superseded) |
| **38th Amendment** | 1975 | President's/Governor's satisfaction to declare emergency or issue ordinances could not be challenged in court |
| **39th Amendment** | 1975 | Election disputes of President, VP, Speaker, PM — not to be heard by High Courts or SC, only by special Parliament-created court |
| **42nd Amendment** | 1976 | Further curtailed judiciary's power (see above) |

### 3.8 Devaluation of the Rupee (June 1966)

- India devalued the rupee by **36.5%**, increasing the dollar's value by **57.4%.**
- (Lal Bahadur Shastri had decided upon devaluation as early as **January 1966** before going to Tashkent — per **B.K. Nehru's account.**)
- Goal: Help India export more and earn dollars to pay for imports.
- **Criticism was severe** — World Bank fell short on commitments; foreign capital didn't come; drought added problems.
- **Medium-term result:** Actually beneficial — India averted famine and bankruptcy; trade deficit reduced drastically by **1970–71.**
- Short-term failure led Indira Gandhi to abandon liberalisation and turn leftward.

### 3.9 Five-Year Plans

**Fourth Five-Year Plan (1969–74):**
- Goal: *"Growth with stability and progressive achievement of self-reliance."*
- Investment outlay **almost double** that of the Third Plan.
- Based on the **Ten Point Programme** of 1967.
- Performance not as good as expected — war with Pakistan, refugee burden, 1973 oil crisis.

**Fifth Five-Year Plan (1974–79):**
- Targeted reducing poverty through consumption needs and socio-economic reforms.
- Coincided largely with the Emergency period.
- Economy grew at **9% in 1975–76** — per capita income grew by over **5%** for the first time.
- Curtailed when Janata government came to power in **1977.**

### 3.10 Green Revolution — Gains Under Indira Gandhi

- Seeds sown under Shastri bore fruit under Indira Gandhi.
- New **dwarf varieties of wheat** adopted by Punjab and Haryana farmers → bumper harvests.
- New varieties of rice, groundnut, and cotton also did well.
- **Shortcomings:** Huge regional variations; areas still dependent on erratic monsoons; growing disparity in farmer incomes; environmental damage from overuse of chemical fertilisers.

## PART 4: The Indo-Pak War of 1971 and Birth of Bangladesh

---

### 4.1 Background in Pakistan

- **General Yahya Khan** (successor to Ayub Khan) called **Pakistan's first-ever election based on adult franchise** in **December 1970.**
- Results: **Awami League** (led by **Mujibur Rahman**) swept East Pakistan; **PPP** (led by **Zulfiqar Ali Bhutto**) won in West Pakistan.
- Awami League won an overall majority — threatening West Pakistani dominance.
- Yahya Khan, with Bhutto's endorsement, **postponed convening the National Assembly** → general strike in East Pakistan.
- **March 1971:** Pakistani military sent tanks and forces into East Pakistan — Mujibur Rahman arrested.

### 4.2 India's Response

- **Millions of refugees** (mostly Hindus) fled to India — camps set up in **West Bengal, Tripura, Meghalaya, Madhya Pradesh, and Orissa.** Central government bore all costs.
- India mounted diplomatic efforts. **Henry Kissinger (US National Security Adviser)** delivered a letter from US President (Richard Nixon) asking India to send refugees back under UN supervision.
- **Nixon favoured Pakistan** (Pakistan had helped Kissinger secretly establish contact with China).
- **USSR agreed with India's position** and offered military equipment.
- **August 1971:** India and USSR signed the **Treaty of Peace, Friendship and Cooperation.**
- India trained **Mukti Bahini** — Bengali guerrillas comprising regular soldiers and volunteers — to fight in East Pakistan.

### 4.3 The War

- **December 3, 1971:** Pakistan attacked India on the western border, targeting airfields and Kashmir.
- India under **General Sam Manekshaw** responded in Kashmir, Punjab with massive air strikes.
- The **Indian Navy** moved towards Karachi; **Western Naval Command under Vice Admiral S.N. Kohli** launched a surprise attack on **Karachi port** (code-named **Trident**).
- **Indian Eastern Naval Command under Vice Admiral Nilakanta Krishnan** created a naval blockade in the Bay of Bengal.
- From **December 4**, aircraft carrier **INS Vikrant** deployed to attack major ports of East Pakistan.
- **December 6, 1971:** India formally recognised the **Provisional Government of the People's Republic of Bangladesh.**
- The **US sent Task Force 74** (led by aircraft carrier **USS Enterprise**) into the Bay of Bengal — but it was only a show of power.
- **December 16, 1971:** The **Instrument of Surrender** was signed at the **Ramna Race Course in Dacca** between:
  - **Lt. General Jagjit Singh Aurora** (GOC-in-C, Indian Eastern Command)
  - **Lt. General A.A.K. Niazi** (Commander, Pakistan Eastern Command)
- **Mujibur Rahman** assumed leadership of the new nation **Bangladesh.**
- **December 16:** Indira Gandhi announced a **unilateral ceasefire** on the western front; Pakistan announced ceasefire the next day.
- Indira Gandhi was hailed as **'Durga'** by many Indians.

### 4.4 The Simla Agreement (July 2, 1972)

- Yahya Khan resigned; **Zulfiqar Bhutto** took over Pakistan.
- Summit at **Simla in June 1972** — India sought a comprehensive treaty; Pakistan preferred a step-by-step approach.
- India wanted a **'no-war pact'** — had to accept a **'renunciation of force.'**
- Kashmir issue left hanging.
- **Line of Control** (LoC) to be respected by both sides — Pakistan added the caveat: *"without prejudice to the recognised position of either side."*
- Agreement (not treaty) signed on **July 2.**

**Key Principles of the Simla Agreement:**
- Both countries to put an end to conflict and confrontation.
- Principles and purposes of the **UN Charter** to govern relations.
- Differences to be settled by **peaceful bilateral negotiations.**
- Respect for each other's **territorial integrity, sovereignty, and political independence.**
- Non-interference in internal affairs.
- Withdrawal of Indian and Pakistani forces to their sides of the **international border.**
- In Jammu and Kashmir, the **Line of Control resulting from ceasefire of December 17, 1971** to be respected by both sides.
- Withdrawals to commence upon entry into force and be completed within **30 days.**
- Heads of both governments to meet again at a mutually convenient time.

## PART 5: Other Key Foreign Policy Developments (Indira Gandhi First Phase)

---

### 5.1 Pokhran I — "Smiling Buddha" (May 18, 1974)

- India conducted its **first nuclear test** in the **Thar Desert of Rajasthan**, near the village of **Pokhran** — underground detonation.
- Officially: **Pokhran I.** Code-named: **'Smiling Buddha.'**
- Formally called a **'peaceful nuclear explosion.'**
- The **first confirmed nuclear test by a non-permanent member of the UN Security Council.**
- Believed to be authorised by Indira Gandhi in light of China becoming a nuclear power.
- Nuclear device was of the **implosion type** (similar to the American bomb 'Fat Man'), developed under **Raja Ramanna** (Director, **BARC — Bhabha Atomic Research Centre**).
- Overseen by **Homi Sethna** (Chairman, Atomic Energy Commission).
- Other scientists: **P.K. Iyengar, R. Chidambaram, Basanti Nagchaudhuri, Waman Dattatreya Patwardhan.**
- International reaction: **Nuclear Suppliers Group** formed to check nuclear proliferation. **Canada suspended assistance** for two heavy water reactors. **Pakistan** cancelled talks and declared it would not accept India's domination.

### 5.2 Other Foreign Relations

- **Bangladesh:** Initially cordial relations with **Mujibur Rahman** (pro-India); he was assassinated in **1975** — Islamist and military regimes followed; relations became uncomfortable.
- **Sri Lanka:** Cordial relations with **Sirimavo Bandaranaike.** In **1974**, India ceded the **tiny islet of Katchatheevu** to Sri Lanka through the **Indo-Sri Lankan Maritime Agreement** to help stabilise Bandaranaike's government. In **1976**, another agreement restricted fishermen from fishing in each other's waters.
- **Soviet Union:** Deepened relations; USSR vetoed US resolution in UN Security Council calling for ceasefire and withdrawal during the 1971 war.
- **United States:** Strained relations — Indira resented aid with strings attached; refused to sign the **NPT (Treaty on Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons);** Nixon had clear bias for Pakistan; she criticised the Vietnam War.
- **West Asia:** Supported Palestinians; opposed Israel. Arab monarchies (Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, UAE) supported Pakistan. Libya viewed Indian intervention in East Pakistan as an attack on Islam.
- **ASEAN:** India viewed ASEAN as a pro-US organisation with SEATO links.
- **Africa:** Under Indira Gandhi, relations improved; she openly supported African liberation struggles.

## PART 6: Regional Developments Under Indira Gandhi

---

### 6.1 Tamil Nadu and Language

- **1967 elections:** **DMK** (formed of middle castes, anti-brahmin, anti-Hindi) won a huge majority — formed the **first non-Congress government** in Tamil Nadu under **C.N. Annadurai.**
- Since then, Tamil Nadu has alternated between **DMK and AIADMK** (the group that separated from DMK under **M.G. Ramachandran**).

### 6.2 Kerala and West Bengal

- **Kerala:** Congress failed; CPI and CPM formed government with **E.M.S. Namboodiripad** as CM again.
- **West Bengal (1969):** Congress lost; coalition of **CPM and Bangla Congress** formed government. **Ajoy Mukherjee** of Bangla Congress was CM; **Jyoti Basu** of CPM was Home Minister.

### 6.3 Naxalite Movement

- **CP(ML)** formed in **1969,** led by **Charu Mazumdar** — those who left CPM saying it betrayed the 'revolution.'
- Began in **Naxalbari** (bordering Nepal and erstwhile East Pakistan) — rural poor mobilised by **Kanu Sanyal** (CPM) around **1967** against landlords.
- Protests turned violent — landlords beheaded. The term **'Naxalites'** grew from this.
- CP(ML) also attacked rival communists; looked to **China and Maoism** for inspiration.
- **Successfully suppressed by the government** — but continued to influence certain areas.

### 6.4 Andhra Pradesh

- Fresh agitation demanding creation of **Telangana** with Hyderabad as capital.
- Naxalites also established themselves in rural areas.
- **N.T. Rama Rao (NTR),** popular film star, formed the **Telugu Desam Party in 1982** — spoke for Telugu-speaking people's self-respect; came to power in state elections end of 1982.

### 6.5 Maharashtra — Shiv Sena

- **Shiv Sena** founded in **1966** in Mumbai by **Balasaheb (Bal) Thackeray** (cartoonist).
- Vociferous demand for **'Bombay for Maharashtrians'** — targeted South Indians for allegedly taking jobs.
- Even attacked homes and commercial establishments of South Indians.
- Came **second to Congress** in Bombay municipal elections.

### 6.6 Kashmir and Sheikh Abdullah

- **1967 elections in Kashmir:** Widely seen as unfair.
- **Sheikh Abdullah** (released from house arrest) came to agree more to Kashmir's accession to India, especially after the **1971 war** changed South Asia's order.
- He agreed not to raise **self-determination** — demanded only **greater autonomy within the Indian Union.**
- Became **Chief Minister of Jammu and Kashmir** and leader of the **National Conference.**
- In **1975**, Indira Gandhi declared Jammu and Kashmir to be a **constituent of India.**

### 6.7 Punjab and Haryana

- **1966:** Indira Gandhi accepted the Akalis' demand — Punjab reorganised on linguistic lines:
  - Southern Hindi-speaking part → **Haryana** (separate state).
  - Hilly north-eastern areas → merged with **Himachal Pradesh.**
  - **Chandigarh** declared a **Union Territory** shared by both states as a capital.
- Demonstrations over Chandigarh's status in **1968–69;** freedom fighter **Darshan Singh Pheruman fasted unto death** — but the temporary arrangement continued indefinitely.

### 6.8 Mizoram

- **1966:** Mizo National Front (MNF) under **Laldenga** demanded sovereign independence of **Greater Mizoram.** Indira Gandhi used the army; IAF carried out strikes in Aizawl — **the only time IAF was used this way in civilian Indian territory.**
- **July 1971:** Mizo Hills turned into a Union Territory.
- **January 1972:** **Union Territory of Mizoram** came into being.
- (Under **Rajiv Gandhi**, Mizoram became a full-fledged state in **1987.**)

### 6.9 Nagaland

- Extremists demanded separate identity outside India; got help from **China.**
- Violent clashes with Indian Army.
- **March 1975:** President's Rule imposed.
- **Shillong Accord (November 1975):** Largest rebel groups agreed to lay down arms and accept the Indian Constitution. A small group did not agree — sporadic violence continued.

### 6.10 New States (1972)

- **Statehood granted to Meghalaya, Manipur, and Tripura** in **1972.**
- **North-East Frontier Agency** made a Union Territory and renamed **Arunachal Pradesh.**

### 6.11 Annexation of Sikkim (1975)

- Sikkim had been a **protectorate of India since 1950.**
- **Sikkim Congress** won massive victory in elections, campaigning for greater political rights.
- The **chogyal** (ruler) failed to suppress the movement; turned to India.
- India prepared a constitution for Sikkim, approved by national assembly in **1974.**
- **1975:** Special referendum — overwhelming majority voted for **merger with India.**
- Sikkim incorporated as India's **22nd state** in **May 1975** after the **Thirty-sixth Amendment** of the Constitution.
- Sikkim is of great **strategic importance** — borders China.

## PART 7: The Janata Party Years (March 1977 – January 1980)

---

### 7.1 Morarji Desai — First Non-Congress Prime Minister

- Janata Party won massive majority in 1977 under the symbol of **'chakrahaldhar.'**
- Three contenders for PM: **Morarji Desai, Charan Singh, Jagjivan Ram.**
- **Acharya Kripalani and Jayaprakash Narayan** chose **Morarji Desai.**
- Desai took oath as PM on **March 23, 1977.**
- **Charan Singh** (Home, later Finance) and **Jagjivan Ram** (Defence) were Deputy PMs.

### 7.2 Fresh State Assembly Elections (June 1977)

Results of state elections:
- **Janata Party** formed governments in: **UP, Bihar, Haryana, Orissa, MP, Rajasthan, Himachal Pradesh.**
- **Punjab:** Janata Party + Akali Dal coalition.
- **Tamil Nadu:** **AIADMK** swept elections; **M.G. Ramachandran** became CM — pioneered the **midday meal scheme** to encourage children (especially girls) to attend school.
- **West Bengal:** Left coalition won; **Jyoti Basu** (CPM) became CM — undertook agrarian reforms benefiting many poor peasants.
- **J&K:** Sheikh Abdullah revived **National Conference** — won comfortable majority in the **first truly fair and free polls in the state since Independence.** National Conference did poorly in Hindu-dominated Jammu.

### 7.3 New President

- **Neelam Sanjiva Reddy** elected President in **June 1977** (necessitated by death of Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed in February 1977).

### 7.4 Restoration of Democratic Rights — The Janata Government's Legacy

The Janata government promptly reversed Emergency measures:

**43rd Amendment (1977):**
- Deleted **Article 31D** giving Parliament powers to curtail legitimate trade union activity under guise of preventing anti-national activities.
- Restored to states the legislative powers to make appropriate provisions for anti-national activities consistent with **Fundamental Rights.**
- Restored the **power of judiciary** to invalidate laws.
- High Courts given power to examine constitutional validity of central laws.

**44th Amendment (1978):**
- Made proclamation of Emergency **more difficult:**
  - Requires **written advice of the cabinet** (not just PM).
  - Parliament must approve within **one month** by a **two-thirds majority.**
  - Renewal needs a **parliamentary vote every six months.**
  - **'Internal disturbances'** replaced with **'armed rebellion'** as cause for Emergency.
  - A special Lok Sabha session can be called by **one-tenth of members** to revoke Emergency; revocation by **simple majority.**
  - **Articles 20 and 21** cannot be suspended even during Emergency.
  - Other fundamental rights not suspended automatically — requires **separate Presidential order.**
  - Emergency provisions can be **challenged in courts.**
- Restored term of Lok Sabha and state assemblies to **5 years.**
- Courts restored power to decide **election disputes of President, PM, Speaker, etc.**
- President can **return cabinet advice once** for review.
- Deleted **Article 31** (Right to Property) from Fundamental Rights — made a **legal right** only.

### 7.5 Economic Contradictions of the Janata

- No single ideology — mix of socialists, trade unionists, pro-business leaders.
- **Sixth Five-Year Plan (1978–83)** — called the **rolling plan** *(targets can be revised annually)* — aimed to boost agriculture and rural industries.
- **George Fernandes** (Industries Minister) promoted economic self-reliance — expelled **Coca-Cola and IBM** from India.
- **Food for Work Programme (April 1977):** Government food grain stocks used to pay workers in kind in rural areas to build roads and water ponds during agricultural off-season.
- **Madhu Dandavate** (Railways Minister) initiated steps to reduce corruption in ticket reservation, repair worn-out tracks, and cushioned wooden seats in sleeper second class with two inches of foam.
- Problems of inflation, fuel shortages, unemployment, and poverty could not be addressed effectively.

### 7.6 Foreign Relations Under Janata

- Made effort to improve ties with **US** — US President **Jimmy Carter** became the **first US president to visit India after Eisenhower.**
- Maintained cordial relations with **USSR** — Morarji Desai and Vajpayee (Foreign Minister) visited Moscow.
- **1979:** **Vajpayee** became the **highest-ranking Indian official to visit Beijing** — diplomatic ties re-established with China (ties had been suspended since the **1962 war.**) Agreed on regular dialogue to resolve territorial disputes, expand trade, and improve border security.

### 7.7 Social Developments

- **OBCs** (Other Backward Classes) had gained economic power from Green Revolution and White Revolution; now sought space in administration.
- **January 1979:** Janata government appointed the **Second Backward Classes Commission — popularly the Mandal Commission** — under **Bindeshwari Prasad Mandal** (former CM of Bihar) to examine whether reservation in central government jobs for OBCs should be implemented.

### 7.8 Futile Commissions and Belchi

- Various commissions to probe Emergency excesses — most famous: **Justice J.C. Shah Commission** (retired SC judge) to enquire into Emergency atrocities. Most allegations could not be proved.
- **May 1977, Belchi (Bihar):** A group of Harijans burnt to death by upper caste mob over a land dispute.
- **Indira Gandhi** travelled to Belchi — the last bit on an **elephant** as there was no clear path. This masterstroke rebuilt her image as champion of the poor and Harijans.

### 7.9 Fall of the Janata Government

- **Morarji Desai-Charan Singh antagonism** grew; Desai dismissed Charan Singh and **Raj Narain** in mid-1978.
- **'Dual membership' issue:** Jana Sangh members refused to give up RSS membership — Socialists and Charan Singh's party walked out.
- Government reduced to a minority; **Morarji Desai resigned in July 1979.**

### 7.10 Charan Singh — PM Who Never Faced Parliament

- **Charan Singh negotiated with Indira Gandhi** for outside support.
- Sworn in as PM towards end of July 1979 — gave Independence Day speech from Red Fort.
- **Indira Gandhi withdrew support just one day before the confidence vote** — because Charan Singh had not disbanded the special courts prosecuting her.
- Charan Singh resigned; president explored other options; none feasible.
- **Lok Sabha dissolved; fresh elections called.**
- Charan Singh remained **caretaker PM** till elections.

## PART 8: Indira Gandhi — Second Phase (January 1980 – October 1984)

---

### 8.1 Return to Power

- **1980 General Elections (Seventh Lok Sabha):** Congress (I) returned with a **strong majority.**
- Indira Gandhi won from **Rae Bareilly (UP)** and **Medak (Andhra Pradesh)** — chose to keep Medak seat.
- **June 1980:** Sanjay Gandhi died in a plane crash while attempting stunts.
- **Rajiv Gandhi** (airline pilot, no political experience) was reluctantly drawn into politics — elected from **Amethi.**

### 8.2 Economy — New Sixth Five-Year Plan (1980–85)

- **Janata's Sixth Plan** curtailed; a new **Sixth Five-Year Plan (1980–85)** launched.
- Focused on increasing growth, industrialisation, reducing poverty and unemployment.
- **Integrated Rural Development Programme (IRDP)** launched on **October 2, 1980.**
- **National Rural Employment Programme (NREP)** launched in **October 1980;** became regular from **April 1981.**
- **1982:** National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development **(NABARD)** established on recommendation of the **Shivaraman Committee.**
- Sixth Plan initiated **first steps towards economic liberalisation** — government launched **Operation Forward in 1982** (cautious, limited impact).

### 8.3 Environmental Legislation

- Indira Gandhi had attended and spoken at the **first UN Conference on Environment (1972).**
- Earlier implemented the **Wildlife Protection Act (1972)** and **Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act (1974).** The Wildlife Protection Act facilitated wildlife sanctuaries, national parks, and **Project Tiger (1973).**
- In her second stint: **Forest (Conservation) Act (1980)** and **Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act (1981)** enacted.

### 8.4 India's Man in Space

- As part of a joint **ISRO-Soviet Intercosmos** programme, IAF pilot **Rakesh Sharma** was selected in **1982** as cosmonaut.
- On **April 2, 1984**, he flew on board **Soyuz T-11** with two Soviet cosmonauts to the space station **Salyut 7.**
- Conducted experiments in **bio-medicine, remote sensing, and effects of yoga during weightlessness.**
- When Indira Gandhi asked how India looked from space, Sharma replied in Hindi: **"Sare jahan se achcha" (the best in the world).**

### 8.5 Siachen Conflict (Operation Meghdoot, April 1984)

- **Siachen Glacier** — between the **Saltoro ridge line** and the **main Karakoram range,** in disputed Kashmir region. Water feeds the **Indus river.**
- Conflict rooted in lack of demarcation beyond map coordinate **NJ9842.**
- Pakistan encouraged expeditions to the glacier based on permits from their government.
- India became aware of this around **1978** and sent an Indian Army expedition as a counter-exercise.
- **Operation Meghdoot (April 1984):** Indian armed forces freed the glacier from Pakistani domination — **Siachen became the world's highest battlefield.**
- India victorious — secured **Sia La and Bilfond La** passes; Pakistan retained **Gyong La.**

### 8.6 NAM Summit (1983)

- India hosted the **1983 NAM (Non-Aligned Movement) summit** at Delhi.
- Indira Gandhi became **NAM chairperson.**
- Brought attention to the link between **disarmament and economic development.**
- Actively worked for a **new international economic order** for developing countries.

### 8.7 Punjab Turmoil and Operation Blue Star

**Background:**
- Akalis wanted greater autonomy; resented that their state was under Central interference.
- **1973: Anandpur Resolution** — demanded greater autonomy; used term **'Sikh nation'** (open to interpretation of separation).
- **Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale** rose to prominence — spoke against Nirankaris, mocked Hindus and 'modern' Sikhs.
- Said to have been initially built up by **Congress as a counterpoise to Akalis** — became "a Frankenstein's monster."
- **June 1980:** Students at the **Golden Temple in Amritsar** declared the formation of **Khalistan** (independent Sikh republic); president to be **Jagjit Singh Chauhan from London.**
- Bhindranwale suspected of assassinations of Nirankari leaders.
- **Republic Day 1983:** Akali legislators resigned en masse from state assembly.
- **October 1983:** Bus stopped; Hindu passengers shot. Centre imposed **President's Rule** in Punjab.
- Bhindranwale moved into the **Akal Takht** — seat of temporal authority for Sikhs.
- By early **1984:** Golden Temple complex being fortified with arms, ammunition, food — under command of **Major General Shubeg Singh** (decorated hero, later dismissed from army).

**Operation Blue Star:**
- Approved by Indira Gandhi on recommendation of **Army Chief A.S. Vaidya.**
- **Night of June 2–3, 1984:** Curfew imposed; communication suspended; electricity interrupted; media censored.
- **Night of June 5:** Army stormed **Harmandir Sahib** under **Major General K.S. Brar**, directed by **General K. Sundarji.**
- Militants had sophisticated weapons — **tanks had to be used** against Akal Takht.
- Army had full control by **morning of June 7.**
- **Bhindranwale and Shubeg Singh found dead.** Many militants and army personnel also killed.

**Aftermath:**
- Caused deep distress to Sikhs worldwide — many left the Indian Army; mutinies by Sikh soldiers reported.
- On **October 31, 1984:** While walking to her office, **Indira Gandhi was shot by her Sikh security guards Beant Singh and Satwant Singh.** She was rushed to **All India Institute of Medical Sciences** but did not survive.
- All India Radio and Doordarshan made the official announcement only in the **evening.**
- Her son **Rajiv Gandhi was sworn in as PM by President Zail Singh** that evening — Congress leaders unanimously decided he should take the post.

## PART 9: The Rajiv Gandhi Years (October 1984 – December 1989)

---

### 9.1 Anti-Sikh Riots (November 1984)

- As Indira Gandhi's body lay in state at **Teen Murti House,** sporadic incidents of anti-Sikh violence deepened.
- By **November 1,** organised brutal mob violence against Sikhs began across Delhi.
- Mobs composed mainly of **"scheduled caste sweepers, Jat farmers, and Gujjar pastoralists"** (per Ramachandra Guha).
- Thousands of Sikhs killed, homes burnt, businesses looted. **Several Congress leaders alleged to have instigated mobs.**
- Police either turned a blind eye or actively helped mobs.
- **Rajiv Gandhi and Home Minister Narasimha Rao** did not call in the army immediately.
- **November 2:** Rajiv Gandhi declared over radio/TV that rioting should stop.
- **November 3:** Army finally got orders.
- On **November 19** (Indira Gandhi's birth anniversary), Rajiv Gandhi said: *"When a great tree falls, the earth around it shakes"* — widely interpreted as justifying the riots.
- Widely called **'genocide'** by many; a tragic blot in India's post-Independence history.
- **Silver lining:** Many Hindus hid and helped Sikh families during the riots.
- **Delhi High Court (2009):** Called the incidents a matter of national shame.

### 9.2 Bhopal Gas Tragedy (December 3, 1984)

- Leak of **toxic methyl isocyanate (MIC) gas** from the **Union Carbide Corporation (UCC)** pesticide plant at **Bhopal.**
- Killed thousands; left many more permanently impaired.
- Gas caused **internal haemorrhage, lung failure, and death** — worst hit were inhabitants of nearby villages and slums.
- One of the **worst industrial tragedies in world history.**
- **UCC** tried to shift culpability to Indian subsidiary **UCIL.**
- First multi-billion dollar lawsuit filed by an American attorney in a US court.
- **March 1985:** India enacted the **Bhopal Gas Leak Disaster Act** — government became sole representative of victims in legal proceedings worldwide.
- All cases removed from US jurisdiction to Indian jurisdiction.
- Under **Supreme Court aegis,** UCC agreed to pay **$470 million** to Indian government as full and final settlement.
- Allegations that American pressure allowed **UCC chairman Warren Anderson** to leave India without arrest.
- Controversy and suffering of people continues.

### 9.3 The 1985 General Elections

- Rajiv Gandhi recommended early elections; held **December 1984** (instead of January 1985).
- **Congress (I) won more than 400 seats** — the largest majority in independent India's Lok Sabha election history till then.
- Partly sympathy wave; largely desire to keep extremism and separatism down.
- **Rajiv Gandhi became the youngest prime minister of India.**
- **March 1985 state elections:** Congress (I) came to power in **8 out of 11 states.**

### 9.4 Tackling Ethnic Tensions

**Punjab:**
- Akali leader **Sant Harcharan Singh Longowal** and Rajiv Gandhi signed an agreement in **July 1985.** Longowal shot dead in **August 1985.**
- State elections in September; **Akali Dal** won with a comfortable majority (vote against extremism).
- Militancy revived; **Operation Black Thunder (May 1988)** under **J.F. Ribeiro and K.P.S. Gill** flushed militants from Golden Temple — a smoother operation.

**Assam:**
- Rajiv Gandhi and **AASU (All Assam Students Union)** signed **Assam Accord on August 15, 1985.**
- President's Rule removed; elections held December.
- AASU became **Asom Gana Parishad** — contested polls; won convincingly.

**Mizoram:**
- **1986 accord:** Mizo rebels surrendered arms; Mizoram granted statehood.
- **Laldenga** — once a fiery rebel — became Chief Minister of **Mizoram,** which became the **23rd state of India in February 1987.**

**Darjeeling:**
- **Gorkha National Liberation Front under Subhash Ghisingh** demanded a separate state for Nepali-speaking people.
- Ghisingh agreed to accept an **autonomous district council** after meeting Rajiv Gandhi.

**Tripura:**
- **Tripura National Volunteers (TNV)** caused violence.
- **August 1988:** Memorandum of Understanding signed — TNV agreed to abjure violence, give up secessionist demands; tribal seats in assembly to be increased; more villages under autonomous tribal councils.

**Jammu and Kashmir:**
- Congress and National Conference formed a **coalition caretaker government (1986).**
- **1987 elections:** Wide-ranging rigging favoured Congress-NC — **Muslim United Front (MUF)** candidates (seeking greater autonomy) were cheated.
- This was the **beginning of militancy in Kashmir** — Kashmiri youth turned to Pakistan for help.

### 9.5 Positive Steps — Rajiv Gandhi's Governance

**Anti-Defection Act (January 1985):**
- Elected member of a legislature cannot join opposition party till next election, or he/she faces disqualification.
- Aimed at curbing corruption and bribery of MPs/MLAs.

**Environmental Legislation (1986):**
- **Environment Protection Act (EPA)** — created **Ministry of Environment and Forests (MoEF).**
- EPA gave Centre authority to close, prohibit, or regulate any industry.
- **1987:** Amendments to **Factories Act, 1948** — states could appoint site appraisal committees for factories using hazardous processes.
- **1989:** **Hazardous Waste (Management and Handling) Rules** enacted.
- (1991 would see the **Public Liability Insurance Act** for immediate relief to those affected by hazardous substance accidents.)

**Panchayati Raj:**
- Tried to amend Constitution to make panchayati elections mandatory — could not be passed in his tenure but paved the way for **73rd and 74th Amendments** later.

**First Steps Towards Liberalising the Economy:**
- First budget (Finance Minister **V.P. Singh**) — simplified licensing, liberalised trade, reduced duties on imports, incentives for exports, reduced tax rates.
- Industry growth was good as a result; however, businessmen and politicians got closer, corruption increased.

**Technology Missions:**
- Six technology missions set up to use science and technology to tackle underdevelopment:
  - **Drinking water mission** (using satellites, geology, civil engineering, biochemistry).
  - Missions for **milk yield improvement** and cow health.
  - Expanding **edible oil production** to curb imports.
  - Improving **health, especially child immunisation against polio.**
  - **Literacy** — spreading TV network to rural areas.
  - **Telecommunications** — Rajiv Gandhi's most famous mission.
- **Sam Pitroda** (US-trained telecom expert) became adviser and Chairman of the **Telecom Commission.**
- **MTNL (Mahanagar Telephone Nigam Limited)** was set up.

**Computerisation:**
- Reduced import duties on components; allowed foreign manufacturers to enter market for quality and competitive prices.
- India emerged as a **major software exporter** as a result.

**Education Policy:**
- Rajiv Gandhi promulgated the **National Policy on Education (NPE) 1986** — special emphasis on removal of disparities and equalising educational opportunity.
- Included **Operation Blackboard** to improve primary school infrastructure.
- **IGNOU (Indira Gandhi National Open University)** set up for adult education.
- Concept of **Navodaya Vidyalaya in every district** — residential schools providing quality education free to poor meritorious children.

### 9.6 Negative Aspects

**Shah Bano Case (1985–86):**
- **Supreme Court** upheld a lower court verdict ordering **Mohammed Ahmed Khan** to pay maintenance to his divorced wife **Shah Bano.**
- Court declared **Section 125 of the CrPC** prevails over personal law in cases of conflict.
- **Conservative Muslims** protested; a private member's bill to exempt Muslims from Section 125 was introduced — **Arif Mohammed Khan** (MoS, Home Ministry) opposed it and it was defeated.
- Thousands of Muslims demonstrated. Rajiv Gandhi government bowed to pressure.
- **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Bill (1986)** — introduced to **overturn the SC judgement.** Whip issued to Congress MPs to vote for it; passed.
- Confined maintenance to the **iddat** (three-month) period; placed onus on divorced wife's relatives or **Wakf Board.**
- **Arif Mohammed Khan resigned in protest.**

**Babri Mosque Gates Opening (February 1, 1986):**
- In **British times**, Babri Mosque in Ayodhya was used by Muslims; Hindus worshipped Ram on an external platform.
- **1949:** Idol of Ram Lalla placed inside the mosque one night; local authorities locked the premises.
- **1980s:** VHP and Hindu groups launched campaign to construct a **Ram Mandir** at the site.
- **February 1, 1986:** Faizabad district judge ordered doors of the mosque be opened for devotees.
- Reports suggested Rajiv Gandhi government **ensured immediate opening** — TV cameras present, suggesting advance knowledge.
- Widely seen as an attempt to **balance the Muslim Women's Act** by placating hard-core Hindus.
- **VHP emboldened** — demand for demolition of the mosque became vociferous.
- Tarnished Rajiv Gandhi's image as a modern, forward-looking leader.

**Bofors Scandal:**
- **March 1986:** India signed agreement with **Swedish arms manufacturer AB Bofors** for supply of **400 x 155mm Howitzer guns.** Deal: **$285 million (approximately Rs 1,500 crore).**
- **April 1987:** Swedish radio claimed **bribes paid to top Indian politicians** and defence personnel.
- **Chitra Subramaniam** (Hindu correspondent in Geneva) revealed **over Rs 64 crore** paid to powerful people.
- Name of **Ottavio Quattrocchi** (Italian businessman close to the Gandhi family) appeared.
- **V.P. Singh** (shifted from Finance to Defence) appeared to have uncovered details — was finally dropped from cabinet.
- Rajiv Gandhi — nicknamed **'Mr Clean'** — suffered **irreparable damage to his image.**
- **July 1988:** Rajiv Gandhi introduced a **draconian press bill** — editor/proprietor could be imprisoned for 'criminal imputation' and 'scurrilous writings' (undefined). The bill was dropped after strong opposition.

**Agrarian Unrest:**
- **1987 drought** — severe; **Kalahandi district in Orissa** particularly affected.
- Farmers' organisations:
  - **Shetkari Sanghatana** (Maharashtra) led by **Sharad Joshi.**
  - **Bharatiya Kisan Union** (Punjab, Haryana) led by **Mahendra Singh Tikait.**
- Demanded higher price for agricultural produce and subsidised power.
- (Note: These were mainly representatives of rich and middle-level farmers, not the really poor.)

### 9.7 The IPKF Misadventure in Sri Lanka

**Background:**
- **LTTE (Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam)** under **Velupillai Prabakaran** demanded an independent Tamil state in Sri Lanka.
- Tamil Nadu sympathised; LTTE used Tamil Nadu as refuge.
- Believed that governments under Indira and Rajiv Gandhi **surreptitiously supported Tamil militants.**
- **June 4, 1987:** India ordered **relief supplies air-dropped** into siege of Jaffna — called **Operation Poomalai (Eagle-Mission-4)** — after warning Sri Lanka not to stop planes. Sri Lanka accused India of "blatant violation of sovereignty."
- Siege of Jaffna was lifted.

**Indo-Sri Lanka Peace Accord (July 29, 1987):**
- Signed by **Sri Lankan President J.R. Jayawardene** and **Rajiv Gandhi** in Colombo.
- (On this visit, Rajiv Gandhi was **assaulted by a Sri Lankan** in the Guard of Honour — escaped serious injury.)
- Terms:
  - Sri Lankan government to devolve power to Tamil-majority areas.
  - Sri Lankan troops to withdraw to barracks in the north.
  - Tamil militants to surrender arms; **LTTE to be dissolved.**
  - **Tamil to be designated an official language of Sri Lanka.**
- **Tamil groups were not party to the talks.**

**The IPKF:**
- **Indian Peace Keeping Force (IPKF)** sent to Sri Lanka's northern and eastern provinces to "guarantee and enforce the cessation of hostilities."
- **LTTE refused to surrender arms** — threatened Tamils who opposed them.
- IPKF and LTTE got into **military confrontation.**
- IPKF captured LTTE headquarters in Jaffna — militants retreated into jungles and used guerrilla tactics.
- Large number of Indian soldiers died; operation was hugely costly.
- Pressure in India to recall the force.
- **IPKF misadventure directly caused the assassination of Rajiv Gandhi on May 21, 1991** — by an LTTE suicide bomber at **Sriperumbudur in Tamil Nadu.**

### 9.8 General Elections of 1989

- **V.P. Singh** floated the **Jan Morcha** (with Arun Nehru and Arif Mohammed Khan), which merged with Janata Party, Lok Dal, and Congress (S) to form **Janata Dal.**
- Janata Dal joined regional parties (DMK, Telugu Desam, Asom Gana Parishad) to form the **National Front.**
- **November 1989:** General elections held — Congress did badly; people had lost faith due to corruption allegations.
- **1989: first election in which no single party got a majority on its own** — marked the beginning of the coalition era.
- Rajiv Gandhi **resigned in December 1989.**

---

## PART 10: V.P. Singh Years (December 1989 – November 1990)

---

### 10.1 Government Formation

- National Front did not have majority but formed government with **BJP and Left parties offering outside support.**
- **Vishwanath Pratap Singh sworn in as PM on December 2, 1989.**
- One of V.P. Singh's first decisions: **recall the IPKF from Sri Lanka.**

### 10.2 Kashmir Situation Worsens

- **December 1989:** **JKLF (Jammu and Kashmir Liberation Front)** kidnapped the daughter of Home Minister **Mufti Mohammad Sayeed.**
- Centre **capitulated** — released jailed militants.
- **Hizb-ul Mujahideen** under **Syed Salauddin** (who had left mainstream politics after 1987 rigged elections) grew more prominent — hardline Islamic agenda.
- Governor changed; **Jagmohan** appointed.
- **Kashmiri Pandits** became targets — many were killed; exodus from the Valley to Jammu and beyond. Many still live in refugee camps.

### 10.3 Mandal Commission Implementation

- **Mandal Commission (Backward Classes Commission)** had submitted its report in **December 1980** — quietly shelved by both Indira Gandhi and Rajiv Gandhi.
- **Main recommendation:** **27% reservation** in central government jobs for Other Backward Castes (OBCs) — beyond the SC/ST reservation.
- Same relaxation in age as for SC/ST to be given to OBCs.
- **August 7, 1990:** V.P. Singh announced in Parliament — government accepted the Mandal Commission report — **27% reservation** for Socially and Educationally Backward Classes in all levels of central government services.
- **Massive protests** — self-immolation bids and suicides by upper caste youth in northern India.
- Supreme Court **stayed the order.**
- **November 1992:** Nine-judge SC bench gave its decision — **six judges upheld** the constitutionality; three offered dissenting view.
- SC conditions: Reservations in total must not exceed **50%;** caste criterion restricted to **recruitment level, not promotions.**
- (By the time of judgement, most parties had reconciled to the order.)
- **Narasimha Rao government (September 1991)** issued a fresh order in favour of Mandal — with preference within the 27% for **poorer sections of OBCs.**

### 10.4 Mandal to Mandir — Rath Yatra and Fall of Government

- **BJP president L.K. Advani** launched the **Ram Rath Yatra** from **Somnath in September 1990** — van converted to look like a chariot — heading to **Ayodhya** through several states.
- Bihar CM **Lalu Prasad Yadav** got Advani **arrested on charges of disturbing peace** and communal tension when the procession entered Bihar.
- Widespread disturbances; many kar sevaks arrested by Mulayam Singh government in UP.
- **BJP withdrew support** to the National Front government.
- V.P. Singh lost the **confidence vote** and **resigned in November 1990.**

---

## PART 11: Chandra Shekhar Government (November 1990 – June 1991)

---

- **Chandra Shekhar** (with 64 MPs, having formed **Samajwadi Janata Party** after leaving Janata Dal) formed government with **outside Congress support.**
- Sworn in as PM on **November 10, 1990.**

**Economic Crisis:**
- Foreign exchange levels fell to a dangerous low.
- Large fiscal imbalances accumulated through the 1980s; huge debt from loans.
- Gulf War caused oil price hike.
- By **January 1991:** Government convinced IMF to approve two loans (Finance Minister: **Yashwant Sinha**).
- Congress withdrew support on flimsy grounds — government could not present the full budget.
- **March 1991:** Government forced to **pledge India's gold reserves** to manage the foreign exchange situation — State Bank of India leased gold confiscated from smugglers. Though criticised, this was an **inevitable move** that helped avert a sovereign payment default and enabled the next government's economic reforms.
- **Chandra Shekhar resigned in March 1991;** Lok Sabha dissolved; fresh elections announced.
- Chandra Shekhar continued as **caretaker PM till June.**

**Elections of 1991:**
- First phase in May; then on **night of May 21,** tragedy struck: **Rajiv Gandhi assassinated at Sriperumbudur, Tamil Nadu** — by LTTE suicide bomber while campaigning.
- Second phase in June.
- Variation in results: Congress did poorly in constituencies that voted before assassination; did remarkably well after.
- **Congress emerged as the largest party with 244 seats** — not a simple majority.
- LTTE's action led to **total loss of support for it in India.**

---

## PART 12: Narasimha Rao Years (June 1991 – May 1996)

---

### 12.1 The Man and the Government

- **P.V. Narasimha Rao** chosen to lead Congress (I) — erudite scholar, knew several languages, Congress loyalist. Had held portfolios of External Affairs, Defence, Human Resources, and Home under Indira and Rajiv Gandhi.
- Formed a **minority government.**
- Had not contested elections in 1991 — won a by-election from **Nandyal, Andhra Pradesh** after being sworn in as PM.

### 12.2 Economic Reforms of 1991 — India's Transformation

**Context:**
- Economy in shambles — precarious balance of payments, high inflation.
- Soviet economic model discredited; China had undertaken market-oriented reforms under **Deng Xiao Ping.**
- Rao opted for **middle path** — market reforms but not free market; minimum pain to masses while achieving high growth.

**Finance Minister: Manmohan Singh** (non-political economist, Chairman of UGC at the time).

**The New Economic Policy of 1991** aimed at:
- Correcting fiscal and balance of payments weaknesses.
- Removing rigidities in various sectors.
- Controlling inflation; releasing industries from unnecessary controls.

**The Three Pillars: Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation (LPG)**

**Key Measures:**

- **Rupee devalued** — to help exports.
- **Fiscal deficit corrected** — bold budget step.
- Gradual reduction of **import duties, income tax, and corporate tax.**
- Quotas on imports of **machinery, equipment, and manufactured intermediate goods removed.**
- Rationalisation of tariff structure; reduction in **customs duties on capital goods.**
- **Imports of technology freed.**
- **Industrial Policy of 1991 (revolutionary):** Rao held Industries portfolio himself.
  - Industrial licensing made applicable only to **environmentally sensitive or security-related industries.**
  - **MRTP Act modified** — removed sections restricting growth or preventing mergers.
  - Industries reserved for public sector **drastically reduced.**
  - Public sector granted **more autonomy.**
  - Private investment welcomed in **infrastructure.**
- Foreign ownership restrictions liberalised; private sector allowed in **insurance, banking, telecom, air travel.**
- **Controller of Capital Issues abolished.**
- **SEBI Act (1992)** and **Security Laws (Amendment)** enacted.
- **National Stock Exchange** set up as computer-based trading system.

### 12.3 Panchayati Raj and Municipal Government

- **73rd Constitutional Amendment:** New Chapter **Part-IX** inserted; **Eleventh Schedule (29 subjects)** for panchayats added. Constitutional implementation of a **Directive Principle.**
- **74th Constitutional Amendment:** New Chapter **Part-IX A** — states have constitutional obligation to adopt municipalities as per the prescribed system.
- Both panchayats and municipal bodies have **reservation for SC/ST and women.**

### 12.4 Security and Space Technology

- Punjab situation controlled; elections in **2002** led to militancy dying down.
- **Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act** passed against Pakistani infiltrators.
- **Prithvi 1 missile** inducted into the army.
- India's **nuclear programme** reportedly advanced under Rao.
- Space: Successful tests of **Augmented Satellite Launch Vehicle** and **Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle.**

### 12.5 Foreign Policy Under Rao

- **USSR disintegrated (1991)** — India had to rethink foreign policy with no Cold War.
- India's relations with **US gradually improved.**
- Built relations with **Japan, Israel, Brazil, South Africa.**
- Launched the **"Look East Policy"** — bringing India closer to ASEAN economically, strategically, and culturally. (Policy taken up by subsequent governments.)
- Visited **China (1993)** — useful when Pakistan brought a Kashmir human rights resolution in the UN: **China and Iran** did not support it.

### 12.6 Negative Aspects — Babri Masjid Demolition (December 6, 1992)

- VHP announced **December 6, 1992** as the date for temple work to begin.
- Thousands of **kar sevaks** converged on Ayodhya.
- Despite RSS and police asking them to stop, groups of kar sevaks moved towards the mosque.
- **BJP leaders (including L.K. Advani)** reportedly tried to call kar sevaks back — to no avail.
- Police did little. **The mosque was attacked and reduced to rubble.**
- **BJP** clarified it was not a party to the vandalism.
- **Communal riots broke out** across India — Bombay among the worst affected; Shiv Sena stoked violence.
- **January 1993:** Bomb blasts in Bombay — orchestrated by mafia dons based in Dubai, allegedly in retaliation for attacks on Muslims.
- Uttar Pradesh government (Kalyan Singh, BJP) dismissed; President's Rule imposed — **only after the destruction.**
- India's image damaged worldwide.

**Liberhan Commission:**
- Appointed **10 days after the demolition** by the Home Ministry.
- One-man commission — **Justice Liberhan** (sitting judge, Punjab and Haryana High Court).
- Submitted report **17 years later in June 2009** (UPA government).
- Indicted **68 people** including **L.K. Advani, Murli Manohar Joshi, Atal Bihari Vajpayee, Kalyan Singh.**
- Identified **Kalyan Singh-led BJP government in UP** as key to execution of the conspiracy.
- Report contained **no criticism of Narasimha Rao** — accepted his contention that it was not constitutionally possible to impose President's Rule before the event.

### 12.7 JMM Bribery Case and Corruption

- **July 1993:** No-confidence motion — JMM members and Janata Dal (Ajit Singh group) voted against it, enabling government to survive.
- Alleged that **JMM members were bribed by Narasimha Rao** through a representative.
- **Special court convicted Rao and colleague Buta Singh** — overturned on appeal in **2002** due to doubt about the credibility of the witness.
- Rao's name also dragged into **Harshad Mehta stock market scam, hawala case, and Lakhubhai cheating case** — cleared in all.

### 12.8 General Elections of 1996

- May 1996: Narasimha Rao resigned after Congress's **resounding defeat.**
- Election fought on three themes: **Ayodhya, economy, corruption.**
- No party got enough seats to form government on its own.
- **BJP won most seats (161);** allies won 26 (total 187).
- Congress came second; **National Front (Janata Dal + Telugu Desam + Left Front)** won 114 seats.

## PART 13: Rise of the Dalit Voice and Regional Politics

---

### 13.1 Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP) and Kanshi Ram

- **Kanshi Ram** — quit government job, mobilised Dalit government employees into **BAMCEF (All India Backward and Minority Communities Employees Federation).**
- Founded the **Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP)** — to represent not just Dalits but OBCs, Muslims, and others.
- Area of influence: mainly **Uttar Pradesh.**
- BSP fought **1984 elections** — no seat won but considerable votes.
- **1993:** Won more than **60 seats** in UP — became a major player alongside Samajwadi Party and BJP.
- **Mayawati** (Kanshi Ram's protégé) won Lok Sabha seat in **1989.**
- **June 1995:** Mayawati created history — became **India's first-ever Dalit woman Chief Minister of Uttar Pradesh.** Came back multiple times; was CM of UP **2007–2012.**

## PART 14: 1996–1999 — Three Prime Ministers

---

### 14.1 Vajpayee's Short-Lived First Term

- **BJP** (largest party, 161 seats) called by **President Shankar Dayal Sharma** to form government.
- **Atal Bihari Vajpayee** sworn in as PM in **May 1996** — found no allies; **resigned after 13 days** rather than lose confidence vote.

### 14.2 United Front Government — Deve Gowda and I.K. Gujral

- **United Front** (13 parties including Deve Gowda's Janata Dal, Tamil Maanila Congress, DMK, Asom Gana Parishad) formed government with **outside Congress support.** Communists joined later.
- Government fell in **April 1997** when Congress withdrew support.
- **I.K. Gujral** sworn in as PM on **April 21, 1997.**

**The Gujral Doctrine:**
A policy for friendship with neighbouring countries based on **sovereign equality and non-interference.** Five principles:
1. With **Nepal, Bangladesh, Bhutan, Maldives, and Sri Lanka** — India does not ask for reciprocity but gives in good faith.
2. No South Asian country will allow its territory to be used against another country in the region.
3. None will interfere in the internal affairs of another.
4. All South Asian countries must respect each other's **territorial integrity and sovereignty.**
5. All disputes to be settled through **peaceful bilateral negotiations.**

**Fall of Gujral Government:**
- **Lalu Prasad Yadav** implicated in the **Fodder Scam** — refused to resign; left to form **Rashtriya Janata Dal (July 1997)** while extending support to United Front.
- **Jain Commission report** (on Rajiv Gandhi assassination conspiracy) criticised **DMK** for tacitly supporting Tamil militants accused in the assassination.
- Congress demanded dismissal of DMK ministers; Gujral refused.
- **Congress withdrew support on November 28, 1997** — Gujral resigned.

### 14.3 1998 General Elections

- **Sonia Gandhi** (Rajiv Gandhi's widow) entered politics and campaigned for Congress.
- **Fourth 'hung' house** since 1989 — coalition era entrenched.
- BJP came ahead; formed alliance with regional parties from Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Orissa, and West Bengal — became the **NDA (National Democratic Alliance).**

---

## PART 15: NDA Years — First Stint (March 1998 – October 1999)

---

### 15.1 Pokhran II — Operation Shakti (May 1998)

- **Series of five nuclear explosions** at **Pokhran Test Range, Rajasthan.**
- Termed **Operation Shakti** — underground detonation of a fission device, fusion devices, and a sub-kiloton device.
- PM **Vajpayee** declared India a **full-fledged nuclear state.**
- Key scientists: **A.P.J. Abdul Kalam** (Scientific Adviser to PM and head of DRDO) and **R. Chidambaram** (Director, Department of Atomic Energy).
- **May 11** is celebrated as **National Technology Day.**
- World was shocked — US implemented the **Glenn Amendment** for the first time; new sanctions imposed.
- **Pakistan** responded with tests **Chagai I and II** (May 1998).
- **UN Security Council** adopted a resolution condemning both.

### 15.2 The Lahore Summit (February 1999)

- Vajpayee worked towards peace with Pakistan.
- **Delhi-Lahore bus service inaugurated in February 1999** — Vajpayee travelled to Lahore by bus.
- **Lahore Declaration** signed by **Vajpayee and Pakistan PM Nawaz Sharif:**
  - Commitment to dialogue, expansion of trade, mutual friendship.
  - Non-interference in each other's internal affairs.
  - Steps to reduce risk of accidental/unauthorised use of nuclear weapons.
  - Condemnation of terrorism in all its forms.

### 15.3 Kargil War (May–July 1999)

- Barely three months after the Lahore Summit: **steady infiltration** by Pakistani soldiers and militants into Kashmir — captured hilltops and unmanned border posts.
- Incursion planned by **Pakistan's military under General Pervez Musharraf** — PM Nawaz Sharif was only told after the plan was underway.
- **Kargil district** was the main centre.
- **Indian Army first informed by shepherds in May 1999.**
- Swift Indian response: Army and Air Force (**Operation Safed Sagar**) coordinated. (Told clearly: **not to cross the Line of Control.**)
- **Operation Vijay** — successful; peaks recaptured, including **Tiger Hill** in the Drass sector.
- These points overlooked the **Srinagar-Leh highway** — critical all-weather road.
- **June:** Nawaz Sharif asked US to intervene; **President Bill Clinton declined** until Pakistani troops withdrew.
- Nawaz Sharif ordered operation to stop.
- **July 26:** War over — India victorious. Over **500 Indian soldiers killed** in fierce combat in treacherous terrain.
- Kargil victory **boosted Vajpayee's image** as a decisive and sensible leader.

### 15.4 Fall of the First NDA Government

- **AIADMK withdrew from NDA.**
- **April 17, 1999:** NDA lost a **dramatic no-confidence vote by a single vote** — generally attributed to **Giridhar Gamang** (who, technically, was still a Lok Sabha MP despite having become CM of Orissa).
- Lok Sabha dissolved by **President K.R. Narayanan;** fresh elections in **September–October 1999.**

---

## PART 16: NDA — Second Stint (October 1999 – May 2004)

---

- Kargil war fresh in public memory; NDA won with majority (support of new constituents including **Janata Dal (United) and DMK**).
- **Vajpayee sworn in as PM for the third time on October 13, 1999.**

### 16.1 Economic and Social Steps

- Carried forward **Narasimha Rao's economic reforms.**
- Infrastructure: **National Highways Development Project, Golden Quadrilateral,** regional airports, **Udaan scheme.**
- **Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana** for rural connectivity.
- New **telecom policy** — shift from high fixed licence fee to **revenue-share based fee.**
- Privatised state monopolies such as **VSNL;** duty-free imports.
- Upgraded **Disinvestment Commission** into a ministry.
- **Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act (2003)** — to institutionalise financial discipline and reduce fiscal deficit.
- **Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan** for universalisation of primary education.
- **Constitution 86th Amendment Act (2002)** — placed **Right to Education among Fundamental Rights (Part-III).**
- **IC 814 hijacking (December 1999):** Indian Airlines flight hijacked to Taliban-ruled Afghanistan. Government released terrorists including **Maulana Masood Azhar** — External Affairs Minister personally escorted terrorists to Afghanistan. Relations with Pakistan dipped.
- **December 2001:** Attack on **Parliament House** by **Lashkar-e-Taiba and Jaish-e-Mohammed terrorists.** Government passed **Prevention of Terrorism Act (POTA).**
- **Kashmir elections (September 2002):** Free and fair — **National Conference voted out;** **Congress and PDP** came to power; **Mufti Mohammad Sayeed** became CM.

### 16.2 The Downside

- **George Fernandes** (Defence Minister) resigned after **Barak Missile Deal scandal** and **Kargil coffin scandal.**
- BJP party chief **Bangaru Laxman** apparently accepting a bribe — one of the **first media sting operations.**
- **Godhra incident (February 2002):** Sabarmati Express caught fire at Godhra (Gujarat); several pilgrims returning from Ayodhya killed. Widely believed a Muslim mob set fire to compartments; reports also said fire started inside. Riots followed — Ahmedabad and Baroda worst affected; called a **pogrom.** CM **Narendra Modi** severely criticised. PM Vajpayee believed to have wanted Modi to resign.

### 16.3 2004 General Election

- **BJP called early elections** with slogan **"India Shining"** — misread ground realities.
- **NDA was defeated.**
- **Congress (under Sonia Gandhi) emerged as single largest party.**

---

## PART 17: UPA Years (May 2004 – May 2014)

---

### 17.1 Formation of UPA Government

- Congress joined minor parties to form **United Progressive Alliance (UPA).**
- **Sonia Gandhi declined the PM's post** despite party entreaties — stating she was following her **"inner voice."** (Her foreign origin would have been exploited by opposition.)
- Nominated **Manmohan Singh** as PM.
- **Manmohan Singh sworn in as PM on May 22, 2004.**
- **Common Minimum Programme (CMP)** guided UPA policies; Communist parties' role made policies appear **'left-of-centre.'**
- **National Advisory Council (NAC)** formed — chaired by Sonia Gandhi; members included social activists with a welfare agenda.

### 17.2 Social Welfare Measures

- **MGNREGA (Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act) (2006):** World's largest welfare scheme of its kind — guaranteed rural households **100 days of paid labour a year.** One-third of work mandated for women.
- **National Rural Health Mission (2005).**
- **Right to Information Act (2005):** Citizens can request information from any 'public authority' which must reply within **30 days.** Public authorities required to computerise records.
- **VAT (Value Added Tax) introduced in 2005** to replace complicated sales tax.

### 17.3 Foreign Relations — Indo-US Nuclear Deal

- PM visited **US in July 2005** to initiate the **Indo-US civilian nuclear agreement.**
- **2006:** President **George W. Bush** visited India — declaration on nuclear agreement made.
- India would allow civil nuclear reactors to be inspected by **IAEA** in exchange for access to American nuclear fuel and technology.
- **October 2008:** India and US finally signed the agreement — after approval from IAEA, Nuclear Suppliers Group, and US Congress.
- **Ended the 30-year-long US ban on nuclear trade with India.**
- **July 2008:** UPA faced a confidence vote — Left parties withdrew support over the deal; government won the trust vote.
- **Nathula Pass reopened in 2006** after more than four decades.
- **2007:** **Pratibha Patil** elected and sworn in as **President of India — first woman to hold the post.**

### 17.4 Terror Attacks

- **2006:** Explosions at a Muslim cemetery in **Malegaon, Maharashtra.**
- **May 2007:** Blast at **Mecca Masjid, Hyderabad.**
- **2007:** Bomb at **Ajmer Dargah, Rajasthan.**
- **February 2007:** Blast on **Samjhauta Express** — many Pakistanis and some Indians killed. Initially Lashkar-e-Toiba and Jaish-e-Mohammed suspected. In **2011**, **NIA** accused eight Hindu extremists. [In **March 2019**, all four were acquitted by NIA court citing lack of evidence.]
- **2008:** Series of bomb blasts in **Jaipur** and **Delhi marketplaces** — Islamic fundamentalists aided by Pakistan.
- **November 26, 2008 — Mumbai Terror Attacks (26/11):**
  - Pakistani terrorists (Lashkar-e-Taiba) entered Mumbai by boat; attacked **Taj Hotel**, **Shivaji rail terminus**, and other locations.
  - Nine terrorists killed; **Ajmal Amir Kasab** captured.
  - Kasab's confession confirmed Pakistan/Lashkar-e-Toiba's role.
  - [**November 11, 2012:** Special court issued death warrant; Kasab **hanged after being found guilty of 80 offences** including waging war against India.]

### 17.5 Telangana Issue

- Congress had promised Telangana state before 2004 elections (allied with **TRS — Telangana Rashtra Samiti**).**Y.S.R. Reddy** (Congress CM of Andhra Pradesh) strongly opposed bifurcation.
- After Y.S.R. Reddy's death in an air crash, **K. Chandrasekhar Rao (KCR)** led the movement.
- **November 2009:** KCR went on a **fast unto death;** Centre announced in December 2009 that the process to form Telangana was being initiated.
- **B.N. Srikrishna Commission** (retired SC judge) appointed; report submitted **December 2010** — rejected by TRS.
- **February 2014:** Parliament cleared the **Telangana Bill.** Andhra Pradesh formally divided.
- Hyderabad to be **shared as capital for ten years.**
- **June 2014:** Telangana formally came into existence; **KCR became first CM.**
- **May 2011:** **West Bengal** ended **34 years of Left Front rule** — **Mamata Banerjee (Trinamool Congress)** became Chief Minister.

### 17.6 Social Welfare Legislation

- **RTE Act (2009):** Every child has right to full-time elementary education; private schools (except minority institutions) required to enrol **25% of children from weaker sections.**
- **Article 21-A** and **RTE Act** came into effect on **April 1, 2010.**

### 17.7 Nirbhaya Case and Criminal Law Amendment

- **December 2012:** Gang rape of **Jyoti Singh** (called 'Nirbhaya' in media) on a private bus in Delhi — she died from injuries.
- **Delhi High Court (March 2014):** All defendants guilty of rape, murder, unnatural offences, destruction of evidence. Death sentence confirmed.
- [**May 2017:** Supreme Court rejected convicts' appeal; upheld death sentence. **July 2018:** SC rejected review petition.]
- Led to widespread protests across India and **South Asia.**
- **Government appointed Justice J.S. Verma Committee (December 22, 2012)** — former Chief Justice of India — to recommend amendments to criminal law.
- **Verma Committee Report (January 2013):** Did not recommend death penalty for rape; suggested prison sentences from 7 years to life. Called for police sensitisation; recommended **no exception to marital rape;** review of **AFSPA** in conflict areas; disqualification of candidates charged with sexual offences.
- **February 2013:** Government promulgated an ordinance; **Criminal Law (Amendment) Act (March 2013)** replaced it.
- New offences defined:
  - **Acid attack:** 10-year jail extendable to life, or fine.
  - **Sexual harassment:** 1–3 years.
  - **Voyeurism:** 3–7 years.
  - **Stalking:** 1–5 years.
  - **Gang rape:** Up to 20 years extendable to life.
  - **Age of consent increased from 16 to 18 years.**
  - Repeat offences: life imprisonment or death.
- Criticism: Does not bring **marital rape** within its ambit; not gender-neutral.

### 17.8 National Food Security Act (2013)

- **National Food Security Act** and **Right to Food Act (2013):** Providing subsidised food grains to beneficiaries of the targeted public distribution system.

### 17.9 Mars Orbiter Mission — Mangalyaan

- **November 2013:** ISRO launched its first interplanetary mission — **Mars Orbiter Mission (Mangalyaan).**
- [**September 24, 2014:** India's space agency became the **fourth agency** to successfully reach Mars orbit (after Russian, American, European space agencies) — and the **first country to succeed in its very first attempt,** at a remarkably low cost.]

### 17.10 Corruption Controversies and Lokpal

- **2G/3G spectrum allocation scandal** — CAG report confirmed allocations at very low rates.
- **Commonwealth Games scandal** — huge sums for infrastructure contracts.
- **Coal block allocation scam (CAG 2012)** — loss to exchequer from favouritism; cast aspersion on Manmohan Singh (coal portfolio held by him 2006–09).
- **Anna Hazare movement (April 2011):** 12-day fast demanding a **Lokpal.** Supported by **India Against Corruption** (co-founded by Magsaysay Award winner **Arvind Kejriwal**).
- Protest spread across India — TV coverage amplified it.
- Government announced Lokpal Committee (five cabinet members + five India Against Corruption nominees) — failed due to deep distrust.
- **Baba Ramdev** led a separate protest demanding repatriation of black money from foreign banks.
- **Lokpal Bill approved by government in July 2011** — rejected by activists for excluding key functionaries.
- Anna Hazare arrested, refused bail, began fast in jail; released.
- Continued fast at **Ramlila Grounds.** Parliament passed a **resolution (moved by Finance Minister Pranab Mukherjee)** to pass a revised Lokpal Bill meeting main demands.
- **Arvind Kejriwal** formed **Aam Aadmi Party (AAP) on November 26, 2012** — went on to win a **stupendous majority in Delhi Assembly elections 2015.**
- **Lokpal and Lokayuktas Bill:** Tabled in Lok Sabha December 2011; passed; tabled in Rajya Sabha — vote failed after marathon debate; referred to select committee May 2012. **Passed by both houses December 2013.** Received **assent from President Pranab Mukherjee** (elected President in 2012) on **January 1, 2014.**
- **Lokpal and Lokayukta Act, 2013:** Mandates establishment of Lokpal for Union and Lokayukta for states. Jurisdiction includes **PM, ministers, MPs, and all public servants.** (Not made a constitutional body; judiciary excluded — main criticisms.)

### 17.11 2014 General Election

- Congress fielded **Rahul Gandhi** (appointed VP of party in **January 2013**) — perceived as lackluster and lacking oratory skills.
- **BJP chose Narendra Modi** (CM of Gujarat) as PM candidate.
- BJP made **development (not sectarian issues)** the campaign plank.
- BJP made effective use of **social media** and **RSS cadres.**
- Fought as part of **NDA** — effective alliances with regional parties.
- **April–May 2014:** Country went to polls for 16th Lok Sabha.
- **Results:** BJP won **282 seats** — majority on its own. Congress won just **44 seats** — worst performance ever.
- BJP's **all-India vote share** increased from ~19% to ~31%.

## PART 18: NDA Government (May 2014 – May 2019)

---

### 18.1 Formation and Objectives

- **May 26, 2014:** Narendra Modi took oath as PM along with council of ministers; administered by **President Pranab Mukherjee** at **Rashtrapati Bhavan forecourts.**
- **First swearing-in ceremony where all SAARC heads were invited** — and all attended.
- Mission: **Sabka Saath, Sabka Vikas** ("Together with all, development for all.") Less government, more governance; cooperative federalism.

### 18.2 Key Economic Policies

**Digital India (August 2014):**
- Launched to electronically empower citizens — connect all government departments and people digitally.
- Improve connectivity of all villages through internet networks.
- **UPI (Unified Payments Interface)** and **BHIM (Bharat Interface for Money)** developed.

**Disbanding Planning Commission → NITI Aayog:**
- Planning Commission scrapped **August 2014.**
- **NITI Aayog (National Institution for Transforming India)** announced **January 1, 2015.**
- Policy think tank; promotes **cooperative federalism** through bottom-up approach.
- Does NOT allocate funds to states.
- Comprises PM as chairperson; Governing Council of all state CMs and LGs of UTs.

**JAM Trinity:**
- **Jan Dhan-Aadhaar-Mobile** — direct subsidy transfer to beneficiary.
- **Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (August 24, 2014):** World's largest financial inclusion scheme; bank accounts without minimum balance, debit cards, access to social security schemes.
- **Aadhaar** (launched by UPA January 2009, strengthened by NDA): **Aadhaar Act enacted March 2016.** UIDAI collects biometric and demographic data; issues 12-digit unique identity number.
- Brought down wastage and corruption in benefit transfers.

**Make in India (September 2014):**
- Flagship programme to boost domestic manufacturing and attract foreign investment.
- Along with: **Start Up India, Stand Up India, Skill India.**

**Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (2016):**
- New law to consolidate and simplify insolvency resolution.
- Replaced provisions in: Sick Industrial Companies Act 1985, Recovery of Debts Act 1993, SARFAESI Act, Companies Act 2013, among others.
- Banks and creditors can recover loans from bankrupt companies in a time-bound manner.

**Benami Transactions (Prohibition) Amendment Act (2016):**
- Empowered authorities to provisionally attach and confiscate benami properties.
- Jail terms of 1–7 years; fines for offenders.

**Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act (RERA) (2016):**
- **RERA** to be established in every state — every real estate project must be registered.

**Fugitive Economic Offenders Act (FEOA) (April 2018):**
- Economic offender fleeing India can be declared a 'fugitive economic offender;' properties can be confiscated.

**Demonetisation (November 8, 2016):**
- PM suddenly announced **Rs 500 and Rs 1,000 currency notes** to cease being legal tender from midnight — to catch corrupt with unaccounted money; curb fake notes and terror financing.
- **Mixed impact:**
  - Disrupted daily life; adversely affected real estate, small enterprises, informal economy, agriculture, supply chains.
  - Brought most cash into formal system; widened tax base.
  - Boosted digital payment systems and financial awareness.
- **Income Declaration Scheme (IDS) 2016:** 64,275 people disclosed **Rs 65,250 crore.**
- **PM Garib Kalyan Yojana (December 2016):** Opportunity to declare unaccounted wealth confidentially; paying 50% fine.
- **BJP performed well in 2017 state elections** (6 out of 7 state assemblies) — including **Uttar Pradesh.**

**Goods and Services Tax (GST):**
- Conceived long ago; **proposed in Union Budget for 2006–07** by UPA Finance Minister.
- Opposition (including BJP) had resisted it earlier.
- **Constitution (101st Amendment) Act (2016)** ratified by required states — enabling GST launch.
- Changed how Centre and states tax goods and services.
- GST Council (Centre and state Finance Ministers) determines tax on goods and services.
- GST replaced multiple central and state taxes; eased flow of goods across state borders.
- **Five petroleum products and alcohol for human consumption** kept outside its ambit.
- Called the **biggest tax reform since Independence.**

### 18.3 Health and Welfare

- **New National Health Policy (January 2015)** — emphasised role of private healthcare.
- Several national health programmes merged with **National Health Mission.**
- **Ayushman Bharat (2018):** Government health insurance scheme.
- **Swachh Bharat Mission (October 2, 2014):** Clean India Mission — nationwide sanitation campaign; aimed at eliminating open defecation and manual scavenging. **WHO (2018) said thousands of diarrhoea deaths averted in rural India** after the mission's launch.
- **Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana:** Increased rural house construction.
- **Deen Dayal Upadhyaya Gram Jyoti Yojana:** Rural electrification — all villages to have electricity connection by 2018.
- **Ujjwala Yojana:** LPG connections to BPL households.
- **Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (2015):** Reducing female infanticide; encouraging girl child education.
- **Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana (2015):** Promotion of welfare programmes for the girl child.
- **MGNREGS:** Despite earlier criticism, not discontinued; improved with geo-tagging of assets for transparency.

### 18.4 Agriculture

- Declared intention to **double farmers' income in real terms by 2022.**
- New crop insurance scheme; higher irrigation funding.
- **PM KISAN (Pradhan Mantri Kisan Samman Nidhi) (2019):** Announced in interim budget — farmers with less than 5 acres (**85% of India's farm holdings**) to get **Rs 6,000 annually.**
- Agriculture remains dependent on monsoons; farmer suicides did not abate.
- Agricultural reforms remained limited — agriculture is a state subject; **APMC Act could not be reformed.**

### 18.5 Infrastructure

- Speeding up of **highway construction.**
- **Bharatmala and Sagarmala projects.**
- Building and revival of regional airports; **Udaan scheme** for air connectivity.
- **Ujala LED bulb scheme** expanded; price halved.
- Modernisation and expansion of railways.

### 18.6 Security

- **Maoist threat** suppressed in many areas — though Chhattisgarh still faced occasional attacks.
- **2015:** National Policy and Action Plan for areas affected by Naxalite/Maoist insurgency.

### 18.7 Kashmir and Pakistan

- **November–December 2014:** J&K state assembly elections. **PDP** emerged as largest party.
- **February 2015:** **BJP and PDP formed coalition** — first time BJP was part of a J&K government. **Mufti Mohammed Sayeed** of PDP became CM.
- **January 2016:** Mufti Mohammed Sayeed died; **Mehbooba Mufti** (his daughter, new PDP head) became CM in **April 2016** — first woman CM of J&K.
- **July 2016:** Killing of **Burhan Wani** (Hizbul Mujahideen commander) by security forces triggered massive street protests; pellet guns used against protesters — caused permanent eye damage in many cases.
- **August 2017:** Abu Dujana (Lashkar-e-Taiba Pakistani commander) killed in Pulwama.
- **June 2018:** BJP pulled out of coalition over differences on Ramzan ceasefire continuation. Governor's Rule imposed.
- **Major terrorist attacks:**
  - **January 2016:** Pathankot Air Force Station attack.
  - **June 2016:** CRPF convoy ambushed in Pampore; **Lashkar-e-Taiba claimed responsibility.**
  - **September 18, 2016:** Uri army camp attack — most serious in 26 years; **Jaish-e-Mohammed suspected.**
  - **September 29, 2016:** India conducted **surgical strikes** across LoC against terrorist launch pads in Pakistan-occupied Kashmir.
  - **February 14, 2019 — Pulwama Attack:** CRPF convoy targeted by vehicle-borne suicide bomber — **40 CRPF personnel killed.** Jaish-e-Mohammed claimed responsibility.
  - **February 26, 2019 — Balakot Airstrike:** IAF Mirage jets crossed LoC and targeted **Jaish-e-Mohammed terror camp in Balakot** with missiles. India called it 'intelligence-led, non-military, pre-emptive' operation. Pakistan disputed extent of damage.
  - **February 27:** Pakistan Air Force attempted retaliation. **IAF MiG-21 Bison shot down a Pakistani F-16** but was also shot down. Pilot **Wing Commander Abhinandan Varthaman** captured by Pakistan — released two days later, lowering tensions.
  - Both countries blocked each other's airspace; opened only in **July 2019.**

### 18.8 Foreign Policy Under Modi

- India's Foreign Secretary **Vijay Gokhale:** *"India has moved on from its non-aligned past. India is today an aligned state—but based on issues."*
- **Sushma Swaraj** became External Affairs Minister — **first woman in India to hold independent charge of the ministry.**
- **'Neighbourhood diplomacy':** Improved relations with Bangladesh, Nepal, Sri Lanka.
- **2015:** Parliament ratified **land exchange deal with Bangladesh** (initiated by UPA).
- **2017 Doklam Stand-off:** China tried to construct road in **Doklam** (plateau at junction of India, Bhutan, China, near the strategic **Siliguri Corridor**) — Indian troops (called by Bhutan) objected. Resolved diplomatically.
- **China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC)** issue — India objects as it passes through Pakistan-occupied Kashmir.
- **2016:** India and US signed **Logistics Exchange Memorandum of Agreement** for logistical support between militaries.
- **2018: COMCASA (Communications Compatibility and Security Agreement)** signed — access to advanced defence systems.
- **Japan-India partnership** progressed greatly — both key partners in the **'free and open Indo-Pacific.'**
- **'Look East Policy' → 'Act East Policy'** — expanding Indian influence in East and Southeast Asia.
- **West Asia:** Balance between Palestine and Israel. India invited to **Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC)** for the first time.
- Reached out to **Saudi Arabia and UAE.**
- Joined **Australia Group** (prevention of biological and chemical weapons proliferation) — now member of three of four nuclear export control regimes.
- Joined **Missile Technology Control Regime (MTCR) in 2016** and **Wassenaar Arrangement (WA) in 2017.**
- NSG membership bid pending — **China opposed.**
- Co-founded **International Solar Alliance** with France.

### 18.9 Social Issues

- **Communal riots practically nil** under Modi.
- However, activities of Hindu nationalist organisations increased; concerns about:
  - **Ghar Wapasi** (Hindu reconversion programme).
  - **'Love jihad'** campaign.
  - Sporadic praise for **Nathuram Godse.**
  - **Anti-Romeo squads** in UP — moral policing.
  - Attacks on Muslims for alleged cow slaughter; Dalits and tribals also attacked.
  - **Lynching incidents** — SC intervened, directing government to take firm action.
- **Supreme Court on Article 377 (2018):** **Decriminalised homosexuality** — overturned its own 2013 stance. (Issue first raised by Naz Foundation in Delhi HC in 2001; Delhi HC held it illegal in 2009; SC overturned Delhi HC in 2013; SC reversed itself in 2018.) Based on **August 2017 right to privacy judgement.**
- **Sabarimala Temple case:** SC allowed women of all ages to enter. Mixed response — major protests in Kerala.
- **Triple talaq:** SC declared it not allowable; government legislated to make it unlawful (took ordinance route).
- **Violence against women** — death penalty for rape of minors below 12; rape cases under **POCSO Act fast-tracked.**

### 18.10 2019 General Election — NDA Returns

- **April–May 2019:** Polls for 17th Lok Sabha in seven phases; counting on **May 23.**
- **NDA won 353 seats; BJP alone won 303 seats** — better than 2014; majority on its own.
- BJP made striking inroads in **West Bengal.**
- **Congress won 52 seats** — better than 2014 but still marginal.
- Landmark: **Highest-ever number of women** elected to Parliament.
- **First non-Congress government to complete a full term and return to power with a majority for a second consecutive term.**
- Modi: **First BJP leader elected for a second term after completing a five-year tenure** — feat previously achieved only by **Jawaharlal Nehru and Indira Gandhi** (both Congress leaders).
- **Narendra Modi administered oath of office by President Ram Nath Kovind on May 30, 2019** at Rashtrapati Bhavan forecourts. Leaders from **BIMSTEC, Kyrgyz Republic, and Mauritius** attended.
- **S. Jaishankar** (former Foreign Secretary) inducted as Foreign Minister.
- **Amit Shah** (BJP president, Modi's close associate) inducted as Home Minister.
- Modi's address to new MPs: Called on members not to discriminate; stated minorities had been "cheated by other parties for generations" and lived in "imaginary fear" — added **'Sabka Vishwas'** to **'Sabka Saath, Sabka Vikas.'**

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Examples — Added for Contemporary Relevance]`

### Connecting Emergency (1975–77) → Safeguards Today

- **[Modern Example]** The **44th Amendment (1978)** provisions requiring **written cabinet advice, two-thirds majority approval, and six-monthly renewal for Emergency** were tested when the Supreme Court, in various judgements, has reaffirmed that the power to impose Emergency is **justiciable** (can be challenged in courts). The **Basic Structure Doctrine** (including democratic governance as a basic feature) prevents Emergency-style subversion of the Constitution.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Freedom of the Press Index** and debates around the **UAPA (Unlawful Activities Prevention Act)** continue to reference the Emergency era as a cautionary tale about the dangers of executive overreach.

---

### Connecting Economic Reforms of 1991 → India Today

- **[Modern Example]** India's **GDP at market prices crossed $3.5 trillion in 2023**, making it the **fifth largest economy in the world** — a direct legacy of the **1991 LPG reforms** initiated by Narasimha Rao and Manmohan Singh. India is projected to become the **third largest economy by 2027.**
- **[Modern Example]** The **PLI (Production Linked Incentive) scheme** launched from **2020–21** is the contemporary version of **Make in India** — incentivising manufacturing across 14 key sectors including electronics, pharmaceuticals, and automobiles. As of 2023, PLI schemes have attracted investments of over **Rs 1 lakh crore.**
- **[Modern Example]** India's **UPI** (Unified Payments Interface) — a direct outcome of the Digital India initiative — processed over **100 billion transactions in 2023,** making India the global leader in real-time digital payments ahead of the US and China.

---

### Connecting Mandal Commission → OBC Reservations Today

- **[Modern Example]** The **Supreme Court's judgement in Indra Sawhney vs Union of India (1992)** (the landmark Mandal case judgement) — which capped total reservations at **50%** and barred reservations in promotions — continues to be the cornerstone of reservation jurisprudence. In **2021,** the Supreme Court, in **Maratha reservation case,** struck down Maharashtra's **16% Maratha reservation** for exceeding the 50% cap (citing Indra Sawhney), reinforcing the principle established three decades earlier.
- **[Modern Example]** In **2021**, Parliament passed the **Constitution (105th Amendment) Act,** restoring States' and Union Territories' power to identify **State OBC lists** — following the SC's 2021 judgement in *Jaishri Laxmanrao Patil vs Chief Minister* which had weakened states' ability to provide OBC reservation in local bodies.

---

### Connecting Babri Masjid → Ayodhya Verdict 2019 and Ram Mandir 2024

- **[Modern Example]** The **Supreme Court's Constitution Bench judgement on November 9, 2019** (*M. Siddiq vs Mahant Suresh Das*) — settled the **Ram Janmabhoomi-Babri Masjid dispute** after decades. The court awarded the disputed land to the Hindu side for the construction of the Ram Mandir, and directed the government to provide an **alternative 5-acre plot to the Sunni Waqf Board** for constructing a mosque. This was a unanimous 5-0 verdict.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Ram Mandir at Ayodhya was consecrated on January 22, 2024** — PM Narendra Modi performed the *prana pratishtha* ceremony. The event brought to a conclusion one of the most contentious issues in India's post-Independence history — originating from events described in this chapter (1949 idol placement, 1986 gate opening, 1992 demolition).

---

### Connecting Pokhran Tests → India's Nuclear Status Today

- **[Modern Example]** India is now recognised as a **de facto nuclear weapon state** outside the NPT framework. As of 2023, India has an estimated **160 nuclear warheads** (per SIPRI). India's **No First Use (NFU) policy** continues from the Vajpayee era, though debates about its revision emerge periodically.
- **[Modern Example]** India's **Nuclear Suppliers Group (NSG) bid** — pending since the Vajpayee era — remains unresolved as **China continues to block it.** This is a live foreign policy challenge directly connected to the Pokhran tests and their aftermath.

---

### Connecting Look East → Act East Policy and ASEAN Today

- **[Modern Example]** Narasimha Rao's **Look East Policy (1991)** was transformed into the **Act East Policy** by the Modi government in **2014** — signalling a more proactive engagement. In **2023**, India held the **ASEAN-India Summit** and deepened ties with the **QUAD** (India, USA, Japan, Australia) — the most significant security grouping in the Indo-Pacific today.

---

### Connecting JAM Trinity → DBT (Direct Benefit Transfer) Today

- **[Modern Example]** The **JAM Trinity (Jan Dhan-Aadhaar-Mobile)** has enabled **Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT)** to transfer over **Rs 34 lakh crore** directly to beneficiaries since its inception (as of 2023). During COVID-19, the **PM Garib Kalyan Yojana** transferred Rs 500 per month to **over 20 crore women** through Jan Dhan accounts — demonstrating the system's power in a crisis.
