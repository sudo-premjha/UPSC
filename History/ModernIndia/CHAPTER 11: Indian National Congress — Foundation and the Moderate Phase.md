# 📖 Chapter 11: Indian National Congress — Foundation and the Moderate Phase

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. The **Indian National Congress (INC)** was founded in **December 1885** in Bombay — not spontaneously, but as the culmination of decades of organised political activity by educated Indians.
> 2. There are **three competing theories** about why the INC was founded: the **Safety Valve theory**, the **Conspiracy theory**, and the **Lightning Conductor theory** — each reflecting a different view of Hume's role and the Congress's origins.
> 3. The **Moderate phase (1885–1905)** was defined by constitutional agitation (working within the law), petitions, and persuasion — NOT confrontation. Their tools were **prayer and petition**.
> 4. The Moderates made **four major contributions**: economic critique of imperialism (drain theory), constitutional reforms, administrative reform campaigns, and defence of civil rights.
> 5. Despite their significant achievements, the Moderates had a **critical weakness**: a narrow social base — they largely excluded the masses, women, and did not demand universal voting rights.

# PART 1: FOUNDATION OF THE INDIAN NATIONAL CONGRESS

---

## 🔷 The Ground Was Already Prepared

By the **later 1870s and early 1880s**, conditions were ripe for an all-India political organisation. The political associations we studied in Chapter 10 (Indian Association, Madras Mahajan Sabha, Poona Sarvajanik Sabha, etc.) had already been doing the groundwork.

The **final shape** to this idea was given by a key figure: **A.O. Hume** — a **retired English civil servant**.

---

## 🔷 The Foundation: Key Facts

**A.O. Hume:**
- Mobilised the leading intellectuals of the time
- With their cooperation, organised the **first session of the Indian National Congress**

**First Session Details:**
| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Venue** | Gokuldas Tejpal Sanskrit College, Bombay |
| **Month/Year** | **December 1885** |
| **Number of Delegates** | **72 delegates** |
| **President** | **Womesh Chandra (or Chunder) Bonnerjee** |

**Pre-cursor:** Before the INC's first session, **two sessions of the Indian National Conference** had been held in **1883 and 1885**, with representatives drawn from all major towns of India. The main architects of the Indian National Conference were **Surendranath Banerjea** and **Ananda Mohan Bose**.

**The Annual Tradition:** Hereafter, the Congress met **every year in December**, in a **different part of the country each time**.

---

## 🔷 Great Leaders of the Early Congress

**Presidents of the Congress during the early phase:**
- **Dadabhai Naoroji** — *thrice president* (a remarkable distinction)
- **Badruddin Tyabji**
- **Pherozeshah Mehta**
- **P. Anandacharlu**
- **Surendranath Banerjea**
- **Romesh Chandra Dutt**
- **Ananda Mohan Bose**
- **Gopal Krishna Gokhale**

**Other prominent leaders:**
- Mahadev Govind Ranade
- Bal Gangadhar Tilak
- Sisir Kumar Ghosh
- Motilal Ghosh
- Madan Mohan Malaviya
- G. Subramania Aiyar
- C. Vijayaraghavachariar
- Dinshaw E. Wacha

**A Historic First:**
> In **1890**, **Kadambini Ganguly** — the **first woman graduate of Calcutta University** — addressed the Congress session. This symbolised the commitment of the freedom struggle to give the **women of India their due status** in national life.

**Beyond the Congress:** Nationalist activity was also carried out through **provincial conferences and associations, newspapers, and literature**.

---

## 🔷 Was the Congress a 'Safety Valve'? — The Three Theories

This is one of the most debated questions in early nationalist history. There are three major positions:

---

### Theory 1: The Safety Valve Theory

**Argument:** Hume formed the Congress with the idea that it would prove to be a **'safety valve'** — a controlled outlet for releasing the **growing discontent of Indians**, preventing a violent explosion of anger.

- To achieve this, Hume convinced **Lord Dufferin** not to obstruct the formation of the Congress.
- **Prominent believer:** Extremist leader **Lala Lajpat Rai** believed in this theory.

---

### Theory 2: The Conspiracy Theory (Marxist View)

**Argument:** The Marxist historian's 'conspiracy theory' was an offspring of the 'safety valve' notion.

- **R.P. Dutt** opined that the Indian National Congress was born out of a **conspiracy to abort a popular uprising** in India, and that the **bourgeois leaders** (middle-class elites) were a party to this conspiracy.

---

### Theory 3: The Lightning Conductor Theory (Modern Historians' View)

**Argument:** Modern Indian historians **dispute** the 'safety valve' idea. Their view:

- The INC represented the **urge of the politically conscious Indians** to set up a national body to express the political and economic demands of the Indians.
- If Indians had convened such a body **on their own**, there would have been **insurmountable (impossible to overcome) opposition** from the officials — such an organisation would not have been allowed to form.
- In the circumstances, as **Bipan Chandra** observes, the early Congress leaders used Hume as a **'lightning conductor'** — i.e., as a **catalyst** to bring together the nationalistic forces, even if under the guise of a 'safety valve'.

**In simple terms:** The nationalists were smart enough to use a British civil servant as a "front" or shield to give their organisation legitimacy and protection from official suppression. They used Hume — Hume didn't use them.

---

> 📌 **Key Association:**
> - **Safety Valve Theory** → Lala Lajpat Rai
> - **Conspiracy Theory** → R.P. Dutt
> - **Lightning Conductor Theory** → Bipan Chandra / G.K. Gokhale (associated view)

---

## 🔷 Aims and Objectives of the Congress

The main aims of the Indian National Congress in its initial stage were to:

1. Found a **democratic, nationalist movement**
2. **Politicise and politically educate** people (make people aware of political issues)
3. Establish the **headquarters for a movement**
4. Promote **friendly relations** among nationalist political workers from different parts of the country
5. Develop and propagate an **anti-colonial nationalist ideology** (a body of ideas opposing British rule)
6. **Formulate and present popular demands** before the government — to unify the people over a common economic and political programme
7. Develop and consolidate a feeling of **national unity** among people irrespective of religion, caste, or province
8. Carefully **promote and nurture Indian nationhood**

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The INC's founding goal of creating "national unity irrespective of religion, caste, or province" finds direct constitutional expression in the **Preamble of the Indian Constitution** — which describes India as a "Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic." The idea of a pan-India political platform that transcends caste, religion, and region — radical in 1885 — is now the foundational principle of Indian democracy. `[Modern Example]` The tradition of the Congress meeting every year in a different city to build national connections has a modern parallel in the **National Development Council (NDC)** meetings and **Inter-State Council** meetings, which bring together leaders from different states. `[Modern Example]` Kadambini Ganguly's historic address in 1890 is recognised today as a foundational moment for women's political participation. The **106th Constitutional Amendment Act (2023)** providing **33% reservation for women in the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies** (to be implemented after delimitation) is the direct constitutional fulfilment of the commitment Ganguly's participation symbolised.

# PART 2: THE ERA OF MODERATES (1885–1905)

---

## 🔷 Who Were the Moderates?

National leaders who dominated Congress policies during the **early period (1885–1905)** are called the **Moderates**. They were:

- **Dadabhai Naoroji**
- **Pherozeshah Mehta**
- **D.E. Wacha**
- **W.C. Bonnerjea**
- **S.N. Banerjea**

They were labelled **'Moderates'** to distinguish them from the **neo-nationalists of the early 20th century**, who were referred to as the **'Extremists'**.

**Their Core Beliefs:**
- Staunch believers in **'liberalism'** (individual rights, rule of law, free speech)
- Believed in **'moderate' politics** — gradual, legal, non-confrontational

---

## 🔷 The Moderate Approach — How Did They Work?

**The Core Belief:**
The Moderates believed that the British **basically wanted to be just** to the Indians — but were **not aware of the real conditions** in India.

Therefore, their logic was:
> *If public opinion can be created in the country and demands presented to the government through resolutions, petitions, and meetings — the authorities will gradually concede these demands.*

**Their Two-Pronged Methodology:**

1. **Internally (Within India):** Create a strong public opinion → arouse consciousness and national spirit → educate and unite people on common political questions
2. **Externally (In Britain):** Persuade the British Government and British public opinion to introduce reforms in India on the lines laid out by the nationalists

**Their Primary Method:** **'Prayer and Petition'** — if that failed, they resorted to **constitutional agitation** (agitation within the framework of the law).

**British Committee:**
- A **British Committee of the Indian National Congress** was established in **London in 1889**, which had **India** as its organ (publication).
- **Dadabhai Naoroji** spent a **substantial portion of his life and income** campaigning for India's case abroad.
- In **1890**, it was decided to hold a session of the Indian National Congress in **London in 1892**, but owing to the **British elections of 1891**, the proposal was postponed and never revived later.

**The Moderates' Political Calculus:**
- Believed that **political connections with Britain** were in India's interest at that stage of history
- Felt the **time was not ripe** for a direct challenge to British rule
- Therefore, aimed to **transform colonial rule** to be as close to **national rule** as possible

---

# PART 3: CONTRIBUTIONS OF THE MODERATE NATIONALISTS

---

## 🔷 Contribution 1: Economic Critique of British Imperialism

This was perhaps the **most intellectually significant** contribution of the Moderates.

**Key Figures:** **Dadabhai Naoroji, R.C. Dutt, Dinshaw Wacha**, and others

**What They Did:**
- Carefully **analysed the political economy** (the relationship between politics and economics) of British rule in India
- Put forward the **"Drain Theory"** to explain British exploitation of India

### The Drain Theory — Explained Simply

> **Drain Theory** = The idea that India's wealth was systematically being transferred to Britain — like water draining out of a tub — leaving India poorer each year.

**What they opposed:**
The transformation of a basically **self-sufficient Indian economy** into a **colonial economy** — meaning:
- A **supplier of raw materials** and food stuffs (to British factories)
- An **importer of finished goods** (from British factories, destroying Indian industries)
- A **field for investment** for British capital (profits going back to Britain)

**The Achievement:**
The Moderates created an **all-India public opinion** that **British rule in India was the major cause of India's poverty and economic backwardness**.

**Specific Economic Demands Made:**

- Severance of India's **economic subservience** to Britain
- Development of an **independent economy** through Indian capital and enterprise
- **Reduction in land revenue**
- **Abolition of salt tax**
- Improvement in **working conditions of plantation labour**
- **Reduction in military expenditure**
- **Encouragement to modern industry** through tariff protection (taxing imports) and direct government aid

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The Drain Theory's intellectual legacy is immense. Economist **Utsa Patnaik's 2018 study** (published by Columbia University Press) estimated that Britain drained approximately **$45 trillion from India** between 1765 and 1938 — giving modern statistical weight to Dadabhai Naoroji's 19th-century argument. This remains a key reference in contemporary debates about **colonial reparations** and **global economic justice**. `[Modern Example]` The Moderates' demand for tariff protection for Indian industries finds its modern echo in India's **'Atmanirbhar Bharat' (Self-Reliant India) initiative (2020)**, which includes **Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes** to boost domestic manufacturing and reduce import dependence — the same structural goal Naoroji and Dutt argued for in the 1880s and 1890s.

---

## 🔷 Contribution 2: Constitutional Reforms and Propaganda in Legislature

### The Reality of Legislative Councils

**Legislative councils** in India had **no real official power till 1920**. Yet, the work done in them by nationalists helped the growth of the national movement.

**The Imperial Legislative Council** — constituted by the **Indian Councils Act (1861)** — was:
- An **impotent (powerless) body**
- Designed to disguise official measures as having been passed by a representative body
- **Indian members were very few** — in the 30 years from **1862 to 1892**, only **45 Indians were nominated** to it
- Most were **wealthy, landed, and with loyalist interests**
- Only a handful of genuine political figures like **Syed Ahmed Khan, Kristodas Pal, V.N. Mandlik, K.L. Nulkar**, and **Rashbehari Ghosh** were among those nominated

### Nationalist Demands (1885–1892)

The nationalist demands for constitutional reforms were centred around two main points:

1. **Expansion of councils** — greater participation of Indians in councils
2. **Reform of councils** — more powers to councils, especially **greater control over finances**

The early nationalists worked with the **long-term objective of a democratic self-government**.

### The Indian Councils Act, 1892

Their demands were meant to have been conceded in **1892** in the form of the **Indian Councils Act**.

---

**📋 Indian Councils Act, 1892 — Main Provisions:**

- The **number of additional members** in Imperial Legislative Councils and the Provincial Legislative Councils was **raised**. In Imperial Legislative Council, the Governor General could now have **10 to 16 non-officials** (instead of six to ten previously).
- Non-official members of the Indian legislative council were to be **nominated by the Bengal Chamber of Commerce** and provincial legislative councils. Members could be **recommended by universities, municipalities, zamindars, and chambers of commerce** — so the **principle of representation was introduced**.
- The **budget could be discussed**
- **Questions could be asked**

**📋 Indian Councils Act, 1892 — Limitations:**

- Officials **retained their majority** in the council — making the non-official voice ineffective
- The 'reformed' Imperial Legislative Council met, during its tenure till **1909**, on an average for only **13 days in a year**, and the number of unofficial Indian members present was only **five out of twenty-four**
- The budget **could not be voted upon**, nor could any amendments be made to it
- **Supplementaries could not be asked**, nor could answers to any question be discussed

---

### Nationalists' Reaction and Further Demands

These reforms were **severely criticised** at Congress sessions. The nationalists were openly dissatisfied. They now demanded:

1. A **majority of elected Indians** (not just nominated members)
2. **Control over the budget** — i.e., the power to **vote upon and amend the budget**

They gave the powerful slogan: **"No taxation without representation"**

(This slogan had originally been used by American colonists before the American Revolution of 1776 — Indian nationalists consciously borrowed it.)

**The Demand Widens:**
- **Dadabhai Naoroji (1904)**, **Gopal Krishna Gokhale (1905)**, and **Lokmanya Tilak (1906)** all demanded **self-government on the lines of the self-governing colonies of Canada and Australia**.
- Leaders like **Pherozeshah Mehta and Gokhale** put government policies and proposals to **severe criticism** in the councils.

### How the Nationalists Turned the Tables

The British had intended to use the councils to incorporate the more vocal Indian leaders — to let them blow off "political steam" — while keeping the councils powerless.

But the nationalists **transformed these councils** into forums for:
- **Ventilating popular grievances**
- **Exposing the defects** of an indifferent bureaucracy
- **Criticising government policies** and proposals
- Raising **basic economic issues**, especially regarding public finance

The nationalists thus **enhanced their political stature** and built a national movement while **undermining the political and moral influence** of imperialist rule — generating **anti-imperialist sentiments** among the public.

**A Key Limitation:** However, the nationalists failed to widen the democratic base of the movement by **not including the masses, especially women**, and **not demanding the right to vote for all**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The Moderates' demand for "No taxation without representation" is now fully realised in the Indian Constitution — **Article 265** states that "no tax shall be levied or collected except by authority of law." The **Parliament's control over the Budget** (Articles 112–117) — which the Moderates fought so hard for — is a cornerstone of Indian democracy. `[Modern Example]` The transformation of legislative councils from rubber-stamp bodies to genuine forums for debate finds its modern echo in contemporary debates about the **quality of parliamentary debate** in India. The **PRS Legislative Research** data shows that the **16th Lok Sabha (2014–19)** passed **205 bills** but sat for only **331 hours** in its last year — raising the same concerns about meaningful legislative scrutiny that the Moderates raised in the 1890s.

---

## 🔷 Contribution 3: Campaign for General Administrative Reforms

The Moderates campaigned on the following specific grounds:

**1. Indianisation of Government Service:**
- **Economic grounds:** British civil servants got very high emoluments (salary + allowances); Indians would be more economical
- **Political grounds:** Salaries of British bureaucrats were remitted (sent) back home and pensions paid in England — all drawn from Indian revenue — this amounted to **economic drain** of national resources
- **Moral grounds:** Indians were being **discriminated against** by being kept away from positions of trust and responsibility

**2. Separation of Judicial from Executive Functions:**
- Demanded that the person who makes administrative decisions (executive) should be different from the person who judges legal cases (judicial) — preventing arbitrary abuse of power

**3. Criticism of Bureaucracy and Judicial System:**
- Criticised the **oppressive and tyrannical bureaucracy** (colonial administration)
- Criticised the **expensive and time-consuming judicial system**

**4. Criticism of Aggressive Foreign Policy:**
- Criticised the aggressive foreign policy that resulted in:
  - **Annexation of Burma**
  - **Attack on Afghanistan**
  - **Suppression of tribals in the North-West**
  - All of which cost heavily for the **Indian treasury**

**5. Demand for Increased Welfare Expenditure:**
- Increase in expenditure on:
  - **Welfare** (health, sanitation)
  - **Education** — especially elementary and technical
  - **Irrigation works** and improvement of agriculture
  - **Agricultural banks** for cultivators

**6. Better Treatment for Indian Labour Abroad:**
- Demanded better treatment for **Indian labour in other British colonies**, where they faced **oppression and racial discrimination**

---

## 🔷 Contribution 4: Protection of Civil Rights

**Civil rights** included the right to **speech, thought, association, and a free press**.

Through an **incessant (non-stop) campaign**, the nationalists were able to:
- Spread **modern democratic ideas**
- Make the **defence of civil rights** an integral part of the freedom struggle

**Notable incidents that increased consciousness:**
- The **arrest of Tilak** and several other leaders and journalists in **1897**
- The **arrest and deportation of the Natu brothers without a trial** — a clear violation of civil rights that caused great public outrage

# PART 4: AN EVALUATION OF THE EARLY NATIONALISTS

---

## ✅ What the Moderates Did Right

Despite not drawing the masses to them, the early nationalists did a great deal to awaken national sentiment. Here is a complete assessment:

1. They represented the **most progressive forces of the time**
2. They were able to create a **wide national awakening** — making all Indians feel they had common interests, needed to rally around a common programme against a common enemy, and — above all — belonged to **one nation**
3. They **trained people in political work** and popularised modern ideas
4. They **exposed the exploitative character** of colonial rule, thus undermining its **moral foundations**
5. Their political work was based on **hard realities**, and not on shallow sentiments, religion, etc.
6. They established the basic political truth that **India should be ruled in the interest of Indians**
7. They created a **solid base for a more vigorous, militant, mass-based national movement** in the years that followed

---

## ❌ Where the Moderates Failed

- They **failed to widen their democratic base** — the scope of their demands did not include the masses
- They did not include **women** in the movement meaningfully
- They did not demand the **right to vote for all**

---

## 🔷 Role of the Masses — Why Were They Absent?

The moderate phase had a **narrow social base** and the masses played a **passive (inactive) role**. The reasons were:

- The early nationalists **lacked political faith in the masses**
- They felt that Indian society had **numerous divisions and subdivisions** (caste, religion, language, region)
- They believed the masses were **generally ignorant** and had **conservative ideas**
- The Moderates felt these **heterogeneous (diverse, mixed) elements** had first to be **welded into a nation** before they entered the political sphere

**The Critical Error in This Thinking:**
> They failed to realise that it was **only during a freedom struggle** and **with political participation** that these diverse elements could come together — not before.

Because of this lack of mass participation, the Moderates **could not take militant political positions** against the authorities.

The **later nationalists (Extremists)** differed from the Moderates precisely on this point — they wanted to **bring the masses in**.

---

# PART 5: ATTITUDE OF THE BRITISH GOVERNMENT TOWARDS THE CONGRESS

---

## 🔷 From Condescension to Open Hostility

The **British Indian Government was hostile** to the Congress **from the very beginning** — despite the latter's moderate methods and emphasis on **loyalty to the British Crown**.

**The official attitude stiffened further after 1887** when the government:
- Failed to persuade the Congress to **confine itself to social issues**
- Watched the Congress become **increasingly critical of colonial rule**

### Government's Response Strategies

**1. Verbal Attack:**
- The government resorted to **open condemnation** of the Congress
- Called the nationalists **"seditious brahmins"** and **"disloyal babus"**
- **Lord Dufferin** called the Congress **"a factory of sedition"**

> *"The Congress is tottering to its fall, and one of my great ambitions while in India is to assist it to a peaceful demise."* — **Lord Curzon (1900)**

**2. Encouraging Counter-Forces:**
- The officials encouraged **reactionary elements** like **Sir Syed Ahmed Khan** and **Raja Shiv Prasad Singh of Benaras** to organise the **United Indian Patriotic Association** to counter Congress propaganda

**3. Divide and Rule:**
- The government tried to **divide the nationalists on the basis of religion**
- Through a policy of **'carrot and stick'**, pitted the **Moderates against the Extremists**

**The Outcome:** Despite all these efforts, the government **failed to check the rising tide of nationalism**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The British government's strategy of labelling nationalists as "seditious" finds an uncomfortable modern parallel in ongoing debates about **sedition law (Section 124A IPC)**. The **Supreme Court of India in S.G. Vombatkere v. Union of India (2022)** stayed all pending trials and proceedings under the sedition law and asked the government to reconsider it. The **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023** — which replaced the IPC — removed Section 124A but introduced a new provision (Section 152) on "acts endangering sovereignty, unity and integrity of India," keeping the debate alive. The colonial-era fear of nationalist speech criminalisation thus remains relevant 130 years later. `[Modern Example]` The Moderates' constitutional method of filing petitions and memoranda to the government has a direct modern descendant in the **Public Interest Litigation (PIL)** system, which allows citizens to approach the Supreme Court directly on matters of public interest. The **PIL mechanism** has been described as "the most powerful tool of judicial activism" — a constitutional evolution of exactly the kind of accessible legal-political action the Moderates pioneered.
