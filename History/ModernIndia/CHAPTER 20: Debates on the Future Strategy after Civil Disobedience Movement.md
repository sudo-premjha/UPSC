# CHAPTER 20: Debates on the Future Strategy after Civil Disobedience Movement

---

## 🔍 Introduction: A Movement Ends, A Debate Begins

The withdrawal of the **Civil Disobedience Movement** left the nationalist movement at a crossroads. Two separate but connected debates now unfolded on what nationalists should do next:

- **First Stage (1934–35):** What course should the national movement take *immediately* — during the phase of non-mass struggle?
- **Second Stage (1937):** Should Congress *accept office* in provinces after elections held under the **Government of India Act, 1935**?

Both debates reflected deep ideological fault lines within the Congress between its conservative, moderate, and radical socialist wings.

---

## 🗣️ Part 1: The First Stage Debate (1934–35) — Three Perspectives

Three distinct perspectives emerged on what nationalists should do immediately after the Civil Disobedience Movement ended.

The **first two were traditional responses**; the **third represented the rise of a strong leftist trend** within the Congress.

---

### Perspective 1: Gandhian Constructive Work

- Continue the programme of constructive work on **Gandhian lines** — khadi, village industries, anti-untouchability, Hindu-Muslim unity, etc.
- Build the movement from the grassroots up.

---

### Perspective 2: Constitutional Struggle and Council Entry

**Advocated by:** M.A. Ansari, Asaf Ali, Bhulabhai Desai, S. Satyamurthy, and B.C. Roy (among others).

**Their Arguments:**
- ✅ In a period of **political apathy** (public disengagement from politics), elections and council work could be used to **keep up political interest and morale** of the people.
- ✅ Participation in elections and council work did **not amount to faith in constitutional politics** — it was a tactical move, not an ideological surrender.
- ✅ Opening **another political front** would help build up the Congress and prepare the masses for the next phase of struggle.
- ✅ This approach would give the Congress **prestige and confidence**, and a strong presence in councils would serve as the equivalent of the movement.

---

### Perspective 3: Nehru's Leftist Call — Continuous Mass Struggle

A **strong leftist trend within the Congress, represented by Nehru**, was critical of *both* constructive work and council entry.

**Why?**
- Both options would **sidetrack political mass action**.
- They would **divert attention** from the main issue — the struggle against colonialism.

**What Nehru Proposed Instead:**
- **Resumption and continuation of non-constitutionalist mass struggle** — because:
  - The situation was still **revolutionary** owing to continued economic crisis.
  - The masses were **ready to fight**.

---

### 🔥 Nehru's Vision — Full Statement

Nehru articulated a bold, socialist vision:

> *"The basic goal before Indian people as before people of the world is abolition of capitalism and establishment of socialism."*

He considered the withdrawal of the CDM and council entry as:
- A **"spiritual defeat"**
- A **"surrender of ideals"**
- A **"retreat from revolutionary to reformist mentality"**

**His Concrete Proposals:**
- **Revise vested interests in favour of the masses** by taking up the economic and class demands of peasants and workers against landlords and capitalists.
- **Organise masses in their class organisations** — **kisan sabhas** (peasant associations) and **trade unions**.
- Allow these **class organisations to affiliate with the Congress**, thus influencing its policies and activities.

> *"There could be no genuine anti-imperialist struggle without incorporating the class struggle of the masses."*

---

## ⚔️ Part 2: Nehru's Opposition to the Struggle-Truce-Struggle Strategy

### What Was the S-T-S Strategy?

A large number of Congressmen led by Gandhi believed in the **Struggle-Truce-Struggle (S-T-S) strategy** — a three-phase cycle:

1. **Struggle Phase** — a mass movement launched against the British.
2. **Truce Phase** — a period of reprieve/rest after the struggle.
3. **Struggle Again** — a resumption of mass movement.

**Rationale for S-T-S:**
- The truce period would enable the masses to **recoup their strength** to fight again.
- It would give the government a **chance to respond** to nationalist demands.
- The masses could not go on sacrificing **indefinitely**.
- If the government did not respond positively, the movement could be **resumed with mass participation**.

---

### Nehru's Critique of S-T-S

Nehru argued that after the **Lahore Congress's call for purna swaraj**, the Indian national movement had reached a stage where there should be a **continuous confrontation and conflict** with imperialism until it was overthrown.

- He advocated **"continuous direct action"** by the Congress.
- No **interposition of a constitutionalist phase** was acceptable.
- *"Real power cannot be won by two annas and four annas."*

Against S-T-S, Nehru proposed a **Struggle-Victory (S-V) strategy** — continuous struggle until final victory, with no truce.

---

## 🤝 Part 3: The Resolution — Yes to Council Entry

Despite the sharp divisions, **Gandhi stepped in to prevent a 1907-type Surat Split** (when the Congress had split into Moderates and Extremists).

**Gandhi's Balanced Position:**

> *"Parliamentary politics cannot lead to freedom but those Congressmen who could not, for some reason, offer satyagraha or devote themselves to constructive work should not remain unoccupied and could express their patriotic energies through council work — provided they are not sucked into constitutionalism or self-serving."*

**Assuring the leftists**, Gandhi said: the withdrawal of the CDM did NOT mean bowing down before opportunists or compromising with imperialism.

### Institutional Action

- **May 1934:** The **AICC met at Patna** to set up a **Parliamentary Board** to fight elections under the aegis of the Congress itself.

### Gandhi's Self-Awareness

Gandhi acknowledged he was "out of tune with powerful trends in the Congress":
- A large section of the intelligentsia **favoured parliamentary politics** — with which he was in fundamental disagreement.
- Another section was estranged because of Gandhi's emphasis on the **spinning wheel as the "second lung of the nation"**.
- The **socialists led by Nehru** also had differences with him.

**October 1934:** Gandhi announced his **resignation from the Congress** — to serve it better "in thought, word, and deed."

### The Socialists' Practical Logic

Nehru and the socialists concluded that:
- The **British must first be expelled** before the struggle for socialism could be waged.
- In an anti-imperialist struggle, **unity around the Congress** — still the only anti-imperialist mass organisation — was indispensable.
- It was therefore better to **gradually radicalise the Congress** than to get isolated from the masses.

### Electoral Result

In the **elections to the Central Legislative Assembly held in November 1934**, the Congress captured **45 out of 75 seats reserved for Indians** — a strong performance.

## 🌍 Modern Context — The Strategy Debate

`[Modern Example]`

### The "Inside vs. Outside" Strategy Debate Lives On
- The Nehru vs. Gandhi debate about whether to work *within* constitutional structures or sustain *outside* mass pressure mirrors debates in contemporary Indian democracy.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Aam Aadmi Party (AAP)** was born from the **Anna Hazare anti-corruption movement (2011–12)** — which itself faced the same question: continue as a pressure movement (Gandhian/Nehruvian mass struggle approach) or enter electoral politics (council entry approach). AAP chose electoral politics in 2013, echoing the Swarajist logic.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Farmers' Protest (2020–21)** explicitly chose to stay *outside* the constitutional framework (refusing to contest elections) — closer to Nehru's continuous confrontation strategy — and ultimately succeeded in getting the three farm laws repealed in November 2021.

## 🏛️ Part 4: Government of India Act, 1935

While the nationalist debate raged, the British produced the **Government of India Act, 1935** — the outcome of the Third Round Table Conference and subsequent parliamentary deliberations.

> *"We framed the Act of 1935 because we thought that was the best way...of maintaining British influence in India."*
> — **Lord Linlithgow**, Viceroy (1936–43)

> *"We are provided with a car, all brakes and no engine."*
> — **Jawaharlal Nehru**

> *"The process of constitutional advance in India is determined by the need to attract Indian collaborators to the Raj."*
> — **B.R. Tomlinson**

The **Government of India Act was passed by the British Parliament in August 1935**. Its main provisions were:

---

### Feature 1: All India Federation

An **All India Federation** was proposed, to comprise:
- All **British Indian provinces**
- All **Chief Commissioner's provinces**
- **Indian states (princely states)**

**But — It Never Actually Came Into Existence!**

Two conditions had to be fulfilled for the federation to form:
1. States with an allotment of **52 seats in the proposed Council of States** must agree to join.
2. The **aggregate population** of such states should be **50 per cent of the total population of all Indian states**.

Since these conditions were **never fulfilled**, the proposed federation **never came up**.

> The **central government carried on until 1946 as per the provisions of the Government of India Act, 1919**.

---

### Feature 2: Federal Level — Executive

- The **Governor General** was the **pivot of the entire Constitution**.
- Subjects were divided into **Reserved** and **Transferred** categories:

| Category | Subjects | Administered By |
|---|---|---|
| **Reserved** | Foreign affairs, defence, tribal areas, ecclesiastical affairs | Governor General on advice of **executive councillors** — NOT responsible to the central legislature |
| **Transferred** | All other subjects | Governor General on advice of **ministers elected by the legislature** — responsible to the federal legislature, must resign on losing confidence |

- The Governor General could act in **his individual judgement** for the security and tranquillity of India.

---

### Feature 3: Federal Level — Legislature (Bicameral)

The **bicameral legislature** (two-house legislature) consisted of:

| House | Members | Composition |
|---|---|---|
| **Council of States** (Upper House) | 260 members | Partly directly elected from British Indian provinces; **40% nominated by princes** |
| **Federal Assembly** (Lower House) | 375 members | Partly indirectly elected from British Indian provinces; **one-third nominated by princes** |

**Curious Anomaly:**
- Elections to the **Council of States** (Upper House) were **direct**.
- Elections to the **Federal Assembly** (Lower House) were **indirect**.
- This was the opposite of what one would logically expect.

**Other Legislative Features:**
- Council of States was a **permanent body** — one-third members retiring every third year.
- **Duration of the Federal Assembly: 5 years**.
- **Three legislative lists**: Federal, Provincial, and Concurrent.
- Federal Assembly members could move a **vote of no-confidence** against ministers.
- Council of States **could NOT** move a vote of no-confidence.
- System of **religion-based and class-based electorates** was further extended.
- **80 per cent of the budget was non-votable** (could not be voted on by the legislature).
- Governor General had **residuary powers** — could:
  - (a) Restore cuts in grants
  - (b) Certify bills rejected by the legislature
  - (c) Issue ordinances
  - (d) Exercise his veto

---

### Feature 4: Provincial Autonomy

This was the most significant operative feature of the Act — and the part that actually came into effect.

**Key Changes:**
- **Provincial autonomy replaced dyarchy** (the old dual governance system).
- Provinces were granted **autonomy and separate legal identity**.
- Provinces were freed from the **"superintendence, direction"** of the Secretary of State and Governor General.
- Provinces henceforth derived their **legal authority directly from the British Crown**.
- Provinces were given **independent financial powers and resources**.
- Provincial governments could **borrow money on their own security**.

**Provincial Executive:**
- **Governor** was the Crown's nominee and representative — exercising authority on the King's behalf in a province.
- Governor had **special powers** regarding:
  - Minorities
  - Rights of civil servants
  - Law and order
  - British business interests
  - Partially excluded areas
  - Princely states
- The Governor could **take over and indefinitely run the administration**.

**Provincial Legislature:**
- **Separate electorates** based on the Communal Award were made operational.
- All members were to be **directly elected**.
- **Franchise was extended** — women got the right on the **same basis as men**.
- **Ministers were to administer all provincial subjects** in a council of ministers headed by a premier.
- Ministers were **answerable to and removable** by the adverse vote of the legislature.
- Provincial legislature could legislate on subjects in **provincial and concurrent lists**.
- **40 per cent of the budget was still not votable**.
- Governor could:
  - (a) Refuse assent to a bill
  - (b) Promulgate ordinances
  - (c) Enact Governor's Acts

---

### Evaluation of the Act — What Was Wrong With It?

| Criticism | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Brakes without an engine** | Numerous 'safeguards' and 'special responsibilities' of the Governor General worked as brakes in the proper functioning of the Act |
| **Governor's extensive powers** | Even in provinces, governors still had sweeping powers |
| **Limited franchise** | The Act enfranchised only **14 per cent of the British Indian population** |
| **Communal electorates extended** | The extension of religion-based and class-based electorates and representation of various interests promoted **separatist tendencies** — which culminated in the Partition of India |
| **Rigid constitution** | Provided a rigid constitution with **no possibility of internal growth** — right of amendment was reserved with the British Parliament |

---

### The British Long-Term Strategy Behind the Act

The Act was not just legislation — it was a **calculated political strategy** to weaken the nationalist movement. Here is how:

- **Suppression was only a short-term tactic.** In the long run, the strategy was to weaken the national movement and **integrate large segments of it** into colonial, constitutional, and administrative structures.
- **Reforms would revive constitutionalist liberals and moderates** who had lost public support during the CDM.
- **Repression followed by reforms** would convince a large section of Congressmen that **extra-legal struggle was ineffective**.
- **Once Congressmen tasted power**, they would be reluctant to go back to politics of sacrifice.
- **Reforms could be used to create dissensions within Congress** — right wing placated through constitutional concessions; radical leftists crushed through police measures.
- **Provincial autonomy** would create powerful provincial leaders who would become autonomous centres of political power — **Congress would be provincialised** and **central leadership would weaken**.

---

### Nationalists' Response

- The 1935 Act was **condemned by nearly all sections** and **unanimously rejected by the Congress**.
- The **Hindu Mahasabha** and the **National Liberal Foundation** declared themselves in favour of working the 1935 Act at both central and provincial levels.
- The Congress demanded instead: **convening of a Constituent Assembly elected on the basis of adult franchise** to frame a constitution for independent India.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Government of India Act, 1935

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The 1935 Act as the Template for India's Constitution
- Despite being universally condemned, the **GoI Act 1935 served as the primary draft template** for the Indian Constitution of 1950.
- Many provisions — federal structure, three lists (Union, State, Concurrent), emergency provisions, the role of the Governor — were directly drawn from the 1935 Act.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Governor's powers controversy** — which has repeatedly made headlines in 2022–24, with Governors in Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Telangana, and Punjab clashing with elected state governments over bills, appointments, and university matters — traces directly to the **discretionary powers preserved for Governors under the 1935 Act** tradition. The **Supreme Court in 2023** (*In Re: Tamil Nadu Governor case*) ruled that Governors cannot indefinitely withhold assent to bills — a modern check on powers inherited from 1935.

### 2. Franchise Extension — Then and Now
- The 1935 Act enfranchised **14 per cent** of the British Indian population — considered a breakthrough at the time.
- `[Modern Example]` India today has **universal adult franchise** (guaranteed by **Article 326** of the Constitution). In the **2024 General Elections**, approximately **96.8 crore voters** were registered — the largest electorate in world history. The contrast between 14% franchise in 1937 and near-100% today illustrates the transformative journey India has taken.

### 3. The Federation That Never Was
- The All India Federation under the 1935 Act never materialised because princely states refused to join.
- `[Modern Example]` This has a modern echo: **Jammu & Kashmir's special status** (under Article 370, abrogated in **August 2019**) and the **integration of Hyderabad (1948)** and **Junagadh** were the final resolution of the "princely states problem" that made the 1935 federation unworkable. The **Delimitation Commission 2020–24** for J&K is a direct continuation of the process of fully integrating former princely territories into India's constitutional framework.

## 🗳️ Part 5: The Second Stage Debate — Office Acceptance (1937)

In **early 1937**, elections to provincial assemblies were announced. The debate on future strategy resumed — but now it was sharper and more specific:

> **"If Congress wins a majority in a province, should it form a government?"**

Everyone agreed the 1935 Act should be **opposed root and branch**. But HOW to oppose it without a mass movement was the question.

There was full agreement that Congress should **fight elections** with a detailed political and economic programme — to deepen anti-imperialist consciousness. But what to do *after* the elections was deeply contested.

---

### Against Office Acceptance: The Leftist Position

**Led by:** Jawaharlal Nehru, Subhash Bose, Congress socialists, and communists.

**Arguments Against Accepting Office:**
- ❌ It would **negate the rejection** of the 1935 Act by the nationalists.
- ❌ It would mean **assuming responsibility without power** (ministers would be answerable to the legislature but governors could override them).
- ❌ It would **take away the revolutionary character** of the movement — constitutional work would sidetrack the main issues of freedom, economic justice, social justice, and removal of poverty.

**Their Counter-Strategy:**
- **Short-term:** Enter councils with the aim of **creating deadlocks** — making the working of the Act impossible (echoing the older **Swarajist strategy**).
- **Long-term:** Increase reliance on workers and peasants; **integrate their class organisations into the Congress** — imparting a socialist direction to the Congress and preparing for the resumption of a mass movement.

---

### For Office Acceptance: The Moderate/Right Position

**Arguments For Accepting Office:**
- ✅ They were equally committed to **combating the 1935 Act** — but work in legislatures was only a **short-term tactic** since a mass movement was not available.
- ✅ **Mass struggle alone** was capable of winning independence — but until it was possible, legislatures were a valid arena.
- ✅ Capture or rejection of office was **not a matter of socialism but of strategy**.
- ✅ Yes, there was a **danger of being sucked in by wrong tendencies** — but the answer was to fight those tendencies, not abandon office.
- ✅ The **administrative field should not be left open** to pro-government reactionary forces.
- ✅ Despite **limited powers**, provincial ministries could be used to **promote constructive work**.

---

### Gandhi's Position

- Gandhi **opposed office acceptance** in CWC meetings initially.
- But by **the beginning of 1936**, he was willing to give a trial to the formation of Congress ministries.

---

### Congress Sessions and the Final Decision

| Session | Location | Date | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lucknow Session** | Lucknow | Early 1936 | Decided to fight elections; **postponed** decision on office acceptance to post-election phase |
| **Faizpur Session** | Faizpur | Late 1937 | Same approach maintained |

**The Congress Resolution:**
> *"Not to submit to this constitution or to cooperate with it, but to combat it both inside and outside the legislatures so that it can be ended."*

## 🗳️ Part 6: The 1937 Provincial Elections

### The Context

In **February 1937**, elections to provincial assemblies were held in **11 provinces**:

> **Madras, Central Provinces, Bihar, Orissa, United Provinces, Bombay Presidency, Assam, NWFP, Bengal, Punjab**, and **Sindh**.

**Historic Scale:**
- These were the **first elections in which a larger number of Indians than ever before were eligible to participate**.
- An estimated **30.1 million persons** — including **4.25 million women** — had been enfranchised (**14 per cent of the total population**).
- **15.5 million** of these — including **917,000 women** — actually exercised their franchise.

---

### Congress Manifesto for the 1937 Elections

The Congress manifesto included:
- ✅ **Total rejection** of the 1935 Act.
- ✅ **Release of political prisoners**.
- ✅ **Removal of disabilities** on the basis of gender and caste.
- ✅ **Radical transformation of the agrarian system**.
- ✅ **Substantial reduction of rent and revenue**.
- ✅ **Scaling down of rural debts**.
- ✅ **Cheap credit**.
- ✅ **Right to form trade unions** and to strike.

> 📌 **Notable Fact:** Gandhi **did not attend a single election meeting** during the 1937 campaign.

---

### Congress' Stunning Performance

| Metric | Result |
|---|---|
| **Seats contested** | 1,161 (out of 1,585 total in 11 provinces) |
| **Seats won** | **716** |
| **Majority in provinces** | All except Bengal, Assam, Punjab, Sindh, and NWFP |
| **Largest party in** | Bengal, Assam, and NWFP |

**Consequence:**
- The prestige of the Congress **rose enormously**.
- Even **Nehru was reconciled to the dominant strategy of S-T-S** (Struggle-Truce-Struggle) — a significant ideological shift.

> **Congress ministries were subsequently formed in:** Bombay, Madras, Central Provinces, United Provinces, Bihar, Orissa, Assam, and NWFP.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Office Acceptance Debate and 1937 Elections

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. "Combating from Within" — A Timeless Strategy
- The Congress's 1937 resolution to "combat the constitution both inside and outside the legislatures" is a phrase that could describe any number of contemporary political strategies.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Opposition's "INDIA Alliance" strategy (2023–24)** in India — contesting elections while simultaneously criticising electoral institutions (EVMs, Election Commission appointments) — mirrors the 1937 Congress's approach of participating in elections while fundamentally rejecting the legitimacy of the framework.

### 2. Women's Franchise — From 917,000 to Crores
- In 1937, only **917,000 women** actually voted — out of 4.25 million enfranchised.
- `[Modern Example]` In the **2024 General Elections**, women's voter turnout **exceeded men's** in several states — a historic milestone. According to the **Election Commission of India**, women's participation has grown from near-negligible in 1937 to **roughly equal with men** by 2024, reflecting the transformative journey of women's political empowerment in India.

### 3. Provincial Autonomy's Modern Relevance — Centre-State Relations
- The concept of **provincial autonomy** introduced by the 1935 Act — and the debate about how much power provinces should have vs. the Centre — remains one of the most contested issues in Indian federalism.
- `[Modern Example]` The **15th Finance Commission (2020–25)** debates about the share of tax devolution to states, and the **GST Council's decision-making structure**, are direct descendants of the fiscal federalism debates that began with the 1935 Act's provisions on provincial financial powers.
