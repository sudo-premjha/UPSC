# 📖 Chapter 10: Beginning of Modern Nationalism in India

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **Indian nationalism was NOT a sudden event** — it grew from a complex mix of factors including colonial policies, Western education, the press, and global liberation movements.
> 2. The British, while exploiting India, **unintentionally created the very conditions** (railways, telegraph, English education, unified laws) that allowed nationalists to organise.
> 3. The **educated middle class** — lawyers, doctors, journalists, teachers — was the engine of early Indian nationalism and later led the Indian National Congress.
> 4. **Political associations** existed in Bengal, Bombay, and Madras **before the Indian National Congress** was founded, gradually evolving from local, elitist bodies to wider, more representative organisations.
> 5. **Racial arrogance and reactionary policies** of British rulers like Lytton — including the Vernacular Press Act, Arms Act, and the Ilbert Bill controversy — acted as powerful catalysts that united Indians against colonial rule.

# PART 1: FACTORS IN THE GROWTH OF MODERN NATIONALISM

---

## 🔷 How Did Indian Nationalism Grow? — The Big Picture

The rise and growth of **Indian nationalism** has traditionally been explained as India's **response to the stimulus generated by the British Raj** — through new institutions, new opportunities, and new resources.

In other words:
- Indian nationalism grew **partly as a result** of colonial policies
- And **partly as a reaction** to colonial policies

But a more complete and accurate picture is that **Indian nationalism was a product of a mix of various factors**:

1. **Worldwide upsurge** of the concepts of **nationalism** (feeling of belonging to one nation) and the **right of self-determination** (a nation's right to govern itself) — initiated by the **French Revolution**
2. The **Indian Renaissance** (cultural and intellectual revival of India in the 19th century)
3. An **offshoot of modernisation** initiated by the British in India
4. A **strong reaction to British imperialist policies** (exploitative, empire-building policies) in India

Let's now explore each major factor in detail.

---

## 🔷 Factor 1: Understanding of Contradictions in Indian and Colonial Interests

As time passed, more and more people came to realise a fundamental truth:

> **Colonial rule** (foreign British rule) was the **major cause of India's economic backwardness**.

People understood that the **interests of Indians** — from all walks of life — were opposed to colonial interests. These sections included:

- Peasants
- Artisans
- Handicraftsmen
- Workers
- Intellectuals
- The educated
- Capitalists

The **nationalist movement arose** specifically to take up the challenge of these contradictions — the inherent conflict between what colonial rule wanted and what Indians needed.

---

## 🔷 Factor 2: Political, Administrative, and Economic Unification of the Country

### The Scale of British Control

The British rule in the Indian subcontinent stretched:
- From the **Himalayas in the north** to **Cape Comorin in the south**
- From **Assam in the east** to the **Khyber Pass in the west**

While large areas of India had been unified before — under the **Mauryas** or later under the **Mughals** — the British created a **larger state** than either of these great empires.

- **Indian provinces**: under **'direct' British rule**
- **Princely states**: under **'indirect' British rule**

### How Unity Was Achieved

The **British sword** imposed **political unity** in India. Added to this:
- A **professional civil service**
- A **unified judiciary**
- **Codified civil and criminal laws** throughout the country

These gave a **new dimension of political unity** to a land that had previously only had **cultural unity** for centuries.

### Why the British Developed Infrastructure

The British developed **railways, roads, electricity, and telegraph** — but their motivations were entirely self-serving:
- **Administrative convenience**
- **Military defence**
- **Economic penetration and commercial exploitation**

### Two-Fold Effect on Nationalism

From the nationalists' point of view, this unification had two important effects:

1. **Economic interconnection**: The economic fate of people from different regions became linked. For example, a **crop failure in one region** affected prices and supply in another region. This created a shared economic consciousness.

2. **Communication and mobilisation**: Modern transport and communication **brought leaders from different regions together**. This was crucial for:
   - Exchange of political ideas
   - Mobilisation and organisation of **public opinion** on political and economic issues

---

## 🔷 Factor 3: Western Thought and Education

The introduction of a **modern system of education** opened up new possibilities. It gave a **new direction to Indian political thinking** — even though the English education system was designed by rulers primarily for **efficient administration** of India (in their own self-interest).

**Key Western thinkers who influenced Indians:**

| Thinker | Key Ideas That Influenced Indians |
|---|---|
| **Milton** | Liberty and freedom of expression |
| **Shelley** | Romanticism, rebellion against tyranny |
| **John Stuart Mill** | Liberty, democracy, individual rights |
| **Rousseau** | Social contract, popular sovereignty |
| **Paine** | Rights of man, democracy |
| **Spencer** | Social progress, evolution |
| **Voltaire** | Reason, secularism, critique of tyranny |

These liberal and radical ideas helped many Indians develop **modern rational, secular, democratic, and nationalist ideas**.

### The English Language as a Unifier

The **English language** served as a bridge — it allowed **nationalist leaders from different linguistic regions** to communicate with each other.

### Experience of Studying in England

Educated Indians who visited **England for higher education** in liberal professions (lawyers, doctors, etc.) experienced two things:
1. The **working of modern political institutions** in a free country
2. The **stark contrast** with India — where even **basic rights were denied to citizens**

### The Middle-Class Intelligentsia

The **ever-expanding English-educated class** formed the **middle-class intelligentsia** — the nucleus of the **newly arising political unrest**. This section:
- Provided **leadership** to Indian political associations
- Eventually led the **Indian National Congress**

---

## 🔷 Factor 4: Role of Press and Literature

The **second half of the 19th century** saw an unprecedented (never seen before) growth of **Indian-owned English and vernacular newspapers** — despite numerous restrictions imposed by colonial rulers.

**Key Data Point:**
> In **1877**, there were about **169 newspapers** published in **vernacular languages** with a circulation reaching the neighbourhood of **1,00,000**.

### What the Press Did for Nationalism

- **Criticised official policies** of the British government
- **Urged the people to unite**
- Spread modern ideas of **self-government, democracy, civil rights, and industrialisation**
- Helped in the **exchange of political ideas** among nationalist leaders from different regions through newspapers, journals, pamphlets, and nationalist literature

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The role of the 19th-century press in spreading nationalist ideas finds a direct parallel in the **digital media revolution of the 21st century**. Just as newspapers united nationalists across linguistic barriers, today **social media platforms and digital news portals** connect citizens across India on political and social issues. However, just as the British imposed the **Vernacular Press Act (1878)** to curb press freedom, today debates about **press freedom** continue — India ranked **161st out of 180 countries** in the **Reporters Without Borders World Press Freedom Index 2023**, raising concerns similar to those 19th-century nationalists fought against. `[Modern Example]` The **Press and Registration of Periodicals Act, 2023** (replacing the 1867 Act) has modernised the regulation of the press — a direct legislative descendant of the framework that colonial-era journalists fought within.

---

## 🔷 Factor 5: Rediscovery of India's Past

Historical researches by both **European and Indian scholars** created an entirely new, positive picture of India's past.

**Key Scholars:**

| European Scholars | Indian Scholars |
|---|---|
| Max Mueller | R.G. Bhandarkar |
| Monier Williams | R.L. Mitra |
| Roth | Swami Vivekananda (later) |
| Sassoon | — |

**What this new picture of India revealed:**
- **Well-developed political, economic, and social institutions**
- A **flourishing trade** with the outside world
- A **rich heritage** in arts and culture
- **Numerous cities** — proof of advanced civilisation

### The Psychological Boost

The theory put forward by European scholars — that the **Indo-Aryans belonged to the same ethnic group** from which **other nations of Europe had evolved** — gave a **psychological boost** to educated Indians.

**The Impact:**
- Indians gained **self-respect and confidence**
- Nationalists could now demolish the **colonial myth** that India had a long history of **servility to foreign rulers**

---

## 🔷 Factor 6: Progressive Character of Socio-Religious Reform Movements

The reform movements we studied in Chapter 9 were not just religious or social — they had **political consequences too**.

- These movements sought to **remove social evils** (like caste discrimination) that **divided Indian society**
- By bringing different sections of society closer together, they proved to be an **important factor in the growth of Indian nationalism**

---

## 🔷 Factor 7: Rise of Middle-Class Intelligentsia

British administrative and economic innovations gave rise to a **new urban middle class** in India's towns.

**Percival Spear's description of this new class:**

> *"The new middle class was a well-integrated all-India class with varied background but a common foreground of knowledge, ideas and values.... It was a minority of Indian society, but a dynamic minority.... It had a sense of unity of purpose and of hope."*

This class came to the forefront because of:
- Its **education**
- Its **new social position**
- Its **close ties with the ruling class** (which gave it both access and insight into colonial functioning)

> **Key role:** The leadership to the **Indian National Congress** in **all its stages of growth** was provided by this class.

---

## 🔷 Factor 8: Impact of Contemporary Movements in the World

India did not exist in isolation. Indian nationalists were deeply inspired by **liberation movements** happening around the world:

- **Rise of new nations** on the ruins of the **Spanish and Portuguese empires** in **South America**
- **National liberation movements** in:
  - **Greece**
  - **Italy**
  - **Ireland** (which had particular resonance as Ireland, like India, was under British rule)

These examples showed Indian nationalists that **freedom from colonial rule was possible**.

---

## 🔷 Factor 9: Reactionary Policies and Racial Arrogance of Rulers

### The Racial Myth

The British deliberately perpetuated **racial myths of white superiority** through a policy of **discrimination and segregation** (keeping races separate and unequal). Indians felt **deeply hurt** by this.

### Lytton's Reactionary Policies

Under **Lord Lytton**, a series of provocative policies were implemented:

- **Reduction of maximum age limit** for the **I.C.S. (Indian Civil Service) examination** from **21 years to 19 years** in **1876** — this directly reduced Indians' chances of joining the civil service
- The **grand Delhi Durbar of 1877** — a lavish celebration held while the country was in the **severe grip of famine** — an obscene display of imperial excess
- **Vernacular Press Act (1878)** — imposed restrictions on Indian-language newspapers
- **Arms Act (1878)** — prevented Indians from possessing weapons

All of these provoked a **storm of opposition** in the country.

### The Ilbert Bill Controversy

This was a particularly revealing episode:

**The Background:**
- **Ripon's Government** sought to abolish *"judicial disqualification based on race distinctions"*
- The aim: to give **Indian members of the covenanted civil service** the same powers and rights as their **European colleagues**

**What Happened:**
- The **European community** in India launched a **stiff opposition** to the Ilbert Bill
- Ripon was forced to **modify the bill** — almost defeating its original purpose

**The Double Lesson for Nationalists:**

1. It became clear that **justice and fair play could not be expected** where the interests of the European community were involved.
2. However, the organised European agitation **also taught the nationalists** how to agitate effectively for certain rights and demands — a powerful lesson in political organisation.

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The 19th-century struggle against racial discrimination in the civil services finds an echo in modern India's debates about **reservation policies** in government services (Articles 15, 16 of the Constitution) and the **lateral entry** into the IAS controversy of 2024, where concerns were raised about bypassing the merit-and-reservation system. `[Modern Example]` The constitutional guarantee against discrimination — **Articles 14, 15, and 16** — is the direct answer to the racial discrimination of the colonial era. The **Supreme Court in E.V. Chinnaiah v. State of Andhra Pradesh (2004)** and subsequent judgments continue to grapple with questions of equality in public employment — debates rooted in the very contradictions that sparked 19th-century nationalism.

# PART 2: POLITICAL ASSOCIATIONS BEFORE THE INDIAN NATIONAL CONGRESS

---

## 🔷 Overview

The **Indian National Congress (founded 1885)** was **not the first political organisation in India**. A number of associations preceded it.

**Early Political Associations (first half of the 19th century):**
- Dominated by **wealthy and aristocratic elements**
- **Local or regional** in character
- Primarily demanded (through long petitions to the British Parliament):
  - Administrative reforms
  - Association of Indians with the administration
  - Spread of education

**Later Political Associations (second half of the 19th century):**
- Increasingly dominated by the **educated middle class** — lawyers, journalists, doctors, teachers
- Had a **wider perspective** and a **larger agenda**

---

## 🔷 Political Associations in Bengal

### 1. Bangabhasha Prakasika Sabha (1836)
- Formed in **1836** by **associates of Raja Rammohan Roy**
- One of the earliest organised political bodies in India

---

### 2. Zamindari Association / Landholders' Society
- Also popularly known as the **'Landholders' Society'**
- Founded to **safeguard the interests of the landlords**
- Limited in its objectives — but historically significant because it marked the **beginning of organised political activity** and the use of **constitutional agitation** (working within the law) for the redressal of grievances

---

### 3. Bengal British India Society (1843)
- Founded in **1843**
- **Object:** *"The collection and dissemination of information relating to the actual condition of the people of British India... and to employ such other means of peaceful and lawful character as may appear calculated to secure the welfare, extend the just rights and advance the interests of all classes of our fellow subjects."*

---

### 4. British Indian Association (1851)
- Formed in **1851** when both the **Landholders' Society** and the **Bengal British India Society merged**
- Sent a **petition to the British Parliament** demanding inclusion of its suggestions in the **renewed Charter of the Company**

**Key demands in the petition:**
1. Establishment of a **separate legislature** of a popular character
2. **Separation of executive from judicial functions**
3. **Reduction in salaries** of higher officers
4. **Abolition of salt duty, abkari (excise duty on alcohol), and stamp duties**

**Partial Success:** The **Charter Act of 1853** provided for the **addition of six members** to the Governor General's council for legislative purposes — a partial acceptance of their demands.

---

### 5. East India Association (1866)
- Organised by **Dadabhai Naoroji in 1866 in London**
- Purpose: To **discuss the Indian question** and influence public men in England to **promote Indian welfare**
- Later, branches were started in **prominent Indian cities**

---

### 6. Indian League (1875)
- Started in **1875** by **Sisir Kumar Ghosh**
- **Object:** *"Stimulating the sense of nationalism amongst the people"* and **encouraging political education**

---

### 7. Indian Association of Calcutta (1876) ⭐ *[Most Important Pre-Congress Association]*

Also known as the **Indian National Association**.

- Founded in **1876** by younger nationalists of Bengal led by **Surendranath Banerjea** and **Ananda Mohan Bose**
- They were getting discontented with the **conservative and pro-landlord policies** of the British Indian Association

**The Most Important Pre-Congress Association — Why?**

It aimed to *"promote by every legitimate means the political, intellectual and material advancement of the people."*

**Specific Goals:**
1. Create a **strong public opinion** on political questions
2. **Unify Indian people** in a common political programme

**Key Campaigns:**
- Protested against the **reduction of age limit in 1877** for candidates of the Indian Civil Service examination
- Demanded **simultaneous holding of civil service examination in England and India**
- Demanded **Indianisation of higher administrative posts**
- Led a campaign against the repressive **Arms Act** and the **Vernacular Press Act**
- Kept **low membership fees** to attract the **poorer sections** to the association
- Opened branches in other towns and cities of **Bengal and even outside Bengal**

**The All-India Conference of 1883:**
- The association sponsored an **all-India conference** which first took place in **Calcutta from December 28 to 30, 1883**
- **More than 100 delegates** from different parts of the country attended
- This made the association a **forerunner of the Indian National Congress** as an all-India nationalist organisation
- It later **merged with the Indian National Congress in 1886**

---

## 🔷 Political Associations in Bombay

### 8. Poona Sarvajanik Sabha (1867)
- Founded in **1867** by **Mahadev Govind Ranade** and others
- **Object:** To serve as a **bridge between the government and the people**

### 9. Bombay Presidency Association (1885)
- Started by **Badruddin Tyabji, Pherozeshah Mehta**, and **K.T. Telang** in **1885**

---

## 🔷 Political Associations in Madras

### 10. Madras Mahajan Sabha (1884)
- Founded in **1884** by **M. Viraraghavachari** (also spelled Veeraraghavachariar), **B. Subramania Aiyer**, and **P. Anandacharlu**

---

# PART 3: PRE-CONGRESS CAMPAIGNS

The associations organised various campaigns **before the Indian National Congress appeared on the scene**. These campaigns were incredibly significant — they established the tradition of organised political agitation in India.

**The Pre-Congress Campaigns included:**

1. **For imposition of import duty on cotton (1875)**
2. **For Indianisation of government service (1878–79)**
3. **Against Lytton's Afghan adventure** (an expensive and controversial military campaign)
4. **Against the Arms Act (1878)**
5. **Against the Vernacular Press Act (1878)**
6. **For the right to join volunteer corps**
7. **Against plantation labour** and against the **Inland Emigration Act** (which restricted workers' freedom to leave plantations)
8. **In support of the Ilbert Bill**
9. **For an All-India Fund for Political Agitation**
10. **Campaign in Britain to vote for pro-India party**
11. **Against reduction in maximum age for appearing in the Indian Civil Service examination** — the **Indian Association** took up this issue and organised an all-India agitation, popularly known as the **Indian Civil Service agitation**

---

🌍 **Modern Context**

> `[Modern Example]` The tradition of organised political campaigns by pre-Congress associations finds a modern parallel in the way **civil society organisations (CSOs)** and **public interest litigations (PILs)** are used today to push for policy change. Just as the Indian Association campaigned for the ICS age limit, organisations today campaign on issues like **UPSC Civil Services age limits** (still debated) or **lateral entry into IAS** (2024 controversy). `[Modern Example]` The demand for **Indianisation of higher services** — one of the central pre-Congress campaigns — was finally resolved through constitutional provisions. Today, **Articles 309–323 of the Indian Constitution** govern public services, and the **All India Services** (IAS, IPS, IFS) are entirely Indian-controlled — the fulfilment of what 19th-century nationalists fought for. `[Modern Example]` The **Indian National Congress at 139 years old (as of 2024)** is a direct institutional descendant of the Indian Association of Calcutta and the other pre-Congress bodies. The first session of the INC in **December 1885** was itself modelled on the all-India conference model pioneered by the Indian Association's 1883 conference.

# 📋 Summary: Factors in the Growth of Modern Nationalism

| Factor | Core Idea |
|---|---|
| **Understanding of contradictions** | Colonial rule caused economic backwardness; all Indians had common interests against it |
| **Political, administrative & economic unification** | Railways, unified laws, and a common government linked India's fate together |
| **Western thought and education** | Exposure to ideas of liberty, democracy, and reason inspired political action |
| **Role of press and literature** | 169 vernacular newspapers (1877) spread nationalist ideas and united leaders |
| **Rediscovery of India's past** | European and Indian scholarship restored Indian pride and self-confidence |
| **Socio-religious reform movements** | Removed social divisions, brought communities closer, fed nationalism |
| **Rise of middle-class intelligentsia** | New educated class provided leadership — a "dynamic minority" (Percival Spear) |
| **Impact of world movements** | South America, Greece, Italy, Ireland showed colonial rule could be resisted |
| **Reactionary policies & racial arrogance** | Lytton's policies + Ilbert Bill controversy united Indians in opposition |
