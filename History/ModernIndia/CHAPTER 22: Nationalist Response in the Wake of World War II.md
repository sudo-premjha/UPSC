## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Key Takeaways to Remember Before You Read:**
>
> 1. **Bose vs. Gandhi — A Congress Civil War:** The **Tripuri Crisis (1939)** saw Subhas Bose win the Congress presidency against Gandhi's preferred candidate but was ultimately forced to resign — revealing a deep ideological split between radical mass action and Gandhian gradualism.
> 2. **India Dragged into WWII Without Consent:** On **September 3, 1939**, Britain declared India's support for the war *without consulting Indians* — the Congress offered conditional support, was rejected, and all Congress ministries resigned in **October 1939**.
> 3. **Three Different Nationalist Responses to WWII:** Gandhi wanted unconditional support; Bose and socialists wanted to exploit Britain's weakness; Nehru recognised Allied justice but refused participation until India was free — Nehru's position won.
> 4. **Pakistan Resolution (March 1940):** The Muslim League passed its landmark resolution at Lahore calling for separate independent states for Muslim-majority areas — a decisive turning point on the road to Partition.
> 5. **Cripps Mission (1942) — A Post-Dated Cheque:** The mission offered dominion status after the war, a constituent assembly, and the right of provinces to secede — but satisfied no one and ultimately failed, described by Gandhi as **"a post-dated cheque on a crashing bank."**

# CHAPTER 22: Nationalist Response in the Wake of World War II

---

## 🔍 Part 1: Congress Crisis on the Method of Struggle

### The Internal Rot

In the aftermath of the Civil Disobedience Movement, the Congress was in some disarray. Gandhi identified several problems:

- **Rising corruption and indiscipline** within the organisation.
- **Rivalries and petty squabbles** among Congress leaders.
- **Bogus membership** and **unethical means** being used to control Congress committees.

**Gandhi's Position:**
- The Congress should **first put its house in order** before a new movement could be launched.
- He also felt the **masses were not yet in the mood for a struggle**.

**Others disagreed** — they felt the struggle should continue without pause.

---

## 🔥 Part 2: Subhas Chandra Bose — Background and Ideology

### Who Was Bose?

- **President of the Bengal Provincial Congress Committee**.
- His main areas of work: **organisation of youth** and promoting the **trade union movement**.
- Bose was a man of **action and radical ideas** — consistently at the more militant end of the Congress spectrum.

**Key Actions That Define His Character:**

| Event | Bose's Stand |
|---|---|
| Motilal Nehru Report (dominion status) | **Opposed** — demanded full independence; announced the **Independence League** |
| Lahore Congress Session (purna swaraj resolution) | **Fully endorsed** |
| Salt Satyagraha (1930) | **Actively participated** — forced the government to arrest him |
| Gandhi-Irwin Pact (1931) | **Vehemently opposed** — especially as the government refused to negotiate on Bhagat Singh's death sentence |

---

### Haripura Session (February 1938)

- Bose was **unanimously elected president** of the Congress at **Haripura, Gujarat, in February 1938**.
- In his presidential address, he spoke about the **revolutionary potential of the Congress ministries** in the provinces.
- He advocated **economic development through planning** and was instrumental in setting up a **National Planning Committee** later.
- The session adopted a resolution that Congress would give **moral support** to those agitating against governance in **princely states**.
- By now, the **international situation was highly disturbed** — clear signs of an impending European war were visible.

---

## ⚔️ Part 3: The Tripuri Crisis (1939) — Bose Wins but Congress Splits

### The Presidential Election (January 1939)

In **January 1939**, Subhas Chandra Bose decided to stand **again** for the president's post in the Congress.

**Gandhi's Opposition:** Gandhi was not happy with Bose's candidature.

**Bose's Platform:** He said he represented the *"new ideas, ideologies, problems, and programmes"* that had emerged from the *"progressive sharpening of the anti-imperialist struggle in India."*

**Against Bose:** Sardar Patel, Rajendra Prasad, J.B. Kripalani, and other CWC members argued that:
- Ideologies and programmes were developed in **Congress bodies like the Working Committee**, not by the president alone.
- The Congress president's position was more **constitutional, representative, and symbolic** of national unity.
- They backed Gandhi's preferred candidate: **Pattabhi Sitaramayya**.

**The Result:**
- **Bose won: 1580 votes vs. 1377 for Sitaramayya**.
- Bose got the full support of the **Congress Socialist Party** and the **communists**.
- Gandhi congratulated Bose but declared: *"Pattabhi's defeat is my defeat."*
- This instantly transformed it into a **Gandhi versus Bose** confrontation.

---

### The Tripuri Session (March 1939)

The Congress session took place at **Tripuri, Central Provinces** (near Jabalpur in present Madhya Pradesh) in **March 1939**.

**The Political Context:**
- The Working Committee was **nominated by the president**, not elected — so Bose's election meant he would now appoint the entire ruling body.
- The sharp ideological and strategic divide between Bose and the Congress mainstream was exposed.
- Bose had **accused the Working Committee leaders** of being ready to compromise with the government on federation.
- Those leaders felt they could not work with a president who had publicly **cast doubts on their nationalistic principles** — they resigned from the Working Committee.

**Bose's Presidential Address at Tripuri:**
Despite being ill, Bose attended and prophesied that an **imperialist war was about to take place in Europe**. He declared:

> *"The time has come for us to raise the issue of Swaraj and submit our national demand to the British government in the form of an ultimatum..."*

He advocated:
- A **six-month ultimatum** to Britain to grant independence.
- If rejected → launch a **mass civil disobedience movement**.
- Take advantage of the **international crisis** to strive for independence.

**Gandhi's Counter-Position:**
- Neither the Congress nor the masses were ready for such an ultimatum.
- **Communal discord**, **class strife**, and **lack of unified vision** would undermine any movement.

**The Pant Resolution:**
- **Govind Ballabh Pant** moved a resolution reaffirming faith in **Gandhian policies** and asking Bose to nominate the working committee *"in accordance with the wishes of Gandhiji."*
- It was **passed without opposition** from the socialists or communists.
- **Why?** The Left realised that a split would reduce them to a splinter group — a united Congress under Gandhi was the best vehicle for national struggle.

**The Standoff:**
- Gandhi said he would NOT impose a working committee on the president — Bose should choose freely.
- Bose refused to nominate a new working committee.
- The fundamental problem: **Bose wanted an immediate struggle led by Gandhi; Gandhi was firm that the time was not ripe**.

Gandhi wrote to Bose:
> *"The views you express seem to be so diametrically opposed to those of the others and my own that I do not see any possibility of bridging them."*

**The Resolution:**
- Bose resigned from the presidency in **April 1939**.
- **Rajendra Prasad** was elected the new Congress president.
- The Congress crisis was **overcome for the present**.

---

### The Forward Bloc and Disciplinary Action

- In **May 1939**, Bose and his followers formed the **Forward Bloc** (at **Makur, Unnao**) as a new party **within the Congress**.
- When he gave a call for an all-India protest on **July 9** against an AICC resolution, the Congress Working Committee took disciplinary action.
- **August 1939:** Bose was:
  - **Removed from the post of president of the Bengal Provincial Congress Committee**
  - **Debarred from holding any elective office in the Congress for three years**

---

### The China Resolution at Tripuri

An important but often overlooked resolution at Tripuri expressed solidarity with China:

> *"The Congress sends its greetings to the people of China and its deepest sympathy in their trials and privations in their struggle against ruthless and inhuman imperialism. It congratulates them on their heroic resistance. The Congress expresses its approval of the sending of a Medical Mission on its behalf to the people of China..."*

## 🤝 Part 4: Gandhi and Bose — Mutual Respect Despite Ideological Differences

Despite their profound disagreements, Gandhi and Bose held **deep respect for each other**.

| Who Said It | What They Said |
|---|---|
| **Gandhi (1942)** | Called Bose the *"Prince among the Patriots"* |
| **Gandhi (on Bose's reported death)** | *"Netaji's patriotism is second to none... His bravery shines through all his actions. He aimed high and failed. But who has not failed."* |
| **Gandhi** | *"Netaji will remain immortal for all time to come for his service to India."* |
| **Bose (radio broadcast, Rangoon, 1944)** | Called Gandhi *"The Father of Our Nation"* — even while expressing his own conviction that force was the only way to win freedom |
| **Bose (on resigning at Tripuri)** | *"I would yield to none in my respect for his personality... it will be a tragic thing for me if I succeed in winning the confidence of other people but fail to win the confidence of India's greatest man."* |
| **Bose (later)** | *"The service which Mahatma Gandhi has rendered to India and to the cause of India's freedom is so unique and unparalleled that his name will be written in letters of gold in our National History—for all time."* |

**What They Shared:**
- Both considered **socialism to be the way forward** for India (though in different forms).
- Both were **religious men** and disliked communism.
- Both worked **against untouchability**.
- Both spoke for **women's emancipation**.

## 💡 Part 5: Gandhi vs. Bose — A Detailed Ideological Comparison

This is one of the most intellectually rich sections in the chapter. Understand it as a *structured contrast*.

---

### 1. Non-Violence vs. Militant Approach

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| Firm believer in **ahimsa and satyagraha** — non-violence as the *only* way | Believed Gandhi's non-violent strategy would be **inadequate to secure independence** |
| Objected to violence because (a) an unarmed mass had little chance in armed rebellion, and (b) violence creates more problems than it solves, leaving behind hatred and bitterness impossible to overcome through reconciliation | Believed **violent resistance alone** could oust alien imperialist rule from India |
| | Saw civil disobedience as effective in paralysing administration — but only if accompanied by a movement prepared to **use violence** for total revolution |

---

### 2. Means and Ends

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| **Purity of means** determines the ends — one cannot use any means to achieve even a desirable end | Had his **eye on the result** — whatever means were available to carry forward the struggle |
| Deep dislike for Fascists and Nazis — would not use them against Britain even in its difficulty | **Took help from Nazis, Fascists, and Imperial Japan** (the Axis Powers) — not from ideological kinship but revolutionary strategy (pragmatism) |
| *"The difference of outlook between him and me as to the means is too well known for comment."* | Openly criticised British hypocrisy — fighting for European freedom while denying independence to India |
| Saw Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan as **dangerous powers**, not just aggressors | Admired the Nazis and Fascists for their **discipline** — though he was against their racial superiority theory and suppression of democracy |

> **Note on Bose's pragmatism:** Bose's supporters argue his association with Germany and Japan was dictated by **revolutionary strategy, not ideological kinship**. He was against Fascist theory of racial superiority and Fascist acceptance of capitalism. He was not a Nazi or Fascist — he supported empowerment of women, secularism, and other liberal ideas.

---

### 3. Form of Government

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| His ideal state is laid out in **Hind Swaraj (1909)** — his nearest approach to sustained political theory | In early writings, supported **democracy** — but later veered towards the view that at least initially, an **authoritarian state** was needed for nation rebuilding and eradication of poverty |
| **Ramrajya** — a utopia needing no representative government, constitution, army, or police force | *"You cannot have a so-called democratic system, if that system has to put through economic reforms on a socialistic basis. Therefore we must have... a State of an authoritarian character..."* (Tokyo University address, 1944) |
| Believed in **stateless society** — increasing power of the state was his *greatest fear* | When he proclaimed the **Provisional Government of Azad Hind (October 21, 1943)**, he named himself head of state, prime minister, minister for war, and minister for foreign affairs — anticipated retaining the position in free India |
| *"I look upon an increase in the power of the state with greatest fear..."* | Called his ideology **'samyavada'** — "a synthesis of what modern Europe calls Socialism and Fascism" (1930 statement) |
| Society composed of **"innumerable, ever-widening, never-ascending village republics"** | After an initial stage of authoritarian rule, anticipated *"a new India and a happy India on the basis of the eternal principles of liberty, democracy and socialism"* |
| Each village a **self-sufficient republic/panchayat** of 5 elected persons (male and female) | |
| *"The ideal State... there is no political power because there is no State."* — Gandhi as **Judith Brown** describes him, is *"properly called an anarchist"* | |
| Democracy requires **high morality** — morality develops responsibility; responsibility protects rights | |
| Laid more emphasis on **duties** than on rights | |

---

### 4. Militarism

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| Against military on principle | **Deeply attracted to military discipline** — grateful for his training in the University Unit of the India Defence Force |
| **Ramrajya** built on truth, non-violence, and self-regulation would need no police or armies | Reviewed his "troops" in full dress uniform at the Calcutta Congress session (1930) — Gandhi and most supporters were uneasy with this display |
| War **demoralises** those trained for it; **brutalises** gentle men | Later formed the **Indian National Army (INA/Azad Hind Fauz)** and the **Rani of Jhansi Regiment** (women's regiment, 1943) |
| Causes of war: racialism, imperialism, fascism + economic inequality and exploitation | Commanded by **Captain Lakshmi Swaminathan (Sahgal after marriage)** |
| If these were eradicated, war would not be necessary | Women in INA received **same treatment as men** — no special privileges |
| Not against **defensive war** — if the innocent were attacked, defence was necessary; but military to be on a minimal scale | |

---

### 5. Ideas on Economy

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| **Decentralised economy without state control** | Economic freedom is the **essence of social and political freedom** |
| Dismissed both **capitalism** (exploitative excesses) and **Western socialism** (connected to industrialisation) | All in favour of **modernisation through industrialisation** |
| Both led people to crave luxury and self-indulgence | India's downfall caused by **inordinate belief in fate and the supernatural** + indifference to modern scientific developments |
| People should get rid of greed and make do with **bare necessities of life** | Backward agriculture had to be **modernised** — surplus agricultural labour could only be absorbed by **industrial development** |
| **Village Sarvodaya** — "back to the roots" — production simultaneous with consumption and distribution; no money economy | At **Haripura Congress session**: *comprehensive scheme of industrial development under state-ownership and state-control* was indispensable |
| Revival of **ancient village communities** — agriculture prosperous, industry decentralised through **small-scale cooperative organisations** | Called for setting up a **planning commission** to advise the national government |
| In a letter to **Henry Polak (1909)**: India's salvation lay in **unlearning** what was learnt — railways, telegraphs, hospitals, lawyers, doctors, and other modern trappings to be discarded | Called for **abolition of landlordism** and **liquidation of agricultural indebtedness** |
| Against **large-scale industrialisation** | Impressed by **Soviet Union's success** through rapid industrialisation |
| Strong objection to **labour-saving machinery** — *"Men go on saving labour, till thousands are without work and thrown on the open streets to die of starvation."* | Industrialisation would: solve unemployment, enable socialism, allow India to compete internationally, improve living standards |
| NOT against instruments saving **individual labour** — *"mechanisation is good when the hands are too few... It is an evil when there are more hands than required...as is the case in India"* | Classified industry into **heavy, medium, and cottage** categories — heavy industries as the backbone; fully aware of the importance of cottage industries |
| The capitalist who amassed wealth was a **thief** — inherited/earned wealth must be shared with society and spent on welfare of all | *"Industrialisation does not mean that we turn our back on cottage industries... we shall have to decide which industries should be developed on a cottage basis and which on a large-scale basis."* |
| **Theory of Trusteeship** — capitalists as trustees who take care of all; workers see capitalists as benefactors → mutual trust → economic equality | |

---

### 6. Religion

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| *"God is Truth and Love; God is ethics and morality; God is fearlessness... He is even the atheism of the atheist."* | Believed in **Upanishadic teachings**; revered the **Bhagavad Gita**; inspired by **Vivekananda** |
| **Primarily a man of religion** — truth and non-violence went beyond narrow sectarianism | Hindu spirituality formed the **essential part of his political and social thought** |
| No higher way of worshipping God than **serving the poor** and identifying God in them | Free of **bigotry and orthodoxy** |
| Different religions are **different paths towards the same destination** | Motivated Indians through **Hindu symbolisms** — but was not sectarian |
| All religions based on the same principles: **truth and love** | Named his force **Azad Hind Fauz** (Free India Army) — included many non-Hindus; common kitchen and common barracks broke caste bonds |
| Religion is a **binding force, not a dividing force** | Common celebrations of **all religious festivals** in the INA |
| Would not conceive of a **State without religion** | *"Free India must have an absolutely neutral and impartial attitude towards all religions and leave it to the choice of individuals..."* — **religion is a private matter; the State has nothing to do with it** |
| | *"Economic issues cut across communal divisions and barriers."* |

---

### 7. Caste and Untouchability

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| Three goals for society: (1) **Eradicating untouchability**, (2) **Maintaining varna distinctions**, (3) **Strengthening tolerance, modesty, and religiosity** | Looked forward to a **socialist revolution** ending traditional social hierarchy — in its place, an **egalitarian, casteless, and classless society** |
| Untouchability incompatible with Swaraj — *"if any Shastra propounded untouchability, that Shastra should be abandoned"* | **Completely rejected** the caste system and social inequality |
| Supported the **varna system** — believed laws of caste were eternal and the base for social harmony | Spoke in favour of **inter-caste marriages** |
| In Gandhi's idealised village, each would be organised around four-fold divisions with **complete system of reciprocity** | Inspired by **Vivekananda** — progress of India possible only with uplift of the downtrodden and so-called untouchables |
| No one would feel differences in status due to complete reciprocity | Spoke **vehemently against untouchability** in public speeches |

---

### 8. Women

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| *"To call women the weaker sex is a libel; it is man's injustice to women."* | Women are the **equals of men** — should be prepared to **fight and sacrifice** for India's freedom |
| Brought women out of their homes and **purdah** into the freedom struggle — considered the **most radical of his ideas** | Arduous campaigner to bring women more fully into national life |
| Opposed **child marriage, dowry system, female infanticide, and treatment of widows** | Presidential address, **Maharashtra Provincial Conference (May 1928)**: *"The status of women should be raised... it is impossible for one half of the nation to win freedom without the active sympathy and support of the other half."* |
| Considered men and women **equal** | As Congress President (1938), set up a **separate planning commission for women** — chaired by **Rani Lakshmi Bhai Rajawade** |
| However, by present standards, he would be considered **patriarchal and traditional** in his view of gender roles — woman as *"presiding deity of the home"*; passive vs. man's active | In **1943**, called on women to serve as **soldiers in the Indian National Army** |
| Wrote (1937): *"I do believe that woman will not make her contribution to the world by mimicking or running a race with man."* | Formed the **Rani of Jhansi Regiment** (1943) — commanded by **Captain Lakshmi Swaminathan (Sahgal)** |
| Wrote (1940): *"She is passive, he is active. She is essentially mistress of the house."* | Majority trained as soldiers; those less suited to combat as nurses/support roles |
| His ideal woman drawn from the figure of **Sita** — virtuous, faithful wife bearing injustice patiently and bravely (**Judith Brown**) | Women received **same treatment as men** — no special privileges |
| Also said (1940): *"domestic slavery of woman is a symbol of our barbarism, and she should be freed from this incubus"* | Believed in **female emancipation** from age-old bondage — social, economic, and political |
| | Supported **abolition of purdah** and **widow remarriage** |
| | Wanted women to get **all-round education** including physical and vocational training, and to be made conscious of **social and legal rights** |

---

### 9. Education

| Gandhi | Bose |
|---|---|
| Against **English system of education** and English as medium of instruction — wanted education in **vernacular** | For **higher education, especially in technical and scientific fields** — wanted an industrial India |
| Advocated **free and compulsory education** for all boys and girls between **7 and 14 years** | *"National Reconstruction will be possible only with the aid of science and our scientists."* |
| Education should be an **integrated approach** to full development of personality — physical training + high moral principles + intellectual development | Wanted Indian students sent **abroad for training** according to a clear plan so they could immediately contribute to building new industries on return |
| Distinguished between **learning and education, knowledge and wisdom, literacy and lessons of life** | |
| *"Literacy in itself is no education."* — morality must be a part of education | |
| Education drawn from Plato — a means of attaining **knowledge and wisdom** placing the seeker on a spiritual path | |
| Conceived **Nai Talim (basic education, 1937)** — freedom from ignorance, illiteracy, superstition, psyche of servitude | |
| Nai Talim: **holistic training of mind and body** — academics + purposeful manual labour; handicrafts, art, and drawing as fundamental teaching tools | |
| Emphasised **vocational education** to make villages self-sufficient | |

## 🌍 Modern Context — Gandhi-Bose Ideological Comparison

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. Bose's Economic Vision — Vindicated by History
- Bose's advocacy for **state-led industrialisation, heavy industries, and a planning commission** was largely what independent India adopted under Nehru's **Five-Year Plans (1951 onwards)**.
- `[Modern Example]` The **NITI Aayog's Vision 2047** document (2023) and India's **National Manufacturing Policy** — including the **PLI (Production Linked Incentive) Scheme (2020–present)** across 14 sectors — reflect Bose's vision of India as an industrial powerhouse. India's manufacturing sector is targeted to reach **25% of GDP by 2025** under Make in India, directly echoing what Bose argued for at Haripura in 1938.

### 2. Gandhi's Decentralised Village Economy — Selectively Revived
- `[Modern Example]` Gandhi's vision of village-based, decentralised, self-sufficient economies finds partial expression in schemes like the **PM SVANidhi Scheme, MGNREGA (rural employment guarantee), and the Gram Swaraj Mission**. The **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020's** emphasis on **vocational training, mother tongue medium instruction**, and holistic development directly mirrors Gandhi's **Nai Talim** philosophy.

### 3. Women in Combat Roles — Bose's Legacy Fulfilled
- Bose's radical 1943 decision to allow women to serve as soldiers in the Rani of Jhansi Regiment is now Indian policy.
- `[Modern Example]` In **2022**, the Supreme Court ruled (in *Agnipath scheme challenges*) that women could be inducted into combat roles in the Indian Army. The **Agniveer Scheme (2022)** has inducted women into combat support roles for the first time. The Indian Air Force inducted its **first women fighter pilots** in 2016, and the Indian Navy deployed its **first women naval officers on warships** in 2022 — a direct continuation of what Bose first implemented in 1943.

## 🌍 Part 6: The Second World War — India's Nationalist Response

### Germany Attacks Poland — War Begins

- **September 1, 1939:** Germany attacked Poland — triggering the Second World War.
- **September 3, 1939:** Britain declared war against Germany.
- The **British Government of India declared India's support for the war** — **without consulting Indian opinion**.

---

### Congress Offers Conditional Support

Though angered by the unilateral action, Congress decided to conditionally support the war effort. The Congress's hostility to Fascism, Nazism, militarism, and imperialism had been **more consistent than the British record**.

**Two Basic Conditions:**
1. After the war, a **constituent assembly** should be convened to determine the political structure of a free India.
2. Immediately, some form of **genuinely responsible government** should be established at the Centre.

**The offer was rejected by Viceroy Linlithgow.**

---

### CWC Meeting at Wardha (September 10–14, 1939)

The official Congress position was debated at the **Wardha session of the Congress Working Committee**. Four distinct positions emerged:

| Leader | Position |
|---|---|
| **Gandhi** | **Unconditional support** to Allied powers — clear distinction between democratic Western Europe and totalitarian Nazis/Fascists; *"I am not willing to embarrass the British government during the war."* |
| **Subhas Bose** + **Acharya Narendra Dev** + **Jayaprakash Narayan** | **No sympathy for either side** — both sides were imperialists fighting to protect/expand colonies. This was the ideal time to **launch a civil disobedience movement** and seize freedom |
| **Jawaharlal Nehru** | Recognised the difference between democratic values and fascism — justice was on the side of Britain, France, and Poland. BUT Britain and France were imperialist powers and *"the war was the result of the inner contradictions of capitalism maturing since the end of World War I."* Therefore: **no Indian participation** until India was free — but also **no advantage** to be taken of Britain's difficulty by starting immediate civil disobedience |
| **Final Decision** | Gandhi was largely isolated; **Nehru's position was adopted** by the CWC |

---

### The CWC Resolution

The CWC resolution:
- **Condemned Fascist aggression**.
- Stated that India could not be party to a war being fought for **democratic freedom** when that freedom was being denied to India.
- If Britain was truly fighting for democracy and freedom, it must **end imperialism in its colonies** and establish full democracy in India.
- The government should **declare its war aims** and explain how principles of democracy would be applied to India after the war.

---

### Government Attitude — Linlithgow's Statement (October 17, 1939)

The government's response was **entirely negative**. Viceroy Linlithgow tried to **use the Muslim League and the princes against the Congress**. He:

- Refused to define British war aims beyond stating that **Britain was resisting aggression**.
- Said it would consult *"representatives of several communities, parties and interests in India, and the Indian princes"* as to how the Act of 1935 might be modified.
- Said it would set up a **"consultative committee"** whose advice could be sought when required.

---

### The British Hidden Agenda

Linlithgow's statement was not an aberration — it was part of a **calculated British strategy**:

- Take advantage of the war to **regain lost ground from the Congress**.
- Provoke the Congress into confrontation, then use the extraordinary situation to acquire **draconian powers**.
- Even before war, **emergency powers** had been acquired for the Centre by amending the 1935 Act.
- **Defence of India Ordinance** was enforced the day war was declared — restricting civil liberties.
- In **May 1940**, a top-secret **Draft Revolutionary Movement Ordinance** was prepared — aimed at **pre-emptive strikes on the Congress**.
- The government could then call upon **Allied troops stationed in India**.
- Also, it could win **liberal and leftist sympathy worldwide** by painting the Congress as **pro-Japan and pro-Germany**.
- These policies received full support from **Prime Minister Winston Churchill** and **Secretary of State Zetland**, who branded the Congress as a **"purely Hindu organisation"**.

Gandhi reacted sharply:
> *"...there is to be no democracy for India if Britain can prevent it."*

---

### Congress Ministries Resign (October 1939)

**October 23, 1939 — CWC Meeting:**
- Rejected the viceregal statement as a **reiteration of the old imperialist policy**.
- Decided **not to support the war**.
- Called upon the **Congress ministries to resign in the provinces**.

---

### Debate on Immediate Mass Satyagraha

After Linlithgow's October 1939 statement, a debate on immediate mass struggle began.

**Gandhi and supporters opposed immediate struggle because:**
- The **Allied cause was just**.
- **Communal sensitiveness and lack of Hindu-Muslim unity** could result in riots.
- The **Congress organisation was in shambles**.
- The **masses were not ready**.

**Instead they advocated:**
- Toning up the Congress organisation.
- Political work among the masses.
- Negotiating until all possibilities of a negotiated settlement were exhausted.

---

### Allahabad CWC Resolution (November 1939)

The CWC passed a resolution observing that the war was being carried on for **imperialist ends** and British imperialism was to remain entrenched in India. The Congress could not associate with such a war.

It reiterated: India's independence and the right of Indians to frame their constitution through a **constituent assembly** should be recognised — only through such an assembly could communal and other problems be tackled.

---

### Ramgarh Session (March 1940) — Maulana Abul Kalam Azad Presides

- All agreed that a battle must be waged — but there was **disagreement over the form**.
- It was decided to **leave the form and timing to Gandhi**.
- Gandhi still favoured continued cooperation at the provincial level and offered **moral support to Britain on a non-violent basis**.
- **Nehru** reiterated that complete independence must be a **precondition** for Congress support.
- **Bose** continued with his strong militant stand — *"Britain's difficulty is India's opportunity."*

**Final Congress Declaration at Ramgarh:**
- The people of India would accept **nothing short of complete independence**.
- Indian freedom could not be within the imperial structure.
- **Sovereignty must rest with the people** in states and provinces.
- *"Congress would resort to civil disobedience as soon as the Congress organisation is considered fit enough or if circumstances precipitate a crisis."*

---

## 🕌 Part 7: The Pakistan Resolution — Lahore (March 1940)

The **Muslim League passed its landmark resolution** calling for:

> Grouping of **geographically contiguous areas** where Muslims are in majority (North-West and East) into **independent states** in which constituent units shall be **autonomous and sovereign**, with **adequate safeguards to Muslims** where they are in minority.

> ⚠️ This was a decisive turning point — the formal articulation of the demand for a separate Muslim nation.

---

## 📜 Part 8: The August Offer (August 1940)

Hitler's stunning successes and the fall of **Belgium, Holland, and France** put England in a conciliatory mood.

The dominant Congress leadership was again in a dilemma — both Gandhi and Nehru strongly opposed the idea of taking advantage of Britain's desperate position.

**Viceroy Linlithgow's August Offer proposed:**
- **Dominion status** as the objective for India.
- **Expansion of the Viceroy's Executive Council** — majority of Indians drawn from major political parties.
- Setting up of a **constituent assembly after the war** where Indians would decide the constitution according to their social, economic, and political conceptions — subject to obligations regarding defence, minority rights, treaties with states, All India services.
- **No future constitution to be adopted without consent of minorities.**

### Responses

| Party | Response |
|---|---|
| **Congress** | **Rejected** the August Offer. Nehru: *"Dominion status concept is dead as a doornail."* Gandhi: "The declaration has widened the gulf between the nationalists and the British rulers." |
| **Muslim League** | **Welcomed** the veto assurance given to the League; reiterated that **partition was the only solution** to the deadlock |

### Evaluation

Despite rejection, this offer was significant:
- For the first time, the **inherent right of Indians to frame their constitution was recognised**.
- The Congress demand for a **constituent assembly was conceded**.
- **Dominion status was explicitly offered**.

**July 1941 Developments:**
- Viceroy's executive council was enlarged — **Indians given a majority of 8 out of 12** for the first time.
- But British retained charge of **defence, finance, and home**.
- A **National Defence Council** was set up — with purely **advisory functions**.

---

## ✊ Part 9: Individual Satyagraha (Late 1940)

The government's adamant position — no constitutional advance until Congress agreed with Muslim leaders — left Congress with no option.

### Why Individual Satyagraha?

Towards the end of 1940, Congress again asked Gandhi to take command. Gandhi initiated a **limited satyagraha on an individual basis**.

**Three Aims:**
1. To show that **nationalist patience was not due to weakness**.
2. To express that people were **not interested in the war** and made no distinction between Nazism and the **"double autocracy"** ruling India.
3. To give the government **another opportunity** to accept Congress demands peacefully.

**The Method:**
- The satyagrahi would make an **anti-war declaration** asserting freedom of speech against the war.
- If not arrested, they would repeat it and begin a march towards Delhi — the **'Delhi Chalo Movement'**.

**Key Participants:**
- **Vinoba Bhave** was the **first** to offer satyagraha.
- **Nehru** was the **second**.
- By **May 1941**, **25,000 people** had been convicted for individual civil disobedience.

---

## 👑 Part 10: Gandhi Designates Nehru as His Successor

Congress leaders were released in **December 1941** — just as Japan began its aggressive actions in Asia.

The **CWC overrode Gandhi's and Nehru's objections** and passed a resolution offering to cooperate with the government in India's defence, subject to:
1. Full independence after the war.
2. Substance of power transferred immediately.

It was at this crucial time that **Gandhi designated Nehru as his chosen successor**.

### Why Nehru Despite Their Differences?

Gandhi and Nehru differed deeply:

| Area | Gandhi | Nehru |
|---|---|---|
| Religion | Believed deeply in his own version of God | Indifferent to religion |
| Economy | Revival of rural economy | Industrialisation as the solution to poverty |
| State power | Sceptical of state power | Believed in the modern state's power to reform society |

Yet, Gandhi chose Nehru. Why? As **Rajmohan Gandhi** writes in *The Good Boatman*:

> Gandhi preferred Nehru to the alternatives — **Patel, Rajaji, Azad, Kripalani, Rajendra Prasad** — because they had **somewhat sectional interests and affiliations**.

> But **Nehru was a Hindu who could be trusted by Muslims, a north-Indian respected in south India, and a man admired by women**.

**What They Shared (Despite Differences):**
- **Patriotism in an inclusive sense** — identified with India as a whole, not with a particular caste, language, region, or religion.
- Both believed in **non-violence** and **democratic government**.
- Both were genuinely **all-India leaders** who gave Indians hope.

## ✈️ Part 11: The Cripps Mission (March 1942)

### Why Was the Mission Sent?

Three reasons:
1. Britain suffered **serious reverses in South-East Asia** — the Japanese threat to invade India seemed real; **Indian support became crucial**.
2. **Pressure from the Allies** (USA, USSR, China) to seek Indian cooperation.
3. Indian nationalists had agreed to support the Allied cause if **substantial power was transferred immediately** and complete independence given after the war.

**The Envoy:** Stafford Cripps — a **left-wing Labourite**, leader of the House of Commons, member of the British War Cabinet, who had **actively supported the Indian national movement**.

---

### Main Proposals of the Cripps Mission

1. An **Indian Union with Dominion status** would be set up — free to decide its relations with the Commonwealth and free to participate in the **United Nations** and other international bodies.
2. After the war, a **constituent assembly** would be convened. Members would be:
   - Partly **elected by provincial assemblies** through **proportional representation**
   - Partly **nominated by the princes**
3. The British government would accept the new constitution subject to two conditions:
   - (i) Any province **not willing to join the Union** could have a **separate constitution and form a separate Union** — a blueprint for Partition.
   - (ii) The constitution-making body and British government would negotiate a **treaty** for transfer of power and protection of racial/religious minorities.
4. Meanwhile, **defence of India would remain in British hands** and the **Governor General's powers would remain intact**.

---

### What Was New — Departures from the Past

| New Feature | Significance |
|---|---|
| Constitution-making **solely in Indian hands** (not "mainly" as in August Offer) | Major concession |
| A **concrete plan** for the constituent assembly | More specific than before |
| Option for any province to **have a separate constitution** | Blueprint for Partition |
| Free India could **withdraw from the Commonwealth** | Major concession |
| Indians allowed a **large share in administration** in the interim period | |

---

### Why the Cripps Mission Failed

**The Congress objected to:**
1. Offer of **dominion status** instead of complete independence.
2. Representation of princely states by **nominees** and not **elected representatives**.
3. Right of provinces to **secede** — against the principle of national unity.
4. Absence of any plan for **immediate transfer of power**.
5. Absence of any real **share in defence** — Governor General's supremacy retained; demand that he be only a constitutional head was not accepted.

> **Nehru and Maulana Azad** were the official negotiators for the Congress.

**The Muslim League objected to:**
1. The idea of a **single Indian Union**.
2. The **machinery for the constituent assembly** and the procedure for accession of provinces.
3. The proposals denied Muslims the right to **self-determination and the creation of Pakistan**.

**Other Groups' Objections:**

| Group | Objection |
|---|---|
| **Liberals** | Secession proposals against **unity and security of India** |
| **Hindu Mahasabha** | Criticised the **basis of the right to secede** |
| **Depressed Classes** | Partition would leave them at the **mercy of caste Hindus** |
| **Sikhs** | Partition would take away **Punjab** from them |

**Procedural Problems:**
- The explanation that proposals were meant to **clothe the August Offer's general provisions with precision** cast doubts on British intentions.
- Cripps had earlier talked of "**cabinet**" and "**national government**" but later said he had only meant an **expansion of the executive council** — seen as backtracking.
- The procedure of accession was **not well-defined**: secession required a **60 per cent majority** in the legislature; if less than 60 per cent, a **plebiscite of adult males** by a simple majority. This **weighed against Hindus in Punjab and Bengal** if they wanted to join the Indian Union.
- It was not clear **who would implement and interpret** the treaty for transfer of power.
- **Churchill, Amery, Linlithgow**, and **Ward** consistently **torpedoed Cripps' efforts**.

**Famous Quotes on the Cripps Mission:**

> *"I have not become His Majesty's first Minister to preside over the liquidation of the British Empire."*
> — **Winston Churchill**

> *"The offer of Cripps really gave us nothing... War had given India an opportunity for achieving her freedom. We must not lose it by depending upon a mere promise."*
> — **Maulana Abul Kalam Azad**

> *"A post-dated cheque on a crashing bank."*
> — **Gandhi** (describing the Cripps scheme — the phrase actually attributed to Gandhi)

> *"The existing structure and autocratic powers would remain and a few of us will become the viceroy's liveried camp followers and look after canteens and the like."*
> — **Nehru**

Talks broke down on the question of the **Viceroy's veto**.

Stafford Cripps returned home, leaving behind a **frustrated and embittered Indian people** who felt the existing situation had become intolerable and that **the time had come for a final assault on imperialism**.

## 🌍 Modern Context — WWII and India's Nationalist Response

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. India's Wartime Demand for a Constituent Assembly — Fulfilled
- The Congress's consistent demand for a **constituent assembly elected on adult franchise** was finally realised with the **Constituent Assembly convened in December 1946** and completing the Constitution by **November 26, 1949**.
- `[Modern Example]` The ongoing debates about **constitutional amendments and the Basic Structure Doctrine** (affirmed by the Supreme Court in *Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala, 1973*, and reaffirmed in multiple subsequent judgments) reflect the enduring relevance of the question the Congress first raised in 1939: **who has the legitimate right to make and change India's constitution?**

### 2. Pakistan Resolution's Legacy
- The **Muslim League's Pakistan Resolution (Lahore, March 1940)** set in motion events that led to the **Partition of August 1947**.
- `[Modern Example]` In 2024, Pakistan and India remain the two most significant products of that resolution, with their relationship deeply shaped by its legacy. The **Kartarpur Corridor (opened 2019)** — allowing Indian Sikhs to visit Gurdwara Darbar Sahib in Pakistan — is a rare example of both nations finding common ground across the Partition's wounds.

### 3. The "Post-Dated Cheque" Problem — Promises Without Timelines
- Gandhi's description of the Cripps proposals as a "post-dated cheque" resonates powerfully in contemporary democratic discourse about promises without clear implementation timelines.
- `[Modern Example]` The critique of **electoral promises without timelines** (like the demand for a legal MSP guarantee for farmers, or universal healthcare) mirrors exactly what Gandhi identified as the flaw in the Cripps offer — promises that sound good but have no binding mechanism for delivery.

### 4. Bose's INA and Recognition
- `[Modern Example]` The **Indian government's commemoration of Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose's 125th birth anniversary (2021–22)** as part of **Amrit Mahotsav** — including the installation of his holographic statue (later permanent granite statue) at **India Gate in September 2022** — reflects the growing national recognition of Bose's contribution, resolving the long historical tension between Congress's treatment of Bose and his actual legacy.
