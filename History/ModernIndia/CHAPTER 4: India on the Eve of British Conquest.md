# 📖 Chapter 4: India on the Eve of British Conquest
### *Unit 2 — Advent of Europeans and Consolidation of British Power in India*

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 big ideas of this chapter.**

1. **The Mughal Empire collapsed from within** — weak rulers after Aurangzeb, a Jagirdari crisis, shifting zamindari loyalties, and constant wars of succession made the empire hollow before any foreign power struck.
2. **External invasions (Nadir Shah in 1739, Ahmad Shah Abdali repeatedly 1748–1767) delivered the killing blows** to an already weakened empire.
3. **Three types of regional states emerged** in the power vacuum — Successor States (Hyderabad, Bengal, Awadh), Independent Kingdoms (Mysore, Rajput states), and New States (Marathas, Sikhs, Jats).
4. **India's economy was not primitive** — it was a globally connected trade powerhouse in textiles, ship-building, and agriculture. But it was technologically stagnant and socially fragmented.
5. **Social divisions — caste, religion, region, and gender inequality** — prevented any unified resistance. This fragmentation is what ultimately made British conquest possible.

---

## 🔑 Setting the Scene: The Mughal Twilight

The first half of the **18th century** saw the dramatic **decline of the mighty Mughal Empire** — once the envy of the world for nearly two centuries. The reign of **Aurangzeb (1658–1707)** marked the beginning of the end.

Historians argue that:
- **Aurangzeb's misguided policies** weakened the stability of the state.
- After his death, **wars of succession and weak rulers** accelerated the decline.
- **Muhammad Shah ruled for 29 years (1719–48)** — a long reign — but was an **incompetent ruler** who could not revive imperial fortunes.

### What Happened During Muhammad Shah's Reign?
- Independent states of **Hyderabad, Bengal, Awadh, and Punjab** were established.
- Several **local chiefs asserted their independence**.
- The **Marathas began making their bid** to inherit the imperial mantle.

# 🏰 PART 1: CHALLENGES BEFORE THE MUGHALS

---

## 1.1 External Challenges: Invasions from the North-West

The **northwestern borders had been neglected** by the later Mughals — very little effort was spent protecting them. This left the empire dangerously exposed.

---

### Nadir Shah's Invasion (1738–39)

- **Nadir Shah**, the Persian emperor, **attacked India in 1738–39**.
- He **conquered Lahore** and **defeated the Mughal army at Karnal on February 13, 1739**.
- **Muhammad Shah was captured** and Delhi was **looted and devastated**.
- Nadir Shah's loot included:
  - The famous **Peacock Throne**
  - The **Kohinoor diamond**
  - An estimated **seventy crore rupees** from the official treasury and the safes of rich nobles
- Nadir Shah acquired the **strategically important Mughal territory to the west of the Indus**, including Kabul.
- India was now again **vulnerable to attacks from the north-west**.

> 💬 *Jon Wilson writes in "India Conquered": "Nader Shah was Mughal emperor for only fifty-seven days, in 1739, but those days created aftershocks that transformed India's politics... Those fifty-seven days laid the ground which allowed the East India Company to conquer territory in India for the first time."*

---

### Ahmad Shah Abdali's Invasions (1748–1767)

- **Ahmad Shah Abdali (or Ahmad Shah Durrani)** was elected successor of Nadir Shah after the latter's death in **1747**.
- He **invaded India several times between 1748 and 1767**.
- The Mughals tried to **buy peace in 1751–52 by ceding Punjab** to him.
- In **1757**, Abdali **captured Delhi** and left behind an **Afghan caretaker** to watch over the Mughal emperor.
- Before leaving, he recognised:
  - **Alamgir II** as the Mughal emperor
  - **Rohilla chief Najib-ud-Daula** as **Mir Bakhshi** (a Mughal administrative title — head of military accounts) of the empire, to act as Abdali's personal 'supreme agent'
- In **1758**, **Najib-ud-Daula was expelled from Delhi** by **Maratha chief Raghunath Rao**, who also **captured Punjab**.
- In **1759**, Ahmad Shah Abdali **returned to India to avenge the Marathas**.
- In **1761**, Abdali **defeated the Marathas in the Third Battle of Panipat**.
- The **last of Abdali's invasions came in 1767**.

---

## 📌 Special Feature: Why Were So Many Empire-Shaking Battles Fought at Panipat?

**Panipat** and its surrounding region — located in **present Haryana on the banks of the Yamuna**, between the fertile plains of the Ganga and Indus rivers — witnessed several battles that **changed the course of Indian history**.

### The Three Battles of Panipat:

| Battle | Year | Fought Between | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First Battle of Panipat** | 1526 | Babur vs. Ibrahim Lodi | Foundation of the Mughal Empire; end of Delhi Sultanate |
| **Second Battle of Panipat** | 1556 | Akbar vs. Hemu | Confirmed continuation of Mughal rule |
| **Third Battle of Panipat** | 1761 | Marathas vs. Ahmad Shah Abdali | End of Maratha ambition to rule all of India |

### Why Was Panipat Always Chosen as the Battlefield?

- **Strategic location:** Invaders coming from the north/northwest through the **Khyber Pass** needed to reach Delhi (the political capital of northern India). Moving armies through the **deserts of Rajasthan** or **dense forest areas** was risky. Panipat was a safer, more accessible confrontation point.
- **Proximity to Delhi:** Easier for Indian rulers to **transport weapons, military personnel, and food supplies** to the battlefield while keeping the capital insulated from the conflict.
- **Flat terrain:** Panipat's surrounding region has **flat ground** — ideal for **cavalry movement**, which was the primary mode of warfare at the time.
- **Grand Trunk Road:** After **Sher Shah Suri built the Grand Trunk Road (1540–45)**, Panipat was directly on this route — easier for armies to reach.
- **Short monsoon season:** The duration of monsoon rainfall in the region is **shorter than other areas**, making military campaigns easier.
- **Local artisan expertise:** The **smiths and artisans of the region** were experts in making warfare materials — both armies could **replenish their war supplies** locally.

## 1.2 Internal Challenges: The Parade of Weak Rulers After Aurangzeb

---

### Bahadur Shah I (ruled 1707–1712)

- After a **nearly two-year-long war of succession**, the **63-year-old Prince Mu'azzam** (Aurangzeb's eldest son) became emperor with the title **Bahadur Shah** (later called **Bahadur Shah I**).
- He **killed his brothers Muhammad Azam and Kam Bakhsh** in the war of succession.
- Historian **Khafi Khan** gave him the title of **'Shahi-Bekhabar'** (the unaware/oblivious king).
- He adopted a **pacific (peaceful) policy** with the **Marathas, Rajputs, and Jats**.
- **Shahu** (the Maratha prince) was **released from Mughal captivity**.
- **Rajput chiefs were confirmed in their respective states**.
- However, **Sikh leader Banda Bahadur** attacked Muslims in Punjab — forcing the emperor to take military action.
- Bahadur Shah I **died in February 1712**.

> 💬 *Historian Sidney Owen writes: "He (Bahadur Shah I) was the last Emperor of whom anything favourable can be said. Henceforth, the rapid and complete abasement and practical dissolution of the Empire are typified in the incapacity and political insignificance of its sovereigns."*

---

### Jahandar Shah (ruled 1712–1713)

- Became emperor with the help of **Zulfikar Khan**, who was appointed **prime minister**.
- Zulfikar Khan introduced the **izara system** (a revenue farming system — leasing out land revenue collection rights to the highest bidder) to improve the empire's finances.
- Jahandar Shah **abolished Jaziya** (a tax on non-Muslims — one of Aurangzeb's most resented policies).

---

### Farrukhsiyar (ruled 1713–1719)

- Came to power by **killing Jahandar Shah** with the help of the **Sayyid brothers — Abdullah Khan and Hussain Ali** (known as **'King Makers'**).
- Followed a **policy of religious tolerance** — abolished **Jaziya and pilgrimage tax**.
- In **1717**, gave **farmans (royal decrees) to the British** — the famous "Magna Carta of the Company" (discussed in Chapter 3).
- In **1719**, the **Sayyid brothers, with the help of Peshwa Balaji Vishwanath**, **dethroned Farrukhsiyar**.
- He was **blinded and killed** — the **first time in Mughal history that an emperor was killed by his own nobles**.

---

### Rafi-ud-Darajat (ruled Feb. 28 – June 6, 1719)

- Ruled for the **shortest period among all Mughal emperors**.

---

### Rafi-ud-Daula (ruled June 6 – Sep. 17, 1719)

- Placed on the throne by the **Sayyid brothers** with the title **Shah Jahan II**.
- Was an **opium addict**.

---

### Muhammad Shah (ruled 1719–1748)

- After Rafi-ud-Daula's death, **Raushan Akhtar** was chosen by the Sayyid Brothers.
- Known in history as **Muhammad Shah "Rangeela"** — a title given due to his **luxurious lifestyle**.
- With the help of **Nizam-ul-Mulk**, he **killed the Sayyid Brothers**.
- In **1724**, **Nizam-ul-Mulk became the wazir** and **founded the independent state of Hyderabad** — a major sign of imperial disintegration.
- In **1737**, **Baji Rao I** (the Maratha Peshwa) **invaded Delhi with a small army of 500 horsemen** — a stunning show of Mughal vulnerability.
- In **1739**, **Nadir Shah defeated the Mughals** at the **Battle of Karnal**, imprisoned Muhammad Shah, and **annexed areas west of the Indus** into the Persian empire.

---

### Ahmad Shah Bahadur (ruled 1748–1754)

- An **incompetent ruler** who left state affairs in the hands of **Udham Bai, the 'Queen Mother'**.
- Udham Bai, given the title **Qibla-i-Alam**, was described as a "lady of poor intellect" who ruled with the help of her paramour, **Javid Khan** (a notorious eunuch).

---

### Alamgir II (ruled 1754–1759)

- A son of Emperor **Jahandar Shah**.
- **Ahmed Shah Abdali** (here referred to as the Iranian invader) reached Delhi in **January 1757**.
- During his reign, the **Battle of Plassey** was fought in **June 1757**.
- **Alamgir II was assassinated**.

---

### Shah Jahan III (ruled 1759–1760)

- Also known as **Muhi-ul-millat**.
- Placed on the throne due to intrigues in Delhi but was later **deposed through Maratha intervention**.

---

### Shah Alam II (ruled Oct. 1760–July 1788; Oct. 1788–Nov. 1806)

- His reign saw two **decisive battles**:
  - **Third Battle of Panipat (1761)**
  - **Battle of Buxar (1764)**
- In **1765**, under the **Treaty of Allahabad (August 1765)**, he was **taken under the East India Company's protection** and resided at Allahabad.
- He issued a **farman granting the Company in perpetuity the Diwani** (the right to collect revenue) of **Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa** — a watershed moment in British conquest.
- In **1772**, the **Marathas took him to Delhi** where he lived till **1803**.
- In **1803**, after the **defeat of Daulat Rao Scindia by the English**, he again **accepted the protection of the English**.
- From this point, the **Mughal emperor became the pensioner (dependent person receiving allowance) of the English**.

---

### Akbar Shah II (ruled 1806–1837)

- He gave the title of **'Raja' to Rammohan Roy**.
- In **1835**, the **East India Company discontinued calling itself a subject of the Mughal emperor** and **stopped issuing coins in the name of the Mughal emperor** — a formal symbolic break.

---

### Bahadur Shah II / Bahadur Shah Zafar (ruled 1837–1857)

- The **last Mughal emperor** ('Zafar' being his pen name).
- The **Revolt of 1857** made a futile attempt to declare him the **Emperor of India**.
- He was **captured by the English and sent to Rangoon (present-day Yangon, Myanmar)**, where he **died in 1862**.
- In **legal terms**, the Mughal Empire **officially ended on November 1, 1858** with the **declaration of Queen Victoria**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **Legacy of the Last Mughal:**
> Bahadur Shah Zafar died in Rangoon in 1862 and was buried there. His tomb in **Yangon (Myanmar)** remains a site of historical significance. In recent years, there have been diplomatic discussions between India and Myanmar about **preserving and promoting the tomb** as a heritage site. The **Archaeological Survey of India (ASI)** has been involved in conversations about restoration, reflecting how this chapter of history continues to have diplomatic relevance today. `[Modern Example]`

# 📉 PART 2: CAUSES OF DECLINE OF THE MUGHAL EMPIRE

---

## 2.1 The Debate Among Historians

Why the Mughal Empire declined is a **subject of ongoing debate**. Scholarly opinion falls into two broad camps:

- **Empire-related (Mughal-centric) view:** The causes lie within the **structure and functioning of the empire itself**.
- **Region-related view:** The causes lie in the **turmoil and instability in different parts** of the empire.

In reality, **both aspects contributed** to the decline.

> 🔑 **Key Point:** The process of disintegration **began during Aurangzeb's reign** but **gained momentum only after his death in 1707**. At the time of his death, the decline was **not yet irreversible** — capable successors could have checked it. But they never came.

---

## 2.2 Major Causes of Decline (Complete List)

### (i) Weak Successors
- The Mughal government was a **personal despotism** (a system where all power rests with one ruler) — its success depended entirely on the character of the reigning emperor.
- The later Mughals were **incapable, weak, and licentious** (immoral/indulgent) monarchs who **neglected administration** and hastened disintegration.

### (ii) Absence of a Definite Law of Succession
- There was **no law of primogeniture** (the right of the firstborn to inherit) — so every emperor's death triggered a **brutal war of succession**.
- This **fostered partisanship at the cost of patriotism** — nobles aligned with different princes instead of serving the empire.

### (iii) Aurangzeb's Religious and Deccan Policies
- His **religious policy antagonised the Rajputs, Sikhs, Jats, and Marathas** — turning the most warlike communities against the empire.
- His **Deccan policy was a complete failure** — it kept the emperor away from the capital for a long duration, draining the treasury and exhausting the army.

### (iv) Degeneration of Rulers and Nobles
- The degeneration of rulers **led directly to the degeneration of the nobility** — factious quarrels and intrigues at court cost the empire dearly.

### (v) Deterioration of the Army
- The army — once a formidable force — deteriorated in quality, discipline, and innovation.

> 💬 *William Irvine writes: "The more I study the period, the more I am convinced that military inefficiency was the principal, if not the sole, cause of that empire's final collapse."*

### (vi) Empire Too Vast to Govern
- The empire had become **too vast and unwieldy** to be efficiently governed from a central authority — especially under weak rulers and with the existing poor conditions of **transport and communication**.

### (vii) External Invasions
- Invasions of **Irani (Nadir Shah)** and **Durrani (Ahmad Shah Abdali) kingdoms** gave a **death blow** to the Mughal empire.

### (viii) Economic Decline
- **Endless wars**, stagnation in agriculture, and decline in trade emptied the royal treasury.
- There was **no significant scientific or technological advance** to improve the stagnant economy.
- **European traders** were making inroads along coastal India, diverting trade revenues.

### (ix) Advent of Europeans
- European companies **interfered in native politics**, hastening the disintegration of the empire.

### (x) Shifting Allegiance of Zamindars
- **Zamindars** (hereditary landowners — the backbone of local administration and revenue collection) — known variously as **rais, rajas, thakurs, khuts, or deshmukhs** — enjoyed hereditary privileges.
- They helped collect revenue and maintained soldiers for local administration.
- Though the Mughals had tried to curb zamindar power and maintain direct contact with peasants, they had **not fully succeeded**.
- During Aurangzeb's reign, there was a **marked increase in zamindar power** — encouraging **regional loyalties** over imperial loyalty.
- Many zamindars helped the nobility **carve out independent kingdoms**.

### (xi) The Jagirdari Crisis
- The **nobility** (comprising those assigned **jagirs — land grants from which they collected revenue** — and **mansabs — military-administrative ranks**) was central to running the empire.
- **Akbar had built a well-knit organisation** for the nobility, but there was always **divisiveness on the basis of religion, homeland, and tribe**.
- Over time, the **number of amirs (nobles) and their mansabs increased sharply** — but there was **little land left to distribute as jagirs**.
- Aurangzeb tried to solve the **acute shortage of jagirs (bejagiri — state of being without a jagir)** by **showing inflated income from jagirs on record** — a shortsighted fix.
- The amirs then tried to **recover this inflated recorded income from their jagirs by pressuring the peasantry** — antagonising both the amirs AND the peasants.
- Wars, luxurious lifestyles of emperors and amirs, and the **reduction in khalisa land (land under direct imperial control)** all burdened the state.
- **Expenditure far exceeded income**.

### (xii) Rise of Regional Aspirations
- Aurangzeb's reign saw **rebellions by Jats, Sikhs, and Marathas** — all fighting to create their own kingdoms.
- Although they didn't succeed in overthrowing the empire initially, they **weakened it continuously**.
- **Bahadur Shah I** tried suppressing the **Rajputs**, which only spurred them to fight harder.
- Later Mughals tried reconciliation with Rajputs — **but it was too late**.
- The **Marathas** initially only wanted control of Maharashtra; but their ambitions grew to **collecting sardeshmukhi and chauth (traditional Maratha taxes levied on territories they "protected")** throughout India.
- By **1740**, they had spread influence over **Gujarat, Malwa, and Bundelkhand**.

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **The Jagirdari Crisis and Modern Land Reforms:**
> The Jagirdari crisis — where landed elites extracted maximum revenue from peasants while the state got weaker — has direct echoes in India's post-independence land reforms. The **Zamindari Abolition Acts** (passed by various states between 1950–1955 after independence) abolished the zamindari system to give land rights directly to tillers. Today, debates around **land acquisition** (the **Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013**), **farmer distress**, and **agricultural reforms** (the contentious 2020 Farm Laws that were repealed in 2021 after massive protests) all echo the ancient conflict between cultivators and those who control land revenues. `[Modern Example]`

# 🗺️ PART 3: RISE OF REGIONAL STATES

---

## 3.1 Three Categories of Regional States

As the Mughal Empire crumbled, new states filled the power vacuum. They fall into **three broad categories**:

| Category | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **(i) Successor States** | Mughal provinces that broke away but still acknowledged Mughal sovereignty in name | **Awadh, Bengal, Hyderabad** |
| **(ii) Independent Kingdoms** | States that emerged due to destabilisation of Mughal control — did not particularly acknowledge Mughal authority | **Mysore, Rajput States** |
| **(iii) New States** | States set up by rebels against the Mughal empire | **Marathas, Sikhs, Jats** |

---

## 3.2 Survey of Regional Kingdoms

---

### Hyderabad (Founded 1724)

- The founder of the **Asaf-Jah house of Hyderabad** was **Kilich Khan**, popularly known as **Nizam-ul-Mulk**.
- The idea of an independent state in the Deccan was **first conceived by Zulfikar Khan** — but he died in **1713** before achieving it.
- **Kilich Khan** was disgusted when the Mughal emperor appointed **Mubariz Khan** as a full-fledged viceroy of the Deccan.
- He fought and **defeated and killed Mubariz Khan at the Battle of Shakr-Kheda (1724)** and assumed control of the Deccan.
- In **1725**, he became viceroy and **conferred on himself the title of Asaf-Jah**.

---

### Awadh (Founded 1722)

- The founder was **Saadat Khan**, popularly known as **Burhan-ul-Mulk** — a **Shia Muslim**.
- He had joined a conspiracy against the Sayyid brothers, which earned him an increased mansab.
- Later driven out of court, he was prompted to **found a new independent state**.
- Saadat Khan **committed suicide** under pressure from **Nadir Shah**, who was demanding a huge tribute.
- He was succeeded by **Safdar Jang** as Nawab of Awadh.

---

### Bengal (Founded c. 1717)

- **Murshid Kuli Khan** was the **founder of the independent state of Bengal**.
- He was a capable ruler who made Bengal a **prosperous state**.
- He was succeeded in **1727 by his son Shuja-ud-din**.
- **Sarfaraz Khan** (next in line) was **killed in 1740 by Alivardi Khan** (the deputy governor of Bihar) at Gheria.
- Alivardi Khan assumed power and made himself independent of the Mughal emperor by **paying a yearly tribute**.

---

### The Rajputs

- The Rajputs tried to **re-establish their independence in the 18th century**.
- Mughal ruler **Bahadur Shah I marched against Ajit Singh (1708)**, who had formed an alliance with **Jai Singh II and Durgadas Rathor**.
- The alliance was broken and the situation was temporarily saved for the Mughals.
- At their peak, the **Rajputs controlled the entire territory from south of Delhi to the western coast**.

---

### Mysore

- Mysore — located at the **junction of the Eastern and Western Ghats** — was ruled by the **Wodeyars**.
- Various powers fought over this territory, making it a constant battlefield.
- Eventually, the state came under the rule of **Haider Ali**, who was involved in **constant warfare with the British**.
- His son **Tipu Sultan** continued this resistance.

---

### Kerala

- **Martanda Varma** established an independent state of **Kerala with Travancore as his capital**.
- He **extended boundaries from Kanyakumari to Cochin**.
- He **organised his army along the Western model** and adopted various measures to develop his state.

---

### The Jats

- The **Jat agriculturist settlers** living around **Delhi, Mathura, and Agra** revolted against Aurangzeb's oppressive policies.
- The **first major Jat rebellion against Mughal forces occurred in Mathura district in 1669** — led by **Gokula** — but was unsuccessful.
- After initial setbacks, **Churaman and Badan Singh** succeeded in setting up the **Jat kingdom of Bharatpur** in the 18th century.
- **Jat power reached its zenith under Suraj Mal**, who:
  - Provided an efficient system of administration
  - Extended territory from the **Ganga in the east to Chambal in the south**
  - Included the Subahs of **Agra, Mathura, Meerut, and Aligarh**
- The Jat state **declined after Suraj Mal's death in 1763** — splitting into small areas controlled by petty zamindars who mainly lived by plunder.

---

### The Sikhs

- In **1675**, Aurangzeb **arrested and executed the ninth Sikh guru, Guru Tegh Bahadur**, who had refused to embrace Islam.
- According to Sikh tradition, the Guru had **supported the rights of Kashmiri Pandits** and protected them from Mughal persecution.
- **Guru Gobind Singh** succeeded Guru Tegh Bahadur and **openly rebelled against Aurangzeb**, transforming the Sikhs into a **militant sect** in defence of their religion and liberties.
- **Banda Bahadur**, who assumed Sikh leadership in **1708**, was defeated and killed.
- In the wake of **Nadir Shah and Ahmad Shah Abdali's invasions**, the Sikhs reasserted their authority.
- They organised themselves into **12 misls (confederacies)** which controlled different parts of the kingdom.

#### Ranjit Singh — The Lion of Punjab
- Credit for establishing a **strong kingdom of Punjab** goes to **Ranjit Singh**.
- He was the son of **Mahan Singh**, leader of the **Sukarchakiya misl**.
- He brought under control the area from the **Sutlej to the Jhelum**.
- **Conquered Lahore in 1799** and **Amritsar in 1802**.
- By the **Treaty of Amritsar with the British**, he acknowledged **British right over the Cis-Sutlej territories** (territories on the British/eastern side of the Sutlej river).
- He **greatly modernised his army** with the help of Europeans — proving to be an efficient administrator.
- Towards the close of his reign, the English forced him to sign the **Tripartite Treaty in 1838** with **Shah Shuja and the English Company**, whereby he agreed to **provide passage to British troops through Punjab** to place Shah Shuja on the throne of Kabul.
- **Ranjit Singh died in 1839.**
- His successors could not maintain the state, and the British soon took control.

---

### The Marathas

- **The most formidable regional power** to emerge from the Mughal collapse.
- Under the capable leadership of the **Peshwas**, the Marathas uprooted Mughal authority from **Malwa and Gujarat**.
- They **claimed the right to be the chief inheritors of the Mughal dominion**.
- Their authority was challenged by **Ahmad Shah Abdali at the Third Battle of Panipat (1761)** — a devastating defeat.
- But the Marathas **quickly recovered** and offered the **most formidable challenge to the English East India Company** in the struggle for political supremacy.

---

### Rohilakhand and Farrukhabad

- These states were a **direct result of Afghan migration into India**.
- Large-scale immigration of Afghans into India took place in the **mid-18th century** due to **political and economic turmoil in Afghanistan**.
- **Ali Muhammad Khan** took advantage of the collapse of authority after Nadir Shah's invasion to set up **Rohilakhand** — a petty kingdom in the **Himalayan foothills between Kumaon in the north and the Ganga in the south**.
- The **Rohillas** (inhabitants of Rohilakhand) suffered heavily at the hands of the **Jats, Awadh rulers, Marathas, and later the British**.
- **Mohammad Khan Bangash** (an Afghan) set up an independent kingdom to the east of Delhi in the area around **Farrukhabad**, during the reigns of **Farrukhsiyar and Muhammad Shah**.

---

## 3.3 Nature and Limitations of Regional States

### What Connected Them to the Mughal Empire?
- Independent political systems that emerged still **maintained ties with Mughal imperial authority** and acknowledged the emperor's importance as an **umbrella** (symbolic figure of legitimacy).
- Even **rebel chieftains of the Marathas and Sikhs** recognised the Mughal emperor as the **supreme authority** in name.

### How They Functioned
- These states had a **regional character**, functioning through **collaborative support** of local groups — zamindars, merchants, local nobles, and chieftains.
- Provincial rulers had to **balance various local interests** to maintain power.
- Exception: **In Mysore, rulers did not recognise local chieftains**.

### Their Key Limitations
- Failed to develop **sound financial, administrative, and military organisation**.
- While some (notably **Mysore**) tried to modernise, **most were backward in science and technology**.
- **Constant warfare** with neighbouring regional powers — wars in which **none could ultimately dominate**.
- Strong enough to **challenge Mughal power, but none could replace it** with a stable all-India polity.
- The **jagirdari crisis intensified** as agricultural income declined and more contenders competed for a share of the surplus.
- Though **trade continued and even prospered**, the **rest of the economy stagnated**.

India's Constitution creates a **strong Centre with powerful states** (Article 1 describes India as a "Union of States"). Today, states like **Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, West Bengal, and Kerala** assert significant regional identities and political independence — sometimes in conflict with the Centre. # 💰 PART 4: SOCIO-ECONOMIC CONDITIONS OF 18TH CENTURY INDIA

---

## 4.1 The Big Picture

India in the **18th century failed to make progress** economically, socially, or culturally at an adequate pace. It became a **land of contrasts**:

- **The common populace:** Impoverished, backward, oppressed, living at **bare subsistence level**.
- **The rich and powerful:** Living a life of **luxury and lavishness**.

> 🔑 **Important Note:** Despite this, the life of Indian masses was, **by and large, better in the 18th century than it was after 100 years of British rule**. This is a crucial point for understanding the impact of colonialism.

---

## 4.2 Agriculture

- Agriculture was **technically backward** but sustained by the **hard labour of peasants**.
- The hard-working peasant class **seldom received the fruits of their labour**.
- Even though agricultural produce **supported the rest of society**, a peasant's own reward was **miserably inadequate**.
- Peasants were forced to pay **exorbitant amounts** to:
  - The **State**
  - The **zamindars**
  - The **jagirdars**
  - The **revenue-farmers** (private individuals who paid a fixed sum to the state and then collected taxes themselves)

> ⚠️ **Important:** This oppression **worsened under British rule** — a critical comparison.

---

## 4.3 Trade and Industry

India was **not an economically backward country** — it was, in fact, a major global trading power.

> 💬 *J.T. Sunderland writes: "India was a far greater industrial and manufacturing nation than any in Europe or any other in Asia. Her textile goods... were famous over the civilised world."*

> 💬 *Shashi Tharoor writes in "An Era of Darkness": "For centuries the handloom weavers of Bengal had produced some of the world's most desirable fabrics, especially the fine muslins, light as 'woven air', that were coveted by European dressmakers."*

### India's Trade Balance
- India was **self-sufficient in handicrafts and agricultural products** — it did not import foreign goods on a large scale.
- Its industrial and agricultural products were in **great demand in foreign markets**.
- **Exports exceeded imports** — the trade balance was maintained by **import of silver and gold**.
- India was historically known as a **"sink of precious metals"** (meaning gold and silver flowed into India and stayed there).

### Items of IMPORT:

| Source Region | Goods Imported |
|---|---|
| **Persian Gulf** | Pearls, raw silk, wool, dates, dried fruits, rose water |
| **Arabia** | Coffee, gold, drugs, honey |
| **China** | Tea, sugar, porcelain, silk |
| **Tibet** | Gold, musk, woollen cloth |
| **Africa** | Ivory, drugs |
| **Europe** | Woollen cloth, copper, iron, lead, paper |

### Items of EXPORT:
- Cotton textiles, raw silk and silk fabrics, hardware, **indigo**, saltpetre, opium, rice, wheat, sugar, pepper and other spices, precious stones, and drugs.

### Important Textile Industry Centres:
> **Dacca, Murshidabad, Patna, Surat, Ahmedabad, Broach, Chanderi, Burhanpur, Jaunpur, Varanasi, Lucknow, Agra, Multan, Lahore, Masulipatnam, Aurangabad, Chicacole, Vishakhapatnam, Bangalore, Coimbatore, Madurai**
> **Kashmir** — centre of **woollen manufactures**.

### Ship-building Industry
- **Maharashtra, the Andhra region, and Bengal** led in ship-building.
- Indian shipping also flourished on the **Kerala coast at Calicut and Quilon**.
- The **Zamorin of Calicut** used the **Muslim Kunjali Maraikkars** (famous seafarers) for his navy.
- **Shivaji Bhonsle's navy** put up a good defence on the west coast against the Portuguese.
- According to **Bipan Chandra**, the **European companies themselves bought many Indian-made ships** for their use.

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **India's Textile Legacy and the "China +1" Opportunity:**
> India's 18th-century dominance in textiles is finding a modern revival. With global supply chains shifting away from China (the "China +1" strategy), India is being positioned as a major textile manufacturing hub. The **PM MITRA (PM Mega Integrated Textile Regions and Apparel) scheme, launched in 2021-22**, proposes to set up **7 integrated textile parks** with an outlay of **₹4,445 crore** to revive India's textile dominance. India's textile exports in **FY 2022-23 were approximately $44.4 billion** — a fraction of the potential that historians say existed in the 18th century. `[Modern Example]`

## 4.4 Status of Education

- Education in **18th-century India** was still **traditional** and could not match the rapid scientific developments happening in the West.
- Knowledge was confined to: **literature, law, religion, philosophy, and logic**.
- The **study of physical and natural sciences, technology, and geography was completely excluded**.
- **Over-reliance on ancient learning discouraged original thought**.

### Elementary Education:

| Community | Type of School | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Hindus** | **Pathshalas** | Reading, writing, arithmetic |
| **Muslims** | **Maktabs** | Reading, writing, arithmetic |

- Children from **lower castes sometimes attended schools**, but **female presence was rare**.

### Higher Education:

| Community | Institution | Subjects |
|---|---|---|
| **Hindus** | **Chatuspathis or Tols** (Bihar and Bengal) | Sanskrit learning |
| **Muslims** | **Madrasahs** | Persian and Arabic |

### Famous Education Centres:
- **Sanskrit education:** Kasi (Varanasi), Tirhut (Mithila), Nadia, and Utkala
- **Persian education:** Azimabad (Patna)
- **Arabic (Quran and Muslim theology):** Required proficiency in Arabic — studied in Madrasahs

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **Education Then and Now:**
> The 18th-century critique of India's education — that it excluded science, technology, and geography — was directly used by colonial administrators like Macaulay to justify imposing Western education. Today, India's **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020** attempts to correct this by integrating **vocational education from Class 6**, emphasising **critical thinking, coding, and mother-tongue-based learning**, and reducing rote memorisation. The irony is that NEP 2020 is partly trying to undo the very damage that the colonial education system — which replaced traditional education — created. India's **Gross Enrolment Ratio (GER) in higher education reached 27.3% in 2021-22** (AISHE Report), still far below the 50% target set by NEP 2020 for 2035. `[Modern Example]`

# 👥 PART 5: SOCIETAL SET-UP OF 18TH CENTURY INDIA

---

## 5.1 Many Castes, Many Sects

The **society of 18th-century India** was characterised by:
- **Traditional outlook and stagnation**
- A certain degree of **broad cultural unity** but deep divisions by **caste, religion, region, tribe, and language**

### Hindu Society:
- Family system was primarily **patriarchal** (power and descent through the male line).
- **Caste was the central feature** of social life.
- Apart from the four varnas, Hindus were divided into **numerous sub-castes** which **permanently fixed one's place in the social scale**.
- Though profession was mainly caste-determined, **exceptions occurred on a large scale** — making caste status **quite fluid in some parts** of the country.
- **Caste councils and panchayats** enforced caste norms and regulations.

### Muslim Society:
- Islam enjoined social equality on Muslims — but they too were **divided by caste, race, tribe, and status**.
- **Religious considerations kept Sunni and Shia nobles apart**.
- Also kept apart: **Irani, Afghan, Turani, and Hindustani Muslim** nobles and officials.
- The **sharif Muslims** (nobles, scholars, priests, army officers) **looked down upon the ajlaf Muslims** (lower-class Muslims) — similar to how higher-caste Hindus treated lower-caste Hindus.
- **Religious conversions occurred** — but caste proved to be a **major divisive force and element of disintegration** in 18th-century India.

---

## 5.2 Position of Women in Society

- In the **patriarchal family system** (except in some social groups in **Kerala**), women **possessed little individuality**.
- **Upper-class women** remained at home.
- **Lower-class women** worked in fields and outside homes, supplementing family income.

### Oppressive Social Customs That Existed:
- **Purdah** (seclusion of women from public view)
- **Sati** (widow self-immolation on husband's funeral pyre)
- **Child marriage**
- **Polygamy** (having multiple wives)

### Specific Issues:
- The plight of the **Hindu widow** was usually miserable.
- The **evil of dowry** was especially widespread in **Bengal and Rajputana**.
- **Raja Sawai Jai Singh of Amber** and **Maratha General Prashuram Bhau** tried to promote **widow remarriage** — but failed.

> 💬 *Abbe Dubois, commenting at the beginning of the 19th century: "A Hindu woman can go anywhere alone, even in the most crowded places, and she need never fear the impertinent looks and jokes of idle loungers."*

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **Women's Rights — From Sati to Today:**
> The social evils of sati, child marriage, and dowry that existed in the 18th century were progressively addressed through legislation. **Sati was abolished by Regulation XVII of 1829** (due to Raja Ram Mohan Roy's efforts). Today, the **Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Bill, 2021** proposes raising the **minimum marriage age for women from 18 to 21 years** — still debated in Parliament. The **Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005** and the **Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961** address age-old social problems. Yet the **National Family Health Survey-5 (NFHS-5, 2019-21)** found that **23.3% of women aged 20-24 were married before age 18** — showing how deeply these 18th-century social patterns persist. `[Modern Example]`

---

## 5.3 The Menace of Slavery

- **European travellers and administrators** who came in the 17th century **reported widespread prevalence of slaves in India**.
- Causes of slavery:
  - **Economic distress**
  - **Famines and natural calamities**
  - **Extreme poverty** — some people were compelled to **sell their own children**.
- Generally, **higher classes of Rajputs, Khatris, and Kayasthas kept women slaves for domestic work**.

### Condition of Slaves in India vs. Europe:
- The **status of slaves in India was better** than in Europe.
- Slaves were typically treated as **hereditary servants rather than menials**.
- **Marriages took place among slaves**, and their offspring were considered **free citizens**.

### Europeans Made It Worse:
- The **advent of Europeans heightened slavery and the slave trade in India**.
- European trading companies **purchased slaves from markets in Bengal, Assam, and Bihar** and took them to European and American markets.
- **Abyssinian slaves** were sold at **Surat, Madras, and Calcutta**.

# 🎨 PART 6: DEVELOPMENTS IN ART, ARCHITECTURE, AND CULTURE

---

## 6.1 Patronage Shifts from Mughal Court to Regional Courts

- The decline of the imperial Mughals forced **talented people to seek patronage** at newly established regional courts like:
  - **Hyderabad, Lucknow, Jaipur, Murshidabad, Patna, Kashmir**

---

## 6.2 Architecture

- **Asaf-ud-Daula** built the famous **Bara Imambara in Lucknow in 1784**.
- **Sawai Jai Singh** (in the first half of the 18th century):
  - Built the **pink city of Jaipur**
  - Built **five astronomical observatories** at **Delhi, Jaipur, Benares, Mathura, and Ujjain** (known as **Jantar Mantar** observatories)
  - Prepared a set of **timetables called Jij Muhammad-shahi** to help people study astronomy
- In **Kerala, South India**: The **Padmanabhapuram Palace** — famous for its architecture and **mural paintings** — was constructed.

---

## 6.3 Painting

- **New schools of painting** were born and achieved distinction.
- The **Rajputana and Kangra schools of painting** became prominent, revealing new vitality and taste.

---

## 6.4 Literature and Language

A distinct feature of 18th-century literary life was the **growth of Urdu language and poetry**. Key figures:

- **Urdu poets:** **Mir, Sauda, Nazir, and Mirza Ghalib** (Ghalib was primarily 19th century)

### Regional Literature Flourished:

| Region | Language | Notable Work/Poet |
|---|---|---|
| **Kerala/South India** | **Malayalam** | **Kalakkathu Kunchan Nambiar** (noted poet, under patronage of Travancore rulers) |
| **Tamil Nadu** | **Tamil** | **Tayumanavar (1706–44)** — best exponent of **sittar poetry**; protested against temple rule abuses and the caste system |
| **Punjab** | **Punjabi** | **Warris Shah** composed **Heer Ranjha** — a romantic epic |
| **Sindh** | **Sindhi** | **Shah Abdul Latif** composed **Risalo** — a collection of poems |

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **The Jantar Mantar Observatories Today:**
> Sawai Jai Singh's five astronomical observatories are a remarkable legacy of 18th-century scientific ambition. The **Jantar Mantar in Jaipur** was inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2010**. The **Jantar Mantar in Delhi** faces significant preservation challenges — in 2023, the **Archaeological Survey of India (ASI)** undertook restoration work as part of broader heritage conservation efforts. These observatories demonstrate that even in a period of political decline, **India had pockets of extraordinary scientific and cultural achievement**. `[Modern Example]`

---

🌍 **Modern Context** `[Modern Example]`

> **Urdu's Status in Modern India:**
> Urdu — which flourished in the 18th century as the language of poetry and court culture — remains one of India's **22 scheduled languages (Eighth Schedule of the Constitution)**. Urdu is the **official language of Jammu & Kashmir and Telangana**. The **National Council for Promotion of Urdu Language (NCPUL)** under the Ministry of Education continues to promote Urdu education. However, Urdu-medium schools have declined significantly — a cultural loss that connects back to the colonial period's disruption of indigenous educational and cultural systems. `[Modern Example]`
