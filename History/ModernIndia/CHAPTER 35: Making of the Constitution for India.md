# 📚 Chapter 35: Making of the Constitution for India

---

## 🗂️ Chapter at a Glance (Read This First!)

> **5 Big Takeaways from this Chapter:**
>
> 1. India's Constitution is the **longest in the world** — its complexity reflects India's enormous size, diversity, and the deep divisions at the time of framing.
> 2. The demand for a **Constituent Assembly** didn't come from the British — it grew organically from the **Indian National Movement**, with roots going back to **1895.**
> 3. The **Constituent Assembly** was NOT perfectly democratic — it was indirectly elected, excluded 70%+ of adults, and accepted communal representation under pressure.
> 4. The actual drafting took **2 years, 11 months, and 18 days** — with **7,000+ amendments proposed** and **2,500+ actually discussed.**
> 5. The real decision-making power lay with **four men: Nehru, Patel, Rajendra Prasad, and Azad** — described by Granville Austin as a *"virtual oligarchy."*

---

## PART 1: What Makes India's Constitution Unique?

---

The **Indian Constitution** came into effect on **January 26, 1950.** It holds the distinction of being the **longest constitution in the world** in terms of its length, content, and complexity.

**Why is it so long and complex?**

- India is an extraordinarily large and diverse country.
- At the time of framing, India was **deeply divided** — by class, caste, community, language, and religion.
- The Constitution was deliberately designed to:
  - **Hold the country together** despite these divisions.
  - Make Indians of different classes, castes, and communities **come together for a shared vision.**
  - **Nurture democratic institutions** in a society that had long been shaped by hierarchy and deference to authority.

---

## PART 2: Roots of the Constitution — It Didn't Start in 1946!

---

Although the Constitution was formally framed between **December 1946 and December 1949**, its roots go much deeper.

### 2.1 Where Did the Ideas Come From?

The Constitution's foundations lie in:
- The **Indian National Movement** against colonial rule.
- Movements for **responsible and constitutional government** in the princely states.

> **Important Clarification:** Historian **Mridula Mukherjee** (in *India Since Independence*) has **rejected** the idea that the British initiated modern, responsible, constitutional government in India — and that the 1950 Constitution was merely the end-product of British constitutional initiatives of **1861, 1892, 1909, 1919, and 1935.**
>
> The truth is: **British concessions at every stage fell far short of what nationalists were demanding.**

---

### 2.2 Early Constitutional Visions — A Timeline

---

#### 🔹 The Constitution of India Bill / Home Rule Bill (1895)

- The first modern constitutional vision for India appeared in the **Constitution of India Bill**, also known as the **Home Rule Bill, 1895.**
- It envisaged **basic human rights** for all citizens of India, including:
  - Freedom of expression
  - Equality before the law
  - Right to the inviolability of one's home *(no one can enter your home without permission)*
  - Right to property
- **Authorship:** There is no conclusive evidence of who wrote it, but **Annie Besant** believed it was **inspired by Tilak.**

---

#### 🔹 Gandhi's Vision (1922)

- In **1922**, **Mahatma Gandhi**, in an article titled **'Independence'** published in ***Young India***, wrote:
  > *Swaraj would not be a free gift of the British Parliament but a declaration of India's full self-expression — the Constitution of India would be framed as per the wishes of the Indians.*

---

#### 🔹 Commonwealth of India Bill (1925)

- Drafted in India with important contributions from **Annie Besant, Tej Bahadur Sapru,** and **V.S. Srinivasa Shastri.**
- **Unanimously accepted** by the executive committee of the Parliamentary Labour Party.
- Had its **first reading in the House of Commons in December 1925** — it was defeated.
- **Why it still mattered:**
  - Had support from very wide sections of Indian opinion.
  - Clearly specified: *"India shall be placed on an equal footing with the self-governing dominions."*

---

#### 🔹 Motilal Nehru's National Demand (February 1924)

- **Motilal Nehru** introduced a resolution in the **Central Legislative Assembly** in **February 1924** that:
  - Gave due regard to **minority rights and interests.**
  - Came to be known as the **"National Demand."**
  - Was **passed by a large majority** in the Assembly.
- **Significance:** For the first time, a demand for a constitution and the **procedure for its adoption** were expounded in clear terms.

---

#### 🔹 The Nehru Report (August 1928)

- Britain's response to the National Demand was to appoint the **all-white Simon Commission** in **November 1927.**
- In response to **Lord Birkenhead's challenge**, the **Nehru Report** was submitted in **August 1928** — an outline of a draft constitution for India.

**Key Features of the Nehru Report:**

- Based on the principle of **dominion status** with full responsible government on the **parliamentary pattern.**
- Asserted that **sovereignty belongs to the Indian people.**
- Laid down a set of **fundamental rights.**
- Provided for a **federal system** with:
  - Maximum autonomy to the units (provinces).
  - **Residuary powers** *(powers not listed anywhere)* vesting in the **central government.**
- Proposed **joint electorates** for elections to the federal lower house and provincial legislatures.
- Provided for **reservation of seats for minorities** in certain cases for a **limited period.**

> **Most of its features were later incorporated in the Constitution of independent India.** This makes it an extremely important document.

---

#### 🔹 The Demand for a Constituent Assembly — Growing Momentum (1929–1942)

| Year | Development |
|---|---|
| **December 1929** | Congress declared **complete independence** as its ultimate goal; Simon Commission boycotted. |
| **1933** | **Jawaharlal Nehru** became the first national leader to enunciate the idea of a **Constituent Assembly.** (M.N. Roy had suggested it earlier.) |
| **1934** | **Congress officially adopted** the demand for a Constituent Assembly, refusing Simon Commission's 1933 recommendations as not representing the will of the people. |
| **1936 (Lucknow session)** | Congress declared: *"No constitution imposed by an outside authority and no constitution which curtails the sovereignty of the people"* would be acceptable. |
| **July 1937** | After Congress accepted office in majority provinces, **Nehru** pressed legislators to pass resolutions rejecting the 1935 Act and demanding a Constituent Assembly. |
| **August 1937** | **CWC** accepted a draft resolution prepared under **Acharya Kripalani.** Between August–October 1937, all Congress-ruled provinces and Sind passed this resolution demanding repeal of the **Government of India Act, 1935.** |
| **September 1937** | **S. Satyamurti** introduced a resolution in the Central Legislative Assembly recommending replacement of the 1935 Act by a constitution framed by a Constituent Assembly. |
| **1938 (Haripura session)** | The same demand was reiterated. |
| **1942 (Cripps Proposals)** | Though **rejected** by Congress as unacceptable overall, the Cripps Proposals conceded that Indians could frame their own constitution through a Constituent Assembly — a significant admission. |

---

#### 🔹 The Final Steps to Formation (1945–1946)

- **September 1945:** The newly elected **Labour government in England** announced plans to create a Constituent Assembly in India.
- **March 15, 1946:** The **Cabinet Mission** came to India and recommended forming:
  - (a) The **Constituent Assembly**
  - (b) An **interim government**

---

## PART 3: Formation of the Constituent Assembly

---

### 3.1 How Was It Elected?

The Constituent Assembly was **elected indirectly** by the Provincial Assemblies (not directly by the people).

**The Election Method:**

- Provinces of British India were grouped into **three categories: A, B, and C.**
- Each province was allotted seats on the basis of **population** — in the ratio of **one member per million people.**
- Seats within each province were divided among **three communities:**
  - **Muslims**
  - **Sikhs**
  - **General** (including Hindus and all others who were not Muslims or Sikhs)
- Representatives were elected using **proportional representation** *(seats distributed based on vote share)* with the **single transferable vote** method.

### 3.2 The Numbers

**Total strength of the Constitution-making body: 389**

| Category | Number of Members |
|---|---|
| From **eleven Governors' Provinces** of British India | **292** |
| From **four Chief Commissioners' Provinces** (Delhi, Ajmer-Merwara, Coorg, British Baluchistan) | **4** |
| **Total from British India** | **296** |
| From **Indian States** (to be nominated by respective rulers) | **93** |
| **Grand Total** | **389** |

### 3.3 Election Results

Elections for the **296 seats** from British Indian Provinces were completed by **July–August 1946:**

- **Congress:** Won **208 seats** — including all General seats except 9.
- **Muslim League:** Won **73 seats** — all but 5 of the seats allotted to Muslims.

**What about the 93 seats for Indian states?**
- Initially, **princely states decided not to participate.**
- However, representatives of some states (**Baroda, Bikaner, Jaipur, Patiala, Rewa, and Udaipur**) entered the Assembly by **April 1947.**
- By **August 15, 1947** and soon after, **all the states** had sent their representatives.

### 3.4 The Muslim League Boycott

- **Jinnah withdrew** his acceptance and caused the **Muslim League to boycott** the Assembly.
- The Congress went ahead regardless and:
  - Appointed an expert committee to draft **Fundamental Rights.**
  - Accepted the Viceroy's invitation to form an **interim government** with **Jawaharlal Nehru as Prime Minister.**

---

## PART 4: The Constituent Assembly in Session

---

### 4.1 Opening Session

- The **Constituent Assembly opened on December 9, 1946** in the **Constitution Hall** — now the **Central Hall of Parliament House, New Delhi.**
- **J.B. Kripalani** (then Congress president) proposed **Dr Sachhidanand Sinha** (oldest member) as provisional president.
- On **December 11, Dr Rajendra Prasad** was elected as the **President of the Constituent Assembly.**

### 4.2 The Objectives Resolution (December 13, 1946)

- **Jawaharlal Nehru** moved the historic **Objectives Resolution on December 13, 1946.**
- It envisaged:
  - A **federal polity** *(union of states with distributed powers)* with residuary powers vesting in the **autonomous units.**
  - **Sovereignty belonging to the people.**
- It gave the Assembly its **guiding principles and philosophy** for constitution-making.

---

## PART 5: Two Constituent Assemblies — India and Pakistan

---

- By the **end of January 1947**, it was clear the Muslim League would never join the Assembly.
- **Jinnah** made an uncompromising call for a **separate Constituent Assembly for Pakistan.**
- On **June 26, 1947**, **Lord Mountbatten** announced the setting up of a **separate Constituent Assembly for Pakistan.**

### The Indian Independence Act, 1947

- Passed with surprising speed; came into force on **July 18, 1947.**
- **Declared the Constituent Assembly of India to be a fully sovereign body.**
- At the **midnight of August 14–15, 1947**, the Assembly **assumed full powers** of governance.
- **Section 8** of the Act conferred on the Constituent Assembly **full legislative power.**

---

## PART 6: Honest Evaluation — Was the Assembly Truly Democratic?

---

The Assembly set up under the **Cabinet Mission Plan** was a result of **compromises** made by the Congress.

**What the Congress had demanded vs. what it got:**

| Congress Demanded | What Actually Happened |
|---|---|
| A **fully sovereign** assembly | Assembly was NOT fully sovereign initially |
| Election based on **universal adult franchise** | Elected indirectly by provincial assemblies on **limited franchise** (1935 Act — based on tax, property, education) |
| No communal representation | **Accepted communal representation** |
| No grouping plan | **Accepted the grouping plan** for provinces |
| Strong central government | Accepted limits on central government powers |

> **The hard truth:** The 1935 Act's limited franchise **excluded more than 70% of the adult population** from voting. The Constituent Assembly thus reflected the composition of provincial assemblies where Congress had a **comfortable majority** — rising to **more than 80%** after Partition.

---

## PART 7: The Ideological Spectrum Inside the Assembly

---

The Assembly was not a monolith. It reflected the **diverse ideological views** present in India at the time:

| Ideological Group | Key Representatives | Core Belief |
|---|---|---|
| **Democratic Socialists** | **Jawaharlal Nehru** | Peaceful parliamentary methods; egalitarian society |
| **Marxian Socialists** | — | Revolutionary reconstruction; opposed private ownership of key means of production |
| **Rightists / Pro-private enterprise** | **Sardar Patel, Purushottam Das Tandon, S.P. Mukherjee** | Supported private enterprise |
| **Gandhians** | — | Decentralised village government through **panchayats** |

> **The dominant influence** on the final Constitution was that of the **liberals and democratic socialists.** The more moderate group (of which Nehru was a member), preferring peaceful parliamentary methods, held sway.

---

## PART 8: The Drafting Process — Step by Step

---

After Independence on **August 15, 1947**, the Constituent Assembly became a **sovereign body** responsible for framing the Constitution AND making ordinary laws.

### The Five Stages of Work:

1. **Stage 1 — Committee Reports:** Various committees presented reports on basic constitutional issues.

2. **Stage 2 — Initial Draft:** **Benegal Narsing Rau** (a judge of the Calcutta High Court and Constitutional Adviser to the Assembly) prepared an **initial draft** based on:
   - Reports of the committees
   - His own research into constitutions of other countries

3. **Stage 3 — Drafting Committee's Draft:** The **Drafting Committee**, under **Dr B.R. Ambedkar**, presented a **detailed draft constitution** which was:
   - Published for **public discussion and comments**
   - Criticisms and counter-criticisms in the press helped **mould the consensus** on specific issues

4. **Stage 4 — Debate and Amendments:** The draft constitution was **debated clause by clause** and amendments were proposed.

5. **Stage 5 — Adoption:** The **Constitution of India was adopted.**

---

### 8.1 The Drafting Committee — Members

| Position | Name |
|---|---|
| **Chairman** | **Dr B.R. Ambedkar** (Law Minister at the time) |
| Member | **Alladi Krishnaswami Iyer** |
| Member | **N. Gopalaswami Ayyangar** |
| Member | **K.M. Munshi** |
| Member | **Saiyad Mohammad Saadulla** |
| Member | **Sir B.L. Mitter** (resigned after first meeting; replaced by **N. Madhava Rao**) |
| Member | **D.P. Khaitan** (died in 1948; replaced by **T.T. Krishnamachari**) |

---

### 8.2 The Journey of the Draft Constitution

| Stage | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Initial draft by B.N. Rau** | Contained **240 Clauses and 13 Schedules** |
| **Second draft by Drafting Committee** | Consisted of **315 Articles and 9 Schedules**; placed before the Assembly on **February 21, 1948** |
| **Third reading** | Commenced **November 14**, finished **November 26, 1949** |
| **Preamble** | Adopted **last** |
| **Total amendments proposed** | **7,000 odd** |
| **Amendments actually discussed** | Nearly **2,500** |
| **Total time taken** | **2 years, 11 months, and 18 days** |

---

### 8.3 The Final Adoption

- On **November 26, 1949** — the people of India, through the Constituent Assembly, **adopted, enacted, and gave to themselves the Constitution** of the **Sovereign Democratic Republic of India.**
- **Dr Rajendra Prasad**, as President of the Assembly, **signed the document.**
- **284 members** actually signed the Constitution on **January 24, 1950** — the last day of the Assembly.

## PART 10: Who Really Controlled the Process?

---

> **Important Nuance for UPSC:** The formal centres of drafting were the Constituent Assembly and the Drafting Committee. But the **Congress leaders held the real power of decision-making.**

- The **Congress Working Committee** was, in many ways, the **real architect of the Constitution** — most important decisions were arrived at based on what Congress leaders suggested.

- **Granville Austin** (historian) points out that **four men** constituted a virtual **oligarchy** *(a small group holding power)* in the Assembly and **dominated proceedings** through the prestige and power they held in both the Congress and the government:

  1. **Jawaharlal Nehru**
  2. **Sardar Patel**
  3. **Rajendra Prasad**
  4. **Abul Kalam Azad**

### The Method of Decision-Making: Consensus

- **Granville Austin** defines **consensus** *(group agreement, not majority vote)* as: *"the manner of making decisions by unanimity or near unanimity."*
- The objective was to:
  - **Smoothen differences**
  - **Arrive at compromises and agreement**
  - Overcome biases
  - Avoid the element of overruling dissent that comes with simple majority decisions

> **B.R. Ambedkar's sharp observation on minorities and majorities:**
>
> *"Any claim for the sharing of power by the minority...[is] called communalism while the monopolising of the whole power by the majority...[is] called Nationalism."*

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Examples — Added for Contemporary Relevance]`

---

### Connecting the Constituent Assembly's Debates → Contemporary Constitutional Amendments

The Constitution framed in 1949 was designed to be a living document — not frozen in time.

- **[Modern Example]** As of **2024**, the Constitution has been amended **106 times** (the 106th Amendment Act, 2023 provides for reservation for women — one-third of seats — in the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies). This reflects the Assembly's wisdom in building in an amendment procedure, while debating over 7,000 amendments during the drafting itself.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Constitution (One Hundred and Fifth Amendment) Act, 2021** restored States' and Union Territories' power to identify Other Backward Classes (OBCs) — a direct echo of the debates inside the Constituent Assembly about the balance of power between Centre and States.

---

### Connecting the Preamble's "Sovereign Democratic Republic" → Recent Supreme Court Judgments

- **[Modern Example]** The **Supreme Court of India**, in the landmark **Basic Structure Doctrine** (reaffirmed repeatedly, most recently in the context of the **NJAC judgment, 2015**, and the **Electoral Bonds judgment, 2024**), has held that the **Preamble reflects the basic structure** of the Constitution — something even Parliament cannot alter. This directly traces back to the Constituent Assembly's adoption of the Preamble as the foundational philosophy.
- **[Modern Example]** In **2024**, the **Supreme Court** in *Ashwini Kumar Upadhyay vs Union of India* reiterated that the word **"Socialist"** and **"Secular"** added to the Preamble by the 42nd Amendment (1976) are consistent with the original intent of the Constituent Assembly debates — connecting present judicial interpretation to 1946–1949 debates.

---

### Connecting the Demand for Universal Adult Franchise → India's Electoral Scale Today

The Constituent Assembly was elected on limited franchise (excluding 70%+ of adults). The framers consciously chose **Universal Adult Suffrage** for free India.

- **[Modern Example]** In the **2024 General Elections**, approximately **96.8 crore voters** were registered — making it the **largest democratic election in human history.** This is the direct fulfilment of the Constituent Assembly's commitment to Universal Adult Franchise, which was a radical departure from the colonial-era limited franchise that shaped the Assembly itself.

---

### Connecting Gandhian Idea of Panchayats → Constitutional Status Today

The Gandhians in the Constituent Assembly advocated for decentralised village governance through panchayats.

- **[Modern Example]** The **73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments (1992)** gave constitutional status to **Panchayati Raj Institutions** — fulfilling the Gandhian vision articulated inside the Assembly. Today, **PM Modi's Vibrant Villages Programme (2023)** focuses on border villages, working through gram panchayats — a living continuation of this vision.
