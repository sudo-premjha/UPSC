# 📘 Chapter 33: Challenges Before the Newborn Nation

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Big Takeaways Before You Begin:**
> 1. **August 15, 1947** marked the end of colonial rule — but India immediately faced **massive challenges** on multiple fronts: territorial, communal, administrative, and economic.
> 2. The **Radcliffe Boundary Award** was drawn hastily in 6 weeks by a man who had **never been to India before**, causing enormous human suffering.
> 3. **Partition triggered one of history's largest forced migrations** — nearly 60 lakh refugees and an estimated 1,80,000 deaths in communal violence.
> 4. **Gandhi's assassination** on January 30, 1948 shook the new nation to its core and led to the banning of the **RSS**.
> 5. The **Communist Party of India (CPI)** initially rejected Indian independence as "fake" and launched armed insurgency — but eventually shifted to **constitutional democracy** by 1951–52.

---

## 🎉 Part 1: The First Day of Independent India

### The Midnight Moment

The **Constituent Assembly of India** met at **11 p.m. on August 14, 1947**. **Rajendra Prasad** presided over the session.

At the **stroke of midnight (August 14–15, 1947)**, in the Constituent Assembly (Parliament House), **Jawaharlal Nehru** delivered his historic **"Tryst with Destiny"** speech as the first Prime Minister of Independent India:

> *"Long years ago we made a tryst with destiny, and now the time comes when we shall redeem our pledge, not wholly or in full measure, but very substantially. At the stroke of midnight hour, when the world sleeps, India will wake to life and freedom..."*

On **August 15, 1947**, **Nehru** hoisted the Indian national flag above the **Lahori Gate of Red Fort** in Delhi.

## ⚠️ Part 3: Challenges Facing the New Nation

Independent India inherited a broken, partitioned, riot-torn country. The challenges can be grouped into three time horizons:

### 🔴 Immediate Challenges:
- Territorial and administrative **integration of princely states**
- Controlling **communal riots**
- **Rehabilitation of nearly 60 lakh refugees** who had migrated from Pakistan
- **Protection of Muslims** living in India and those going to Pakistan from communal gangs
- Avoiding **war with Pakistan**
- Dealing with **Communist insurgency**

### 🟠 Medium-Term Challenges:
- **Framing the Constitution** of India
- Building a **representative, democratic, and civil libertarian** political order
- Conducting **elections**
- **Abolition of the feudal set-up** in agriculture

### 🟡 Long-Term Challenges:
- **National integration**
- **Economic development**
- **Poverty alleviation**

> Both India and Pakistan faced these consequences of partition. The **Independence Act** had also laid the procedure for resolving three specific problems:
> 1. Settlement of **boundaries** between the two nations
> 2. **Division of the Indian Civil Services** and other services personnel
> 3. **Division of military assets** and formations

## 🗺️ Part 4: The Radcliffe Boundary Award and Communal Riots

### How the Boundary Was Drawn

The provinces of **Punjab and Bengal** were to be divided. The respective legislative assemblies met in two sections — one representing Muslim-majority districts, the other the rest. They voted by simple majority for partition.

**The resulting division:**

| Region | Territory (sq. miles) | Population | Muslim Population |
|---|---|---|---|
| **West Punjab** (→ Pakistan) | 62,000 | 15.7 million | 11.85 million |
| **East Punjab** (→ India) | 37,000 | 12.6 million | 4.37 million |
| **West Bengal** (→ India) | 28,000 | 21.2 million | 5.3 million |
| **East Bengal/East Pakistan** | 49,400 | 39.10 million | 27.7 million |

**The Minority Problem Created:**
- **~20 million non-Muslims** found themselves in Pakistan
- **~42 million (later reduced to 35 million) Muslims** found themselves in India

On both sides of the **Radcliffe Line** (the boundary drawn by Sir Cyril Radcliffe), large religious minorities were now stranded in the "wrong" country.

---

### Problems with the Boundary Commission

The British government appointed the **Boundary Commission** under the chairmanship of **Sir Cyril Radcliffe** in **absurd hurry**. Major problems included:

- Radcliffe had **very limited knowledge of India** (had **never been east of Gibraltar** before coming to India)
- He used **out-of-date maps and census materials**
- He was given only **six weeks** to draw the boundaries
- The **census of 1941** — the basis for decisions — was itself **faulty**

### Factors to be Considered (But Poorly Addressed):
- Religious demography (the primary deciding factor)
- Rivers as natural boundaries
- Administrative units
- Economic viability
- Railway and roadway connectivity
- **Canal systems** (critically important in Punjab)
- The **Sikhs**, as a third party (Hindus and Muslims being two parties), were demographically scattered throughout Punjab. Their demand that all Sikh holy shrines be included in **East Punjab (India)** further complicated matters.

> 💬 *"With limitations of time, knowledge and understanding, it was virtually impossible to deal adequately with the often vital accessories of a boundary line..."* — **Percival Spear**

> 💬 *"If villages weren't bisected by the boundary they were separated from the villagers' fields, railway stations from the towns they served and communities from the resources on which they relied."* — **Walter Reid**, *Keeping the Jewel in the Crown*

### The Deliberate Delay in Announcing the Award:
- The report of the Boundary Commission was **ready by August 12**.
- But **Lord Mountbatten intentionally made it public after August 15** — so that the **British would not be held responsible** for the resulting communal riots and repercussions.

> 💬 *"The systematic failure of British governments to contemplate or prepare for any planned transfer of power to India is epitomised by the fact that a man of Radcliffe's background and lack of experience... should have been asked to embark on such a fundamental task so very late in the day."* — **Walter Reid**

---

### The Scale of Violence

Communal riots had started in **August 1946** itself, but with the announcement of partition, the situation became far worse.

- **Armed bands of Sikhs/Hindus and Muslims** roamed cities and countryside of Punjab committing mass atrocities.
- Refugee trains reportedly arrived carrying **only dead bodies**.
- Estimated deaths: **~1,80,000** (60,000 from the west; 1,20,000 from the east)

### Region-by-Region Violence:

| Region | Situation |
|---|---|
| **Punjab** | Most violent — maximum murders, rapes, abductions |
| **Bengal** | Less violence — due to **Gandhi's presence and fasts** |
| **Delhi** | Riots began with massacre of Muslims (in revenge for Punjab); Gandhi's fasts had temporary impact |
| **Bihar** | October 1946 — Hindu peasants (allegedly instigated by Hindu landlords to divert attention from agrarian problems) killed Muslims |
| **Garhmukteswar (United Province)** | Hindu pilgrims killed thousands of Muslims |
| **Bihar & UP (post-partition)** | Due to **Gandhi's initiatives**, no further massacres |

### Why Was the Violence So Hard to Control?

- The governor general assembled a **boundary force of 50,000 men** anticipating danger.
- But **Nehru's decision to not allow British troops** into the matter proved devastating.
- The boundary force itself **got divided along communal affiliations**.
- **European officers were busy preparing to leave India**.
- According to **Lockhart** (Commander-in-Chief of the Indian Army, August 15 – December 31, 1947), widespread disorder could have been controlled if all civil and armed services personnel had been placed in their respective new countries promptly.

## ⚖️ Part 5: Challenges of Dividing Resources

### Division of Civil Government

A **Partition Council** was set up, presided over by the **Governor General**, with **two representatives each from India and Pakistan**.

- Helped by a **Steering Committee** consisting of **H.M. Patel** (India) and **Muhammad Ali** (Pakistan) at operational level.
- All civil servants were given the option to choose which dominion to serve.
- **~1,60,000 employees** opted for transfer (India → Pakistan or Pakistan → India).

**For Indian Civil Service personnel:**
- **Indian members:** Continued in service in their country of choice on existing scale.
- **European officers:** Could continue on existing pay/pension, OR opt for **special compensation and early retirement**.

---

### Division of Finances

The division of cash balances and public debt created severe tensions.

- **Pakistan wanted a one-fourth share** of total cash balances.
- India pointed out that only a small portion represented **real cash needs** — the rest was an **anti-inflationary mechanism** (a tool to prevent too much money causing price rises).
- India withheld Pakistan's share of the **'sterling balance'** — a debt owed by the British jointly to both dominions for Indian contributions to **World War II** — amounting to approximately **Rs 550 million**.
- India's justification: anger at Pakistan for attempting to **seize Kashmir by force**.
- **Gandhi** saw this as unnecessarily spiteful. He went on a fast and made ending the fast conditional on transferring the money owed to Pakistan.
- Gandhi **succeeded** in pressuring the Congress leadership to release the funds.

> *Note: According to some scholars, this became one of the reasons for **Gandhi's assassination** by a Hindu fanatic.*

---

### Division of Defence Personnel and Equipment

- A **Joint Defence Council** was set up, headed by **Auchinleck** as **Supreme Commander**.
- Decision: Muslim-majority units → Pakistan; Non-Muslim units → India.
- But due to serious differences, the **post of Supreme Commander was abolished**.
- British troops began leaving India on **August 17, 1947**, completing the process by **February 1948**.

---

## 🕊️ Part 6: The Assassination of Gandhi

On the evening of **January 30, 1948**, at his usual prayer meeting at **Birla mansion, New Delhi**, **Mahatma Gandhi was shot dead by Nathuram Godse**.

### The Nation's Response:

**Nehru (on All India Radio):**
> *"The light has gone out of our lives and there is darkness everywhere… The best prayer we can offer him and his memory is to dedicate ourselves to truth and to the cause for which the great countryman of ours lived and for which he died."*

**Sardar Patel:**
> *"It is a shame for us that the greatest man of the world has had to pay with his life for the sins which we have committed. We did not follow him when he was alive; let us at least follow his steps now he is dead."*

### Godse's Stated Motive:
At his trial, **Nathuram Godse** declared he had killed Gandhi because of Gandhi's **"consistent pandering to the Muslims, culminating in his last pro-Muslim fast [which] at last goaded me to the conclusion that the existence of Gandhi should be brought to an end immediately."**

**Godse was tried and sentenced to death.**

---

### Banning of the RSS

- On **February 4, 1948**, the **Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS)** was **banned by the government**.
- Reasons cited:
  - Right-wing extremism represented by RSS seen as **harmful to national unity**
  - RSS seen to have a hand in **Punjab violence**
  - RSS attracted support of many refugees
  - Rumours that **RSS members had celebrated Gandhi's death**
  - Nehru believed groups like RSS had **"the blood of Mahatma Gandhi on their hands"** even if they dissociated from the killing

### Lifting of the Ban:
The ban was **lifted in July 1949** after RSS accepted the following conditions:
- Restrict itself to **cultural activities only** — no politics
- **Renounce violence and secrecy**
- Publicly profess **loyalty to the Indian Constitution and the tricolour**
- Organise itself on **democratic principles**

## 🏠 Part 7: Rehabilitation and Resettlement of Refugees

The displaced people were **refugees** in the true sense — they had NOT left their homes voluntarily.

Neither India nor Pakistan organised an **orderly exchange of population**.

The Indian government's response:
- Established an **Emergency Committee of the Cabinet** for the crisis in Delhi
- Created a **Ministry of Relief and Rehabilitation**
- The concept of **'evacuee property'** (property to be protected for possible future return of those who left) became empty rhetoric — it was impossible to force refugees to vacate houses of departed Muslims. This later made the **return of refugees impossible**.

---

### Rehabilitation in East Punjab:

- Refugees housed temporarily in **refugee camps (run till 1949)**
- For **urban refugees**: Industrial and vocational training schemes; grants to start small businesses
- For **rural refugees**: Land grants, agricultural loans, housing subsidies
- **Problem:** A pattern of **differentiated entitlements** was observed — refugees with **class, caste, and political connections** got better deals, while **depressed classes** were largely ignored

---

### The Bengal Problem (More Prolonged and Complicated):

The Bengal refugee crisis unfolded in waves:

- **By 1948:** Only a small group of high-caste, landed, or middle-class Hindus migrated — they arranged individual property/job exchanges
- **December 1949 – January 1950:** Fresh violence in **Khulna** → large number of peasants started leaving East Pakistan
- **February 1950:** Anti-Muslim riots in India → **~1 million Muslims** forced to leave West Bengal
- This further worsened anti-Hindu violence in East Pakistan
- **By 1951:** About **15 lakh Hindu refugees** arrived in West Bengal
- **Nehru's response:** The Indian government **did NOT recognise these migrants as refugees** and Nehru tried to send them back

---

### The Delhi Pact on Minorities (Nehru-Liaquat Pact) — April 8, 1950

To resolve the refugee problem and restore communal peace (especially in Bengal), **Indian PM Jawaharlal Nehru** and **Pakistani PM Liaquat Ali Khan** signed an agreement on **April 8, 1950**, known as the **Delhi Pact on Minorities** or the **Nehru-Liaquat Pact**.

**Key Provisions:**
- Appointment of **ministers from minority communities** in both countries at central and provincial levels
- Setting up of **Minority Commissions**
- Setting up of **Commissions of Inquiry** to investigate causes of communal riots in Bengal and recommend preventive measures
- Including representatives of minority communities in the **cabinets of East Pakistan and West Bengal**
- Deputing **two central ministers** (one from each government) to remain in affected regions
- Creating an agency to **recover and rehabilitate 'abducted' women** (this was criticised by many scholars)

**Why the Pact Failed:**
- The idea to encourage refugees to return to their original homes **failed**
- Both governments failed to **restore confidence** among refugees
- Properties of refugees were declared **enemy property**

> *Note: India brought amendments to the **Enemy Property Act, 1968** in **2016** as well.*

**Criticism of the Pact:**
- Severely criticised by Hindu nationalists like **Syama Prasad Mukherjee** and **K.C. Neogy**
- **Mukherjee resigned from the Nehru cabinet in protest** — he believed the refugee problem could only be solved through **transfer of population** and acquisition of certain territories from Pakistan

---

### Where Were Refugees Settled in India?

| Region | Refugees Accommodated From |
|---|---|
| **Delhi** (Lajpat Nagar, Rajinder Nagar, Punjabi Bagh, Nizamuddin East, Kingsway Camp) | West Pakistan |
| **Punjab** (incl. present Haryana) and **Himachal Pradesh** | West Pakistan |
| **Gujarat, Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh** | Sindhi Hindus |
| **Ulhasnagar, Maharashtra** (called 'city of joy') | Sindh region specifically |
| **West Bengal, Assam, Tripura**, and other north-eastern states | East Pakistan (present Bangladesh) |
| **Andaman Islands** | Some refugees (Bengalis now form the largest linguistic group in parts of Andaman Islands) |

## ☭ Part 8: Communists and Independence — The Insurgency Challenge

### Background: Communist Opposition to Independence

In **September 1948**, the Indian Army intervened and took control of **Hyderabad** (the Nizam offering little resistance) to maintain law and order. But Hyderabad's internal politics was complicated by the **Telangana Movement** led by communists.

**Timeline of Communist Radicalism:**

- **December 1947:** The **Communist Party of India (CPI)** denounced Indian independence as **'fake'** — with the slogan *'ye azadi jhooti hai'* (this freedom is false). They termed the Congress government as **stooges of Anglo-American imperialism** and feudal forces.

- **February–March 1948:** At its **Second Congress in Calcutta**, the CPI adopted its **'Political Thesis'** — formally declaring that the national government established on August 15, 1947 was the **"major enemy of the Indian people"** and needed to be changed through **"general revolution."**

- The strategy adopted became known as the **B.T. Ranadive line** (after B.T. Ranadive, then General Secretary of CPI). Their declared goal: *"The present state will be replaced by a people's democratic republic — a republic of workers, peasants and oppressed middle classes."*

**Communist insurgency spread to:**
- **West Bengal** — revival of the **Tebhaga Movement** + urban insurgency in Calcutta
- **Hyderabad** — Telangana Movement

---

### Why Were Communists Sceptical About Independence?

Three key reasons:

1. **Class Struggle Argument:** They believed armed insurgency against the Congress (alleged as "collaborationist bourgeoisie" — a ruling class that collaborates with foreign interests) was necessary to **shift mass attention** from communal hatred to class struggle.

2. **International Communist Wave:** The late 1940s and early 1950s saw communist successes in **China, Malaya, Indonesia, the Philippines, and Burma (Myanmar)**. In **September 1947**, Russia announced the **'A. Zhdanov thesis'** as an answer to the **Marshall Plan** (the US plan to rebuild post-war Europe), encouraging more activism by international communist parties.

3. **Misreading India:** According to **Ramachandra Guha**, the CPI leadership — encouraged by initial successes of the Telangana Movement — **misconceived scattered disillusionment with Congress as revolutionary potential** and thought it was the *"beginning of Red India."*

---

### From Armed Insurgency to Constitutional Democracy

The communist movement **remained localised** in Hyderabad and West Bengal. Mass support was **sporadic and conditional** — people were not yet ready to reject the Congress so soon after Independence.

**Government's stern response:**
- Indian armed forces continued **'police action'** in the Hyderabad region
- **CPI was banned in West Bengal in March 1948**
- **January 1948:** A security act was passed to **imprison communist leaders without trial**

**Internal divisions within CPI:**
- Divisions between the **"Chinese line"** and the **"Russian line"** within the leadership grew wider, especially after the failure of a proposed **railway strike on May 9, 1949**

**The Turning Point:**
- **September 1950:** Prominent communist leaders **Ajoy Ghosh, S.A. Dange, and S.V. Ghate** publicly criticised the organisation for **faulty strategies** and failure to understand the true picture of independent India.

**The Historic Shift:**
- **October 1951:** At the **Third Party Congress of CPI**, held in Calcutta, a major policy shift was endorsed:
  - Decided to **withdraw the Telangana Movement**
  - Decided to forge an **inclusive front of peasants, workers, and middle classes**
- The government **lifted the ban** on CPI
- Indian communists **participated in the general elections of 1951–52**, completing their shift from **insurrectionist path to constitutional democracy**

## 🌍 Modern Context

> `[Modern Example]` — *These sections connect historical concepts to present-day events and policies.*

### On Partition, Refugees & Borders:
- The **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA), 2019** directly links to the partition-era problem of Hindu, Sikh, and Buddhist minorities facing persecution in Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Bangladesh — the same communities whose displacement began with the Radcliffe Line. The Supreme Court is currently hearing challenges to the CAA. `[Modern Example]`
- The **National Register of Citizens (NRC)** in Assam (updated 2019) is directly connected to the issue of refugees from East Pakistan (1971 and earlier waves) settling in Assam — a problem that began with the Bengal partition of 1947. `[Modern Example]`

### On Rehabilitation of Displaced Persons:
- The **Enemy Property Act, 1968** — which declared properties of those who migrated to Pakistan as "enemy property" — was significantly **amended in 2016** (mentioned in the original text), making it harder for legal heirs of those who migrated to reclaim properties in India. `[Modern Example]`
- The **Nehru-Liaquat Pact of 1950** remains historically relevant: its mechanism of minority protection commissions is echoed in modern demands for bilateral India-Pakistan dialogue on minority rights. `[Modern Example]`

### On Communal Violence & Constitutional Order:
- The **Prevention of Communal and Targeted Violence (Access to Justice and Reparations) Bill** — discussed but not passed — represents India's ongoing struggle with the legacy of partition-era communal violence. `[Modern Example]`
- The Supreme Court's **2023 guidelines on hate speech** directly address the communal tension that made the 1947 riots possible. `[Modern Example]`

### On Communist Politics & Electoral Democracy:
- The CPI's 1951–52 decision to enter constitutional politics set a precedent. In the **2021 Kerala Assembly elections**, the **Left Democratic Front (LDF)** led by the **CPI(M)** won a historic consecutive second term — showing the long journey from armed insurgency (1948) to stable democratic governance. `[Modern Example]`

### On the RSS Ban and Its Lifting:
- The conditions under which the **RSS ban was lifted in 1949** (professing loyalty to the Constitution and tricolour, renouncing violence, restricting to cultural activities) remain historically significant. The **RSS's current role** in Indian political life and its relationship with the ruling **BJP** continues to be a subject of academic and public debate. `[Modern Example]`
