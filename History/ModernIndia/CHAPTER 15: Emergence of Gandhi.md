## 📦 Chapter at a Glance
> **Read this first. These are the 5 core ideas of this chapter.**

1. The **post-World War I period** created a perfect storm of conditions for nationalist resurgence — economic hardship, broken British promises, and the inspiring example of the **Russian Revolution (1917)**.
2. The **Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (Government of India Act, 1919)** introduced **dyarchy** in provinces — a complex, unworkable system that gave Indians the appearance of power without its substance.
3. **Gandhi's formative years in South Africa (1893–1914)** were where he developed, tested, and refined **Satyagraha** — the technique of non-violent resistance that would transform Indian politics forever.
4. Gandhi's **three early struggles in India** — **Champaran (1917), Ahmedabad (1918), and Kheda (1918)** — proved that Satyagraha could work at scale and won him the deep trust of the Indian masses.
5. The **Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (April 13, 1919)** was the decisive turning point — it convinced Gandhi and India that **cooperation with British rule was morally impossible**, and laid the foundation for the Non-Cooperation Movement.

## 🔷 PART 1: Why Did Nationalist Resurgence Happen After WWI?

After the First World War, both **conditions inside India** and **influences from abroad** created a situation ripe for a massive national upsurge against foreign rule.

---

### 1️⃣ Post-War Economic Hardships

India contributed enormously in **men and money** to the British war effort. Thousands of Indian men died on various war fronts. The food, ammunition, and costs of the army were funded by **taxes on Indians**.

When the war ended, every section of Indian society was suffering:

- **Industry:** First prices rose sharply, then a **recession (economic slowdown)** combined with increased foreign investment pushed many industries to the brink of closure. Industrialists now demanded **protection against imports** and government aid.
- **Workers and Artisans:** Faced **unemployment** and bore the heaviest burden of **high prices**
- **Peasantry:** Crushed under **high taxation and poverty**, waiting for a leader to protest
- **Soldiers:** Soldiers returning from the battlefields found their country **more impoverished and less free** than when they had left — a shocking and radicalising experience
- **Educated Urban Classes:** Facing **unemployment** and deeply aware of the **racism** embedded in British attitudes

---

### 2️⃣ Expectations of Political Gains — Betrayed

Indians — including Gandhi and most nationalists — had **cooperated with the British war effort**. A huge number of Indian troops sacrificed their lives.

After the war, Indians expected **significant political concessions** as a reward. These expectations were not met — which created an atmosphere of deep resentment and betrayal.

---

### 3️⃣ Nationalist Disillusionment with Imperialism Worldwide

During the war, the **Allied powers promised** their colonies an era of **democracy and self-determination** after the war to keep them loyal.

But the **Paris Peace Conference** and subsequent peace treaties made it brutally clear: the imperialist powers had **no intention of loosening their hold over the colonies**. In fact, they divided the colonies of the defeated powers among themselves.

**Result:**
- The mutual propaganda of the war had further **eroded the myth of white cultural and military superiority**
- The post-war period saw a **resurgence of militant nationalist activity** throughout Asia and Africa — in **Turkey, Egypt, Ireland, Iran, Afghanistan, Burma, Malaya, the Philippines, Indonesia, Indo-China, China, and Korea**

---

### 4️⃣ Impact of the Russian Revolution (November 7, 1917)

The **Bolshevik Party** of workers overthrew the **Czarist regime** (the old Russian monarchy) and founded the world's **first socialist state — the Soviet Union** — under **Vladimir Ilyich Ulyanov (Lenin)**.

The new Soviet Union did something remarkable:
- **Unilaterally renounced** Czarist imperialist rights in China and the rest of Asia
- **Gave rights of self-determination** to former Czarist colonies in Asia
- **Gave equal status** to Asian nationalities within its borders

**The message sent to the world:** Immense power lay with the people. The masses were capable of **challenging the mightiest of tyrants** — provided they were organised, united, and determined.

## 🔷 PART 2: Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms and the Government of India Act, 1919

The British government, unwilling to genuinely share power with Indians, once again adopted its **"carrot and stick" policy**:
- **The Carrot:** The insubstantial **Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms**
- **The Stick:** Repressive measures like the **Rowlatt Act**

Following the July 1918 announcement of constitutional reforms (in line with Montagu's August 1917 statement), the **Government of India Act, 1919** was enacted. The reforms are also called the **Montford Reforms**.

---

### 📍 Main Feature 1: Provincial Government — Introduction of Dyarchy

**Dyarchy** (rule of two — dual governance at the provincial level) was the centrepiece of the reforms.

#### How Dyarchy Worked:

**The governor** was the executive head in the province.

**Subjects were divided into two lists:**

| List Type | Subjects Included | Who Administered |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Reserved** | Law and order, finance, land revenue, irrigation, etc. | Governor through his **executive council of bureaucrats** |
| **Transferred** | Education, health, local government, industry, agriculture, excise, etc. | **Ministers** nominated from among elected members of the legislative council |

**Key rules of the system:**
- The **ministers were responsible to the legislature** — they had to resign if a **no-confidence motion** (a vote of no-trust) was passed against them
- But the **executive councillors were NOT responsible** to the legislature — they answered only to the governor
- If the **constitutional machinery failed**, the governor could **take over the administration of transferred subjects** as well
- The **Secretary of State for India and the Governor General** could interfere in **reserved subjects** but had restricted scope to interfere in transferred subjects

#### Provincial Legislature:
- **Provincial legislative councils were further expanded** — **70 per cent of members were to be elected**
- The system of **communal and class electorates** (separate voting pools for different communities) was **further consolidated**
- **Women were also given the right to vote**
- Legislative councils could **initiate legislation**, but the **governor's assent was required**
- The governor could **veto bills** and **issue ordinances** (temporary laws bypassing the legislature)
- The legislative councils could **reject the budget**, but the governor could **restore it** if necessary
- **Legislators enjoyed freedom of speech**

---

### 📍 Main Feature 2: Central Government — Still No Responsible Government

**No responsible government** (government answerable to an elected legislature) was created at the all-India level.

#### Central Executive:
- The **Governor General was the chief executive authority**
- Two lists for administration — **central** and **provincial**
- In the **Viceroy's Executive Council of eight**, **three were to be Indians**
- The Governor General **retained full control** over reserved subjects in the provinces
- The Governor General could **restore budget cuts, certify bills rejected by the central legislature**, and **issue ordinances**

#### Central Legislature — Bicameral for the First Time:

A **bicameral** (two-house) arrangement was introduced:

**Lower House — Central Legislative Assembly:**
- **145 members total**: 41 nominated + 104 elected
- Of the 104 elected: **52 General, 30 Muslims, 2 Sikhs, 20 Special**
- **Tenure: 3 years**

**Upper House — Council of State:**
- **60 members total**: 26 nominated + 34 elected
- Of the 34 elected: **20 General, 10 Muslims, 3 Europeans, 1 Sikh**
- **Tenure: 5 years** | Only **male members**

*(Figures as given by Subhash Kashyap)*

**What legislators could do:** Ask questions and supplementaries, pass adjournment motions, vote on part of the budget — but **75 per cent of the budget was still NOT votable**. Some Indians did find their way into important committees, including finance.

#### One Important Central Change:
- The **Secretary of State for India** was henceforth to be **paid out of the British exchequer** (British treasury) — not from Indian funds. This was a long-standing nationalist demand finally conceded.

---

### 📍 Drawbacks of the Montford Reforms

The reforms had serious, fundamental flaws:

1. **Franchise (right to vote) was extremely limited** — the electorate was extended to only about **one-and-a-half million** for the central legislature, while India's population was around **260 million**
2. At the centre, the **legislature had no control** over the Viceroy and his executive council
3. **Division of subjects was unsatisfactory** at the centre
4. **Allocation of seats** for the central legislature to provinces was based on arbitrary notions of "importance" — Punjab's military importance, Bombay's commercial importance
5. At the provincial level, the **division of subjects and parallel administration** was **irrational and unworkable** — subjects like irrigation, finance, police, press, and justice were all "reserved"
6. **Provincial ministers had no control over finances or the bureaucrats** — leading to constant friction. Ministers were often not consulted on important matters and could be overruled by the governor on anything he considered "special"

---

### 📍 What Indian Leaders Said About the Reforms

> *"The Government of India Act, 1919 forged fresh fetters for the people."*
> — **Subhash Chandra Bose**

> *"The Montford Reforms...were only a method of further draining India of her wealth and of prolonging her servitude."*
> — **M.K. Gandhi**

> *"Never in the history of the world was such a hoax perpetrated upon a great people as England perpetrated upon India."*
> — **Dr. Rutherford**, British Member of Parliament

> *"When the Cabinet used the expression 'ultimate self-government' they probably contemplated an intervening period of 500 years."*
> — **Lord Curzon**

The Congress met in a **special session in August 1918 at Bombay** under **Hasan Imam's presidency** and declared the reforms to be **"disappointing"** and **"unsatisfactory"**, demanding effective self-government instead.

**Tilak** termed the Montford reforms **"unworthy and disappointing — a sunless dawn"**, while **Annie Besant** called them **"unworthy of England to offer and India to accept"**.

## 🔷 PART 3: The Making of Gandhi — Early Career and South Africa

### Who Was Gandhi?

**Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi** was born on **October 2, 1869** in **Porbandar** in the princely state of **Kathiawar in Gujarat**. His father was a **diwan (minister)** of the state. After studying law in England, Gandhi went to **South Africa in 1893** in connection with a case involving his client, **Dada Abdullah**.

In South Africa, he witnessed the **ugly face of white racism** — the humiliation and contempt to which Asians (who had gone as labourers) were subjected. He decided to **stay and organise the Indian workers** to fight for their rights. He remained in South Africa until **1914**.

---

### The Three Categories of Indians in South Africa

1. **Indentured Indian labour** — mainly from South India, who had migrated after **1890** to work on sugar plantations
2. **Merchants** — mostly **Meman Muslims** who had followed the labourers
3. **Ex-indentured labourers** — who had settled with their children in South Africa after their contracts expired

Most were **illiterate**, had little or no English, and had accepted racial discrimination as a fact of daily life.

**Key disabilities they suffered:**
- Denied the **right to vote**
- Could only reside in **prescribed locations** that were insanitary and congested
- In some colonies, Asians and Africans **could not stay outdoors after 9 p.m.**
- Could not use **public footpaths**

---

### Phase 1: Moderate Phase of Struggle (1894–1906)

During this phase, Gandhi relied on **petitions and memorials** to authorities in South Africa and Britain, believing that once informed of Indian suffering, the authorities would act — as Indians were, after all, British subjects.

**Key actions:**
- Set up the **Natal Indian Congress** to unite different sections of Indians
- Started a newspaper — **Indian Opinion**

---

### Phase 2: Passive Resistance / Satyagraha (1906–1914)

From 1906, Gandhi moved to **passive resistance or civil disobedience**, which he named **Satyagraha** (devotion to truth).

#### Campaign 1: Satyagraha against Registration Certificates (1906)

New legislation made it compulsory for Indians to carry **certificates of registration with their fingerprints** at all times.

Gandhi's response:
- Formed the **Passive Resistance Association** to defy the law and accept all penalties
- **Satyagraha was born** — resisting adversaries without violence
- The government **jailed Gandhi** and others
- Authorities later used **deceit** to make defiant Indians register — Indians retaliated by **publicly burning their registration certificates**
- This exposed the South African government internationally
- Eventually, a **compromise settlement** was reached

#### Campaign 2: Against Restrictions on Indian Migration

Indians defied a new law restricting migration by **crossing from one province to another** and **refusing to produce licences**. Many were jailed.

#### Campaign 3: Against Poll Tax and Invalidation of Indian Marriages

- A **poll tax of three pounds** was imposed on all **ex-indentured Indians** — crippling for people who earned **less than ten shillings a month**
- A **Supreme Court order** invalidated all marriages not conducted according to **Christian rites** and registered by the registrar — implying that **Hindu, Muslim, and Parsi marriages were illegal** and children from such marriages were illegitimate
- This was treated as a **profound insult** to the honour of Indian women
- **Many women were drawn into the movement** in anger — a significant expansion of the movement's social base

---

### The Tolstoy Farm (1910)

As sustaining the struggle became difficult, Gandhi founded the **Tolstoy Farm in 1910** — named by his associate **Herman Kallenbach** after the **Russian writer and moralist** whom Gandhi admired and corresponded with.

**Its purpose:**
- House the **families of satyagrahis** and give them a means of sustaining themselves
- Be an **experiment in education**

**This was Gandhi's second such institution.** His first was the **Phoenix Farm** (established **1904** in **Natal**), inspired by his reading of **John Ruskin's *Unto This Last*** — a critique of capitalism that extolled the virtues of a simple life of love, labour, and human dignity.

**Life at Tolstoy Farm:**
- **Manual work and instruction** went hand in hand
- **Vocational training** for "all-round development of boys and girls"
- **Co-educational classes** — boys and girls worked together
- Activities included: general labour, cooking, scavenging, sandal-making, simple carpentry, and messenger work
- Manual work like sweeping, scavenging, and fetching water was seen as essential to **psychological, social, and moral well-being**
- Gandhi's goal: To inculcate **ideals of social service, citizenship, and respect for manual work** from early formative years

The farm operated until **1913**.

#### Campaign 4: Against the Transvaal Immigration Act

Indians **illegally migrated from Natal into Transvaal** to protest the Act. They were jailed. **Miners and plantation workers went on a lightning strike**.

In India, **Gokhale** toured the whole country mobilising public opinion. Even **Viceroy Lord Hardinge** condemned the repression and called for an impartial enquiry.

#### Final Compromise

Through negotiations involving **Gandhi, Lord Hardinge, C.F. Andrews, and General Smuts**, the **Government of South Africa conceded** major Indian demands:
- Abolition of the **poll tax**
- Recognition of **registration certificates**
- Recognition of **marriages solemnised according to Indian rites**
- Promise to treat **Indian immigration** sympathetically

---

### What Gandhi Learned from South Africa — 4 Key Lessons

1. The **masses had immense capacity** to participate in and sacrifice for a cause that moved them
2. He could **unite Indians of different religions, classes, and genders** under one leadership
3. Leaders sometimes have to **take decisions unpopular with their own enthusiastic supporters**
4. He evolved his **own distinct style of leadership, politics, and techniques of struggle** on a limited scale, free from competing political currents

---

## 🔷 PART 4: Gandhi's Technique of Satyagraha

Gandhi evolved **Satyagraha** in South Africa. It was based on **truth and non-violence**. He combined:
- Elements from **Indian tradition**
- The **Christian requirement** of turning the other cheek
- The **philosophy of Tolstoy** — that evil is best countered by non-violent resistance

### Core Principles of Satyagraha:

- A satyagrahi must never submit to what he considers **wrong** — but must always remain **truthful, non-violent, and fearless**
- Works on the principles of **withdrawal of cooperation and boycott**
- Methods include: **non-payment of taxes**, and **declining honours and positions of authority**
- A satyagrahi must be ready to **accept suffering** in the struggle against wrongdoing — this suffering is part of his love for truth
- Even while struggling against the wrongdoer, a true satyagrahi would have **no ill feeling or hatred** for the wrongdoer
- A true satyagrahi would **never bow before evil**, whatever the consequences
- **Only the brave and strong** could practise satyagraha — it was not for the weak or cowardly; even **violence was preferred to cowardice**
- Thought was never to be separated from practice — in other words, **ends could never justify the means**

---

## 🔷 PART 5: Gandhi Returns to India — Early Struggles (1915–1918)

Gandhi returned to India in **January 1915**. His South Africa work was well-known to both the educated classes and the masses.

**His initial approach:**
- Toured the country for **one year** to understand the condition of the masses firsthand
- Decided **not to take any position on political matters** for at least one year
- He was convinced about the **limitations of moderate politics**
- He was also **not in favour of the Home Rule agitation** then popular — he felt it was not the right time to agitate while Britain was at war
- He was convinced that the **only effective technique for nationalist aims was non-violent satyagraha**
- He declared he would **join no political organisation** unless it too accepted the creed of **non-violent satyagraha**

During **1917 and 1918**, Gandhi fought three crucial local battles — his first experiments on Indian soil.

---

### ⚔️ Battle 1: Champaran Satyagraha (1917) — First Civil Disobedience

**The Problem:**
- **Rajkumar Shukla** invited Gandhi to look into the problems of farmers in **Champaran, Bihar**
- **European planters** had been forcing peasants to grow indigo on **3/20 parts of their total land** — this was called the **tinkathia system**
- When **German synthetic dyes replaced indigo** towards the end of the 19th century, the planters began demanding **high rents and illegal dues** from peasants to maximise profits before the peasants could switch crops
- Peasants were also forced to **sell produce at prices fixed by the Europeans**

**Gandhi's arrival and defiance:**
- Gandhi reached Champaran, joined by **Rajendra Prasad, Mazharul-Haq, Mahadev Desai, Narhari Parekh, and J.B. Kripalani**
- Authorities **ordered Gandhi to leave** immediately
- Gandhi **defied the order** and preferred to face punishment
- This passive resistance of an unjust order was a **novel method at that time**
- Authorities retreated — **permitted Gandhi to make an enquiry**

**Outcome:**
- Government appointed a committee and **nominated Gandhi as a member**
- Gandhi convinced authorities to **abolish the tinkathia system**
- Peasants to be **compensated for illegal dues** extracted from them
- As a compromise with planters: only **25 per cent of the money taken** to be compensated
- Within **a decade**, the planters left the area entirely

**Gandhi had won the first battle of civil disobedience in India.**

Other popular leaders associated with Champaran Satyagraha: **Brajkishore Prasad, Anugrah Narayan Sinha, Ramnavmi Prasad, and Shambhusharan Varma**

---

### ⚔️ Battle 2: Ahmedabad Mill Strike (1918) — First Hunger Strike

**The Problem:**
- In **March 1918**, Gandhi intervened in a dispute between **cotton mill owners of Ahmedabad** and workers over the **discontinuation of a plague bonus**
- Workers demanded a **50 per cent wage rise** to cope with **wartime inflation** (which had doubled prices of food grains, cloth, and other necessities)
- Mill owners were ready to give only a **20 per cent wage hike**
- Workers went on strike; striking workers were **arbitrarily dismissed**; owners tried to bring in **weavers from Bombay**

**Key personalities:**
- Workers approached **Anusuya Sarabhai** — a social worker and — crucially — the **sister of Ambalal Sarabhai** (one of the mill owners and president of the **Ahmedabad Mill Owners Association**, founded in 1891)
- Anusuya Behn asked Gandhi to intervene
- Gandhi, despite being a **personal friend of Ambalal**, took up the **workers' cause**
- *(It was Anusuya Behn who later formed the **Ahmedabad Textile Labour Association in 1920**)*

**Gandhi's strategy:**
- Asked workers to demand **35 per cent increase** (not 50 per cent)
- Advised workers to remain **non-violent while on strike**
- When negotiations stalled, Gandhi undertook his **first-ever fast unto death** to strengthen the workers' resolve
- The fast also put **pressure on the mill owners**

**Outcome:**
- Mill owners agreed to submit the issue to a **tribunal**
- The strike was withdrawn
- The tribunal ultimately awarded workers a **35 per cent wage hike** — Gandhi's exact demand

---

### ⚔️ Battle 3: Kheda Satyagraha (1918) — First Non-Cooperation

**The Problem:**
- A **drought in 1918** caused crop failure in **Kheda district, Gujarat**
- Under the **Revenue Code**, if yield was less than **one-fourth the normal produce**, farmers were entitled to **remission** (waiver) of land revenue
- The **Gujarat Sabha** (a peasants' organisation) submitted petitions to provincial authorities requesting **suspension of revenue assessment for 1919**
- The government **refused** and threatened to **seize farmers' property** if taxes were not paid

**Gandhi's role:**
- Asked farmers **not to pay the taxes**
- Gandhi was primarily the **spiritual head** of the struggle

**The real organisational heroes:**
- **Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel** and a group of devoted Gandhians — **Narahari Parikh, Mohanlal Pandya, and Ravi Shankar Vyas** — went village to village, organised the people, and provided **political leadership**
- Patel organised a **tax revolt** that different ethnic and caste communities of Kheda supported

**What made it remarkable:**
- **Discipline and unity** were maintained throughout
- Even when the government **seized farmers' personal property, land, and livelihood**, the vast majority **did not abandon Patel**
- **Gujaratis elsewhere** sheltered the relatives and property of protesting peasants
- Indians who tried to **buy confiscated lands were socially ostracised** (boycotted)

**Outcome:**
- Government agreed to:
  - **Suspend the tax** for the year in question and the next
  - **Reduce the increase in rate**
  - **Return all confiscated property**

**Long-term impact:** The struggle at Kheda brought a **new awakening among the peasantry**. They became aware that they would not be free of injustice and exploitation until their country achieved **complete independence**.

---

### Gains from All Three Struggles

- ✅ Gandhi **demonstrated the efficacy** (effectiveness) of Satyagraha to the people
- ✅ He **found his feet among the masses** and came to understand their **strengths and weaknesses** deeply
- ✅ He **acquired the respect and commitment of many people**, especially the youth

## 🔷 PART 6: The Rowlatt Act — The "Black Act"

### Background

Just as Indians expected massive political gains after their war contributions, the British gave them the **Montford Reforms** (of very limited scope) AND the shockingly repressive **Rowlatt Act** — making Indians feel deeply betrayed.

### What Was the Rowlatt Act?

- **Six months before** the Montford Reforms were to take effect, two bills were introduced in the **Imperial Legislative Council**
- One was dropped; the other — an **extension to the Defence of India Regulations Act, 1915** — was **passed in March 1919**
- Official name: **Anarchical and Revolutionary Crimes Act**
- Popular name: **Rowlatt Act**
- Based on recommendations of the **Rowlatt Commission** — headed by **British judge Sir Sidney Rowlatt** — set up to investigate the **'seditious conspiracy' of the Indian people**

**What the Rowlatt Commission had recommended:**
- Activists should be **deported or imprisoned without trial** for two years
- Even **possession of seditious newspapers** would be adequate evidence of guilt

### What the Rowlatt Act Actually Did:

- Allowed political activists to be **tried without juries** or imprisoned **without trial**
- Allowed **arrest without warrant** on mere suspicion of 'treason'
- Suspects could be tried **in secrecy without legal help**
- A **special cell of three High Court judges** would try such suspects — with **no court of appeal above that panel**
- This panel could accept **evidence not acceptable** under the Indian Evidence Act
- The **law of habeas corpus** (the right to challenge unlawful detention — the bedrock of civil liberty) was effectively **suspended**
- Reimposed strict **wartime restrictions on freedom of speech and assembly**
- Armed the government with powers to deal with anything it chose to call **terrorism or revolutionary tactics**

### The Legislative Response

**All elected Indian members of the Imperial Legislative Council voted AGAINST the bill** — but were outvoted by official nominees.

All elected Indian members — including **Muhammad Ali Jinnah, Madan Mohan Malaviya, and Mazhar Ul Haq** — **resigned in protest**.

---

## 🔷 PART 7: Satyagraha Against the Rowlatt Act — First Mass Strike

Gandhi called the Rowlatt Act the **"Black Act"** and argued that not everyone should be punished for **isolated political crimes**.

### Gandhi's Call to Action

Gandhi called for a **mass protest at the all-India level**. Having seen constitutional protest meet with repression, he:
- Organised a **Satyagraha Sabha**
- Roped in younger members of **Home Rule Leagues** and **Pan-Islamists**

**Forms of protest chosen:**
- Observance of a **nationwide hartal (strike)** accompanied by **fasting and prayer**
- **Civil disobedience** against specific laws
- **Courting arrest and imprisonment**

### The Radical Transformation This Represented

This movement marked a complete break from the past:

1. The **masses had found a direction** — they could now **'act'** rather than just verbally express grievances
2. **Peasants, artisans, and the urban poor** were to play an increasingly important role in the struggle from now on
3. The **orientation of the national movement turned permanently towards the masses**
4. Gandhi declared: **salvation would come when the masses were awakened and became active in politics**

### Before the Satyagraha Even Launched...

Satyagraha was to be launched on **April 6, 1919**, but before it could begin, there were **large-scale violent, anti-British demonstrations** in **Calcutta, Bombay, Delhi, Ahmedabad**, and elsewhere.

In **Punjab**, the situation became explosively volatile due to:
- Wartime repression
- Forcible army recruitments
- Ravages of disease

The Army had to be called in. **April 1919 saw the biggest and most violent anti-British upsurge since 1857.**

The **Lieutenant Governor of Punjab, Sir Michael O'Dwyer**, is said to have used **aircraft strafing** against violent protestors.

---

## 🔷 PART 8: Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (April 13, 1919)

### The Build-Up in Amritsar

**Amritsar was the worst affected by violence.** Initially, the protests there were peaceful — Indians shut their shops and the empty streets expressed their displeasure at British betrayal.

**April 9:** Two nationalist leaders — **Saifuddin Kitchlew** and **Dr Satyapal** — were **arrested without any provocation** (they had only addressed protest meetings) and taken to an unknown destination.

**April 10:** Thousands came out to show solidarity with the arrested leaders. The police resorted to firing in which **some protestors were killed**. Tension exploded. In the subsequent riot, **five Englishmen were reportedly killed** and **Marcella Sherwood**, an English woman missionary, was beaten up.

Troops were sent immediately. **Brigadier-General Reginald Dyer** was the senior British officer responsible for imposing martial law and restoring order. By the time he arrived, the city had actually **returned to calm** and protests had become peaceful.

Dyer nonetheless issued a **proclamation on April 13** (which was also **Baisakhi**):
- Forbidding people from leaving the city without a pass
- Banning demonstrations, processions, or assemblies of more than three people

---

### The Massacre

On **Baisakhi day**, a large crowd — mostly from **neighbouring villages, unaware of the prohibitory orders** — gathered at **Jallianwala Bagh** (a popular public venue enclosed by walls with only one main exit) to celebrate the Baisakhi festival. Local leaders had also called for a protest meeting there.

**The crowd was approximately 20,000 people.** It is not clear how many were political protestors — the majority were festival-goers. The meeting had proceeded **peacefully**, passing two resolutions: one calling for repeal of the Rowlatt Act, and another condemning the April 10 firing.

**Then Brigadier-General Dyer arrived.**

- Troops **surrounded the gathering** under Dyer's orders
- They **blocked the only exit point**
- Without **any warning** and without **any instruction to disperse**, they **opened fire** on the unarmed crowd
- Men, women, and children were fired upon as they desperately tried to flee
- **Precisely 1,650 bullets were fired** into the crowd

**The casualties:**
- **British official sources:** 379 identified dead, approximately 1,100 wounded
- **Indian National Congress estimate:** Approximately 1,000 killed, more than 1,500 injured

---

### The Aftermath — A Reign of Terror

The massacre was followed by **uncivilised brutalities** on the inhabitants of Amritsar:
- **Martial law** was proclaimed in Punjab
- **Public floggings** and other humiliations were perpetrated
- In one notorious instance: Indians were forced to **crawl on their bellies** down the road on which the English missionary had been assaulted

---

### The National Reaction

- The **entire nation was stunned**
- **Rabindranath Tagore** renounced his **knighthood** in protest
  > *"The very least that I can do for my country is to take all consequences upon myself in giving voice to the protest of the millions of my countrymen."* — **Tagore to the Viceroy**
- **Gandhi** gave up the title of **Kaiser-i-Hind** (bestowed by the British for his work during the Boer War)
- Gandhi was overwhelmed by the total violence and **withdrew the movement on April 18, 1919**

---

### The Historical Significance

> *"Jallianwala Bagh massacre was the 'decisive moment when Indians were alienated from British rule'."*
> — Historian **A.P.J. Taylor**

- **General Dyer**, by his actions, **ensured the beginning of the end of the British Raj**
- Gandhi declared that **cooperation with a 'satanic regime' was now impossible**
- He realised that the **cause of Indian independence was morally righteous**
- The way to the **Non-Cooperation Movement** was now open

**Long-term political impact:**
- **Bhagat Singh** was just **11 years old** at the time of the massacre
- For **Bhagat Singh's Bharat Naujawan Sabha**, the massacre would later serve as a powerful symbol to overcome the political apathy that followed the Non-Cooperation Movement
- **Udham Singh** (who bore the name **Ram Mohammad Singh Azad**) later **assassinated Michael O'Dwyer** — the Lieutenant Governor who presided over the brutal suppression — and was **hanged in 1940** for his deed
- His ashes were **returned to India in 1974**

## 🔷 PART 9: The Hunter Committee of Inquiry

### Formation

The massacre shocked Indians and many British people. **Secretary of State for India Edwin Montagu** ordered an inquiry.

On **October 14, 1919**, the Government of India announced the **Disorders Inquiry Committee** — more widely known as the **Hunter Committee/Commission** after its chairman, **Lord William Hunter** (former Solicitor General for Scotland and Senator of the College of Justice in Scotland).

**Purpose:** To investigate the recent disturbances in **Bombay, Delhi, and Punjab**, their causes, and the measures taken to deal with them.

**Three Indian members:**
1. **Sir Chimanlal Harilal Setalvad** — Vice-Chancellor of Bombay University, and advocate of the Bombay High Court
2. **Pandit Jagat Narayan** — lawyer and Member of the Legislative Council of the United Provinces
3. **Sardar Sahibzada Sultan Ahmad Khan** — lawyer from Gwalior State

### Dyer's Testimony

Dyer was called before the committee. He was **unrepentant**. He stated:
- His intention had been to **strike terror throughout Punjab** and reduce the **moral stature of the 'rebels'**
- He believed he could have dispersed the crowd without firing but they would have "come back again and laughed" — making, in his words, "a fool of myself"
- He did **not make any effort to tend to the wounded** after the shooting — he did not consider it his job

### The Final Report (March 1920)

The report **unanimously condemned Dyer's actions**, stating:
- The lack of notice to disperse was **an error**
- The length of firing showed **a grave error**
- Dyer's motive of producing a "moral effect" was to be **condemned**
- Dyer had **overstepped the bounds of his authority**
- There had been **no conspiracy to overthrow British rule** in Punjab

**The minority report of the Indian members** went further:
- The prohibitory proclamations were **insufficiently publicised**
- There were **innocent people** in the crowd
- There had been **no violence in the Bagh** beforehand
- Dyer should have helped the wounded or instructed civil authorities to do so
- Dyer's actions were **"inhuman and un-British"** and had **greatly injured British rule's image** in India

### The Indemnity Act — The "White Washing Bill"

Before the Hunter Committee even began proceedings, the government had passed an **Indemnity Act** to protect its officers. This was called the **"white washing bill"** and was severely criticised by **Motilal Nehru** and others.

The Hunter Committee **imposed no penal or disciplinary action** because Dyer's actions had been condoned by his superiors (later upheld by the Army Council).

### Churchill's Response

In England, **Secretary of State for War Winston Churchill** reviewed the report in the House of Commons. Despite being no lover of Indians, Churchill **condemned the Amritsar events as "monstrous"**.

**Former Prime Minister H.H. Asquith** called it *"one of the worst outrages in the whole of our history"*.

The cabinet agreed Dyer was a **"dangerous man"** — he was dismissed from his command in **March 1920** and recalled to England. No **legal action** was taken against him — he drew **half pay and received his army pension**.

### The Divided British Response

Dyer was NOT universally condemned:
- In the **House of Lords**, most peers **favoured Dyer** and passed a motion in his support
- The **Morning Post** raised a sum of **26,000 pounds for Dyer** — a famous contributor was **Rudyard Kipling**
- In a deeply ironic development, the **clergy of the Golden Temple, led by Arur Singh, honoured Dyer by declaring him a Sikh**

This honouring of Dyer by the priests of **Sri Darbar Sahib, Amritsar**, was one of the triggers behind the intensification of demands for **reforming the management of Sikh shrines** — already being voiced by societies like the **Khalsa Diwan Majha** and the **Central Majha Khalsa Diwan**. This resulted in the launch of the **Gurudwara Reform Movement**.

### The Congress Committee

The INC appointed its own non-official committee including **Motilal Nehru, C.R. Das, Abbas Tyabji, M.R. Jayakar, and Gandhi**. The Congress view:
- Dyer's act was **inhuman**
- There was **no justification** for the introduction of martial law in Punjab

## 🌍 Modern Context

### [Modern Example 1] — Tinkathia System and Modern Farmer Distress
The **forced tinkathia system** that Gandhi fought in Champaran — where European planters dictated what peasants must grow and at what price — finds its modern parallel in debates about **contract farming** and **farm laws**. The **Farm Laws of 2020** (which were ultimately **repealed in November 2021** after a year-long farmers' protest) were seen by farmers as potentially creating a similar power imbalance — where large corporations could dictate cropping patterns and prices to small farmers. The farmers' protest at Delhi's borders in 2020–21 — involving over 700 deaths, according to Samyukta Kisan Morcha — was the largest sustained agricultural agitation since independence, directly echoing the Champaran spirit.

### [Modern Example 2] — Rowlatt Act and UAPA 2019
The **Rowlatt Act's** most dangerous provisions — arrest without warrant, trial without jury, detention without bail — are directly mirrored in the **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), amended in 2019**. The 2019 amendment allowed **individuals to be designated as terrorists** without trial. In **NIA vs. Zahoor Ahmad Shah Watali (2019)**, the Supreme Court upheld strict bail restrictions under UAPA. However, in **Union of India vs. K.A. Najeeb (2021)**, the Supreme Court reaffirmed that constitutional courts can grant bail when trials are indefinitely delayed — showing the same tension between security and liberty that the Rowlatt Act once created.

### [Modern Example 3] — Jallianwala Bagh — Centenary and British Apology
In **April 2019**, on the **centenary of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre**, British Prime Minister **Theresa May** described the massacre as a **"shameful scar on British Indian history"** but stopped short of issuing a formal apology. The **renovated Jallianwala Bagh National Memorial** was inaugurated by Prime Minister **Narendra Modi in August 2021**. The renovation was however **controversial** — historians and heritage experts criticised changes to the memorial's architecture that they said **diluted the historical authenticity** of the site, particularly the replacement of the original narrow passageway through which Dyer's troops entered.

### [Modern Example 4] — Satyagraha's Global Legacy
Gandhi's **Satyagraha technique** continues to inspire global protest movements. The **Black Lives Matter movement (2020)** in the USA, following the murder of George Floyd, explicitly invoked Gandhian non-violent resistance principles. The **Fridays for Future climate movement** led by **Greta Thunberg** — involving over 7.6 million participants across 161 countries in its September 2019 strike — also draws directly on the civil disobedience tradition that Gandhi first developed in South Africa and then refined in India.

### [Modern Example 5] — Dyarchy's Legacy: Governor vs. State Government Conflicts
The **dyarchy system** introduced by the Montford Reforms — where some subjects were under elected ministers and others under appointed officials — although abolished by the Constitution, has a living legacy in the **ongoing tensions between Governors and elected State Governments**. In **2021–2023**, multiple constitutional crises erupted in **Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Telangana, and West Bengal**, where Governors delayed assent to bills, dismissed ministers, or made controversial appointments. The **Supreme Court in 2023** (in cases involving the Tamil Nadu and Punjab Governors) ruled that Governors **cannot indefinitely withhold assent** to Bills — a ruling that directly addresses the same executive-legislature tension that made dyarchy unworkable in 1919.
