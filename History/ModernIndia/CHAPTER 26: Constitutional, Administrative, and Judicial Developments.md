# 📖 Chapter 26: Constitutional, Administrative, and Judicial Developments
### Unit 9 — India Under British Rule: Governance and Other Aspects

---

## 📦 CHAPTER AT A GLANCE
> **Read this first — 5 Big Ideas of this Chapter:**
>
> 1. 🏛️ Between **1773 and 1947**, a series of Acts gradually shifted power from the East India Company → British Crown → (eventually) Indian hands.
> 2. ⚖️ Every constitutional change was designed to serve **British imperial interests** — but unintentionally planted seeds of **modern democratic governance** in India.
> 3. 👔 **Civil Services** were deliberately kept out of Indian reach through language barriers, location of exams, and steadily lowered age limits.
> 4. 🚔 The **Police** and **Military** were reorganised after 1857 primarily as tools of **imperial control**, not public welfare.
> 5. 🏘️ **Local Self-Government** evolved slowly — **Lord Ripon's 1882 Resolution** was the most important milestone, but real financial power was always withheld.

---

## 🕰️ BACKGROUND: FROM TRADE TO RULE

- The **East India Company** was established in **1600** as a *trading body*.
- By **1765**, it had transformed into a *ruling power*.
- This shift had **little immediate impact** on Indian governance.
- But the period **1773 to 1858** (Company Rule) and **1858 to 1947** (Crown Rule) brought massive constitutional and administrative changes.

> 💡 **Key Idea to Remember:** These changes aimed to serve British imperial interests — but *unintentionally* introduced elements of the **modern State** into India's political and administrative system.

---

## 🏛️ PART 1: CONSTITUTIONAL DEVELOPMENT (1773–1858)

---

### 📍 Starting Point: After the Battle of Buxar (1764)

After the **Battle of Buxar (1764)**, the East India Company received the:
- **Diwani** *(right to collect revenue)* of **Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa**

**Financial obligations created:**
- Annual subsidy → to **Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II**
- Annual pension → to **Nawab of Awadh, Shuja-ud-Daula**

**Two Indians appointed as Deputy Diwans:**
- **Mohammad Reza Khan** → for Bengal
- **Raja Shitab Rai** → for Bihar

---

### ⚠️ 1767: First British Government Intervention
The British government demanded a **10% share in the plunder** — amounting to **4 million pounds annually**.

---

### 🔴 The Crisis: Dual System of Government (1765–72)

For **seven years**, a deeply flawed "dual system" *(two-headed, unaccountable governance structure)* operated:

| Who | Had | But Lacked |
|-----|-----|------------|
| The Company | Authority | Responsibility |
| Indian Representatives | Responsibility | Authority |

**Results of this broken system:**
- 🔴 **Rampant corruption** — Company servants used private trading to enrich themselves
- 🔴 **Excessive revenue collection** and oppression of the peasantry
- 🔴 **Company went bankrupt**, while individual servants flourished

> This crisis forced the British government to step in. A **gradual increase in controlling laws** now began.

---

### 📜 1. THE REGULATING ACT OF 1773

> **Simple Explanation:** Britain's first law to directly control the Company's affairs in India.

**Key Provisions:**
- First time **British government officially got involved** in Indian affairs
- Recognised the Company's role had extended **beyond trade** into administrative and political fields
- Introduced **centralised administration** *(power concentrated at one centre)*
- Company directors required to submit all correspondence on **revenue, civil, and military affairs** to the government
  - ✅ *First time the British Cabinet got the right to control Indian affairs*
- **In Bengal:** A **Governor General** + **council of four members** to govern, functioning by **majority rule**
  - **Warren Hastings** and four others named in the Act; later ones appointed by the Company
- A **Supreme Court** to be established in Bengal with **original and appellate jurisdictions** *(power to hear new cases AND appeals)*
  - ⚠️ In practice, Supreme Court's jurisdiction clashed with the council — created problems
- Governor General could exercise *some* powers over **Bombay and Madras** — vague provision, caused many problems
- The whole scheme was based on **checks and balances** *(one branch limits another's power)*

**Amendments of 1781 (Fixes to the 1773 Act):**
- Supreme Court's jurisdiction **defined** — within Calcutta, it would administer the **personal law of the defendant**
- Government servants given **immunity** for actions done in official capacity
- **Social and religious usages** of subjects to be honoured

---

### 📜 2. PITT'S INDIA ACT OF 1784

> **Simple Explanation:** Made the Company a subordinate arm of the British State.

**Key Provisions:**
- The Company became practically a **subordinate department of the State**
- Company's territories in India officially termed **'British possessions'**
- A **Board of Control** set up, consisting of:
  - Chancellor of Exchequer
  - A Secretary of State
  - Four members of the Privy Council (appointed by the Crown)
- This Board controlled the Company's **civil, military, and revenue affairs**
- All dispatches had to be **approved by the Board**
- ✅ Created a **dual system of control** *(Board of Control in London + Court of Directors)*
- In India: Governor General to have a **council of three** (including commander-in-chief)
- **Bombay and Madras** made subordinate to the Governor General
- A **general prohibition** placed on aggressive wars and treaties *(breached often in practice)*

---

### 📜 3. THE ACT OF 1786

- **Cornwallis** wanted combined powers of both **Governor General AND Commander-in-Chief**
- The new Act granted this demand
- Cornwallis was allowed to **override the council's decision** if he personally owned responsibility for it
- This provision was **later extended to all Governors General**

---

### 📜 4. THE CHARTER ACT OF 1793

**Key Provisions:**
- Company's **commercial privileges renewed** for the next 20 years
- After paying expenses, interest, dividends, and salaries from Indian revenues, the Company was to pay **5 lakh pounds annually** to the British government
- **Royal approval** made mandatory for appointment of Governor General, Governors, and Commander-in-Chief
- Senior Company officials **debarred from leaving India** without permission — doing so treated as resignation
- Company empowered to give **licences** (known as 'privilege' or 'country trade') to individuals and employees to trade in India
  - ⚠️ These licences **paved the way for opium shipments to China**
- **Revenue administration separated from judiciary** — led to disappearance of the **Maal Adalats** *(revenue courts)*
- **Home Government members** to be paid out of **Indian revenues** — this continued up to **1919**

---

### 📜 5. THE CHARTER ACT OF 1813

> **Context:** In England, businessmen wanted an end to the Company's monopoly due to the spirit of **laissez-faire** *(free market, minimal government interference)* and **Napoleon's Continental System** (which closed European ports to Britain).

**Key Provisions:**
- Company's **monopoly over trade in India ended** — but retained trade with China and in **tea**
- Company's shareholders given a **10.5% dividend** on India's revenue
- Company to retain possession of territories and revenue for **20 more years** without affecting Crown's sovereignty
  - ✅ *Constitutional position of British territories in India explicitly defined for the FIRST TIME*
- Powers of the **Board of Control** further enlarged
- **₹1 lakh per year** to be set aside for revival, promotion, and encouragement of **literature, learning, and science** among Indians
  - ✅ *Important: First statement of the State's responsibility for education*
- Regulations of **Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta Councils** now required to be laid before the **British Parliament**
- **Separate accounts** to be kept for commercial transactions and territorial revenues
- **Christian missionaries** permitted to come to India and preach

---

### 📜 6. THE CHARTER ACT OF 1833

> **Simple Explanation:** Created the most centralised administration yet — and opened the door to European colonisation.

**Key Provisions:**
- Lease of 20 years to the Company **further extended**; territories to be governed in the **name of the Crown**
- Company's **monopoly over trade with China and in tea also ended** completely
- **All restrictions on European immigration** and acquisition of property in India **lifted**
  - ⚠️ Paved the way for wholesale **European colonisation of India**

**Massive Administrative Centralisation:**
- Governor General given power to **superintend, control, and direct all civil and military affairs**
- **Bengal, Madras, Bombay**, and all other territories placed under **complete control** of the Governor General
- All revenues raised under the **authority of the Governor General** with complete control over expenditure
- **Madras and Bombay** drastically deprived of their **legislative powers** — left only with right to propose laws to the Governor General

**Other Key Provisions:**
- A **law member** added to the Governor General's council for professional legal advice
- Indian laws to be **codified and consolidated**
- **No Indian citizen** to be denied employment under the Company on basis of **religion, colour, birth, or descent**
  - ⚠️ Reality was different — but this declaration became the **"sheet-anchor of political agitation"** in India
- Administration urged to **ameliorate conditions of slaves** and ultimately **abolish slavery**
  - ✅ Slavery was abolished in **1843**

---

### 📜 7. THE CHARTER ACT OF 1853

**Key Provisions:**
- Company to **continue possession of territories** unless Parliament decided otherwise
- Strength of **Court of Directors reduced to 18**
- Company's **patronage over services dissolved** — recruitment now through **open competitive examination**
- The **law member** became a **full member** of the Governor General's Executive Council
- Separation of **executive and legislative functions** progressed — **six additional members** added for legislative purposes
- **Local representation** introduced in the Indian legislature — the legislative wing became known as the **Indian Legislative Council**
  - ⚠️ BUT: Laws still needed Governor General's assent; Governor General could **veto** any bill

---

### 📜 8. THE ACT FOR BETTER GOVERNMENT OF INDIA, 1858

> **Context:** The **Revolt of 1857** had exposed the Company's limitations in governing a complex country. Accountability was minimal.

**Key Provisions:**
- India to be **governed by and in the name of the Crown** through a **Secretary of State** and a **council of 15**
- Secretary of State held initiative and final decision; council was **only advisory**
  - ✅ *The dual system introduced by Pitt's India Act finally came to an END*
- **Governor General became the Viceroy** — prestige increased, though authority did not necessarily
- The transfer of power from Company to Crown was **more formality than substance**

> As the text aptly puts it: *"It gave a decent burial to an already dead horse — the Company's administration."*

---

## 🏛️ PART 2: DEVELOPMENTS AFTER 1858 TILL INDEPENDENCE

---

### 📜 9. INDIAN COUNCILS ACT, 1861

**Key Advances:**
- The principle of **non-official representatives in legislative bodies** was accepted
- Laws to be made after due **deliberation** — law-making no longer the exclusive business of the executive
- **Portfolio system** introduced by **Lord Canning** — laid foundations of **cabinet government** in India
  - Each branch of administration had an official head responsible for its administration
- **Legislative powers restored** to Bombay and Madras; foundation of **legislative devolution** *(distributing law-making power to provinces)* laid

**⚠️ Serious Weaknesses of the 1861 Act:**
- Could NOT discuss important matters or **any financial matters** without prior government approval
- **No control over the budget**
- Could NOT discuss executive action
- Final passing of a bill needed **Viceroy's approval**
- Even if Viceroy approved, **Secretary of State could disallow** legislation
- Indian non-officials were from **elite sections only** — not representative of Indian opinion

---

### 📜 10. INDIAN COUNCILS ACT, 1892

> **Context:** The **Indian National Congress**, founded in **1885**, saw reform of councils as the *"root of all other reforms."*

**Key Provisions:**
- Number of **non-official members increased** in both central (Imperial) and provincial legislative councils
- **Indian Legislative Council** (the Governor General's Legislative Council) was enlarged
- **Universities, district boards, municipalities, zamindars, trade bodies, and chambers of commerce** empowered to recommend members to provincial councils
  - ✅ Introduced the **principle of representation** *(people having a voice in governance)*
- Though the word **'election'** was firmly avoided, an element of **indirect election** was accepted
- Members now entitled to **express views on financial statements** on the floor of legislatures
- Members could ask **questions** on public interest matters after giving **six days' notice**

---

### 📜 11. INDIAN COUNCILS ACT, 1909 — *Morley-Minto Reforms*

> **Simple Explanation:** First attempt to bring a representative and popular element into governance — but deeply flawed.

**Key Provisions:**
- Strength of the **Imperial Legislative Council increased**
- For the first time, an **Indian member** taken into the Executive Council of the Governor General
  - **Satyendra Prasad Sinha** — first Indian to join as **law member**
- Members of **Provincial Executive Councils** increased
- **Powers of legislative councils** (both central and provincial) increased

**⚠️ Critical Flaws:**
- Real power **remained with the government**; councils left with no function but **criticism**
- **Separate electorates for Muslims** introduced — created new and dangerous problems
- Muslims given **representation in excess of their population strength**
- **Income qualification** for Muslim voters kept **lower than for Hindus**
- System of election was **very indirect**
- Representation of the people at large remained **remote and unreal**

---

### 📜 12. GOVERNMENT OF INDIA ACT, 1919 — *Montague-Chelmsford Reforms*

> **Context:** In **August 1917**, Britain declared for the first time that its objective was to *gradually* introduce **responsible government** in India — but only as part of the British Empire.

**Key Provisions:**
- **Indian Legislative Council at the Centre replaced** by a **bicameral** *(two-house)* system:
  - **Council of State** (Upper House)
  - **Legislative Assembly** (Lower House)
  - Each house to have a **majority of directly elected members**
  - ✅ **Direct election introduced** — though franchise *(right to vote)* restricted by property, tax, or education qualifications
- **Communal representation extended** — separate electorates now for **Sikhs, Christians, and Anglo-Indians** (in addition to Muslims)
- **Dyarchy** *(dual governance — some subjects under Indian ministers, others under British)* introduced in provinces — a substantial step toward power transfer
- **Provincial legislature** to consist of **one house only** (legislative council)
- **Provincial and central budgets separated** for the first time — provincial legislatures authorised to make their own budgets
- **High Commissioner for India** appointed — to be based in London for six years, looking after Indian trade in Europe; some functions of Secretary of State transferred to this position
- **Secretary of State for India** now to be paid by the **British Exchequer** (not Indian revenue) — undoing an injustice from the **Charter Act of 1793**

**⚠️ Limitations of the 1919 Act:**
- No fulfilment of demand for **responsible government**
- Structure continued to be **unitary and centralised** despite some provincial devolution
- **Dyarchy in provinces failed**
- Central Legislature had **no power to replace the government**
- Powers in legislation and financial control were **limited and subject to overriding powers of the Governor General**
- Governor General could **veto bills** or **enact laws** he considered essential for safety/tranquility
- As **Subhash Kashyap** observes: the Indian legislature under the Act of 1919 was *"only a non-sovereign law-making body, powerless before the executive in all spheres."*

---

### 🔍 THE SIMON COMMISSION

- The 1919 Act had provided for a **Royal Commission** 10 years later to review its working
- In **November 1927** — **two years ahead of schedule** — the British government announced the **Indian Statutory Commission** (Simon Commission)
- **Report submitted: 1930**

**Recommendations:**
- **Abolish dyarchy**
- Extend **responsible government in provinces**
- Establish a **federation of British India and Princely States**
- **Continue communal electorates**

**What followed:**
- **Three Round Table Conferences** held to consider proposals
- **White Paper on Constitutional Reforms** published in **March 1933** — provisions for federal set-up and provincial autonomy
- A **joint parliamentary committee under Lord Linlithgow** set up
- **Report (1934):** A federation would be set up if **at least 50% of Princely States** were ready to join
- This report became the basis of the **Government of India Act, 1935**

---

### 📜 13. GOVERNMENT OF INDIA ACT, 1935

> **Simple Explanation:** The most detailed constitutional document India had seen — with **321 articles and 10 schedules** — but Indians had no hand in drafting it.

**Key Provisions:**

**Federation:**
- Contemplated an **All-India Federation** including Governors' Provinces, Chief Commissioners' Provinces, and willing Indian States
- Each Princely State ruler willing to join had to sign an **'instrument of accession'** specifying the extent of authority surrendered to the federal government
- ⚠️ The **All-India Federation never came into being** due to opposition from various parties

**Legislature:**
- **Federal Legislature** to be **bicameral**:
  - **Council of States** (Upper House — permanent body)
  - **Federal Legislative Assembly**
- Provision for **joint sitting** in case of deadlock
- **Three subject lists:** Federal Legislative List, Provincial Legislative List, and Concurrent Legislative List
- **Residuary powers** — subject to Governor General's discretion
- Governor General could **veto** bills passed by federal legislature
- Even acts assented to by Governor General could be **disallowed by the King-in-Council**

**Dyarchy:**
- **Dyarchy at federal level** — introduced (ironically, after Simon Commission had rejected it)
- **Dyarchy in provinces abolished** — provinces given **autonomy** (distinction between Reserved and Transferred Subjects removed; full responsible government established subject to safeguards)

**Provinces:**
- **Provinces derived power directly from the British Crown**
- Given **independent financial powers** — could borrow money on their own security
- **Provincial legislatures expanded**; **bicameral legislatures** in **six provinces**: Madras, Bombay, Bengal, United Provinces, Bihar, and Assam; five other provinces retained unicameral legislatures

**Franchise and Representation:**
- **'Communal electorates' and 'weightage'** *(giving extra seats beyond population share)* extended to **depressed classes, women, and labour**
- **Franchise extended** — about **10% of total population** got the right to vote

**Judiciary:**
- Provided for a **Federal Court** (established in **1937**) with original and appellate powers — to interpret the 1935 Act and settle inter-state disputes
- ⚠️ The **Privy Council in London** continued to dominate this court

**Other:**
- **India Council of the Secretary of State abolished**
- **Provincial autonomy introduced on April 1, 1937**, but Central government continued under the **1919 Act** with minor amendments
- The operative part of the 1935 Act remained in force till **August 15, 1947**

**Critical Evaluation of the 1935 Act:**
- ✅ An endeavour to give India a **written constitution**
- ✅ A step toward **complete responsible government**
- ❌ **Rigid constitution** — no possibility of internal growth
- ❌ **Right of amendment reserved for British Parliament** — Indians had no say
- ❌ Extension of **communal electorates** promoted **separatist tendencies** — ultimately contributing to **Partition of India**
- ❌ **Unanimously rejected by the Congress** — which demanded a **Constituent Assembly** elected on the basis of **adult franchise**

**Subsequent Developments (briefly):**
- **August Offer (1940)**
- **Cripps Proposals (1942)**
- **C.R. Formula (1944)** — to seek cooperation of Muslim League
- **Wavell Plan (1945)**
- **Cabinet Mission**
- **Mountbatten Plan (1947)**
- **Indian Independence Act, 1947**

## 👔 PART 3: EVOLUTION OF CIVIL SERVICES IN INDIA

---

### 📍 Origins

- The term **'civil service'** was originally used to distinguish Company servants in **commercial affairs** from those in **military and naval services**
- Gradually, civil servants were given broader responsibilities and authority

---

### 👤 Cornwallis' Role (Governor General, 1786–93)

**Cornwallis was the FIRST to organise civil services.** He tackled corruption by:
- ✅ **Raising civil servants' salaries**
- ✅ **Strict enforcement** of rules against private trade
- ✅ **Debarring** civil servants from taking presents, bribes, etc.
- ✅ Enforcing **promotions through seniority** *(not favouritism)*

---

### 👤 Wellesley's Role (Governor General, 1798–1805)

- **1800:** Set up **Fort William College** for training new recruits
- **1806:** Court of Directors disapproved of Wellesley's college
- Instead, **East India College** set up at **Haileybury, England** to give **two years' training** to recruits

---

### 📜 Charter Act of 1853 — Open Competition

- Ended the Company's **patronage** over civil services
- Recruitment to be through **open competition** henceforth
- ⚠️ BUT: Indians were **barred from high posts** from the very beginning

**Why were Indians excluded? Three key reasons:**
1. Belief that only the English could establish administrative services serving **British interests**
2. Belief that Indians were **incapable, untrustworthy**, and insensitive to British interests
3. High competition among **Europeans themselves** for lucrative posts — no incentive to share with Indians

> Cornwallis had bluntly declared: *"Every native of Hindustan is corrupt."*

> The **Charter Act of 1793** had already reserved all posts worth **500 pounds per annum** for **covenanted servants** *(those formally contracted)* of the Company.

> Although the **Charter Act of 1833** theoretically threw services open to Indians, these provisions were **never really implemented**.

> After **1857**, the **Proclamation of 1858** declared British intention to include Indians *"freely and impartially"* — but this too remained largely on paper.

---

### 📜 Indian Civil Service Act of 1861

- Certain offices **reserved for covenanted civil servants**
- Examination held in **England, in English**, based on **classical Greek and Latin learning**
- Maximum permissible age **progressively reduced:**
  - **23** (1859) → **22** (1860) → **21** (1866) → **19** (1878)
  - ⚠️ This deliberate reduction made it almost **impossible for Indians** to qualify (they had to travel to England, learn Latin/Greek, etc.)
- **1863: Satyendra Nath Tagore** became the **first Indian to qualify** for the Indian Civil Service

---

### 📜 Statutory Civil Service (1878–79)

- **Lytton** introduced this — **one-sixth of covenanted posts** to be filled by **Indians of high families**
- Through **nominations by local governments**, subject to approval by Secretary of State and Viceroy
- ❌ The system **failed and was abolished**

---

### 📜 Congress Demand and the Aitchison Committee (1886)

**Congress demands (from 1885):**
- Lowering of **age limit** for recruitment
- Holding examination **simultaneously in India AND Britain**

**Aitchison Committee on Public Services (1886)**, set up by **Dufferin**, recommended:
- Dropping the terms **'covenanted' and 'uncovenanted'**
- Classification into three tiers:
  - **Imperial Indian Civil Service** — examination in England
  - **Provincial Civil Service** — examination in India
  - **Subordinate Civil Service** — examination in India
- **Raising age limit to 23**

> In **1893**, the **House of Commons** passed a resolution supporting **simultaneous examination** in India and England — but it was **never implemented**.

> **Kimberley**, the Secretary of State, stated: *"It is indispensable that an adequate number of members of civil service shall always be Europeans."*

---

### 📜 Montford Reforms, 1919

Three key points:
- Stated a realistic policy: *"If a responsible government is to be established in India, the more Indians we can employ in public service, the better"*
- Recommended **simultaneous examination** in India and England
- Recommended **one-third of recruitments** be made in India itself — to be raised annually by **1.5%**

---

### 📜 Lee Commission (1924)

**Recommendations:**
- Secretary of State should continue to recruit the **ICS, Irrigation branch of Engineers, Indian Forest Service**, etc.
- Recruitments for **transferred fields** (education, civil medical service) to be made by **provincial governments**
- **Direct recruitment to ICS on 50:50 parity** (Europeans: Indians) to be reached in **15 years**
- A **Public Service Commission** to be immediately established (as required by the **Government of India Act, 1919**)

---

### 📜 Government of India Act, 1935

- Recommended establishment of:
  - **Federal Public Service Commission**
  - **Provincial Public Service Commissions**
- ⚠️ BUT: Positions of **control and authority remained in British hands**
- Process of **Indianisation** did NOT put effective political power in Indian hands — Indian bureaucrats continued to act as **agents of colonial rule**

---

### 📊 Critical Evaluation of Civil Services Under British Rule

**Two key methods of keeping Indians out:**

1. **Structural barriers:**
   - ICS exam held in **London, in English**, with **classical Greek and Latin** as subjects
   - Age limit reduced from **23 (1859) to 19 (1878)** under Lytton — deliberately making it harder

2. **Power concentration:**
   - All **key, high-paying positions occupied by Europeans**
   - Slow **Indianisation after 1918** under nationalist pressure, but senior positions stayed with Europeans

> **Ultimate conclusion:** Indianisation of civil service had **not transferred effective power into Indian hands**. Indian civil servants continued to serve **imperialist interests**.

---

## 🌍 MODERN CONTEXT — Civil Services

> `[Modern Example]` — *AI-added section for current affairs enrichment.*

- **[Modern Example]** Post-independence, India established the **Union Public Service Commission (UPSC)** under **Article 315** of the Constitution — directly addressing colonial exclusion. Today, the **Civil Services Examination (CSE)** is held across India with **no language or origin barrier**.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Baswan Committee Report (2016)** and **ongoing debates about lateral entry** into the IAS (with **45 lateral entry appointments made in 2023**) echo the colonial-era question: *Who should run the bureaucracy, and on whose behalf?*
- **[Modern Example]** In **2023**, the government notified **lateral entry** into Joint Secretary and Director level posts — a policy that revives debates about whether the **steel frame** (IAS) still serves citizen interests or bureaucratic ones — a question rooted in colonial civil service history.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Mission Karmayogi** scheme (2020) — a National Programme for Civil Services Capacity Building — is India's modern answer to colonial-era **Haileybury College**, aiming to shift from **rule-based** to **role-based** governance.

## 🚔 PART 4: EVOLUTION OF POLICE SYSTEM IN MODERN INDIA

---

### 📍 Pre-Colonial Police System

- Pre-colonial governments (Mughals and native states) were **autocratic** with **no separate or formal police system**
- However, **watch guards** protected villages at night since ancient times
- Under **Mughal rule:**
  - **Faujdars** — maintained law and order
  - **Amils** — revenue collectors who also dealt with rebels
  - **Kotwal** — maintained law and order in **cities**
- During **dual rule in Bengal, Bihar, Orissa (1765–72):**
  - **Zamindars** responsible for maintaining **thanedars** *(local police officers)* for law and order
  - ⚠️ Zamindars often **neglected duties** and were said to have **colluded with dacoits** and shared their loot

---

### 📅 Chronological Evolution of the Police System

#### 1770
- Institutions of the **faujdar and amils abolished**

#### 1774
- **Warren Hastings restored** the institution of faujdars
- Zamindars asked to assist faujdars in suppressing dacoits, violence, and disorder

#### 1775
- **Faujdar thanas** *(police posts)* established in major towns of large districts
- Assisted by several smaller police stations

#### 1791 — Cornwallis' Reform
- **Cornwallis organised a regular police force**
- Modernised the old Indian system of **thanas** *(circles)* in a district
- Each district placed under a **daroga** *(an Indian officer)* with a **Superintendent of Police (SP)** at the district head
- **Zamindars relieved of police duties**

#### 1808 — Mayo
- **SP appointed for each division**
- Assisted by **spies (goyendas)** *(intelligence informers)*
- ⚠️ These spies **committed depredations** (abuses) on local people

#### 1814
- By **Court of Directors' order**: appointment of **darogas and their subordinates abolished** in all Company possessions **except Bengal**

#### 1828–35 — Bentinck's Changes
- **Bentinck (Governor General, 1828–35)** abolished the office of SP
- **Collector/Magistrate** now headed police in his jurisdiction
- **Commissioner** in each division acted as SP
- ⚠️ Result: **Badly organised police force** — heavy burden on collector/magistrate
- **Presidency towns** were first to have collector/magistrate duties separated

---

### 📜 Indian Police Act, 1861

Based on recommendations of the **Police Commission (1860):**

**Structure recommended:**
- A system of **civil constabulary** — maintaining village watchmen in direct relationship with the larger constabulary
- Hierarchy:
  - **Inspector-General** → head of province
  - **Deputy Inspector-General** → head of a range
  - **SP** → head of a district

**Results:**
- Police gradually **curbed criminal acts** like **dacoity** *(organised robbery)* and **thugee** *(ritual murder/robbery gangs)*
- ⚠️ BUT: Attitude towards the **public remained unsympathetic**
- ⚠️ Police used to **suppress the national movement**
- The British **did NOT create an All-India Police**
- The 1861 Act provided guidelines for **provincial police setup** — ranks uniformly introduced across the country

---

### 📜 Fraser Commission / Police Commission (1902–03)

Under **Curzon (Governor General)**, the **Police Commission under Sir Andrew Frazer** recommended:

- **Junior officials should NOT be promoted** to high positions — **senior officials to be directly recruited**
- Opening of **training schools** for constables and officers
- **Increasing strength** of police force in all provinces
- Allowing policemen to **visit villages** for inquiries
- **Increasing salaries**
- Creating a **Criminal Intelligence Department at the Centre**

**What happened:**
- **Department of Criminal Intelligence (DCI)** attached to the Government of India — became the **central domestic and foreign intelligence agency**
- **Criminal Investigation Departments (CIDs)** set up in **all provinces** of British India
- In **1929:** CID was divided into:
  - **Special Branch**
  - **CID**
  - **Crime Branch**

> ⚠️ In the years that followed, the Indian police became an **agency to sustain the British Raj** — and in suppressing the nationalist movement, the **police lost the empathy of the Indian masses**.

## ⚔️ PART 5: MILITARY UNDER THE BRITISH

---

### 📍 Pre-1857: Two Separate Military Forces

Before the **Revolt of 1857**, two distinct military forces operated in India:

| Force | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Queen's Army** | Serving troops on duty in India — part of Crown's military force |
| **Company's Troops** | Mix of European regiments (Britons) + Native regiments (locally recruited Indians with British officers) |

---

### 📍 Reorganisation After 1857

> As **Dufferin warned in December 1888:** *"...the British should always remember the lessons which were learnt with such terrible experience 30 years ago."*

**Two main objectives of reorganisation:**
1. **Prevent another revolt** — the primary reason
2. **Use Indian Army** to defend territory from other imperial powers: Russia, Germany, France, etc.

**Strategic division of roles:**
- **Indian branch** → used for expansion in Asia and Africa
- **British section** → used as an **army of occupation** — the ultimate guarantee of British hold over India

---

### 📊 Key Discriminatory Military Policies

**1. Ensuring European Dominance:**
- **Commissions of 1859 and 1879** insisted on the principle of a **one-third white army** (vs. 14% before 1857)
- Proportion of Europeans to Indians carefully **fixed:**
  - **Bengal Army:** 1 European : 2 Indians
  - **Madras and Bombay Armies:** 2 Europeans : 5 Indians
- **Strict European monopoly** over key locations and departments: **artillery, tanks, armed corps**
- Indians given **inferior quality rifles** till 1900
- Indians **NOT allowed in high-tech departments** till the **Second World War**

**2. Officer Rank Discrimination:**
- **No Indians allowed in officer rank**
- Highest rank an Indian could reach **till 1914:** **Subedar** *(junior commissioned officer)*
- Only **from 1918 onwards** were Indians allowed in **commissioned ranks**
- As late as **1926**, the **Indian Sandhurst Committee** was visualising a **50% Indianised officer cadre for 1952!**

**3. Divide and Rule Policy:**
- Indian branch reorganised on the basis of **"balance and counterpoise"** *(divide-and-rule)*
- The **1879 Army Commission** stated: *"Next to the grand counterpoise of a sufficient European force comes the counterpoise of natives against natives."*

**4. The 'Martial Races' Ideology:**
- From the **late 1880s**, under **Lord Roberts** (Commander-in-Chief, **1887–1892**), an ideology of **'martial races' and 'non-martial races'** developed
- Assumed: Good soldiers could come **only from specific communities**
- Used to justify discriminatory recruitment **towards Sikhs, Gurkhas, and Pathans** — who had helped suppress the revolt and were marginal social groups (less likely to be affected by nationalism)
- Soldiers from **Awadh, Bihar, Central India, and South India** who had participated in the revolt were declared **'non-martial'**

**5. Further Fragmentation:**
- **Caste and communal companies** introduced in all regiments
- Indian regiments made **mixtures of various socio-ethnic groups** to balance each other
- **Communal, caste, tribal, and regional consciousness** deliberately encouraged to check **nationalist feelings**

> **Charles Wood, Secretary of State for India**, said: *"I wish to have a different and rival spirit in different regiments, so that Sikh might fire into Hindu, Gorkha into either, without any scruple in case of need."*

**6. Isolation from Nationalism:**
- Conscious efforts to **isolate soldiers** from the general population
- Newspapers, journals, and **nationalist publications prevented** from reaching soldiers

> ⚠️ On the whole, the **British Indian Army remained a costly military machine** — maintained for imperial, not Indian, interests.

- **[Modern Example]** **Agnipath Scheme (2022)** — India's new short-term military recruitment scheme — reignited debates about the **nature, purpose, and inclusivity** of military service, echoing colonial-era questions about who serves and why.

## ⚖️ PART 6: DEVELOPMENT OF JUDICIARY IN BRITISH INDIA

---

### 📍 Pre-Colonial Judicial System

India's pre-colonial judicial system had three major weaknesses:
- **No proper procedures**
- **No organised hierarchy of courts** (from highest to lowest)
- **No proper distribution of courts** proportional to area served

**How disputes were settled:**
- **Hindu disputes** → caste elders, village panchayats, or zamindars
- **Muslim disputes** → the **qazi** *(religious judicial officer)* located in provincial capitals, towns, and qasbas *(large villages)*
- **Rajas and Badshahs** were the fountainhead of justice — dispensing justice could be **arbitrary** *(based on personal whim)*

---

### 📍 Beginning of Common Law: Mayor's Courts (1726)

- The beginning of a **common law system** *(based on recorded judicial precedents — earlier decisions)* can be traced to the establishment of **'Mayor's Courts'** in **Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta in 1726** by the East India Company

---

### 👤 Reforms under Warren Hastings (1772–85)

**Civil Courts:**
- **District Diwani Adalats** established in districts to try **civil disputes**
  - Placed under the collector
  - **Hindu law** for Hindus, **Muslim law** for Muslims
  - Appeals lay to the **Sadar Diwani Adalat** (functioning under a president and two Supreme Council members)

**Criminal Courts:**
- **District Fauzdari Adalats** set up to try **criminal disputes**
  - Under an Indian officer assisted by **qazis and muftis**
  - Under general supervision of the collector
  - **Muslim law** administered here
  - Appeals for **capital punishment** and **acquisition of property** lay to the **Sadar Nizamat Adalat at Murshidabad** (headed by a deputy nizam — an Indian Muslim — assisted by chief qazi and chief mufti)

**Supreme Court (under Regulating Act, 1773):**
- **Supreme Court established at Calcutta**
- Competent to try **all British subjects** within Calcutta and subordinate factories — including **Indians and Europeans**
- Had **original and appellate jurisdictions**
- ⚠️ Jurisdiction **frequently clashed** with other courts

---

### 👤 Reforms under Cornwallis (1786–93) — Separation of Powers

- **District Fauzdari Courts abolished** — replaced by **circuit courts** at Calcutta, Dacca, Murshidabad, and Patna
  - These had **European judges** and acted as courts of appeal for both civil and criminal cases
- **Sadar Nizamat Adalat** shifted to **Calcutta** — placed under Governor General and Supreme Council members, assisted by chief qazi and chief mufti
- **District Diwani Adalat** renamed as **District, City, or Zila Court** — under a **district judge**
- **Collector** now responsible **only for revenue administration** — no magisterial functions

**The Cornwallis Code — A Six-Level Gradation of Civil Courts (important for memorisation):**

| Level | Court |
|-------|-------|
| 1 (Lowest) | **Munsiff's Court** — under Indian officers |
| 2 | **Registrar's Court** — under a European judge |
| 3 | **District Court** — under the district judge |
| 4 | **Four Circuit Courts** — provincial courts of appeal |
| 5 | **Sadar Diwani Adalat** at Calcutta |
| 6 (Highest) | **King-in-Council** — for appeals of **5,000 pounds and above** |

**The Cornwallis Code Established Four Principles:**
1. **Separation** of revenue and justice administration
2. **European subjects** also brought under jurisdiction
3. **Government officials answerable** to civil courts for official actions
4. Principle of **sovereignty of law** *(law is supreme, even over officials)* established

---

### 👤 Reforms under William Bentinck (1828–33)

- **Four Circuit Courts abolished** — functions transferred to collectors under supervision of commissioner of revenue and circuit
- **Sadar Diwani Adalat AND Sadar Nizamat Adalat** set up at **Allahabad** for people of the Upper Provinces
- **Language change:** Persian replaced as court language — suitors now had the option to use **Persian or a vernacular language**; in the Supreme Court, **English replaced Persian**

**1833 — Macaulay Law Commission:**
- Set up under **Macaulay** for codification of Indian laws
- This led to:
  - **Civil Procedure Code (1859)**
  - **Indian Penal Code (1860)**
  - **Criminal Procedure Code (1861)**

---

### 📅 Later Developments

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| **1860** | Europeans can claim **no special privileges** except in criminal cases; no judge of **Indian origin** could try them |
| **1865** | **Supreme Court and Sadar Adalats merged** into **three High Courts** at Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras |
| **1935** | Government of India Act provided for a **Federal Court** (set up in **1937**) — could settle disputes between governments and hear limited appeals from High Courts |

---

### 📊 Evaluation of the Judiciary Under British Rule

**✅ Positive Aspects:**
- **Rule of law established**
- **Codified laws replaced** religious and personal laws of rulers
- Even **European subjects brought under jurisdiction** (though with privilege in criminal cases)
- **Government servants made answerable** to civil courts

**❌ Negative Aspects:**
- Judicial system became **more complicated and expensive** — the rich could manipulate it
- **Ample scope for false evidence, deceit, and chicanery** *(trickery)*
- **Dragged-out litigation** meant **delayed justice**
- **Courts became overburdened** as litigation increased
- **European judges** often **unfamiliar with Indian usage and traditions**

---

## 🌍 MODERN CONTEXT — Judiciary

> `[Modern Example]` — *AI-added section for current affairs enrichment.*

- **[Modern Example]** The **Indian Penal Code (1860)**, drafted by Macaulay, was finally replaced by the **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023** — along with the **Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS)** and **Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA)** — ending 163 years of colonial criminal law. These came into force on **July 1, 2024**.
- **[Modern Example]** The problem of **court overburdening** — noted as a negative of the British judicial system — persists. As of **2024**, India has **over 5 crore (50 million) pending cases** across all courts, according to the **National Judicial Data Grid (NJDG)**.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Supreme Court's All India Judges' Association judgment** and the **National Court Management Systems (NCMS)** policy are modern efforts to fix the colonial legacy of **delayed justice**.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Supreme Court's Constitution Bench judgment in S.G. Vombatkere vs Union of India (2022)** struck down **Section 124A (Sedition)** of the IPC as requiring reconsideration — directly addressing a colonial-era law used to suppress nationalists like Bal Gangadhar Tilak and Mahatma Gandhi.

## 🏗️ PART 7: MAJOR CHANGES IN ADMINISTRATIVE STRUCTURE AFTER 1857

---

### 📍 Genesis: A New Stage of Colonialism

**Why did the British change their administrative approach after 1857?**

The British were quick to **learn from 1857** — an organised mass action could pose a serious challenge to British rule.

**Two-pronged strategy:**
1. **Narrow the ruler-subject gap** — to reduce alienation of the masses from administration
2. **Associate natives in administration** — to better understand customs, traditions, and values for more effective rule

**International Context (Second half of 19th century):**
- **Industrial Revolution** spread and intensified
- New industrial powers emerged: **USA, Japan, and European countries**
- **Cut-throat competition** for colonies and sub-colonies for:
  - Raw materials
  - Markets for manufactured goods
  - Capital investment
- **British supremacy in finance and trade came to an end**

**British Investments in India:**
- Large-scale investments in **railways and loans to Government of India**
- Smaller investments in **tea plantations, coal-mining, jute mills, shipping, trade, and banking**

> All these factors together inaugurated a **new stage of colonialism** in India — focused on **consolidating British economic and commercial interests**.

> This reactionary trend was reflected in the policies during the vice-royalties of **Lytton, Dufferin, Lansdowne, Elgin**, and, above all, **Curzon**.

## 🏢 PART 8: ADMINISTRATION — CENTRAL, PROVINCIAL, AND LOCAL

---

### 🏛️ Central Government (Post-1858)

**1858 Act Changes:**
- Power transferred from East India Company to **British Crown**
- Governed through a **Secretary of State** (a member of British Cabinet) assisted by a **council of 15**
- Secretary of State answerable to **British Parliament**
- All initiative and final decisions with the Secretary; council **only advisory**
- The **Viceroy** (previously Governor General) continued to govern India — assisted by an **Executive Council** whose members headed various departments

**The Legislative Council under the 1861 Act:**
- A **fifth member** (a jurist) added to Viceroy's Executive Council
- For legislation, Viceroy could add **6 to 12 additional members** — at least half had to be **non-officials** (Indian or English)

**Weaknesses of this Legislative Council:**
- Could NOT discuss important matters or **any financial matters** without prior government approval
- **No control over the budget**
- Could NOT discuss executive action
- Final bill passage required **Viceroy's approval**
- Even Viceroy-approved legislation could be **disallowed by Secretary of State**
- Indian non-officials were **princes, landlords, diwans, etc.** — NOT representative of Indian opinion
- Viceroy could issue **ordinances** of **six months' validity** in emergencies

> The legislative council's only real function was to **endorse official measures** and give them the *appearance* of having been passed by a legislative body. The British Government remained **an alien despotism** *(foreign authoritarian power)*.

---

### 🏛️ Provincial Government

- **Indian Councils Act, 1861** returned legislative powers to **Madras and Bombay** (taken away in 1833)
- Later, legislative councils established in other provinces

**Three Presidencies (Bombay, Madras, Calcutta):**
- Enjoyed **more rights and powers**
- Administered by a **Governor + Executive Council of three** (appointed by the Crown)

**Other Provinces:**
- Administered by **Lieutenant Governors and Chief Commissioners** (appointed by Governor General)

**Financial Decentralisation — Step by Step:**

| Year | Step | Who Did It |
|------|------|-----------|
| **1870** | Fixed sums from central revenues for police, jails, education, medical services, and roads given to provinces | **Lord Mayo** |
| **1877** | Land revenue, excise, general administration, law and justice transferred to provinces; provinces got fixed share of stamps, excise, income tax | **Lord Lytton** |
| **1882** | Revenue divided into three groups: **General** (entirely central), **Provincial** (entirely provincial), and **Shared** | — |

> ⚠️ Despite these steps, the **central government remained supreme** — both central and provincial governments were completely subordinated to the **Secretary of State and British Government**.

---

### 🏘️ Local Bodies

**Purpose:** Decentralise administration through **municipalities and district boards** to administer local services (education, health, sanitation, water supply, roads) financed through **local taxes**.

**Five Reasons the British Established Local Bodies:**

1. **Financial difficulties** due to overcentralisation made decentralisation imperative
2. **Modern civic amenities** from Europe needed to be transplanted in India given increasing economic contact
3. **Rising nationalism** had improvement in basic facilities on its agenda
4. **Associating Indians** with administration (in limited ways) without undermining British supremacy — to check increasing **politicisation of Indians**
5. **Utilising local taxes** for local welfare — to counter public criticism of British reluctance to tax the wealthy

---

### 📅 Evolution of Local Government — Stage by Stage

#### 1864–1868: First Local Bodies
- Local bodies first formed — but consisted of **nominated members**, headed by **district magistrates**
- Seen essentially as **instruments of additional tax collection** — not genuine self-governance

#### Mayo's Resolution of 1870
- **Financial decentralisation** began under **Lord Mayo**
- Provincial governments authorised to use **local taxation** to balance budgets
- Covered services like medical, education, and roads transferred to provincial control
- ✅ **Beginning of local finance**

> **Mayo emphasised:** *"Local interest, supervision and care are necessary for success in the management of the funds devoted to education, sanitation, medical relief and local public works."*

- Provincial governments (Bengal, Madras, North-Western Province, Punjab) passed **municipal acts** to implement the policy

---

#### 🌟 Ripon's Resolution of 1882 — The Most Important Milestone

> **Lord Ripon is called the 'Father of Local Self-Government in India'** for this resolution.

**Main Points:**
- Development of local bodies advocated as both an **administrative improvement AND an instrument of political and popular education**
- Policy of administering local affairs through **urban and rural local bodies** with definite duties and suitable revenue sources
- **Non-officials to be in MAJORITY** in these bodies — and could be elected if officials thought it feasible to introduce elections
- **Non-officials to act as chairpersons**
- **Official interference to be MINIMUM** — only to revise and check acts of local bodies, NOT to dictate policies
- Official executive sanction still required for: raising loans, alienation of municipal property, imposition of new taxes, works beyond prescribed cost, framing rules and bye-laws

**Acts passed between 1883–1885** greatly altered constitution, powers, and functions of municipal bodies.

**⚠️ But — drawbacks remained:**
- Elected members were in **minority** in all district boards and many municipalities
- **Franchise was very limited**
- **District boards** continued to be headed by district officials (non-officials gradually came to head municipalities)
- Government retained **strict control** — could **suspend or supersede** local bodies at will
- Bureaucracy thought Indians were **unfit for self-government**
- **Lord Curzon** actually took steps to **increase official control** over local bodies (in the closing decades of the 19th century)

---

#### Royal Commission on Decentralisation (1908)

The Commission pointed out **lack of financial resources** as the main stumbling block.

**Recommendations:**
1. **Village panchayats** to be entrusted with more powers: judicial jurisdiction in petty cases, minor village works, village schools, small fuel and fodder reserves — with adequate income sources
2. **Sub-district boards** to be established in every taluka or tehsil with separate duties and revenue sources
3. **Withdrawal of restrictions on taxation powers** — stop regular grants-in-aid from provincial governments except for large projects
4. **Municipalities to take responsibility for primary education**; if willing, for middle vernacular schools; government to relieve them of secondary education, hospitals, relief, police, veterinary works charges

#### Government of India Resolution, 1915
- Contained official views on the Decentralisation Commission recommendations
- ⚠️ Most recommendations **remained on paper** — condition of local bodies unchanged from Ripon's time

#### Resolution of May 1918
- Reviewed local self-government in light of the **August 20, 1917 announcement** (gradual responsible government)
- Suggested local bodies be made **as representative as possible** — with **real, not nominal authority**

---

#### Under Dyarchy (Post-1919)
- **Local self-government made a 'transferred' subject** under popular ministerial control by the **Government of India Act, 1919**
- Each province allowed to develop local self-institutions according to **provincial needs**
- ⚠️ BUT: Finance was a **'reserved' subject** — Indian ministers couldn't do much due to **lack of funds**

> **Simon Commission (May 1930)** found lack of progress of village panchayats (except in United Provinces, Bengal, and Madras). It suggested **increasing provincial control** over local bodies — a retrograde step. It also noted deterioration in finances of local bodies since 1919 reforms.

---

#### Government of India Act, 1935 and After
- **Provincial autonomy** gave further impetus to local self-governing institutions
- Portfolio finance now under **popular ministries** — funds could be made available for local bodies
- **Demarcation between provincial and local finance** (from 1919) was **scrapped**
- New provincial acts gave **more authority** to local bodies

**⚠️ BUT:**
- Financial resources and taxation powers remained at **roughly Ripon-era levels**
- After 1935, **new restrictions placed** on powers of local bodies to levy taxes on trades, callings, professions, and municipal property
- Provincial governments **ignored the liberal taxation policy** recommended by the Decentralisation Commission (1908)

> 📌 **Constitutional Direction (Post-Independence):** **Article 40** of the Constitution directs state governments to organise village panchayats as effective organs of local self-government. The **73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments** were aimed at plugging loopholes in the structure of local self-governing institutions in rural and urban areas respectively.

---

## 🌍 MODERN CONTEXT — Local Self-Government

> `[Modern Example]` — *AI-added section for current affairs enrichment.*

- **[Modern Example]** The **73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992)** — implementing **Article 40** — mandated elected Panchayati Raj Institutions with **29 subjects in the 11th Schedule**, **reservation for women (33%)**, and **State Finance Commissions** — directly addressing the financial weakness that colonial local bodies always suffered from.
- **[Modern Example]** The **74th Constitutional Amendment (1992)** did the same for **Urban Local Bodies (ULBs)** with **18 subjects in the 12th Schedule**.
- **[Modern Example]** As of **2023**, over **3.1 lakh Gram Panchayats** exist in India with elected representatives — a massive democratic achievement compared to the nominated, controlled local bodies of British India.
- **[Modern Example]** The **XV Finance Commission (2021–26)** allocated **₹2.36 lakh crore** directly to local bodies — addressing the fundamental colonial-era problem of **financial dependence** of local bodies.
- **[Modern Example]** The **Supreme Court in Suresh Mahajan vs State of Madhya Pradesh (2022)** reinforced that **state governments cannot arbitrarily supersede elected local bodies** — directly correcting the colonial practice of governments suspending local bodies "at will".
