# 📖 Chapter 32: The Movement of the Working Class

---

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Big Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **Modern Indian working class** was born with the railways — thousands of construction workers were its earliest members.
> 2. Indian workers faced a **double exploitation** — from both foreign (British) AND native capitalist classes — making their struggle inseparable from the **national independence movement**.
> 3. Early nationalists (Moderates) were **largely indifferent** to labour's cause — fearing labour laws would hurt Indian-owned industries.
> 4. **AITUC (1920)** was India's first national trade union body — founded with deep Congress involvement but later influenced by **communism, Gandhism, and socialism** in complex ways.
> 5. The working class movement was **never purely economic** — it was always intertwined with political events: Swadeshi, Non-Cooperation, Civil Disobedience, and Quit India.

---

## PART 1: ORIGINS — HOW THE INDIAN WORKING CLASS WAS BORN

---

### 1.1 The Entry of Modern Industry

The second half of the **19th century** marked a turning point — **modern industry entered India**.

The sequence of industrialisation:

- **Railways first** → Thousands of construction workers → These workers were the **harbingers** *(early signs/forerunners)* of the modern Indian working class
- **Ancillary industries** *(supporting industries that grow alongside railways)* developed next
- **Coal industry** developed fast → employed a large working force
- Then came the **cotton and jute industries**

---

### 1.2 The Double Burden of Indian Workers

Indian workers faced the **same exploitation as workers during Europe's industrialisation**:

- Low wages
- Long working hours
- Unhygienic and hazardous working conditions
- Employment of child labour
- Absence of basic amenities

BUT India had an **additional, unique burden** — **colonialism**.

**The Two Antagonistic Forces Indian Workers Faced:**

1. **Imperialist political rule** (British political domination)
2. **Economic exploitation** by BOTH foreign AND native capitalist classes

> 💡 **Key Insight:** This double exploitation made the Indian working class movement **inevitably political**. Workers could not simply demand better wages — they also had to fight for national freedom. The two struggles became **intertwined**.

---

## PART 2: EARLY EFFORTS (Pre-1905)

---

### 2.1 The Moderates' Indifference to Labour

The **early nationalists, especially the Moderates**, took a disappointing stance on labour:

- Were **indifferent** to labour's cause
- **Distinguished** between workers in Indian-owned factories and those in British-owned factories
- Believed **labour legislation would reduce the competitive advantage** of Indian-owned industries
- Did NOT want to **divide the national movement along class lines**
- Did **NOT support** the Factory Acts of **1881 and 1891** for all these reasons

> ⚠️ **Result:** Early attempts to improve workers' conditions were **philanthropic** *(charity-based, not rights-based)*, **isolated, sporadic**, and aimed only at **specific local grievances** — not systemic change.

---

### 2.2 Pioneer Efforts: A Timeline

| Year | Person/Event | What Happened |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **1870** | **Sasipada Banerjea** | Started a **workingmen's club** and newspaper **Bharat Shramjeevi** |
| **1878** | **Sorabjee Shapoorji Bengalee** | Tried to get a bill providing better working conditions passed in the **Bombay Legislative Council** |
| **1880** | **Narayan Meghajee Lokhande** | Started newspaper **Deenbandhu** and set up the **Bombay Mill and Millhands Association** |
| **1899** | **Great Indian Peninsular Railways Strike** | First strike by GIP Railways — got widespread support; **Tilak's Kesari and Maharatta** had been campaigning for it for months |

**Notable pro-labour nationalist leaders:**
- **Bipin Chandra Pal**
- **G. Subramania Aiyar**

Both demanded better conditions for workers and pro-labour reforms.

## PART 3: DURING THE SWADESHI UPSURGE (1905-1908)

---

### 3.1 Workers Enter the Political Arena

During the Swadeshi movement, workers moved beyond workplace grievances and **participated in wider political issues**.

**Key Strikes Organised By:**
- **Ashwini Coomar Banerjee**
- **Prabhat Kumar Roy Chaudhuri**
- **Premtosh Bose**
- **Apurba Kumar Ghosh**

**Sectors Where Strikes Were Organised:**
- Government press
- Railways
- Jute industry

**Attempts to Form Trade Unions** were made but were **not very successful**.

---

### 3.2 South India: Subramaniya Siva and Chidambaram Pillai

- **Subramaniya Siva** and **Chidambaram Pillai** led strikes in **Tuticorin and Tirunelvelli**
- Both were **arrested**

**The Biggest Strike of This Period:**
- Organised after **Tilak's arrest and trial** — showing how tightly politics and labour were linked

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Labour and Political Movements

> `[Modern Example]` The tradition of workers joining political movements continues today. The **Bharat Bandh of March 2022**, called by central trade unions in protest against privatisation and labour code reforms, saw participation from over **20 crore workers** across sectors — echoing the Swadeshi-era pattern of workers striking for causes beyond their own factories. The **four Labour Codes (2019-2020)**, which replaced 29 central labour laws, have been opposed by virtually all major trade unions on grounds eerily similar to those raised by early labour activists: inadequate worker protection and state support for capital over labour.

## PART 4: FIRST WORLD WAR AND AFTER (1914-1920)

---

### 4.1 War Creates Worker Discontent

The First World War and its aftermath created a paradox:

**Who benefited:**
- Industrialists → Rise in exports + soaring prices + massive profiteering opportunities

**Who suffered:**
- Workers → **Very low wages** despite rising prices

> This gap between industrial profits and worker wages created deep **discontent among workers**.

---

### 4.2 Three International Events That Changed Everything

Three global developments gave the Indian working class movement a **new revolutionary dimension**:

1. **Establishment of a socialist republic in the Soviet Union** — proved that workers could actually govern a country
2. **Formation of the Comintern** *(Communist International — global organisation coordinating communist parties)* — connected Indian workers to global communist networks
3. **Setting up of the International Labour Organisation (ILO)** — gave international legitimacy to workers' rights

---

### 4.3 Gandhi's Entry and Worker Mobilisation

The emergence of **Gandhi** transformed the national movement into a **broad-based mass movement**.

- Emphasis was now placed on **mobilising workers AND peasants** for the national cause
- A need was felt to organise workers into **trade unions**

**Gandhi's Direct Labour Action:**
- Helped organise the **Ahmedabad Textile Labour Association (1918)**
- Through a protest (a fast), secured a **27.5% wage hike**
- Later, the **arbitrator's award ensured a 35% raise**

> 💡 This was one of the earliest successful uses of Gandhian non-violent protest in a purely labour dispute.

---

## PART 5: THE AITUC — INDIA'S FIRST NATIONAL TRADE UNION

---

### 5.1 Foundation

> **All India Trade Union Congress (AITUC)** was founded on **October 31, 1920**

**First Leadership:**
- **President**: **Lala Lajpat Rai** (also the Indian National Congress president for that year)
- **General Secretary**: **Dewan Chaman Lal**

**Lajpat Rai's Historic Formulation:**
> *"Imperialism and militarism are the twin children of capitalism."*

This was the **first time** capitalism was explicitly linked with imperialism by an Indian leader in the context of labour.

---

### 5.2 Congress and the AITUC — A Close but Complicated Relationship

**Key Congress leaders who were closely associated with AITUC:**

- **C.R. Das** — presided over the **3rd and 4th sessions** of AITUC
- **Nehru, Subhas Bose, C.F. Andrews, J.M. Sengupta, Satyamurthy, V.V. Giri, Sarojini Naidu** — all kept close contacts

**Gaya Session of Congress (1922):**
- Welcomed the formation of AITUC
- Formed a **committee to assist it**

**C.R. Das's Warning:**
> Congress should take up workers' and peasants' cause and incorporate them into the swaraj struggle — *or else workers would get isolated from the movement.*

---

### 5.3 Ideological Influences on the AITUC

Three major ideological streams shaped the AITUC:

1. **Social Democratic ideas** of the British Labour Party — initial influence
2. **Gandhian Philosophy** — non-violence, trusteeship *(wealthy holding wealth in trust for society)*, and class-collaboration *(workers and owners cooperating, not fighting)*
3. **Communist/Marxist ideas** — growing influence especially in the late 1920s

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Trade Unions Today

> `[Modern Example]` The AITUC founded in 1920 is still active today, affiliated with the Communist Party of India. India currently has **12 Central Trade Union Organisations (CTUOs)** recognised by the government. The **INTUC** (affiliated to Congress) and **BMS** (affiliated to RSS/BJP) are among the largest. In **2023**, the government reported over **9,000 registered trade unions** in India. However, only about **2% of India's workforce** is formally unionised — reflecting that the colonial-era challenge of organising India's vast informal workforce remains as urgent as ever. The **e-Shram portal (2021)**, which registered over **28 crore unorganised workers**, is the first systematic attempt to bring informal workers into a social protection framework.

## PART 6: THE TRADE UNION ACT, 1926

---

### What the Act Did

The **Trade Union Act, 1926** was a landmark piece of legislation:

- **Recognised trade unions as legal associations** — gave them formal existence under law
- Laid down conditions for **registration and regulation** of trade union activities
- Secured **immunity** *(protection from legal action)* — both **civil AND criminal** — for trade unions from prosecution for **legitimate activities**
- BUT imposed some **restrictions on political activities** of trade unions

> 💡 This act gave Indian workers their first formal legal protection for collective action.

---

## PART 7: THE LATE 1920s — MILITANCY, REPRESSION, AND CONSPIRACY

---

### 7.1 Communist Influence and Industrial Unrest

The **late 1920s** saw a sharp leftward shift in the working class movement:

- **Strong communist influence** gave the movement a **militant and revolutionary character**
- **1928**: A **six-month-long strike** in **Bombay Textile Mills**, led by the **Girni Kamgar Union** *(literally: Mill Workers Union)*
- The **entire year of 1928** witnessed **unprecedented industrial unrest** across India

**Key Communist Leaders Who Emerged:**
- **S.A. Dange**
- **Muzaffar Ahmed**
- **P.C. Joshi**
- **Sohan Singh Joshi**

---

### 7.2 Government Crackdown: Two Repressive Laws

Alarmed at the growing strength of the trade union movement under "extremist" (communist) influence, the government struck back with legislation:

**1. Public Safety Ordinance (1929)**
- Allowed the government to detain and deport individuals seen as threats to public order

**2. Trade Disputes Act (TDA), 1929** — the more significant law:

- Made **compulsory** the appointment of **Courts of Inquiry and Consultation Boards** for settling industrial disputes
- Made it **illegal** to strike in **public utility services** (posts, railways, water, electricity) unless each individual worker gave **one month's advance notice** to administration
- **Forbade** trade union activity that was:
  - Coercive in nature
  - Purely political in nature
  - In the form of **sympathetic strikes** *(workers in one industry striking to support workers in another)*

> ⚠️ These laws severely restricted the most powerful tools of organised labour.

---

### 7.3 Meerut Conspiracy Case (1929) — A Global Scandal

**What Happened:**

- **March 1929**: The government arrested **31 labour leaders**
- The trial lasted **three and a half years**
- **Convicted**: **Muzaffar Ahmed, S.A. Dange, Joglekar, Philip Spratt, Ben Bradley, Shaukat Usmani**, and others

**Two Outcomes:**

| Outcome | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| **Got worldwide publicity** | Made the Indian labour movement internationally visible |
| **Weakened the working class movement** | Key leaders were jailed for years; movement lost momentum |

> 💡 The Meerut Case is one of the most dramatic political trials in colonial Indian history — involving both Indian and British communist activists.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: State vs. Labour

> `[Modern Example]` The use of sedition and conspiracy laws against labour activists has a long colonial history. In recent years, the **UAPA (Unlawful Activities Prevention Act)** has been invoked against trade union leaders and labour activists — drawing parallels to the Meerut Conspiracy Case. The **Supreme Court's 2022 judgment in S.G. Vombatkere vs. Union of India** read down Section 124A (Sedition) and suspended its operation pending review, directly relevant to the tradition of using "public safety" legislation against dissent — a practice that began with colonial-era laws like the Public Safety Ordinance of 1929.

---

## PART 8: THE 1930s — SPLITS, RECONCILIATION, AND THE LEFT FRONT

---

### 8.1 Civil Disobedience and the Post-1931 Dip

- **1930**: Workers **participated in the Civil Disobedience Movement**
- **After 1931**: A **dip in the working class movement** occurred

**Why? A Major Split in 1931:**
- The **corporatist trend** *(belief in cooperation between classes/industry groups)* led by **N.M. Joshi** broke away from the AITUC
- Set up the **All India Trade Union Federation**
- This division weakened the movement significantly

---

### 8.2 Reunification: The Left Front of 1935

- **1935**: Communists **rejoined the AITUC**

The new **left front** consisted of:
- **Communists**
- **Congress Socialists**
- **Leftist nationalists** like **Nehru** and **Subhas Bose**

This was one of the strongest left coalitions in Indian history.

---

## PART 9: UNDER CONGRESS MINISTRIES (1937-1939)

---

### A Favourable Period for Workers

- The **AITUC supported Congress candidates** during the **1937 elections**
- Congress governments came to power in several provinces
- These ministries gave a **fillip** *(boost/encouragement)* to trade union activity
- Congress ministries were **generally sympathetic** to workers' demands
- **Many legislations favourable to workers were passed**

> 💡 This was one of the few periods when government policy and labour movement goals aligned — showing what was possible with sympathetic political leadership.

---

## PART 10: SECOND WORLD WAR AND AFTER (1939-1947)

---

### 10.1 The Communists' Dramatic U-Turn

**Phase 1 (1939-1941): Opposition to the War**
- Workers **initially opposed** the War
- The AITUC and communist-influenced unions called it an "imperialist war"

**Phase 2 (After 1941): Support for the War**
- When **Russia joined the war** on behalf of the Allies after Germany attacked the Soviet Union, everything changed
- Communists now described the war as a **"peoples' war"** and supported it
- Communists **dissociated themselves from the Quit India Movement (1942)**
- Advocated a policy of **industrial peace**

> ⚠️ **Critical Point:** This U-turn — supporting British war efforts and opposing Quit India — severely damaged the credibility of the Communist Party of India among nationalists and is still a major historical controversy.

---

### 10.2 Post-War Upsurge (1945-1947)

The years **1945 to 1947** saw workers' most dramatic political actions:

| Year | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1945** | **Dock workers of Bombay and Calcutta** refused to load ships taking supplies to warring troops in **Indonesia** |
| **1946** | Workers went on strike **in support of the Naval Ratings** (during the Royal Indian Navy Mutiny) |
| **1946-47** | Strikes by workers of **posts, railways, and many other establishments** in the last year of foreign rule |

> 💡 These actions showed that the working class was a central actor in the final push for independence — not just an economic pressure group.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Labour and National Politics

> `[Modern Example]` The post-war worker upsurge of 1945-47 finds a modern echo in the **2020-21 Farm Laws protests**, where millions participated in what became one of the world's largest sustained protest movements. While this was primarily an agrarian movement, **central trade unions** formally supported it, showing the continued link between labour, peasant, and political movements that was first forged in the colonial era. The government's eventual **repeal of the three Farm Laws in November 2021** also echoed historical precedents — like the repeal of the Vernacular Press Act in 1882 — of mass movements forcing legislative reversal.

## PART 11: AFTER INDEPENDENCE

---

### The Post-1947 Phase

After Independence, the working class movement became associated with **various political ideologies**:

- Different political parties (Congress, Communist Party, Socialist Party, BJP/RSS) each built their own affiliated trade unions
- The unified nationalist character of the movement — which had held together under colonial rule — fragmented along party and ideological lines

### 🌍 Final Modern Context: The Unfinished Agenda

> `[Modern Example]` The core challenges of India's working class movement — identified as early as the 1880s — remain unresolved:
>
> - **Informal workforce**: Over **90% of India's workers** remain in the informal sector with no union protection, no social security, and no minimum wage enforcement — as revealed by the **Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) 2022-23**.
> - **Minimum wage**: The **Code on Wages (2019)** promised a universal minimum wage but its implementation has been delayed — state governments have not notified the floor wages.
> - **Gig workers**: The **Rajasthan Platform Based Gig Workers Act (2023)** — the first of its kind in India — attempts to provide social security to Ola/Uber/Zomato drivers and delivery workers, echoing the 1881 Factory Act's first attempt to regulate new categories of workers.
> - **Child labour**: Despite the **Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act, 2016**, India still has an estimated **10.1 million child labourers** (Census data) — the very problem the 1881 Factory Act first attempted to address.
