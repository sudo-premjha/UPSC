> **5 Key Takeaways to Remember Before You Read:**
>
> 1. **All-White Commission, All-India Boycott:** The **Simon Commission (1927)** was boycotted almost unanimously by Indians because it had **no Indian member** — seen as a direct insult to India's right to self-determination.
> 2. **Birkenhead's Challenge Accepted:** The British Secretary of State Lord Birkenhead challenged Indians to draft their own agreed constitution — Indians responded with the **Nehru Report (1928)**, the *first major Indian attempt* to draft a constitutional framework.
> 3. **Nehru Report's Bold Recommendations:** It proposed **dominion status, joint electorates, linguistic provinces, 19 fundamental rights** (including universal adult suffrage), and responsible government — but it satisfied almost no one fully.
> 4. **Jinnah's Fourteen Points (1929):** When the Nehru Report failed to accommodate Muslim League demands, **Jinnah** responded with **14 Points** that became the foundation of all future Muslim League propaganda — a critical turning point towards communal polarisation.
> 5. **A Movement Reactivated:** The Simon Commission controversy re-energised Indian politics, brought a **new generation of youth** into activism, and gave radical forces — Nehru, Subhas Bose — a platform to push for **complete independence** over dominion status.

# CHAPTER 18: Simon Commission and the Nehru Report

---

## 🔍 Introduction: Why Was a Commission Needed?

The **Government of India Act, 1919** had a built-in provision: a commission would be appointed **10 years from its date** to study how the governance scheme was working and suggest new steps.

However, the British did not wait the full 10 years.

---

## 🏛️ Part 1: Appointment of the Indian Statutory Commission

### What Was the Simon Commission?

An **all-white, seven-member Indian Statutory Commission** — popularly known as the **Simon Commission** (after its chairman, **Sir John Simon**) — was set up by the British government under **Prime Minister Stanley Baldwin's** leadership on **November 8, 1927**.

**Composition of the Commission:**
- **4 Conservatives**
- **2 Labourites**
- **1 Liberal**

> 📌 **Important Note:** The commission was actually under the **joint chairmanship of Sir John Simon and Clement Attlee** — who would later become Britain's Prime Minister (and the one who oversaw Indian Independence in 1947).

**The Commission's Mandate:** To recommend to the British government whether India was ready for further **constitutional reforms** (changes in governance structure) and along what lines.

---

### Why Was It Set Up Early (Before 1929)?

Although constitutional reforms were due only in **1929**, the Conservative government acted early for a key political reason:

- The Conservatives **feared defeat by the Labour Party** in the upcoming elections.
- They did not want to leave the question of Britain's "most prized colony" in what they called **"irresponsible Labour hands"**.
- So they rushed to set up the commission on their own terms before potentially losing power.

---

### Background: Why Were Reforms Already Being Discussed?

By the mid-1920s, the **failure of the 1919 Act** to create stable imperial governance had triggered several inquiries:

| Commission/Committee | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Lee Commission** (Royal Commission on the Superior Civil Services in India) | **1923** | To examine organisation, service conditions, and recruitment methods for Europeans and Indians in civil services. Recommended that the statutory public service commission under the 1919 Act be established without delay. |
| **Muddiman Committee** (Report of the Reforms Enquiry Committee) | **1924** | To examine the working of the Constitution set up in 1921 under the 1919 Act. Report was *not unanimous* — majority suggested minor changes; **minority (non-official Indians) strongly criticised dyarchy** (dual governance — partly Indian, partly British controlled) and wanted it abolished immediately and the Constitution made democratic. |
| **Linlithgow Commission** (Royal Commission of Agriculture) | **1926** | To examine India's agricultural and rural economy. Submitted report in **1928** with comprehensive recommendations for agrarian improvement. Notably recommended **improving the quality of Indian cattle by importing foreign bulls** of better quality to breed with Indian cows. |

> 📌 **The Political Trigger:** **Lord Birkenhead**, the Conservative **Secretary of State for India**, had repeatedly claimed that Indians were **incapable of formulating a concrete scheme of constitutional reforms** with wide support. He was largely responsible for appointing the Simon Commission.

---

## 😡 Part 2: Indian Response to the Simon Commission

### Why Were Indians So Angry?

The Indian response was **immediate and nearly unanimous** in opposition. There were two core reasons for the anger:

1. **Exclusion of Indians:** The commission had **no Indian member** — all seven were British.
2. **The Insulting Implication:** The very idea that **foreigners would decide whether Indians were fit for self-government** was seen as:
   - A violation of the principle of **self-determination** (the right of people to decide their own governance).
   - A **deliberate insult** to the self-respect of Indians.

---

### Congress Response

- The **Congress session in Madras (December 1927)**, held under the presidency of **M.A. Ansari**, decided to **boycott the commission "at every stage and in every form"**.
- At this same session, **Nehru succeeded in getting a snap resolution passed** declaring **complete independence** as the goal of the Congress — a significant and bold step.

---

### Response of Other Groups

| Group/Organisation | Response |
|---|---|
| **Liberals of the Hindu Mahasabha** | Supported Congress boycott |
| **Muslim League (majority faction under Jinnah)** | Supported the boycott |
| **Muslim League under Muhammad Shafi (Lahore session, 1927)** | Supported the government — *did NOT boycott* |
| **Unionists in Punjab** | Did NOT boycott |
| **Justice Party (South India)** | Did NOT boycott |

> 📌 **Note:** The Muslim League held **two separate sessions in 1927** — one at **Calcutta under Jinnah** (opposed the Simon Commission) and another at **Lahore under Muhammad Shafi** (supported the government). This reflects the **split within the League** at this time.

---

### Public Response: A Nation Rises

- The commission landed in **Bombay on February 3, 1928**.
- A **countrywide hartal** (strike/closure) was organised on that day, with **mass rallies** held across India.
- Wherever the commission went, people greeted it with:
  - **Black flag demonstrations**
  - **Hartals**
  - Chants of **"Simon Go Back"**

> 📌 **Who coined "Simon Go Back"?** It is widely believed that **Yusuf Meharally** — Indian freedom fighter, member of the Congress Socialist Party, and Mayor of Bombay — coined the catchy slogan. Some also believe it was **Lala Lajpat Rai**.

**A New Generation Wakes Up:**
- A significant feature of this upsurge was that a **new generation of youth** got their **first taste of political action**.
- Youth played the most active part, giving the protests a **militant flavour**.
- **Youth leagues and conferences got a real fillip** (boost).
- **Nehru and Subhas Bose** emerged as leaders of this new wave — both travelled extensively, addressed conferences, and presided over them.
- This youth upsurge also provided fertile ground for **radical socialist ideas**, reflected in the emergence of groups like:
  - **Punjab Naujawan Bharat Sabha**
  - **Workers' and Peasants' Parties**
  - **Hindustani Sewa Dal (Karnataka)**

---

### Police Repression

- Police came down heavily on demonstrators.
- **Lathi charges** did not spare even senior leaders.
- **Jawaharlal Nehru** and **G.B. Pant** were **beaten up in Lucknow**.
- **Lala Lajpat Rai** received **severe blows on his chest** during a lathi charge in **October 1928**.
- He died from these injuries on **November 17, 1928**.

> *"The blows, which fell on me today, are the last nails driven into the coffin of British Imperialism."*
> — **Lala Lajpat Rai**

---

## 🔍 Special Section: Dr. Ambedkar and the Simon Commission

This is a critically important section that is often overlooked.

**Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** was appointed by the **Bombay Legislative Council** to work with the Simon Commission. In **October 1928**, Ambedkar appeared before the commission and made the following arguments:

### Ambedkar's Demands Before the Simon Commission

- **Universal adult franchise** for both males and females alike. *(Significantly, universal adult franchise was not yet guaranteed in most European countries at the time.)*
- **Provincial autonomy** in the provinces.
- **Dyarchy at the Centre** (not its abolition at the Centre).

### Memorandum on behalf of Bahishkrita Hitakarini Sabha

Ambedkar submitted a memorandum on the rights and safeguards needed for the **depressed classes** (now known as Scheduled Castes/Dalits). His key arguments:

- There was **no organic link** between the depressed classes and the Hindu community — they should be regarded as a **distinct and independent minority**.
- The depressed classes needed **far greater political protection** than any other minority in British India because of:
  - **Educational backwardness**
  - **Economically poor condition**
  - **Social enslavement**
  - Certain **grave political disabilities** from which no other community suffered

### His Specific Demands for the Depressed Classes

1. **Representation on the same basis as the Mohammedan minority** (i.e., protected seats).
2. **Reserved seats** for the depressed classes if universal adult franchise was granted.
3. If universal franchise was **not** granted → a campaign for a **separate electorate** for the depressed classes.
4. Certain **safeguards in the constitution** (or in the form of advice to the governor) regarding the **education of the depressed classes and their entry into public services**.

> **What Did the Simon Commission Actually Do?**
> The Simon Commission's report *did* grant reserved seats to the depressed classes — but with a condition: candidates had to first get their **competence endorsed by the governor of the province**. Ambedkar was **most displeased** with this condition. In any case, this report eventually **remained a dead letter** (was never implemented).

## 📊 Part 3: Impact of the Simon Commission on the National Movement

The Simon Commission controversy had a **two-fold impact** on Indian politics:

**(i) Stimulus to Radical Forces:**
- It gave a boost to radical forces demanding not just complete independence but **major socio-economic reforms on socialist lines**.
- The Congress, which had no active programme at the time, got an issue around which it could **once again forge mass action**.

**(ii) Challenge to Indian Unity:**
- **Lord Birkenhead's challenge** to Indian politicians — to produce an agreed constitution that had the support of wide sections of Indian opinion — was **accepted** by various political sections.
- For a brief moment, **prospects for Indian unity seemed bright**.

---

## 📝 Part 4: Simon Commission Recommendations (May 1930 Report)

The commission published a **two-volume report in May 1930**. Key recommendations:

| Recommendation | Details |
|---|---|
| **Dyarchy** | **Abolish dyarchy** at the provincial level; establish **representative government in provinces** with **provincial autonomy** |
| **Governor's Powers** | Governor to have **discretionary power** regarding internal security and protecting different communities |
| **Provincial Councils** | **Increase the number of members** in provincial legislative councils |
| **Centre** | **Rejected** parliamentary responsibility at the Centre; Governor General to have **complete power to appoint cabinet members** |
| **High Courts** | Government of India to have **complete control** over high courts |
| **Communal Electorates** | **Retain separate communal electorates** (and extend to other communities) — but only until Hindu-Muslim tensions died down |
| **Franchise** | **No universal franchise** |
| **Federalism** | Accepted the idea of federalism — but **not in the near future**; suggested a **Consultative Council of Greater India** including representatives of both British provinces and princely states |
| **NWFP and Baluchistan** | Both should get **local legislatures** and the right to be **represented at the Centre** |
| **Sindh** | Should be **separated from Bombay** |
| **Burma** | Should be **separated from India** — as it was not a natural part of the Indian subcontinent |
| **Army** | Indian army should be **Indianised**, though British forces must be retained |

> ⚠️ **Important Note:** By the time the report was published in May 1930, it was **no longer relevant** — several events (Civil Disobedience Movement, Round Table Conferences) had already overtaken it.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Simon Commission

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. "No Indian on the Committee" — A Principle That Still Matters
- The core objection to the Simon Commission — that **foreigners/outsiders cannot decide matters of self-governance** — echoes in modern debates about **sovereignty and self-determination**.
- `[Modern Example]` India's consistent position in international forums like the **UN Human Rights Council** — that external parties should not interfere in its internal governance decisions (e.g., on Kashmir, or on judicial matters) — reflects the same principle of **self-determination** that Indians asserted in 1927.

### 2. Ambedkar's Legacy Before the Commission
- `[Modern Example]` Ambedkar's demand before the Simon Commission for **constitutional safeguards for the depressed classes** foreshadowed what he would later enshrine in the **Constitution of India (1950)** — Articles **15, 16, 17** (abolition of untouchability), and **Articles 330–342** (reservation in legislatures and services for SCs and STs).
- The ongoing debate in 2024 about **sub-classification within SC reservations** (the **Supreme Court's landmark 7-judge bench judgment in *State of Punjab vs. Davinder Singh* (August 2024)** — allowing states to sub-classify within SC/ST categories for reservations) is a direct continuation of the questions Ambedkar first raised before the Simon Commission in 1928.

### 3. Lala Lajpat Rai's Death — Echoes in Modern Protest Policing
- `[Modern Example]` The death of Lala Lajpat Rai from lathi blows during a protest in 1928 remains a reference point in debates about **police use of force against protesters**.
- In **2020**, during the **Farmers' Protest** and earlier during the **anti-CAA protests (2019–20)**, the Supreme Court and various High Courts intervened to regulate police use of force against peaceful protesters, reflecting the same civil liberties concerns.

## 📜 Part 5: The Nehru Report (1928) — India Drafts Its Own Constitution

### Background: Accepting Birkenhead's Challenge

Lord Birkenhead had repeatedly challenged Indian politicians, saying they could never produce an agreed constitutional scheme. Indians decided to prove him wrong.

- An **All Parties Conference** met in **February 1928**.
- It appointed a sub-committee under the **chairmanship of Motilal Nehru** to draft a constitution.
- This was the **first major attempt by Indians to draft a constitutional framework** for their own country.

### Members of the Nehru Committee

> **Motilal Nehru** (Chairman), **Tej Bahadur Sapru, Subhas Chandra Bose, M.S. Aney, Mangal Singh, Ali Imam, Shuaib Qureshi**, and **G.R. Pradhan**.

- The report was **finalised by August 1928**.
- The recommendations were **unanimous except on one point**: while the **majority favoured dominion status** (self-governing status within the British Empire), a **section wanted complete independence** as the basis — with the majority giving the dissenting section **liberty of action**.

---

## 📋 Part 6: Main Recommendations of the Nehru Report

The report confined itself to **British India** — it envisaged that British India would eventually link up with the princely states on a **federal basis**.

**(i) Dominion Status:**
- Dominion status (self-governing status like Canada or Australia, within the British Empire) as the desired form of government.
- This angered the **younger, militant section** of the Congress — especially **Jawaharlal Nehru**.

**(ii) Rejection of Separate Electorates:**
- Rejected separate electorates (where only Muslims could vote for Muslim candidates, etc.) — which had been the basis of constitutional reforms so far.
- Instead, proposed **joint electorates** (all communities vote together) with:
  - **Reservation of seats for Muslims** at the Centre and in provinces where they were in the **minority**.
  - **No reservation** in provinces where Muslims were in the **majority** (Punjab and Bengal).
  - Reservations to be **proportional to Muslim population** in those areas, with the right to contest additional seats.

**(iii) Linguistic Provinces:**
- Provinces to be reorganised on **linguistic lines** — a pioneering and far-sighted recommendation.

**(iv) Nineteen Fundamental Rights:**
The report listed **19 fundamental rights**, including:
- **Equal rights for women**
- **Right to form unions**
- **Universal adult suffrage** (voting rights for all adults)

**(v) Responsible Government at the Centre and in Provinces:**

For the **Centre:**
- An Indian Parliament consisting of:
  - **House of Representatives:** 500 members, elected on the basis of **adult suffrage**, **5-year tenure**
  - **Senate:** 200 members, elected by provincial councils, **7-year tenure**
- The central government to be headed by a **Governor General**, appointed by the British government but **paid out of Indian revenues**, who would act on the **advice of the central executive council** responsible to the Parliament.

For the **Provinces:**
- Provincial councils to have a **5-year tenure**.
- Headed by a **Governor** acting on the **advice of the provincial executive council**.

**(vi) Protection of Muslim Interests:**
- **Full protection** to the cultural and religious interests of Muslims.

**(vii) Separation of State and Religion:**
- **Complete dissociation of State from religion** — a landmark secular commitment.

---

## 🔥 Part 7: The Communal Controversy — Why the Nehru Report Satisfied No One

### Background: The Delhi Proposals (December 1927)

Before the Nehru Report was drafted, a large number of Muslim leaders had met at Delhi at the **Muslim League session (December 1927)** and evolved **four proposals** — these are known as the **'Delhi Proposals'**. They were *accepted by the Madras session of the Congress (December 1927)*:

1. **Joint electorates** in place of separate electorates — but with **reserved seats for Muslims**.
2. **One-third representation** to Muslims in the **Central Legislative Assembly**.
3. Representation to Muslims in **Punjab and Bengal** in **proportion to their population**.
4. Formation of **three new Muslim-majority provinces**: **Sindh, Baluchistan**, and **North-West Frontier Province**.

---

### Hindu Mahasabha's Demands

The **Hindu Mahasabha** was **vehemently opposed** to:
- Creating new Muslim-majority provinces.
- Reservation of seats for Muslim majorities in Punjab and Bengal (which would ensure Muslim control over legislatures in both).

The Hindu Mahasabha also demanded a **strictly unitary structure** (all power at the Centre, not distributed to provinces). This attitude **complicated matters greatly**.

---

### The Dilemma of the Nehru Committee

The committee found itself in an impossible position:
- If **Muslim League demands** were accepted → **Hindu communalists** would withdraw support.
- If **Hindu communalist demands** were satisfied → **Muslim leaders** would be estranged.

**Concessions Made to Hindu Communalists in the Nehru Report:**
- Joint electorates everywhere, but **reservation for Muslims only where they are in the minority** (not in Punjab and Bengal where they are in the majority).
- **Sindh** to be detached from Bombay **only after dominion status was granted** and **subject to weightage given to the Hindu minority in Sindh**.
- The political structure was **broadly unitary** — **residual powers** (powers not specifically listed) rested with the **Centre**, not the provinces.

---

### Jinnah's Proposed Amendments (All Parties Conference, Calcutta, December 1928)

At the All Parties Conference in Calcutta in December 1928, **Jinnah** proposed **three amendments** on behalf of the Muslim League:

1. **One-third representation** to Muslims in the **central legislature**.
2. **Reservation for Muslims** in Bengal and Punjab legislatures **proportionate to their population** — until adult suffrage was established.
3. **Residual powers to provinces** (not the Centre).

**These demands were NOT accommodated.**

---

## 📋 Part 8: Jinnah's Fourteen Points (March 1929)

After his proposals were rejected, Jinnah returned to the **Shafi faction of the Muslim League** and in **March 1929** presented **14 Points** that were to become the **basis of all future Muslim League propaganda**.

| Point | Demand |
|---|---|
| **1** | **Federal Constitution** with residual powers to provinces |
| **2** | **Provincial autonomy** |
| **3** | No constitutional amendment by the Centre without **concurrence of the constituent states** |
| **4** | All legislatures and elected bodies to have **adequate Muslim representation** in every province — without reducing a Muslim majority in any province to a minority or equality |
| **5** | Adequate representation to Muslims in **services and self-governing bodies** |
| **6** | **One-third Muslim representation** in the central legislature |
| **7** | **One-third Muslims** in any cabinet at the Centre or in provinces |
| **8** | **Separate electorates** (a reversal of his earlier acceptance of joint electorates) |
| **9** | No bill or resolution in any legislature to be passed if **three-fourths of a minority community** consider it against their interests |
| **10** | Any territorial redistribution **not to affect the Muslim majority** in Punjab, Bengal, and NWFP |
| **11** | **Separation of Sindh** from Bombay |
| **12** | **Constitutional reforms** in NWFP and Baluchistan |
| **13** | **Full religious freedom** to all communities |
| **14** | **Protection of Muslim rights** in religion, culture, education, and language |

---

## ⚖️ Part 9: Why the Nehru Report Found No Takers

The Nehru Report managed to displease almost every major political group:

| Group | Reason for Dissatisfaction |
|---|---|
| **Muslim League** | Demands for separate electorates and reservation in majority provinces rejected |
| **Hindu Mahasabha** | Creation of new Muslim-majority provinces and Muslim reservations in Punjab and Bengal |
| **Sikh communalists** | Various communal representation concerns |
| **Younger Congress (Nehru, Subhas Bose)** | **Dominion status** seen as a step backward — they wanted complete independence |

**The response of the younger nationalists:**
- **Jawaharlal Nehru and Subhas Chandra Bose** rejected the modified Congress goal of dominion status.
- They jointly set up the **Independence for India League** to campaign for complete independence.

## 🌍 Modern Context — Nehru Report and Communal Representation

`[Modern Example]`

### 1. The Nehru Report's Secular Vision — Unfinished Business
- The Nehru Report's call for **complete dissociation of State from religion** was visionary. This principle was enshrined in the Constitution through the **42nd Amendment (1976)** which added the word "secular" to the Preamble.
- `[Modern Example]` In **2024**, the Supreme Court (Constitution Bench) upheld the words "secular" and "socialist" in the Preamble in the case ***Ashwini Kumar Upadhyay vs. Union of India***, affirming that these are foundational values that cannot be challenged — a vindication of what Motilal Nehru's committee proposed nearly 100 years ago.

### 2. Linguistic Provinces — Nehru Report's Enduring Legacy
- The Nehru Report's recommendation for **linguistic provinces** was ahead of its time.
- `[Modern Example]` India's linguistic reorganisation, completed largely through the **States Reorganisation Act (1956)** (based on the **Fazl Ali Commission/States Reorganisation Commission**), is a direct realisation of what the Nehru Report proposed in 1928. The most recent example of this is the creation of **Telangana (2014)** as a separate state, reflecting continuing demands for linguistically and culturally defined states.

### 3. Jinnah's 14 Points — Historical Significance
- Jinnah's 14 Points, born out of the failure of the Nehru Report, are considered a **critical turning point** in the path towards Partition.
- `[Modern Example]` Historians and political scientists studying the **Two-Nation Theory** and the path to the **Partition of 1947** consistently cite the **1928–29 breakdown** over the Nehru Report and Jinnah's 14 Points as the moment when Hindu-Muslim political unity became effectively impossible within a single constitutional framework. This is relevant for UPSC essays and GS-I analysis of Partition's causes.

### 4. Fundamental Rights in the Nehru Report
- The Nehru Report listed **19 fundamental rights** in 1928 — including equal rights for women and universal adult suffrage — years before the Constitution was drafted.
- `[Modern Example]` The **Right to form unions** (one of the 19 rights proposed) is now guaranteed under **Article 19(1)(c)** of the Constitution. In **2024**, the debate around the **Labour Codes** and their impact on trade union rights is directly linked to this foundational right that Indian nationalists first articulated in 1928.
