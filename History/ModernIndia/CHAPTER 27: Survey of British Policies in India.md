# 📖 Chapter 27: Survey of British Policies in India

## 📦 Chapter at a Glance

> **5 Big Takeaways Before You Begin:**
>
> 1. **Divide and Rule** was the British Empire's most dangerous weapon — used to split Hindus, Muslims, castes, regions, and classes against each other.
> 2. **Three Land Revenue Systems** (Permanent Settlement, Ryotwari, Mahalwari) were all designed to extract maximum money from India — and all three devastated farmers.
> 3. **Labour Laws** were introduced NOT out of concern for Indian workers, but due to pressure from British textile capitalists who feared Indian competition.
> 4. **Social and Cultural Policy** shifted from cautious modernisation (pre-1858) to openly siding with conservative, orthodox, and communal forces (post-1858).
> 5. **Foreign Policy** was guided entirely by British imperial interests — India's money and soldiers were used for Britain's wars and territorial expansion.

## PART 1: ADMINISTRATIVE POLICIES

---

### 1.1 The Big Shift After 1857

Before 1857, the British had at least *pretended* to want to modernise India. After 1857, that pretence was dropped.

The new justification became: *"Indians are not fit to govern themselves and need British supervision."*

This was not a belief — it was a **policy tool** to maintain control.

---

### 1.2 Divide and Rule 🔴

> *"I am sorry to hear of the increasing friction between the Hindus and Mohammedans... Of the two, the latter is least risky."*
> — **Hamilton, Secretary of State, 1897**

This quote alone reveals the deliberate British strategy.

**The British systematically created divisions along multiple lines:**

- **Princes vs. their own people** (states' subjects)
- **Region vs. Region**
- **Province vs. Province**
- **Caste vs. Caste**
- **Hindus vs. Muslims** ← the most politically dangerous

**How was the Hindu-Muslim split engineered?**

- After the 1857 revolt, Muslims faced immediate repression.
- After **1870**, British authorities shifted strategy — they began to **use educated, upper/middle-class Muslims** as a counterweight against the growing tide of nationalism.
- How? By **using conflicts over scarce resources** — government jobs, educational seats, political representation — as tools to create a split **along religious lines** among educated Indians.
- These conflicts were not accidental; they were **inherent in colonial underdevelopment** and were deliberately sharpened.

> 💡 **Key Insight:** The British did not *create* communalism from scratch — they found existing tensions and *systematically amplified* them for political survival.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Divide and Rule

> `[Modern Example]` The consequences of the British policy of communal division are still debated today. The **Sachar Committee Report (2006)** documented the socio-economic backwardness of Indian Muslims — a legacy tracing back partly to the colonial disruption of composite communities. Contemporary debates around the **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA), 2019** and the **delimitation controversies** often invoke the colonial history of divide and rule as a warning about identity-based politics.

---

### 1.3 Hostility Towards Educated Indians

> *"Systems of nomination, representation and election were all means of enlisting Indians to work for imperial ends."*
> — **Anil Seal**

The **emerging middle class** of India was doing something dangerous (from the British perspective) — they were:

- Analysing the **exploitative, colonial character** of British rule
- Demanding **Indian participation in administration**

When the **Indian National Congress was founded in 1885**, the British did not see a reform movement — they saw a **challenge to their authority**.

**Their response:**

- Adopted a **hostile attitude** toward educated nationalist leaders
- Opposed **modern education** for Indians
- Tried to ensure that educated Indians remained tools of imperial administration, not critics of it

---

### 1.4 Attitude Towards Zamindars

Needing allies against nationalists, the British turned to the **most reactionary social groups**:

- **Princes**
- **Zamindars (landlords)**

These groups were now praised as the **"natural" and "traditional" leaders** of the people.

**Concrete action taken:**

- Lands of most **Awadh taluqdars** (large landholders) that had been **confiscated before 1857** were **restored to them** after the revolt.
- Zamindars and landlords' **interests and privileges were protected** — even at the expense of peasants.
- In return, zamindars saw the British as **guarantors of their very existence** and became firm supporters of the empire.

> 💡 This created a triangle: **British Empire → protected Zamindars → exploited Peasants.**

---

### 1.5 Attitude Towards Social Reforms

Having chosen to side with conservative forces, the British:

- **Withdrew support for social reforms** — they had realised that reform had "aroused the wrath of orthodox sections."
- **Encouraged caste consciousness and communal identity** — which only helped reactionary forces grow stronger.

---

### 1.6 Underdeveloped Social Services

Money was endlessly spent on:
- **Army**
- **Civil administration**
- **Wars of imperial expansion**

This left **almost nothing** for:
- Education
- Health
- Sanitation
- Physical infrastructure (roads, bridges, etc.)

> ⚠️ **Legacy Alert:** The text specifically notes this is *"a legacy which still haunts this country."* Whatever social services did exist catered **only to elite sections and urban areas**.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Underdeveloped Social Services

> `[Modern Example]` India's **public health expenditure** was just **1.2% of GDP** in 2019-20, one of the lowest in the world — a reflection of the colonial neglect of social infrastructure. The **COVID-19 pandemic (2020-21)** brutally exposed these gaps. The government's **PM-ABHIM (Ayushman Bharat Health Infrastructure Mission, 2021)** with an outlay of ₹64,180 crore was launched specifically to address the colonial-era deficit in health infrastructure. Similarly, the **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020** targets raising public education expenditure to **6% of GDP** — a target India has never historically met.

---

### 1.7 Labour Legislations

#### The Irony of Origin

Working conditions in **19th-century Indian factories and plantations** were horrific:

- **Long working hours** — for men, women, AND children
- **Low wages**
- **Overcrowded, poorly ventilated, poorly lit** workplaces
- **Practically no safety measures**

The cruel irony? The **first-ever demand** for regulating worker conditions in India did NOT come from Indian workers or Indian reformers.

It came from the **Lancashire textile capitalist lobby** in Britain.

**Why?** They feared Indian industry — with its **cheap, unregulated labour** — would become a **competitive rival**. To level the playing field, they demanded a commission to investigate factory conditions.

---

#### Timeline of Labour Laws

**1875** → First commission appointed to investigate factory conditions

**The Indian Factory Act, 1881** — primarily addressed **child labour** (ages 7-12):

- Employment of children **under 7 years prohibited**
- Working hours restricted to **9 hours/day** for children
- Children to get **4 holidays per month**
- **Hazardous machinery** to be properly fenced off

**The Indian Factory Act, 1891** — improved on 1881 Act:

- Raised minimum age from **7 to 9 years** and maximum from **12 to 14 years**
- Reduced maximum working hours for children to **7 hours/day**
- Fixed maximum working hours for **women at 11 hours/day** with a **1.5-hour interval**
- *(Men's working hours were still left unregulated!)*
- Provided **weekly holiday** for all

---

#### The Plantation Exception — A Deliberate Loophole

> ⚠️ **Critical Point:** These laws **did NOT apply** to British-owned **tea and coffee plantations**.

Why? Because British planters had massive economic and political power. Instead, the government **helped these planters** by passing laws that:

- Made it **virtually impossible** for a labourer to **refuse work** once a contract was signed
- Made breach of contract a **criminal offence**
- Gave planters the **right to get a defaulting labourer arrested**

Workers in these plantations were treated **like slaves**.

More labour laws were passed in the **20th century** under nationalist pressures, but overall working conditions remained deplorable.

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Labour Laws

> `[Modern Example]` India's colonial-era fragmented labour law system (29+ central laws) was overhauled through **four Labour Codes** passed between 2019-2020: the Code on Wages (2019), Industrial Relations Code (2020), Code on Social Security (2020), and Occupational Safety Code (2020). These consolidate and modernise the legacy system. However, critics argue that some provisions — like easier "hire and fire" rules — echo colonial-era power imbalances. The **tea garden workers of Assam**, descendants of those excluded from colonial protections, still rank among India's most vulnerable workers, with average daily wages under ₹250 as recently as 2021.

---

### 1.8 Restrictions on Freedom of the Press

Nationalists quickly used **new printing technology** to:
- Educate public opinion
- Criticise government policies
- Later, to **arouse national consciousness**

**Key Timeline:**

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1835** | **Metcalfe** lifted restrictions on the Indian press |
| **1878** | **Lytton** imposed the **Vernacular Press Act** — restricted Indian-language press |
| **1882** | Vernacular Press Act **repealed** under public protest |
| Post-1882 | Press enjoyed **relative freedom** for ~2 decades |
| **1908 & 1910** | Press **restrictions reimposed** during Swadeshi and anti-partition movements |

> (For more detail, refer to the chapter: *'Development of Press in India'*)

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Press Freedom

> `[Modern Example]` India ranked **161 out of 180 countries** in the **Reporters Without Borders Press Freedom Index 2023** — a significant drop from 142 in 2020. The **IT Rules 2021** and subsequent amendments have been criticised by press freedom groups as echoing the spirit of the Vernacular Press Act — targeting digital media that is critical of the government. The **Supreme Court** has repeatedly upheld press freedom as part of Article 19(1)(a) but has also noted the state's power to impose reasonable restrictions.

---

### 1.9 White Racism

The **notion of white superiority** was not accidental — it was **systematically maintained** through:

- Excluding Indians from **higher grades of civil and military services**
- Excluding Indians from **railway compartments, parks, hotels, clubs**
- **Public displays of racial arrogance** — beatings, blows, and even murders (reported as accidents)

> *"We could only govern by maintaining the fact that we were the dominant race—though Indians in services should be encouraged, there is a point at which we must reserve the control to ourselves, if we are to remain at all."*
> — **Lord Elgin**

> *"The English were an imperial race, we were told, with God-given right to govern us and keep us in subjection..."*
> — **Jawaharlal Nehru**

## PART 2: REVENUE POLICIES

---

### 2.0 Why Land Revenue Mattered

Agriculture was — and remains — the **main occupation** of rural India.

When the British obtained **Diwani rights** *(right to collect revenue)* of Bengal in **1765**, the agrarian economy was the backbone of all other economic sectors.

The British introduced new **land tenure systems** and **revenue administration policies**. The core aim was simple:

> **Extract maximum income from land — with little care for the cultivators.**

---

### 2.1 Hastings' System (The Izaredari / Farming System)

**Context:** The **rapacity and corruption** of Company employees contributed to one of the worst famines in Bengal in **1769-70**.

When **Warren Hastings** became Governor General, he introduced the **Izaredari System** (also called the Farming System) to bring order to revenue collection.

**How it worked:**
- Power to collect land revenue given to **contractors ("farmers")**
- Selected on basis of **highest bids** (whoever promised to pay the most from a district)
- Land was "farmed out" to the **highest bidders**
- Settlement was for **5 years** (quinquennial settlement)
- Made **annual** in 1777

**Why it failed:**

- Contractors were **revenue farmers** — they cared only about profit, not peasant welfare
- Amounts promised **far exceeded** the land's actual productive capacity
- Traditional zamindars were seen merely as **tax gatherers with no proprietary rights** and were even **discouraged from bidding** — many hereditary zamindars were ousted
- Corruption meant less revenue actually reached the government
- The country was **impoverished**, agriculture declined, and items the British traded in (silk, cotton) diminished
- The **expected revenue surplus never materialised**

---

### 2.2 Permanent Settlement (Zamindari System)

#### Background

- **1776**: **Philip Francis** (member of Hastings' council) recommended a permanent land revenue settlement
- Lord **Cornwallis** was sent as Governor General with instructions to make the settlement **permanent**
- Cornwallis set up a committee: **himself + Sir John Shore + James Grant**

#### Why Cornwallis Favoured Zamindars

- Cornwallis was himself a member of the **landed aristocracy of Britain** — he wanted Indian zamindars to behave like English landlords and improve the land
- Also, it was **simpler** to collect revenue from a smaller number of zamindars than from millions of cultivators

#### Geographic Coverage

- Covered **~19% of British-ruled territory**
- Introduced in **Bengal and Bihar**
- Extended to **Orissa, Banaras (Varanasi), and northern Madras**

#### Key Features

- **1790**: 10-year settlement made with zamindars
- **1793**: Settlement made **permanent**
- **Proprietary rights** over land given to zamindars
- Zamindars collected revenue from **ryots (cultivators)** who became tenants
- Zamindars kept **1/10th to 1/11th** of revenue; rest went to the Company
- Zamindars could **sell, mortgage, or transfer** the land; heirs could inherit it

**The Sunset Clause (1794):**
> If tax was not paid by **sunset of a specific date**, the zamindari was **taken over and auctioned** — rights transferred to the new owner

**1793, 1799, 1812 Regulations:**
- Zamindars could **seize tenants' property** for non-payment of rent — **without any court permission**

#### Shortcomings

- **Very high revenue rates** — no margin for floods, droughts, or calamities → many zamindaris were seized and sold
- **Absentee landlordism** grew — merchants, government officials bought auctioned lands
- High rates forced zamindars to divide estates into small lots called **patni taluq**, renting them to **patnidars** — beginning of **subinfeudation** *(layered sub-renting of land)*
- Zamindars were required to issue written agreements (**pattas**) to each cultivator — but **never did**, leaving peasants at zamindars' mercy
- **Peasantry was reduced to serfdom** *(bonded-labour-like servitude)*
- Zamindars **did nothing to improve land** — only focused on rent extraction
- Revenue was **fixed permanently** — Company could NOT increase it to meet growing expenses

---

### 2.3 Ryotwari System

#### Origin

- **Thomas Munro** and **Captain Alexander Read** were sent in **1792** to administer the recently acquired **Baramahal region** of Madras Presidency
- They devised a system of collecting **directly from villages**
- When Munro became **Governor of Madras Presidency in 1820**, he formalised this as the **Ryotwari System**

#### Why the Company Preferred It

No intermediaries = **more revenue directly to the government**

#### The Madras Experience

- Munro reduced tax to **one-third of gross produce**
- But this one-third was nearly equal to the **economic rental** *(maximum rent the land could bear)*
- State demanded its money **regardless of actual yield or market prices**
- This continued for **nearly 30 years** — peasants sank into deep poverty
- In unstructured districts, tax was fixed arbitrarily — based on what the ryot paid in **earlier years** (called **"putcut" assessment**)
- Tax was often beyond ryots' capacity to pay — **coercion and torture** were used to extract revenue
- Many ryots fell into the hands of **moneylenders**

**1855 Madras Torture Commission Report:**
- Revealed **coercion, bribery, and corruption** by subordinate officials of the collectorate

**Post-1855 Reform:**
- Scientific survey and fresh assessment of revenue
- Revenue rate: **half of net produce**
- Settlement made for **30 years**

**1864**: Reformed settlement introduced → agricultural prosperity followed, interrupted by famines of **1865-66** and **1876-78**

#### Bombay Presidency

- Direct collection from peasants began in **1813-14** (Gujarat first)
- After **1818** (British conquest of Peshwa's territory), ryotwari introduced under **Elphinstone** (Governor of Bombay, 1819-1827), a disciple of Munro
- Based on survey of **1824-28**; state demand fixed at **55% of net produce**
- Most surveys were **faulty** → over-assessment → cultivators deserted fields → large tracts went fallow
- Around **1836**: **Wingate and Goldsmith** improved the system
- Modified assessment covered most of **Deccan by 1865**
- Extended to **Berar, East Bengal, parts of Assam, and Coorg**

> 📝 **Note:** The Ryotwari system was devised when **Utilitarian ideas** (especially **David Ricardo's theory of rent**) had begun to influence policy planning in India.

#### Geographic Coverage

- Covered **~51% of British-ruled area** — Bombay and Madras Presidencies, Assam, and other parts

#### Key Features

- **Ownership and occupancy rights** vested in the ryot
- No limit on land ownership; ryots free to **sublet, transfer, or sell**
- Ryots paid tax **directly to the Company** — 45% to 55% of estimated production
- Revenue was **not fixed** — could be raised when production increased
- Settlement was **periodically revisable** (not permanent)
- In theory, ryots could choose land to cultivate; in practice, **forced to cultivate** even unwanted land
- Barren government land could be cultivated but **revenue had to be shared**
- Land **confiscated** if revenue not paid

#### Shortcomings

- **Overassessment** was a chronic problem
- Collection was **inflexible and often involved torture**
- Officers could be **bribed** during assessment — corruption flourished
- Non-cultivating landlords could get registered as owners → real cultivators became **tenants, servants, or bonded labourers**
- High tax and harassment **devalued land** — few wanted to buy it

---

### 2.4 Mahalwari System

#### Origin

- **1819**: **Holt Mackenzie** (Secretary to Board of Commissioners) recommended this system for Northern India
- Formalised by **Regulation VII of 1822**
- Initial version failed due to: complex survey methods + high revenue demands + harsh extraction
- Made worse by **agricultural depression of 1828** → arrears accumulated, land uncultivated, few buyers

**1833 Reform (under Governor General William Bentinck):**
- **Regulation of 1833** simplified the procedure for estimating produce
- **Merttins Bird** — known as the **"Father of Land Settlements in Northern India"** — supervised the new scheme
- Survey showed field boundaries of cultivated and fallow lands
- State share fixed at **66% of rental value**; settlement for **30 years**

**1855 (Lord Dalhousie's Direction):**
- State demand limited to **50% of rental value**

#### Geographic Coverage

- Introduced in **~30% of British-ruled area**
- Covered major parts of: **North Western Provinces, Central Provinces, Punjab** (with variations)
- Also known as **"mauzawar"** in North Western Provinces and **"malguzari"** in Central Provinces

#### Key Features

- **Mahal** *(Hindi word for house/estate)* was the unit of revenue assessment
- A mahal = **one village or a group of villages**
- Revenue determined on the basis of **produce of the mahal**
- **Village community** = owner of land; individual ownership with cultivator
- Each farmer paid **his individual share** of tax
- **Village headman (lambardar)** or community of village leaders responsible for collecting and paying tax to the Company
- State's share: **66% under Bentinck**, later **reduced to 50%**
- Concept of **average rents for different soil classes** introduced
- Land revenue **revised periodically**
- It was a **dual system** — settlement done collectively with community AND with individual landlords

> 📝 **Note:** This is sometimes called a **"modified zamindari system"** — the village headman linked individual cultivators and the government, but did NOT have a zamindar's ownership rights.

#### Shortcomings

- Required officials to record **all rights of cultivators, zamindars, and others** — practically impossible to implement fully
- Calculations were often **inaccurate or based on guesswork** — collectors manipulated them to increase government revenue
- Led to **exorbitant tax assessments** that ruined village communities
- Moneylenders and merchants **ousted old cultivating proprietors** or turned them into tenants
- Led to **impoverishment and dispossession** of cultivating communities of North India
- Popular resentment found expression in the **uprisings of 1857**
- From the Company's perspective: **more was spent on collection than was collected** — administratively inefficient

---

### 2.5 Overall Impact of British Land Revenue Systems

| System | Area Covered | Where Applied |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| **Permanent Settlement** | ~19% | Bengal, Bihar, Orissa, Banaras, N. Madras |
| **Ryotwari System** | ~51% | Bombay, Madras Presidencies, Assam |
| **Mahalwari System** | ~30% | NW Provinces, Central Provinces, Punjab |

**All three systems were disastrous. Here's why:**

- **Revenue was always too high** → zamindars defaulted → property seized; farmers indebted for life
- **New private ownership** of land created, but its benefits **never reached cultivators**
- **Peasantry was impoverished** → rural indebtedness became endemic
- **Permanent zamindar rights** → land ownership became deeply **unequal**
- Land became **saleable, mortgageable, and alienable** — protecting government revenue above all else
- Village community **split into two hostile groups**: land-owning class and landless peasantry
- **Stability of the Indian village was permanently shaken**
- **Village artisans** became jobless and were converted into **landless labourers**
- **Law courts, lawyers' fees, and formal procedures** replaced the old body of customs — making the system expensive and alien to ordinary people
- **Moneylenders and middlemen** became the de facto rulers of the village economy
- **Absentee landlordism** emerged through subletting of revenue collection rights
- **Commercialisation of agriculture** was forced by exorbitant revenue demands — farmers had to grow cash crops to pay taxes rather than food for survival

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Land Revenue Legacy

> `[Modern Example]` The legacy of British land revenue systems is directly visible in:
> - **India's agrarian crisis**: The 2021 **Farm Laws** controversy and the subsequent farmer protests were rooted in long-standing anxieties about corporate control of land — anxieties that trace back to the colonial alienation of land from cultivators.
> - **PM-KISAN (2019)**: Provides ₹6,000/year directly to small farmers — a recognition that Indian farmers remain economically vulnerable after decades of independence.
> - **Debt crisis**: According to the **NSSO (2019)**, over 50% of Indian farmer households are indebted — a continuation of the moneylender trap created by colonial revenue systems.
> - **Land Titling**: The **SVAMITVA Scheme (2020)** — providing property rights to residents of rural inhabited land through drone surveys — is a 21st-century attempt to correct the ambiguity in land records created by colonial-era faulty surveys.

## PART 3: BRITISH SOCIAL AND CULTURAL POLICY

---

### 3.1 Pre-1813: Non-Interference

Until 1813, the British followed a **strict policy of non-interference** in Indian social, religious, and cultural life.

---

### 3.2 Why Policy Changed After 1813

Three major revolutions in Europe forced a rethinking:

**(i) Industrial Revolution (18th century onwards)**
- Created **industrial capitalism** *(economic system based on factories and manufactured goods)*
- British industrialists wanted to make India a **big market for their goods**
- This required at least **partial modernisation** of Indian society

**(ii) Intellectual Revolution**
- Gave rise to new attitudes in mind, manners, and morals

**(iii) French Revolution**
- Spread the message of **liberty, equality, and fraternity**
- Unleashed forces of **democracy and nationalism**

**Key Thinkers of the New Wave:**
- *In thought*: **Bacon, Locke, Voltaire, Rousseau, Kant, Adam Smith, Bentham**
- *In literature*: **Wordsworth, Byron, Shelley, Charles Dickens**

---

### 3.3 Characteristics of the New Thought

**(i) Rationalism** — faith in reason and scientific attitude
**(ii) Humanism** — love of man; belief that every person is an end in themselves, not a tool for others' happiness → gave rise to **liberalism, socialism, and individualism**
**(iii) Doctrine of Progress** — nothing is static; all societies must change; humans can remodel nature and society on just, rational lines

---

### 3.4 Three Schools of Thought Among British Administrators

These new ideas created **sharp conflicts** among British officials in India:

#### 🔵 The Conservatives
- Advocated **introducing as few changes as possible**
- Believed Indian civilisation was *different* from European, not necessarily *inferior*
- If Western ideas were to be introduced, it must be **gradually and cautiously**
- Prioritised **social stability**
- **Early representatives**: Warren Hastings, Edmund Burke
- **Later representatives**: Munro, Metcalfe, Elphinstone
- > Most British officials in India leaned conservative throughout

#### 🔴 The Paternalistic Imperialists
- Became influential especially **after 1800**
- Were **sharply critical** of Indian society and culture
- Used this criticism to **justify** India's economic and political enslavement

#### 🟢 The Radicals
- Went **beyond** both Conservatives and Imperialists
- Applied **advanced humanistic and rational thought** to India
- Believed India **had the capacity to improve** and must be helped to do so
- Wanted India to join the modern world of **science and humanism**
- Advocated introduction of **modern Western science, philosophy, and literature**
- Some British officials arriving **after 1820** were Radicals
- Strongly supported by **Raja Rammohan Roy** and like-minded reformers

> ⚠️ **Important Nuance:** Even Radicals had limits. They wanted modernisation only within the boundaries set by the **needs of better exploitation** of India's resources. Safety and perpetuation of British rule was the **top priority** — everything else was secondary.

---

### 3.5 Indian Renaissance

Several Indian reformers took the initiative for social change and pushed for legislation:

- **Rammohan Roy**
- **Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar**
- **B.M. Malabari**

These reformers worked to get the government to pass laws **removing social evils embedded in so-called tradition**.

> *(For detailed discussion, refer to the chapter: 'Religious and Social Reform')*

---

### 3.6 The Government's Dilemma: "Colonial Modernisation"

The British government was caught in a bind:

> *Too much modernisation might generate forces hostile to their interests.*

So they chose **"colonial modernisation"** *(partial modernisation — introducing it in some areas, blocking it in others)*:

- **Introduce** modern education and administration (to serve imperial needs)
- **Block** political consciousness and democratic participation (which would challenge imperial control)

---

### 3.7 Role of Christian Missionaries

Missionaries viewed Christianity as a **superior religion** and sought to spread it through **Westernisation**, believing it would destroy Indian faith in their own culture.

**Their three-way alliance:**

- **With Radicals** — whose scientific approach they believed would undermine native culture and beliefs
- **With Imperialists** — because law, order, and British supremacy were essential for their work
- **With Business/Capitalists** — promising that Christian converts would be **better customers** for British goods

---

### 3.8 British Retreat After 1858

After 1858, the policy of "hesitant modernisation" was gradually **abandoned entirely**.

Why? Because Indians had become **apt pupils** — they rapidly moved toward:
- Modernising their own society
- Asserting their culture
- Demanding governance based on **liberty, equality, and justice**

The British response to this development:
- Sided openly with **socially orthodox and conservative elements** of society
- Actively **encouraged casteism and communalism**

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Social and Cultural Policy

> `[Modern Example]` The colonial legacy of encouraging caste consciousness has had lasting effects. The debate around **OBC sub-categorisation** (addressed by the **Supreme Court in August 2024**, allowing sub-classification within SC/ST/OBC categories for reservations) reflects how caste identity — partly hardened and codified by colonial census practices — continues to shape Indian politics. The **Census 2021** (delayed significantly) includes for the first time a **Socio-Economic and Caste Census** component — showing that caste data, first systematically collected by the British for divide-and-rule purposes, remains a contested political tool today.

## PART 4: BRITISH POLICY TOWARDS PRINCELY STATES

---

### 4.1 The Two-Point Policy: "Subordinate Union"

After 1857, British policy towards the roughly 565 princely states was guided by two principles:

1. **Use and perpetuate** them as a **bulwark (buffer) of the empire**
2. **Subordinate them completely** to British authority

---

### 4.2 Shift from Annexation to Accommodation

**Before 1857:** The **Doctrine of Lapse** (Dalhousie's policy) aggressively annexed princely states.

**After 1857:** Policy reversed completely:
- Annexation was **abandoned**
- New policy: **"Depose or punish — but do NOT annex"**
- **Territorial integrity** of states was guaranteed
- Right to **adopt an heir** was guaranteed — reversing Dalhousie's use of this to justify annexation
- States were cultivated as a **buffer against future political unrest**
- Rewarded for **loyalty during the 1857 revolt**

---

### 4.3 How Subordination Was Completed

The fiction that Indian states were **"independent, sovereign states"** equal to the British Crown was ended when:

- **1876**: Queen Victoria adopted the title **Kaiser-i-Hind (Queen Empress of India)** — asserting British **sovereignty over ALL of India**, including princely states

**Lord Curzon** later made clear that **princes ruled only as agents of the British Crown**.

Through **paramountcy** *(supreme authority exercised by British over princely states)*:
- British Government could **interfere in internal affairs** of states through Residents
- Could **appoint and dismiss** ministers and officials

**Modern communications helped tighten control:**
- Railways, roads, telegraph, canals, post offices — all extended British reach into princely territories

**Suppression of nationalism in princely states:**
- Rise of nationalist, democratic sentiment in these states worried the British
- British helped suppress these movements — seeing them as a threat to their own survival
- As a positive outcome: British did help these states adopt **modern administrative institutions**

> *(For more detail, refer to chapter: 'Indian States under British Rule')*

---

### 🌍 Modern Context: Princely States Integration

> `[Modern Example]` The integration of 562 princely states into independent India (1947-49), led by **Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel** and V.P. Menon, resolved what the British had deliberately kept as a source of fragmentation. The legal instrument used — the **Instrument of Accession** — was built on the same paramountcy framework the British had created. The **accession of Hyderabad (Operation Polo, 1948)** and **Junagadh** remain case studies in how colonial-era political structures required forceful resolution at Independence. **Article 291 and Article 362** (Privy Purses) — eventually abolished by the **26th Constitutional Amendment, 1971** — were the last remnants of the British system of patronising princely states.

### 5.1 Core Principle: British Interests, Indian Blood

### 5.2 Why India Was Drawn Into External Conflicts

**Reason 1: Drive for Natural Frontiers**
- Political and administrative consolidation of India + modern communication systems pushed the British to seek **natural, geographical frontiers** for internal cohesion and defence
- This sometimes led to **border clashes** with neighbouring states

**Reason 2: Three Strategic Aims of the British Empire in Asia and Africa**

| Aim | Explanation |
|-----|-------------|
| **(i) Protect the Indian Empire** | India was the "jewel in the crown" — the most valuable colonial possession |
| **(ii) Expand British commercial and economic interests** | New markets, trade routes, raw materials |
| **(iii) Keep rival European powers at arm's length** | Especially **Russia** (the "Great Game" in Central Asia) and **France** |

These three aims led to:
- British **expansion and territorial conquests** outside India's natural frontiers
- **Conflicts with other European imperial powers**

### 🌍 Modern Context: Foreign Policy Legacy

> `[Modern Example]` The **Durand Line** (1893) — the boundary between British India and Afghanistan drawn by Sir Mortimer Durand — remains one of the most contested borders in the world today, unrecognised by Afghanistan and a source of Pakistan-Afghanistan tensions. The **McMahon Line** (1914) — drawn at the Shimla Convention — is the basis of India's claim on Arunachal Pradesh and remains the core of the **India-China border dispute** that erupted violently in the **Galwan Valley clash (June 2020)**, resulting in casualties on both sides. These "colonial cartographic decisions" continue to shape 21st-century geopolitics.
