### **I. The Mameluk (Slave) Sultans: A Chronology of Consolidation**

This period marks the transition from mere conquest to the establishment of a state. The primary struggle was establishing a strong monarchy against Turkish nobles (*Chahalgani*) and external threats.

#### **1. Qutbuddin Aibak (1206–1210)**

* **Succession:** Turkish slave of Muizzuddin (Ghori); succeeded him in 1206.
* **Key Achievement:** Severed links with Ghazni (ruled from Lahore).
* *Significance:* This prevented India from being drawn into Central Asian politics and allowed the Delhi Sultanat to develop independently.


* **Death:** Died in **1210** from injuries while playing *Chaugan* (Polo).

#### **2. Iltutmish (1210–1236)**

* **Status:** Son-in-law of Aibak. Regarded as the **real consolidator** of Turkish conquests.
* **Initial Challenges:**
* **Heredity Check:** Had to defeat Aibak’s son to claim the throne.
* **Rebellions:** Ali Mardan (Bengal/Bihar), Qubacha (Multan/Lahore), and Rajputs (Kalinjar, Gwaliyar, Ajmer) declared independence.


* **Diplomatic Masterstroke (The Mongol Threat):**
* In **1221**, Changez Khan (Genghis Khan) reached the Indus pursuing the Khwarizmi prince Jalaluddin.
* Iltutmish politely refused asylum to Jalaluddin.
* *Result:* Saved India from a Mongol invasion at a time when the Sultanat was weak.


* **Conquests:**
* Secured the West: Ousted Qubacha from Multan/Uchch (frontier reached Indus).
* Secured the East: Defeated Iwaz (who took the title Sultan Ghiyasuddin) in Bengal.
* Rajputana: Recovered Gwaliyar and Bayana; raided Ujjain and Nagda (Mewar), though faced repulses in Gujarat.



#### **3. Raziya (1236–1239)**

* **Nomination:** Iltutmish nominated his daughter over his sons—a novel step in medieval history.
* **The Conflict:** Struggle between Monarchy vs. **The Forty (*Chahalgani*)**.
* The Turkish chiefs wanted a puppet; Raziya refused to be one.


* **Reforms & Actions:**
* Discarded the veil (purdah), held court openly, hunted, and led armies.
* Defeated the Wazir, Nizam-ul-Mulk Junaidi.
* Tried to create a non-Turk nobility (e.g., favored **Yaqut Khan**, an Abyssinian).


* **Downfall:** Accused of violating modesty. Rebellions in Lahore and Sirhind. Captured by Altunia, married him, but both were killed by bandits in 1240.

#### **4. The Era of Balban (1246–1287)**

* **Rise to Power:**
* Initially the *Naib* (Deputy) to Sultan Nasiruddin Mahmud.
* Briefly ousted by **Imaduddin Raihan** (an Indian Muslim) in a conspiracy by Turkish nobles (1253) but returned to power due to his military strength.
* Ascended the throne in **1265**.


* **Theory of Kingship:**
* Claimed descent from the mythical Iranian hero **Afrasiyab**.
* **Exclusion Policy:** Championed Turkish nobility; excluded Indian Muslims and non-nobles from power.
* **Despotism:** Broke the power of the *Chahalgani* (The Forty). Poisoned his cousin Sher Khan to eliminate rivalry.


* **Administrative Reforms:**
* **Diwan-i-Arz:** Reorganized the military department. Pensioned off old soldiers.
* **Spy System:** Spies appointed in every department to check nobles.
* **Court Etiquette:** Introduced Persian customs **Sijada** (prostration) and **Paibos** (kissing feet) to exalt the monarchy.


* **Policy of "Blood and Iron":**
* Adopted ruthless measures to restore law and order in the Doab, Awadh, and Katehar (Rohilkhand).
* Forests were cleared, robbers killed, and Afghan soldier colonies established to safeguard roads.

### **II. The Mongol Threat (North-West Frontier)**

The geography of the Hindu Kush mountains meant that controlling the **Kabul-Ghazni-Qandahar** line was essential for India's defense. The Sultanat often failed to hold this line, bringing the threat to the Indus and eventually Delhi.

| Year/Period | Event | Outcome/Strategy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **1221** | Changez Khan arrives at Indus pursuing Jalaluddin. | Iltutmish refuses asylum; Changez retreats. India saved. |
| **1241** | Tair Bahadur (Mongol) attacks Lahore. | Governor fled; Lahore sacked and depopulated. |
| **1245** | Mongols invest Multan. | Balban marches to save the situation. |
| **Balban's Reign** | Constant Pressure. | **Frontier receded from Jhelum to Beas river.** Balban built forts at Bhatinda/Sunam and stayed in Delhi to monitor the frontier. |
| **1292** | Abdullah (grandson of Halaku) attacks with **1,50,000 horses**. | Defeated by Jalaluddin Khalji. **4,000 Mongols** convert to Islam and settle in Delhi ("New Muslims"). |
| **1299** | Qutlugh Khwaja attacks with **2,00,000 men**. | **First serious attempt to conquer Delhi.** Cut off Delhi's communications. Alauddin Khalji fought them; Mongols eventually withdrew. |
| **1303** | Mongols attack with **1,20,000 men**. | Alauddin Khalji retreated to **Siri Fort**. Mongols withdrew after 2 months. |

**Impact on Delhi Sultanat:**

1. **Centralization:** The threat forced the Sultans to maintain a strong centralized army.
2. **Cultural Hub:** Scholars and theologians fleeing Mongols in Central Asia flocked to Delhi, making it a center of Islamic learning.
3. **Economic Strain:** High cost of maintaining standing armies.

### **III. Internal Rebellions & Territorial Consolidation**

The Sultanat faced a "Two-Front War": Mongols in the West and internal rebellions in the East and Rajasthan.

#### **1. The Problem of Bengal (The East)**

Bengal was difficult to rule from Delhi due to distance and geography.

* **Iwaz (Ghiyasuddin Sultan):** Functioned independently until defeated by Iltutmish (1226-27).
* **Tughril (1280):** Rebelled during Balban's reign. Balban launched a savage campaign (his only distant expedition), hunted Tughril down, and executed his followers.
* **Bughra Khan:** Balban's son. Preferred ruling Bengal over the dangerous throne of Delhi. He and his successors ruled Bengal independently for the next **40 years**.

#### **2. Rajput Resistance (Rajasthan & Doab)**

Rajput Rajas and Zamindars constantly tried to regain independence.

* **Katehariya Rajputs:** Frequently raided Badaun/Amroha. Balban crushed them using his "Blood and Iron" policy.
* **Mewat:** Bhatti Rajputs in Mewat (near Delhi) plundered up to the city outskirts. Balban hunted them down and cleared forests.
* **Ranthambhor & Gwaliyar:** Frequently changed hands. Captured by Aibak/Iltutmish, recovered by Rajputs, and besieged again.

### **Current Context & Analysis**

> **From a Modern Perspective:**
> * **The North-West Frontier:** The struggle to control the Kabul-Qandahar line (modern Afghanistan) parallels modern geopolitical struggles. Historically, whoever controls this "gateway" influences the security of the Indian subcontinent.
> * **Balban's "Blood and Iron":** This can be analyzed as an early form of state-enforced internal security doctrine, prioritizing order over civil liberties to prevent anarchy—a debate relevant in governance today.
> * **Raziya Sultan:** She remains a symbol of women's leadership in a patriarchal era. Her failure was political (opposition from nobles), not due to a lack of capability.
