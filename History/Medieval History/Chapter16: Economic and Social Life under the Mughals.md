## 1. Economic Conditions: Agriculture and Trade

The Mughal era saw massive economic expansion, integration with world markets, and the influx of bullion (gold/silver).

### A. Agriculture (The Backbone)

* **Status:** India had a diversified economy. The countryside fed a growing urban population and exported grain (rice/sugar) to neighboring countries.
* **Crops:**
* **Staples:** Wheat, Rice, Gram, Barley, Pulses.
* **Cash Crops (High Revenue):** Cotton, Indigo, Sugarcane, Oilseeds.
* **New Crops (17th Century):** **Tobacco** and **Maize** were introduced.
* **New Crops (18th Century):** Potato and Red Chillies.


* **Sericulture:** Silk cultivation in Bengal grew so much that India stopped importing silk from China.
* **State Support:** Taccavi loans and incentives were given for expansion.
* **Peasant Rights:** Peasants had hereditary rights to cultivate land and could not be evicted as long as they paid revenue. They could even sell land (with community consent).

### B. Trade and Commerce

* **Inter-Regional Trade:**
* **Bengal:** Sugar, Rice, Muslin, Silk.
* **Gujarat:** Entry point for foreign goods. Exported fine textiles (*Patola*) to North India.
* **Coromandel Coast:** Textile production center.
* **Lahore/Multan:** Hubs for overland trade to Central Asia.
* **Agra/Burhanpur:** Nodal points connecting North and South.


* **Financial System:**
* **Hundis:** Letters of credit used to transfer money safely.
* **Sarrafs (Shroffs):** Money changers who acted as private banks.
* **Insurance:** Goods could be insured against loss in transit.


* **Transport:** River boats for bulk goods; carts/camels for luxury items. **Banjaras** were specialized traders for bulk transport.

### C. Foreign Trade

* **Exports:** Textiles ("Calicoes"), Indigo, Saltpetre (for gunpowder), Spices.
* **Imports:** Horses (West Asia), Metals (Tin, Copper), Luxury items (Ivory, Glassware), Silk/Porcelain (China).
* **Bullion Influx:** India had a favorable balance of trade. Gold and silver flowed in from Europe and Asia. Bernier called India the *"Sink of Gold and Silver"* (though exaggerated, it reflects the trend).

## 2. Social Life: Standard of Living

A society of stark contrasts: "Ostentatious wealth vs. Grinding poverty."

### A. The Common People (Peasants/Artisans)

* **Clothing:** Scanty. Men wore *langota*; women wore saris. Woolens were rare; shoes were expensive (barefoot was common).
* **Diet:** Better than clothing. Rice, millet, pulses (*Khicheri*). Ghee and oil were cheap; Salt/Sugar were expensive.
* **Housing:** Mud houses with thatched roofs. Little furniture (cots/bamboo mats).
* **Inequality in Villages:**
1. **Khudkasht:** Rich peasants with their own land/ploughs. Dominated the village.
2. **Muzarian:** Tenants who tilled others' land.
3. **Landless Labourers (Kamin):** Often untouchables; suffered most during famines.



### B. The Ruling Class (Nobles & Zamindars)

* **Nobles:** A privileged class.
* **Composition:** Composite (Turks, Afghans, Iranis, Indian Muslims, Rajputs, Marathas).
* **Lifestyle:** Extremely luxurious. Large harams, fine clothes, imported fruits (ice was a luxury).
* **Income:** High salaries (via Jagirs).
* **Investment:** Many nobles invested in trade/ships (e.g., **Mir Jumla** owned a fleet).


* **Zamindars:** Rural gentry with hereditary rights to collect revenue. Lived in forts (*Garhis*). Had their own armed retainers.

### C. The Merchant Class

* **Status:** Large, organized, and professional.
* **Communities:**
* **Gujarat:** Hindus, Jains, Bohra Muslims.
* **Rajasthan:** Marwaris (Oswals, Maheshwaris, Agarwals).
* **South:** Chettis, Muslim merchants of Malabar.
* **North/Overland:** Multanis, Khatris, Afghans.


* **Wealth:** Merchant princes like **Virji Vohra** (Surat), **Abdul Ghafur Bohra**, and **Malay Chetti** were comparable to European merchant princes.
* **Lifestyle:** Lived well but sometimes hid wealth to avoid being squeezed by officials (according to Bernier).

### D. The "Middle Strata"

* Not a "Middle Class" in the modern sense (no political rights).
* Included petty shopkeepers, master craftsmen, hakims, musicians, scholars, and lower officials.
* Often held small revenue-free land grants (*Madad-i-Maash*).

## 3. The Coming of European Traders

The 17th century saw the entry of the Dutch, English, and French, challenging the Portuguese monopoly.

### A. The Portuguese (Decline)

* Controlled the sea routes in the 16th century (*Cartaz* system).
* **Decline:** Lost **Ormuz** to the English/Persians (1622) and **Hughli** to Shah Jahan (1632). Remained confined to Goa, Daman, Diu.

### B. The Dutch (United East India Company)

* **Focus:** Spice Islands (Indonesia).
* **India Connection:** Needed Indian textiles to exchange for spices in SE Asia.
* **Centers:** Masulipatam (1606), Pulicat, Surat.

### C. The English (East India Company)

* **Arrival:** Captain Hawkins (1608). Set up factory at **Surat** (1612) after defeating the Portuguese.
* **Strategy:**
* Focused on textiles (*Calicoes*) and Indigo.
* Developed **Madras** (Fort St. George) and **Bombay**.
* Expanded to Bengal (Hughli/Balasore) for Silk and Saltpetre.


* **Impact:** Indian textiles became a rage in England. By the end of the 17th century, English trade was booming.

### D. Nature of European Trade

* **Limited Impact initially:** They could not oust Indian traders.
* **Cooperation:** Europeans often freighted goods of Indian merchants on their ships to survive.
* **Shift:** European nations tried to acquire territories to pay for exports using local revenues (since they ran out of silver to pay). This led to colonization later.

> **🌍 Current Context:**
> * **Globalization:** The Mughal era was an early form of globalization where Indian textiles clothed people from "Aden to Achin."
> * **Cash Crops:** The introduction of Tobacco and Maize in the 17th century changed Indian agriculture permanently, similar to the Green Revolution later.
