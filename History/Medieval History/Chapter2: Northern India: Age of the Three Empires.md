## 1. Introduction: The Geopolitical Shift (800–1000 AD)

After the decline of Harsha (7th Century), the political landscape changed. Unlike the Guptas or Harsha, no single power could control the entire Ganga valley *and* the periphery.

* **The Geographical Limit:** To build a North Indian empire, a ruler needed the **Ganga Valley** (resources/population) + **Gujarat** (trade ports). **Malwa and Rajasthan** were the essential links between them.
* **The Three Major Powers:**
1. **Palas** (East).
2. **Pratiharas** (West & Upper Gangetic Valley).
3. **Rashtrakutas** (Deccan - acted as a bridge between North and South).


* **Common Features:** All provided stability, expanded agriculture (canals/ponds), and patronized arts/temples.

---

## 2. The Struggle for Domination: The Tripartite Struggle

There was a constant struggle for the control of **Kanauj**.

* **Why Kanauj?** It was the symbol of sovereignty in North India (like Delhi later) and controlled the rich resources of the Upper Gangetic valley.
* **The Contenders:** Palas vs. Pratiharas vs. Rashtrakutas.

### A. The Pala Empire (Eastern India)

* **Founder:** **Gopala** (AD 750).
* *Unique Fact:* He was not royal by birth; he was **elected** by notable men to end anarchy.


* **Dharmapala (Son of Gopala):**
* Ruled till AD 810.
* Briefly controlled Kanauj and held a grand *Darbar*.
* **The Struggle:** Clashed with Pratiharas and Rashtrakutas. Was often defeated by the Rashtrakuta king Dhruva, but managed to survive.
* **Revival of Education:** Revived **Nalanda University** (set aside 200 villages for it) and founded **Vikramasila University**.


* **Devapala (Son of Dharmapala):**
* Extended control to Pragjyotishpur (Assam), Orissa, and parts of Nepal.


* **Foreign Accounts:**
* **Sulaiman (Arab Merchant):** Called the kingdom *Ruhma*. Mentioned they had a massive army (50,000 elephants) and employed thousands just for washing clothes!
* **Tibetan Chronicles:** Palas were great patrons of Buddhism.



**Cultural & Trade Relations:**

* **Tibet:** Sent scholars (Santarakshita and Atisa) to Tibet; introduced a new form of Buddhism there.
* **Southeast Asia:** Profitable trade with Malaya, Java, Sumatra (Sailendra Dynasty).
* *Example:* The **Sailendra king** (Buddhist) requested Devapala to endow 5 villages for a monastery at Nalanda.



> **🏛️ Current Context:**
> The ancient **Nalanda University**, revived by the Palas, has been re-established as a modern international university in Rajgir, Bihar (Nalanda University Act, 2010), symbolizing India's historical soft power.

---

### B. The Pratihara Empire (Western India)

* **Origins:** **Gurjara-Pratiharas** (Likely pastoralists/fighters like Jats).
* **Strategic Role:** They acted as a shield for India. **Nagabhatta I** decisively defeated the Arab rulers of Sind (AD 738), stopping the Muslim expansion into India for centuries.
* **Bhoja (The Greatest Ruler):**
* Also known as **Mihir Bhoja** or *Adivaraha* (devotee of Vishnu).
* Rebuilt the empire and made **Kanauj** the capital (held for a century).
* Maintained the **best cavalry** in India (imported horses from Central Asia/Arabia).


* **Mahendrapala I:** Extended empire to North Bengal and Kathiawar.
* **Patronage:** The great Sanskrit poet **Rajashekhar** lived at their court.

**Foreign Accounts (Al-Masudi - Baghdad traveler):**

* Visited in 915–16 AD.
* Called the kingdom *al-Juzr* (Gurjara).
* Described the immense size of the army (4 divisions fighting Muslims in the North, Rashtrakutas in the South, and Palas in the East).

**Decline:**

* Weakened by **Rashtrakuta invasions** (Indra III sacked Kanauj in 915; Krishna III invaded in 963).
* Lost Gujarat (the hub of trade), leading to economic decline.

---

### C. The Rashtrakuta Empire (The Deccan)

* **Capital:** **Manyakhet** (Malkhed, near Sholapur).
* **Founder:** **Dantidurga**.
* **Significance:** Lasted the longest; most powerful of the three; acted as a cultural/economic bridge between North and South.

**Key Rulers:**

1. **Govinda III:** A warrior. Defeated rulers from Kanauj in the North to Kerala/Lanka in the South.
2. **Amoghavarsha (814–878):**
* Ruled for 64 years.
* **Nature:** Preferred religion/literature over war. Wrote the **first Kannada book on poetics**.
* **Builder:** Built Manyakhet to rival the city of Indra.


3. **Krishna III:** Last brilliant ruler. Defeated the Cholas (Parantaka I) and set up a pillar of victory at **Rameshwaram**.

**Religion & Culture:**

* **Tolerance:** Patronized Shaivism, Vaishnavism, and Jainism.
* **Islam:** Allowed Muslim traders to settle, build mosques, and preach Islam (this boosted foreign trade).
* **Architecture:** **Krishna I** built the famous **Rock-cut Shiva temple at Ellora**.
* **Language:** Patronized Sanskrit, Prakrit, and **Apabhramsha** (forerunner to modern Indian languages).

> **🌍 Current Context:**
> The **Ellora Caves** (Maharashtra) are a UNESCO World Heritage site today, celebrated for religious harmony as they contain Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain temples side-by-side, reflecting the Rashtrakuta policy of tolerance.

## 3. Political Ideas and Organization

The administration was based on the Gupta and Harsha models but with growing feudal characteristics.

### A. The Monarchy

* **King:** Absolute power, Commander-in-chief, head of justice.
* **Succession:** Generally hereditary (eldest son), but often contested (wars between brothers were common).
* **Philosophy:**
* King adopted titles like *Maharajadhiraj*.
* **Medhatithi (Thinker):** Stated that an individual has the right to bear arms for self-defense and **right to oppose an unjust king**. (This contradicted the extreme "Divine Right" view).



### B. Administration Hierarchy

The empire was divided into units for governance. Note the difference in terminology:

| Unit | Pala / Pratihara Term | Rashtrakuta Term | Head Official |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Province** | Bhukti | Rashtra | Uparika / Rashtrapati |
| **District** | Mandala / Visaya | Visaya | Visayapati |
| **Sub-District** | Pattala | Bhukti | -- |
| **Village** | Grama | Grama | Village Headman |

### C. The Army

* **Structure:** Infantry, Cavalry, and Elephants.
* **Elephants:** Palas had the largest number.
* **Cavalry:** Pratiharas had the finest (imported horses).
* **Source:** Regular hereditary troops + Levies supplied by feudal vassals.

### D. Feudalism (The Rise of Samantas)

* **Direct vs. Indirect Rule:** The King directly ruled only the central area. The rest was ruled by **Vassals (Samantas/Chiefs)**.
* **Role of Vassals:** Paid fixed tribute, supplied troops, attended Darbar. They were autonomous internally but often rebelled.
* **Feudalisation of Administration:** Officials (and even village headmen) were paid via **Rent-Free Land Grants** instead of cash.
* This made officials hereditary and powerful.
* **Nad Gavundas** (Deccan revenue officers) later became Deshmukhs/Deshpandes.



### E. Village Administration

* **Headman:** Hereditary; helped by village elders (*Grama Mahajana*).
* **Committees:** Specifically in the **Rashtrakuta** empire (Karnataka region), village committees managed schools, tanks, and temples.
* **Law & Order:** Managed by *Koshta Pala* (Kotwal).

### F. Religion and State

* **Secular Politics:** Politics (*Arthashastra*) was kept separate from Religion (*Dharmashastra*).
* **Role of King:** Protect the social order (Varnas) but not force religious beliefs.
* **Tolerance:** Kings patronized all religions (Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism) to legitimize their power.
