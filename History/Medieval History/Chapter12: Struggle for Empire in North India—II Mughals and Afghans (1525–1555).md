# **Struggle for Empire in North India – II: Mughals and Afghans (1525–1555)**

---

## **1. Central Asia and Babur’s Background**

### **Political Situation in Central Asia (15th–16th Century)**
- **Timur’s Empire:** Unified Iran and Turan; extended from Volga to Indus.
- **After Timur:** Empire fragmented due to Timurid tradition of partitioning.
- **Rise of New Powers:**
  - **Uzbeks** (Sunni Muslims) – expanded into Trans-Oxiana.
  - **Safavids** (Shia Muslims) – rose in Iran.
  - **Ottoman Turks** – expanded into Eastern Europe and Iran.
- **Babur’s Early Life:**
  - Born 1483; inherited Farghana (small state in Trans-Oxiana) at age 12.
  - Attempted to capture Samarqand twice but lost to Uzbeks under Shaibani Khan.
  - Moved to Kabul (conquered 1504) after being displaced.

### **Babur Turns Towards India**
- **Motivations:**
  - Lure of India’s wealth.
  - Claim to Punjab (inherited from Timurid ancestors).
  - Meager income from Kabul, Qandhar, Badakhshan.
  - Need to strengthen resources against Uzbek threat.
- **Political Opportunity:**
  - Ibrahim Lodi’s centralizing policies alienated Afghan chiefs (e.g., Daulat Khan Lodi, governor of Punjab).
  - Daulat Khan and Rana Sanga invited Babur to invade.

---

## **2. Babur’s Conquest of India**

### **Initial Campaigns (1518–1525)**
- 1518–1519: Captured Bhira (gateway to Hindustan).
- 1520–1521: Captured Bhira, Sialkot, Lahore; withdrew due to revolt in Qandhar.
- 1525: Daulat Khan rebelled; Babur defeated him and gained Punjab.

### **First Battle of Panipat (April 20, 1526)**
- **Forces:**  
  - Ibrahim Lodi: ~100,000 men, 1,000 elephants.  
  - Babur: ~12,000 (+ Indian allies), superior tactics.
- **Babur’s Tactics:**
  - Used Ottoman (Rumi) device: chained carts + breastworks for artillery.
  - Deployed gunpowder artillery effectively (master gunners Ustad Ali & Mustafa).
  - Cavalry flanking maneuvers.
- **Outcome:**
  - Ibrahim Lodi killed; 15,000+ Afghan soldiers dead.
  - Babur gained Delhi, Agra, treasures.
- **Significance:**
  - Broke Lodi power but did not fully establish Mughal rule.
  - Introduced gunpowder artillery as decisive in Indian warfare.

### **Challenges After Panipat**
- Hot weather, homesick soldiers, hostile populace.
- Babur insisted on staying in India: “Not for us the poverty of Kabul again.”
- Rajput confederacy under **Rana Sanga** now primary threat.

### **Battle of Khanwa (1527)**
- **Causes:** Rana Sanga saw Babur as threat; Afghans (Mahmud Lodi) joined him.
- **Babur’s Preparation:**
  - Declared jihad, banned wine, abolished customs tax for Muslims.
  - Fortified position at Khanwa with carts, trenches, artillery.
- **Forces:** Sanga’s forces larger (200,000 claimed), Babur’s smaller but disciplined.
- **Outcome:** Mughal artillery + flanking attacks defeated Rajputs. Sanga escaped, later poisoned.
- **Aftermath:** Babur secured Delhi-Agra region; captured Gwalior, Dholpur, Chanderi.

### **Conflict with Afghans in East (1529)**
- Afghans regrouped under Mahmud Lodi + Nusrat Shah of Bengal.
- **Battle of Ghagra:** Babur crossed Ganga, forced Afghan-Bengal retreat but no decisive victory.
- **Agreement:** Afghan chiefs retained Bihar; treaty with Bengal.
- Babur returned to Agra; died en route to Kabul (1530).

---

## **3. Significance of Babur’s Advent**

### **Geopolitical Impact**
- Kabul and Qandhar integrated with North India → security from invasions for ~200 years.
- Boosted India’s trans-Asian trade (Kabul–Qandhar as trade hubs).

### **Military Innovations**
- Effective combination of artillery + cavalry.
- Gunpowder widely adopted → favored larger, centralized states.

### **Administrative & Cultural**
- Restored prestige of monarchy (Timurid lineage).
- Personal leadership: shared hardships with soldiers.
- Patron of literature (wrote *Baburnama* in Turkish), naturalist, garden builder.
- Religious policy: Orthodox Sunni but not bigoted; no widespread temple destruction.

## **4. Humayun’s Reign (1530–1540) and Conflict with Sher Shah**

### **Early Challenges**
- Inherited precarious finances, unconsolidated administration.
- **Brother Rivalry:** Kamran took Lahore, Multan; Humayun accepted to avoid civil war.

### **Gujarat Campaign (1535–1536)**
- **Bahadur Shah of Gujarat:** captured Malwa, threatened Rajasthan, aided Afghan rebels.
- **Humayun’s Response:**  
  - Captured Mandu, Champaner, rich treasuries.  
  - Placed Gujarat under brother Askari → mismanagement → lost Gujarat and Malwa.
- **Outcome:** Bahadur Shah’s threat ended (drowned 1537).

### **Rise of Sher Khan (Sher Shah Sur)**
- **Early Career:** Jagirdar in Jaunpur; served Bihar ruler; gained power.
- **Strengthened Position:** Controlled Bihar, recruited Afghans, received subsidies from Bahadur Shah.
- **Conflict with Humayun:**
  - 1532: Humayun defeated Afghans at Dadrah, besieged Chunar.
  - Sher Khan surrendered Chunar, promised loyalty (sent son as hostage).
  - 1537: Sher Khan captured Bengal (Gaur) while Humayun besieged Chunar.

### **Humayun’s Bengal Campaign & Downfall**
- Rejected Sher Khan’s offer (keep Bengal, pay tribute).
- Marched to Gaur (Bengal) but got isolated.
- **Battle of Chausa (1539):**  
  - Sher Khan’s surprise attack; Humayun barely escaped (swam across Ganga).
  - 7,000 Mughals killed; immense treasure lost.
- **Battle of Kanauj (1540):**  
  - Mughal forces defeated due to poor coordination, Sher Khan’s superior generalship.
  - Humayun became a fugitive.

### **Humayun in Exile (1540–1545)**
- Wandered Sindh, Rajasthan; brothers turned against him.
- Sought help from Iran, regained Qandhar and Kabul (1545).
- Returned to India (1555) after Sur empire weakened; died 1556 (fall from library).

---

## **5. Sher Shah Sur and the Sur Empire (1540–1555)**

### **Sher Shah’s Expansion**
- Empire: Bengal to Indus (except Kashmir), Malwa, Rajasthan.
- **Conquest of Rajasthan:**  
  - Battle of Samel (1544) vs. Maldeo of Marwar – Afghan victory using ruse + artillery.  
  - Captured Ajmer, Jodhpur, Chittor.
- **Death:** Died during siege of Kalinjar (1545) from gunburst injuries.

### **Administrative Reforms**
- **Law & Order:** Stern action against rebels, robbers; safe roads.
- **Roads & Sarais:**
  - Grand Trunk Road (Indus to Sonargaon).
  - Agra–Jodhpur–Chittor road; Lahore–Multan road.
  - Built ~1700 sarais (rest houses) every 8 km; provided food, security, postal service.
- **Trade Policy:**
  - Customs duty only at Bengal border (Sikrigali) and Indus.
  - Local headmen responsible for merchants’ safety.
- **Currency:** Introduced pure silver *rupee* (standard for centuries).
- **Land Revenue (Key Reform):**
  - Measurement of sown land (*patta* system).
  - Crop rates (*ray*) – state’s share = 1/3 produce.
  - Classification: good, middling, bad land.
  - Peasant protection: “If I oppress them, they will abandon villages.”
- **Army Reforms:**
  - Direct recruitment; descriptive roll (*chehra*); horse branding (*dagh*).
  - Strong artillery, cantonments.
- **Justice:** Qazis appointed; village panchayats retained. Islam Shah (son) codified laws.

### **Architecture & Culture**
- Tomb at Sasaram (architectural masterpiece).
- Purana Qila (Delhi) with mosque.
- Patronized Hindi literature (e.g., Malik Muhammad Jaisi’s *Padmavat*).

### **Religious Policy**
- Orthodox but pragmatic; continued jizyah; nobility mostly Afghan.
- No systematic liberal policies like later Akbar.

---

## **6. Present-Day Connections (Optional Insights)**
- **Grand Trunk Road:** Basis for modern NH2 (Delhi–Kolkata) and NH1 (Delhi–Amritsar).
- **Sher Shah’s Rupee:** Precursor to Mughal & British Indian currency systems.
- **Administrative Legacy:** *Patta* system influenced later Mughal *zabt* and British land settlements.
- **Babur’s Memoirs (*Baburnama*):** Valuable source for ecology, society of early 16th-century India.
- **Military Evolution:** Gunpowder technology changed Indian warfare, leading to centralized empires.

## **7. Summary Timeline**
- **1526:** Panipat – Babur defeats Ibrahim Lodi.
- **1527:** Khanwa – Babur defeats Rana Sanga.
- **1529:** Ghagra – Babur checks Afghans + Bengal.
- **1530:** Humayun ascends; Kamran seizes Punjab.
- **1535–36:** Humayun’s Gujarat campaign.
- **1539:** Chausa – Sher Shah defeats Humayun.
- **1540:** Kanauj – Sher Shah deposes Humayun.
- **1540–45:** Sher Shah expands, reforms administration.
- **1545:** Sher Shah dies at Kalinjar.
- **1555:** Humayun regains Delhi after Sur decline.
