# **Cultural Development in India (1300–1500)**

---

## **1. Introduction: Turkish Impact on Indian Culture**
- **Turks were not barbarians:** They had absorbed Arabo-Persian culture (Islamic, with advancements in science, navigation, literature).
- **Interaction with Indians:** Led to a new enriched culture through periods of destruction and construction.
- **Areas of assimilation:** Art, architecture, music, literature, customs, rituals, science, and technology.

---

## **2. Architecture**

### **Early Phase: Adaptation & New Forms**
- **Initial approach:** Converted temples/monasteries into mosques (e.g., Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque, Delhi; Arhai Din ka Jhonpra, Ajmer).
- **Decoration:** Avoided human/animal figures; used floral scrolls, Quranic verses (arabesque), and Hindu motifs (bell, lotus, swastika).
- **New architectural elements:**
  - Arch and dome (borrowed from Rome via Byzantium).
  - Use of light, strong mortar.
  - Allowed large, pillar-less halls.

### **Key Monuments & Styles**
- **Qutab Minar (13th century):**
  - Started by Aibak, completed by Iltutmish.
  - Unique: balconies, red & white sandstone, marble panels.
- **Khalji period:**
  - Alai Darwaza: first scientifically constructed dome.
- **Tughlaq period:**
  - Tughlaqabad fort, artificial lake.
  - **Features:** Sloping walls ("batter"), combination of arch + lintel/beam, use of greystone, minimal decoration, lotus motif.
  - Hauz Khas: pleasure resort with madrasa.
- **Lodi period:**
  - Tombs on high platforms, octagonal shapes, gardens (e.g., Lodi Garden).
  - Blended Rajasthani-Gujarati styles (balconies, kiosks).
- **Regional styles developed** in Bengal, Gujarat, Malwa, Deccan.

## **3. Religious Ideas & Beliefs**

### **Background: Islam in India Pre-Turks**
- Present in Sindh (8th century), Punjab (10th century), Kerala (Arab settlers).
- Al-Biruni’s *Kitab-ul-Hind* familiarized West Asia with Hindu ideas.
- Indian philosophies may have influenced early Islamic thought.

### **The Sufi Movement**
- **Origin:** Islamic mystics, emphasized love and personal devotion.
- **Al-Ghazzali (d. 1112):** Reconciled mysticism with Islamic orthodoxy.
- **Sufi orders (silsilahs):** 12 orders; based in *khanqahs* (hospices) with *pir-murid* (teacher-disciple) relationship.
- **Influences:** Possible Buddhist/Hindu yogic impact (fasting, breath control).
- **Two main orders in India:**
  1. **Chishti:**
     - Founded by Khwaja Muinuddin Chishti (Ajmer).
     - Saints: Bakhtiyar Kaki, Farid-ud-Din Ganj-i-Shakar, Nizamuddin Auliya, Nasiruddin Chiragh-i-Delhi.
     - Lifestyle: Simple, austere; mixed with lower classes; used Hindi; musical recitals (*sama*); avoided politics.
  2. **Suharwardi:**
     - Active in Punjab/Multan.
     - Saints: Shaikh Shihabuddin Suharwardi, Hamid-ud-Din Nagori.
     - Accepted state service, held official posts.

### **The Bhakti Movement**
- **Origins:** In Vedas; popularized in south India (7th–12th centuries) by Alvars and Nayanars.
- **Spread to North:** Via saints like Namadeva (Marathi poet) and Ramananda (Prayag/Banaras).
- **Ramananda’s reforms:** Worship of Rama; disciples from all castes (Kabir, Ravidas, Sena).
- **Social context:** Brahmans lost prestige after Turkish conquest; Sufi ideas of equality also influential.

### **Key Bhakti Saints**
- **Kabir (15th century):**
  - Rejected idol worship, pilgrimages, caste.
  - Emphasized one God (called by many names).
  - Mission: religion of love unifying all castes/creeds.
- **Guru Nanak (1469–1538):**
  - Emphasized one God, purity of conduct, need for guru.
  - Rejected rituals, caste distinctions.
  - Laid foundation for Sikhism.

### **Vaishnavite Movement**
- **Focus:** Worship of Rama and Krishna.
- **Major figures:**
  - Chaitanya (Bengal/Orissa): ecstatic worship through song/dance (*kirtan*).
  - Mirabai (Rajasthan), Surdas (UP), Narsinha Mehta (Gujarat).
- **Philosophy:** Vedantic monism (unity of God and creation).
- **Humanistic approach:** Open to all castes/creeds.

### **Convergence of Sufi & Bhakti Ideas**
- **Sufi adoption of Indian ideas:** Ibn-i-Arabi’s "Unity of Being" (*Tauhid-i-Wajudi*) popular.
- **Cultural exchange:** Sufis used Hindi songs; wrote commentaries on Hindu themes (e.g., Abdul Wahid Bilgrami’s *Haqaiq-i-Hindi*).
- **Result:** A shared platform for inter-religious understanding, influencing Akbar’s *tauhid*.

## **4. Literature**

### **Sanskrit Literature**
- **Continued prominence:** Philosophy (Ramanuja, Madhava), *kavya*, drama, medicine, astronomy, law.
- **Legal texts:** *Mitakshara* (Vijnaneshwar), Chandeshwar’s works.
- **Limited engagement with Islamic texts:** Only a few translations (e.g., Jami’s *Yusuf-Zulaikha*).

### **Arabic & Persian Literature**
- **Arabic:** Restricted to Islamic scholars; digests of Islamic law (e.g., *Fatawa-i-Alamgiri*).
- **Persian:** Main administrative/literary language under Turks.
- **Amir Khusrau (1252–1325):**
  - Prolific poet; proud Indian.
  - Created *sabaq-i-hindi* (Indian style Persian).
  - Wrote Hindi verses; contributed to music.
- **Historiography:** Ziauddin Barani, Afif, Isami.

### **Translations & Cross-Cultural Exchange**
- **Persian translations of Sanskrit texts:**
  - *Tuti Nama* (Zia Nakhshabi), *Kok Shastra*, medical/music texts under Firuz Tughlaq.
  - Rajatarangini, Mahabharata translated under Zain-ul-Abidin (Kashmir).
- **Sanskrit translations** of Arabic/Persian works on mathematics, astronomy.

### **Regional Languages**
- **Growth aided by:** Bhakti saints, regional kingdoms’ patronage.
- **Examples:**
  - Bengali: Mahabharata/Ramayana translated under Nusrat Shah.
  - Hindi: Used by Sufis (Malik Muhammad Jaisi).
  - Marathi, Telugu, etc., used for administration/literature.
- **Amir Khusrau’s observation** (14th century): Regional languages long used for daily life.

---

## **5. Fine Arts (Music)**
- **Turkish contribution:** New instruments (rabab, sarangi), Persian musical modes.
- **Amir Khusrau:** Introduced Perso-Arabic *ragas*; associated with sitar (disputed) and tabla (later development).
- **Integration under Firuz Tughlaq:** *Ragadarpan* translated to Persian.
- **Regional patronage:**
  - Sultan Husain Sharqi (Jaunpur).
  - Raja Man Singh (Gwaliyar): *Man Kautuhal* included new musical modes.
- **Result:** Distinct north Indian style influenced by Persian elements.

---

## **6. Present-Day Connections (Optional Insights)**
- **Architectural legacy:** Qutab Minar, Alai Darwaza, Lodi Gardens are preserved monuments; Tughlaq/Lodi styles influenced Mughal architecture (e.g., tombs, gardens).
- **Sufi-Bhakti impact:** Ideas of Kabir, Nanak, Chaitanya remain influential in promoting interfaith harmony; Sikhism and Kabir Panth are living traditions.
- **Language evolution:** Regional languages that matured in this period are modern India’s official languages; Persian’s administrative influence lasted until British colonial period.
- **Musical synthesis:** Hindustani classical music’s evolution reflects Perso-Arabic influences; instruments like sitar and sarangi are central to Indian music today.
- **Cultural integration:** This period laid groundwork for India’s composite culture, reflected in modern secularism and pluralism.

---

## **7. Summary: Key Cultural Trends (1300–1500)**
- **Architecture:** Evolution from temple conversion to Indo-Islamic synthesis; arch/dome construction mastered.
- **Religion:** Sufi and Bhakti movements emphasized devotion, equality, and convergence of Hindu-Muslim ideas.
- **Literature:** Sanskrit remained strong; Persian became link language; regional languages flourished.
- **Music:** Integration of Indian and Perso-Arabic traditions created new forms.
- **Overall:** A period of significant cultural interaction, leading to a unique Indo-Islamic synthesis that shaped later Indian civilization.
