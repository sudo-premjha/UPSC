## **1. Birth & Early Challenges (1542–1556)**
- **Birth:** Akbar was born in 1542 at Amarkot, while his father Humayun was fleeing.
- **Childhood:** Captured by his uncle Kamran after Humayun fled to Iran; reunited with parents after the capture of Qandhar.
- **Accession:** Crowned at Kalanaur (Punjab) in 1556 at age 13, while fighting Afghan rebels.
- **Initial Threat:** Afghan forces under **Hemu** (Adil Shah’s wazir) captured Agra and Delhi after defeating Mughals near Delhi.
- **Bairam Khan’s Role:** As regent (wakil), he rallied Mughal forces. The **Second Battle of Panipat (1556)** was won after Hemu was struck by an arrow and captured. Akbar effectively had to reconquer his empire.

---

## **2. Early Phase – Contest with the Nobility (1556–1567)**
### **Bairam Khan’s Regency (1556–1560)**
- Expanded empire from Kabul to Jaunpur, Ajmer, Gwalior.
- Growing friction as Akbar matured; Bairam Khan accused of favoring Shias.
- **Akbar’s Move:** In 1560, Akbar dismissed Bairam Khan from Delhi. Bairam rebelled but surrendered after 6 months; offered option to retire to Mecca.
- **Aftermath:** Bairam Khan was assassinated en route by an Afghan with a personal grudge. Akbar married his widow (Akbar’s cousin) and raised his son **Abdur Rahim Khan-i-Khanan**.

### **Challenges from Nobles & Family**
- **Maham Anaga & Adham Khan:** After Bairam Khan’s fall, Akbar’s foster family gained influence. Adham Khan murdered the acting wazir; Akbar had him thrown from the fort (1561).
- **Uzbek Nobles’ Rebellion:** Powerful in eastern UP, Bihar, Malwa; rebelled repeatedly (1561–1567). Akbar finally crushed them in 1567 after marching from Lahore to Jaunpur.
- **Mirza Hakim’s Threat:** Akbar’s half-brother, ruler of Kabul, attacked Lahore (1567) but retreated when Akbar advanced.

---

## **3. Early Expansion of the Empire (1560–1576)**
### **Malwa (1561–1562)**
- Ruled by **Baz Bahadur** (known for arts, romance with Rupmati). First expedition under Adham Khan captured Malwa but cruelty caused revolt.
- After Bairam Khan’s rebellion, Akbar sent another expedition; Baz Bahadur fled, later became a Mughal mansabdar.

### **Garh-Katanga (Gond Kingdom)**
- Ruled by **Rani Durgavati** (widow of Sangram Shah’s son). Mughal governor Asaf Khan attacked (1564); Rani fought bravely, committed suicide to avoid capture.
- Asaf Khan plundered the capital; Akbar later forced him to disgorge gains and restored the kingdom to Chandra Shah (younger son of Sangram Shah).

### **Rajasthan (1567–1570)**
- **Siege of Chittor (1568):** Key to central Rajasthan. After 6 months, Mughals stormed it; 30,000 defenders massacred. Statues of Rajput warriors Jaimal and Patta erected at Agra gate.
- **Ranthambhor, Jodhpur, Bikaner, Jaisalmer** submitted. Only **Mewar** under Rana Udai Singh (later Rana Pratap) resisted.

### **Gujarat (1572–1573)**
- Fertile, rich trade province. Akbar marched via Ajmer; Ahmedabad surrendered.
- Fought Mirzas at Cambay; saw sea first time, met Portuguese traders.
- After Akbar left, rebellion broke out; Akbar raced back in 9 days (normally 6 weeks) with 3000 men and defeated 20,000 rebels at Ahmedabad (1573).

### **Bengal & Bihar (1574–1576)**
- Afghans under **Daud Khan** declared independence. Akbar sent forces; Patna captured.
- Daud defeated and executed in battle in Bihar (1576), ending last Afghan kingdom in north India.

---

## **4. Administrative System**
### **Land Revenue (Mughal Innovations)**
- Initially followed **Sher Shah’s system** (measurement, crop rates).
- Problems: Central prices caused peasant hardship.
- **Dahsala System (1580):** Based on average produce & prices of previous 10 years; state share = 1/3 of produce, converted to cash.
- **Assessment Methods:**
  1. **Zabti** – Measurement system (Todar Mal associated).
  2. **Batai** – Division of crop between state and peasant.
  3. **Nasaq** – Rough calculation based on past payments.
- **Land Classification:** Polaj (annually cultivated), Parati (fallow), Chachar (uncultivated 2–3 years), Banjar (longer fallow). Concessional rates for bringing new land under cultivation.
- **Peasant Support:** Loans (taccavi) for seeds, tools; hereditary cultivation rights.

### **Mansabdari System & Army**
- **Ranks (Mansabs):** From 10 to 5000 (later 7000). Divided into **zat** (personal rank/salary) and **sawar** (cavalry to maintain).
- **Dagh System:** Branding of horses; descriptive roll (chehra) of soldiers.
- **Army Composition:** Mixed contingents (Mughal, Rajput, Pathan, Hindustani).
- **Salaries & Jagirs:** Mansabdars paid via jagir assignments or cash.
- **Transport & Animals:** Required to maintain horses, elephants, camels, carts.
- **Akbar’s Innovations:** Gentleman troopers (ahadis), efficient artillery, river flotilla. Weakness: No strong navy.

---

## **5. Organization of Government**
### **Central Government**
- **Wakil** – Prime minister (post weakened under Akbar).
- **Diwan** – Head of revenue (chief financial officer).
- **Mir Bakhshi** – Head of military, intelligence, recommendations for appointments.
- **Mir Saman** – In charge of imperial household, workshops (karkhanas).
- **Chief Qazi** – Head of judiciary; combined with **Chief Sadr** (charitable grants).
- **Provincial Administration:** 12 subas (provinces) from 1580, each with governor (subedar), diwan, bakhshi, sadr, qazi, waqia-navis.

---

## **6. Relations with Rajputs**
### **Policy of Alliance**
- **Matrimonial Alliances:** Married daughter of Raja Bhara Mal of Amber (1562); gave religious freedom to Hindu wives, honored their families.
- **Service & Autonomy:** Rajput rulers given high mansabs, governorships (e.g., Man Singh in Bengal, Bhagwant Das in Lahore).
- **Religious Toleration:** Abolished jizyah (1564) and pilgrim tax.

### **Resistance from Mewar**
- **Rana Pratap** refused personal homage. Battle of **Haldighati (1576)**: Mughals (Man Singh) vs. Rana’s forces (Rajputs + Afghans). Rana escaped, continued guerrilla warfare.
- Mughals occupied Udaipur, Kumbhalgarh; but after 1585, Akbar focused northwest, Rana recovered territories.
- **Rana died in 1597**; struggle continued under son Amar Singh.

### **Settlement under Jahangir**
- After campaigns, Amar Singh’s son Karan Singh submitted (1613). Jahangir restored Mewar’s territories, allowed Chittor but without fortifications. Alliance strengthened.

---

## **7. Rebellions & Further Expansion**
### **Major Rebellion (1580–1581)**
- Causes: Strict dagh system, resentment against revenue reforms, religious orthodoxy upset by Akbar’s liberal views.
- Rebels in Bengal-Bihar declared Mirza Hakim (Kabul) emperor.
- Akbar sent Todar Mal (east) and Man Singh (Punjab); crushed revolt, then marched to Kabul (1581) but handed it to his sister.

### **Northwest & Other Expansions**
- **Kashmir, Ladakh, Baltistan** annexed (1586–1589).
- **Sindh (1590), Orissa, Cooch-Bihar, East Bengal (Dacca)** conquered.
- **Deccan** – Advance up to Ahmadnagar by 1600 (later detailed separately).

---

## **8. Towards Integration: Religious & Social Reforms**
### **Religious Policies**
- **Ibadat Khana (1575):** Debates among Muslims, later including Christians, Hindus, Jains, Zoroastrians. Debates turned bitter; discontinued 1582.
- **Mahzar (1579):** Akbar as arbiter in Islamic juristic differences (not “Decree of Infallibility”).
- **Tauhid-i-Ilahi (Divine Monotheism):** Sufi-style order emphasizing ethics, loyalty to Akbar. Not a new religion; limited membership.
- **Sulh-i-Kul:** Policy of universal peace, tolerance.

### **Social & Cultural Initiatives**
- **Translation Projects:** Mahabharata, Ramayana, Bible, Quran (into Persian).
- **Social Reforms:** Restricted sati (unless voluntary), legalized widow remarriage, raised marriage age, restricted alcohol.
- **Education:** Emphasis on moral education, mathematics, astronomy, logic, agriculture.
- **Patronage:** Navaratna (nine jewels) – artists, poets, musicians at court.

---

## **9. Legacy**
- **Political Integration:** Unified north India, started Deccan expansion.
- **Administrative Systems:** Land revenue (zabti/dahsala), mansabdari – foundations for future Mughals.
- **Cultural Integration:** Secular state, religious tolerance, syncretic culture.
- **Military & Frontiers:** Scientific northwest frontier, strong army organization.

---

## **Present-Day Relevance (Optional Additions)**
- Akbar’s **sulh-i-kul** finds echoes in modern India’s secular constitution.
- Administrative decentralization (12 provinces) resembles today’s federal structure.
- Land revenue systems influenced later British-era settlements.
- Cultural patronage set a precedent for later Indo-Islamic art and architecture (e.g., Fatehpur Sikri).
- Diplomatic matrimonial alliances resemble modern coalition-building.
