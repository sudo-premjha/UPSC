# Sequence of Social Changes

## 1. Introduction: From Stone Age to Urbanism

* **Pre-Vedic Society:** No written texts exist. Archaeology reveals people lived in small groups (Palaeolithic) subsisting on hunting/gathering.
* **Transition:** Food production began towards the end of the Stone Age.
* **Harappan Urbanism:** Peasant villages in the Indus basin blossomed into the urban society of Harappa.
* **The Gap:** After Harappa disappeared, urbanism did not reappear in the subcontinent for about **1500 years**.

---

## 2. Tribal and Pastoral Phase (Rig Vedic)

Society was primarily **pastoral, semi-nomadic, and egalitarian**.

* **Cattle as Wealth:**
* **Gomat:** A wealthy person (literally "one who possesses cows").
* **Raja:** Called *Gopa* or *Gopati* (protector of cows).
* **Duhitr:** Daughter (literally "one who milks").
* **Govala:** Buffalo (literally "cow-haired").


* **Economy:**
* Subsistence level production.
* **Bali:** Voluntary presents given to the chief by tribesmen.
* **Spoils of War:** The principal income for chiefs.


* **Social Structure:**
* No serving order (Shudras) yet.
* Ranks existed (Princes/Priests) but no rigid social classes.
* **Amsa/Bhaga:** Share of booty distributed to ordinary tribesmen in folk assemblies.



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **The Cow:** The cultural and religious centrality of the cow (*Gau*) in modern India traces its roots directly to this Rig Vedic period where cattle were the literal currency and definition of wealth.

---

## 3. Agriculture and the Origin of Upper Orders (Later Vedic)

The move from Punjab to Western UP marked the shift to **Agriculture**.

* **Political Change:** Territorial chiefdoms emerged.
* **Tribute:** *Bali* became a tribute paid by peasants to nobles, who then donated to priests.
* **Military:** No professional army. The **Vis** (peasantry) constituted the *Sena* (Army/Force).
* **Social Gap:** The gap between the *Vaishya* (peasant) and *Rajanya* (noble) was not very wide yet; kings were expected to lend a hand to the plough.

---

## 4. The Varna System (Post-Vedic / Age of Buddha)

Three processes coincided to transform society around the 5th Century BC: **Aryanization, Ironization, and Urbanization**.

### **The Transformation**

* **Iron Technology:** Use of iron axe (to clear forests) and iron ploughshare (to till deep) created a massive agricultural surplus.
* **Surplus:** Allowed peasants to support artisans, princes, and priests, leading to the **Magadhan Empire**.
* **Labor:** Slaves and wage-earners became regular features (unlike Vedic times).

### **The Varna System as a Tool**

The Varna system was devised to ensure the regular collection of surplus (taxes/tithes) through religious/social sanction rather than just force.

1. **Twice-Born (Citizens):** Brahmanas, Kshatriyas, Vaishyas. Entitled to Vedic studies.
2. **Shudras (Non-Citizens):** Excluded from Vedic studies; meant to serve the higher orders.

* **Discrimination:**
* **Interest Rates on Loans:** Brahmana (2%), Kshatriya (3%), Vaishya (4%), Shudra (5%).
* **Purity:** Brahmanas developed loathing for manual labor (roots of untouchability).



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Manual Labor Stigma:** The text notes that as Brahmanas withdrew from physical labor, they considered it "impure." This cultural stigma against manual labor persists in India today, where "white-collar" jobs are vastly preferred over vocational trades.

---

## 5. Social Crisis (The Kali Age)

Around the **3rd–4th Century AD**, the old social order faced a deep crisis.

* **The Crisis (*Kaliyuga*):**
* Characterized by **Varnasamkara** (intermixture of social orders).
* **Resistance:** Vaishyas (peasants) and Shudras (labourers) refused to perform their producing functions (paying taxes/providing labour) due to oppression.


* **State Response:**
* Coercion (*Danda*) advocated by texts like Manu.
* **Structural Change:** The state began granting land to priests and officials to handle the crisis locally.



---

## 6. Rise of Landed Classes (Feudalism)

To solve the crisis of tax collection and law enforcement, the state shifted from cash salaries to **Land Grants**.

* **The Mechanism:**
* Kings granted land to Brahmanas and officials (instead of cash salaries).
* Beneficiaries were given the right to collect taxes and maintain law and order.


* **Impact:**
* **Expansion:** Brahmanas in backward/tribal areas taught tribals agriculture and loyalty, expanding the agrarian base.
* **Assimilation:** Tribal peasants were absorbed into society as **Shudras**.
* **Narrowing Gap:** Independent Vaishyas lost status, while Shudras became sharecroppers, narrowing the gap between the two.


* **Women:** Status of upper-class women deteriorated (e.g., rise of **Sati** in Rajasthan), while lower-class women retained economic freedom.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Landlordism:** The "new type of social formation" (Feudalism) described here created the class of **Zamindars** or landlords. Though the Zamindari system was legally abolished in independent India (1950s), the social power dynamics of land ownership remain a critical issue in rural India.

---

## 7. Summary

Ancient Indian society was not unchanging; it evolved through distinct stages:

1. **Palaeolithic/Neolithic:** Food gathering/producing.
2. **Harappan:** Urban.
3. **Rig Vedic:** Pastoral/Tribal (Egalitarian).
4. **Later Vedic:** Agricultural (Peasant Militia).
5. **Post-Vedic:** Varna-divided (Class Society).
6. **Gupta/Post-Gupta:** Feudal (Landlord-Tenant).

---

## 8. Chronology

| Period | Event |
| --- | --- |
| **5th C BC** | Use of iron tools transforms Vedic society into a caste-divided order. Rise of rural/urban settlements in Gangetic basin. |
| **1st–2nd C AD** | Peak of trade and urbanism. |
| **3rd–4th C AD** | **The Kali Crisis:** Social unrest; refusal of producers to pay taxes. |
| **5th–6th C AD** | **Rise of Landlords:** Widespread land grants leading to the feudal system. |
