# The Significance of Maurya Rule

## 1. State Control and Bureaucracy

The Mauryan state was marked by royal absolutism and a massive administrative apparatus.

* **The King:** Kautilya described the king as *Dharmapravartaka* (promulgator of social order). Ashoka asserted that royal orders were superior to other orders.
* **Bureaucracy:** The state maintained a vast number of officers, more than any other period in ancient history, to enforce control over the annexed regions (Anga, Vaishali, Kalinga, etc.).
* **Espionage:** An elaborate spy system collected intelligence on foreign enemies and internal officers, and even used superstition to collect money.
* **Salary Disparity:** Functionaries were paid in cash (*Panas*—silver coins).
* **High Officials:** Ministers, High Priests, Commanders received **48,000 panas**.
* **Lowest Officers:** Received as little as **10 to 60 panas**.



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Pay Gaps:** The text highlights a massive disparity in Mauryan salaries (a ratio of roughly 1:800 between the lowest and highest paid). While modern corporate pay gaps are often scrutinized, this shows that significant income inequality within administrative structures has deep historical roots in India.

---

## 2. Economic Regulations

The state exercised strict control over the economy, managed by **27 Superintendents (*Adhyakshas*)**.

* **Areas of Control:** Agriculture, trade, weights/measures, mining, weaving, and liquor sale.
* **Agriculture & Irrigation:** The state provided irrigation and regulated water supply. Officials measured land (similar to practices in Egypt) for tax assessment.
* **Labour and Slavery:**
* Slaves and hired labourers were employed on state farms.
* **Megasthenes vs. Reality:** Megasthenes claimed there were no slaves in India, but Indian texts confirm domestic slavery existed since Vedic times. However, India was not a "slave society" like Greece or Rome; here, **Shudras** performed the labor tasks serving the three higher varnas.



---

## 3. Transport and Communication

Royal control relied on effective transport, centered at Pataliputra.

* **River Routes:** Royal agents used the Ganges, Son, Punpun, and Gandak rivers.
* **Road Network:**
* **The Royal Road:** Connected Pataliputra to the North-West frontier (Peshawar) via Vaishali and Champaran.
* **Other Links:** Roads connected Pataliputra to Central India (via Sasaram/Mirzapur) and Kalinga.


* **Comparison:** Mauryan control over settled areas matched that of the Mughals or the East India Company.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **The Grand Trunk Road:** The "Royal Road" mentioned here, running from Pataliputra to Peshawar, is the precursor to the famous **Grand Trunk Road** (GT Road). While often attributed to Sher Shah Suri (medieval period), the alignment was actually established during the Mauryan era to link the capital to the frontier.

---

## 4. Taxation and Currency

The Mauryas established a landmark system of taxation, prioritizing assessment over storage.

* **Key Officials:**
* **Samaharta:** Highest officer for **assessment** and collection (considered more important).
* **Sannidhata:** Chief custodian of the **treasury** and storehouse.


* **Taxes:** Collected in both cash and kind. The *Arthashastra* lists so many taxes that "very little would have been left to the people."
* **Currency:** **Punch-marked silver coins** (bearing peacock/crescented hill symbols) were the imperial currency. This uniform currency facilitated trade and tax collection across a wide area.

---

## 5. Art and Architecture

The Mauryas introduced stone masonry on a wide scale.

* **The Palace:** Megasthenes compared the Pataliputra palace to those in Iran. Excavations at Kumrahar (Patna) reveal an 84-pillared hall.
* **Stone Pillars:**
* Made of single pieces of buff-coloured **Chunar sandstone**.
* Noted for the high technical skill of the "Mauryan Polish" (shining like NBPW pottery).
* Transporting and erecting these huge blocks was a feat of engineering.


* **Caves:** Began the practice of hewing caves from rocks for monks (e.g., **Barabar Caves** near Gaya).
* **Terracotta:** Produced on a large scale (animals and mother goddesses), though modelled by hand.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **National Emblem:** The polished stone pillars mentioned in the text often featured animal capitals. The most famous, the **Lion Capital of Ashoka** from Sarnath, is today the **National Emblem of India**, and the wheel on its base (Ashoka Chakra) is at the center of the Indian Flag.

## 6. Spread of Material Culture

The Mauryan Empire acted as a vehicle for spreading the advanced material culture of the Gangetic basin to the periphery (Bengal, Kalinga, Andhra, Karnataka).

* **Elements of Culture:** Iron tools (ploughshares), writing, punch-marked coins, burnt bricks, ring wells, and Northern Black Polished Ware (NBPW).
* **Mechanism:** Conquests, trade, and missionary activities (Buddhist/Jain) spread these technologies.
* **Impact:**
* **Burnt Bricks:** Boon for moist climates (like Bengal) where mud structures didn't last.
* **Ring Wells:** Allowed settlements away from river banks.
* **Steel:** Spread of steel making likely aided jungle clearance in the Deccan/Kalinga.


* **State Formation:** The Mauryan model stimulated state formation among the **Satavahanas** (Deccan) and the **Cheti** kingdom (Kalinga). Even the southern kingdoms (Cholas, Pandyas, Cheras) were influenced by the Mauryan state structure.

## 7. Causes of the Fall of the Maurya Empire

The empire began to disintegrate after Ashoka's exit in **232 BC**.

### **A. Brahmanical Reaction**

* Ashoka’s policy of **Vyavaharasamata** (equality of law) and **Dandasamata** (equality of punishment) for all Varnas angered the Brahmanas, who were accustomed to privileged treatment in the Dharmashastras.
* Result: New dynasties (Shungas, Kanvas, Satavahanas) were Brahmanical and revived Vedic sacrifices.

### **B. Financial Crisis**

* Maintaining the largest army and bureaucracy in ancient times drained the treasury.
* Ashoka’s large donations to Buddhist monks worsened the situation. In the end, they had to melt gold images to meet expenses.

### **C. Oppressive Rule**

* Provincial governors often oppressed the people.
* Citizens of **Taxila** revolted against "wicked bureaucrats" (*dushtamatyas*) during Bindusara's and Ashoka's reigns.

### **D. New Knowledge in Outlying Areas**

* As iron technology and material culture spread from Magadha to the periphery, Magadha lost its military and economic superiority. New kingdoms (Shungas, Kanvas, Chetis) rose using the same technology.

### **E. Neglect of North-West Frontier & The Great Wall**

* **Global Context:** In **220 BC**, the Chinese ruler **Shih Huang Ti** built the **Great Wall of China** to shield against Scythian attacks.
* **Ashoka's Failure:** Ashoka, focused on missionary work, did not fortify the North-West frontier.
* **Consequence:** Nomadic Scythian movements pushed the Greeks, Parthians, and Shakas toward India. The Greeks invaded in **206 BC**, followed by others.

**The End:** The empire was finally destroyed in **185 BC** when **Pushyamitra Shunga** (a Brahmana general) killed the last Mauryan ruler, Brihadratha.
