# Crafts, Commerce, and Urban Growth (200 BC–AD 250)

## 1. Crafts and Craftsmen

The period of the Shakas, Kushans, Satavahanas, and the first Tamil states (200 BC–AD 250) was the "golden age" of crafts and commerce in ancient India.

### **Expansion of Occupations**

* **Literary Evidence:**
* *Digha Nikaya* (Pre-Maurya): Mentions nearly 2 dozen occupations.
* *Mahavastu* (This period): Catalogues 36 kinds of workers in Rajgir alone.
* *Milinda Panho*: Enumerates **75 occupations** (60 connected to crafts).


* **Location:** While texts associate artisans with towns, excavations (e.g., Karimnagar in Telangana) show distinct artisan quarters in villages too.

### **Key Industries**

1. **Metallurgy:**
* 8 crafts associated with working gold, silver, lead, tin, copper, brass, iron, and jewels.
* **Iron & Steel:** Telangana was the richest region for iron artifacts (weapons, balance rods, sickles). Indian steel/cutlery was exported to Abyssinian ports and prestigious in West Asia.


2. **Textiles:**
* **Mathura:** Famous for a special cloth called *Shataka*.
* **South India:** Dyeing was a thriving craft (brick dyeing vats found at Uraiyur and Arikamedu).


3. **Luxury Goods:**
* Ivory work, glass manufacture, bead-cutting, and shell industry.
* **Glass:** Knowledge of glass-blowing reached India around the beginning of the Christian era.


4. **Coin Minting:**
* Numerous coin moulds found. One Satavahana mould could turn out 6 coins at a time.
* Craftsmen also made "fake" Roman coins.



### **Guilds (Shrenis)**

* Artisans were organized into guilds.
* **Banking Function:** Lay devotees deposited money with guilds (e.g., potters, oilmen, weavers) to earn interest, which funded Buddhist monks.
* **Mathura:** A chief deposited money with a **flour-makers' guild** to feed 100 Brahmanas daily.

## 2. Types of Merchants

Merchants were divided into high, low, and middle ranks.

* **Sresthi:** Chief merchant of the *Nigama*.
* **Sarthavaha:** Caravan leader/Head of the corporation of merchants.
* **Vanija:** Petty merchants dealing in fruits, roots, cooked food, etc.
* **Gandhika:** Perfumers/All-purpose merchants (precursors to the modern *Gandhi* surname).
* **Agrivanija:** Possibly predecessors of the **Agrawals** (wholesale merchants).

---

## 3. Trade Routes and Centres

Trade shifted from overland routes to sea routes due to political disruptions (Parthians/Kushans) and the discovery of the **Monsoon**.

* **Sea Routes:** Sailors used monsoons to sail directly from the Arabian coast to India's west coast.
* **Key Ports:**
* **West:** Broach (most important), Sopara.
* **East:** Arikamedu, Tamralipti.


* **Overland Routes (Uttarapatha):**
* Connected Taxila to Broach via Mathura and Ujjain.
* **Ujjain:** Vital meeting point for routes from Kaushambi and Mathura.



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **The Uttarapatha:** The route described here—Taxila to Punjab to Yamuna bank to Mathura—closely mirrors the alignment of the historical **Grand Trunk Road** and modern **National Highway 44 (North-South Corridor)**, showing that India's logistical backbone has remained constant for millennia.

## 4. Foreign Trade (Indo-Roman Trade)

The thriving trade with the Roman Empire was the economic highlight of the period.

### **Exports from India**

* **Spices:** Pepper (called *Yavanapriya* or "dear to the Greeks").
* **Luxury Goods:** Muslin, pearls, jewels, ivory, precious stones.
* **Iron:** Cutlery (highly valued by Roman nobles).
* **Transit Trade:** Chinese Silk passed through India to Rome (diverted due to Parthian hostilities).

### **Imports to India**

* **From Rome:** Wine, amphorae, pottery (Arretine ware), lead (for Satavahana coins), glass jars, and bronze statues.
* **Balance of Trade:** Heavily in India's favor.
* **Pliny's Complaint (AD 77):** Lamented that Rome was being drained of gold to pay for Indian luxuries.
* **Roman Coins:** Over 6,000 gold and silver Roman coins found in India (mostly south of Vindhyas).



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **"Yavanapriya":** The Sanskrit word for pepper, *Yavanapriya*, literally means "Beloved of the Yavanas (Greeks/Romans)." It highlights how central the spice trade was to India's early globalization—a legacy that continued through the colonial era.

---

## 5. Money Economy

This period evidences the largest number of coins in ancient Indian history.

* **Gold:** Kushans issued **Dinar** type gold coins (influenced by Rome).
* **Base Metals:** Day-to-day transactions used Lead, Potin (Satavahanas), Copper (Kushans), and Bronze.
* **Penetration:** Money economy penetrated deep into the lives of common people in towns and suburbs, more than any other period.
* **Issuers:** Coins were issued not just by kings, but also by cities, tribes (Yaudheyas, Nagas), and merchant guilds.

## 6. Urban Growth

Growing crafts and commerce fueled the peak of urbanization in ancient India.

### **North India (Kushan Period)**

* **Prosperous Towns:** Vaishali, Pataliputra, Varanasi, Mathura, Hastinapur, Indraprastha.
* **Excavations:** Reveal superior brick constructions in the Kushan phase (e.g., 7 levels of Kushan phase at Sonkh vs. only 1 Gupta level).
* **Sachnan Kot:** Largest Kushan town in North India (near Lucknow), covering 9 sq. km.

### **Western India (Shaka Period)**

* **Ujjain:** Nodal point for trade and a center for bead manufacturing (agate/carnelian from Sipra riverbed).

### **Deccan and South (Satavahana Period)**

* **Prosperous Towns:** Tagar, Paithan, Amaravati, Nagarjunakonda, Broach, Arikamedu.
* **Telangana:** Excavations show settlements coterminous with Pliny's "30 walled towns."

### **Decline**

* **3rd Century AD:** Urban decline began.
* **Causes:** Fall of Kushan/Satavahana empires + Ban on trade with India by the Roman Empire.
* **Impact:** Artisans and merchants were impoverished; towns like Mathura and those in the Deccan decayed.
