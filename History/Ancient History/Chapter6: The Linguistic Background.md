### **Chapter 6: The Linguistic Background**

#### **Principal Language Groups**

India is home to a vast array of languages. According to *The Linguistic Survey of India* compiled by Grierson, there are nearly **180 languages** and about **550 dialects** spoken in the region. These languages fall into four major families:

1. **Austro-Asiatic**
2. **Tibeto-Burman**
3. **Dravidian**
4. **Indo-Aryan**

---

#### **1. Austro-Asiatic Family**

This group is considered the earliest language family in India, primarily represented by **Munda speech**.

* **Origins & Migration:** Anthropologists trace the Austric people to Australia around 40,000 BC. It is believed they migrated from Africa to Southeast Asia and Australia via the Indian coast roughly **50,000 years ago**, a time when language had likely been invented.
* **Subfamilies:**
* **Austronesian:** Spoken in Australia and Southeast Asia.
* **Austro-Asiatic:** Spoken in the Indian subcontinent. It has two main branches:
* **Mon-Khmer:** Spoken in the Khasi and Jaintia hills (Meghalaya) and the Nicobar Islands.
* **Munda:** Spoken in a much larger area by tribes like the **Santhals** (the largest tribal group in the subcontinent), Mundas, and Hos in Jharkhand, Bihar, West Bengal, and Odisha. Traces are also found in the Himalayas.





---

#### **2. Tibeto-Burman Family**

A branch of the **Sino-Tibetan** family, this group has a massive speaker base globally (China, Tibet, Myanmar).

* **Distribution in India:** Extends along the Himalayas from northeastern Assam to northeastern Punjab.
* **Speakers:** Various tribes in North-Eastern states (Tripura, Sikkim, Assam, Meghalaya, Arunachal Pradesh, Nagaland, Mizoram, Manipur) and the Darjeeling area of West Bengal use as many as **116 dialects**.
* **Literature:** Unlike Indo-Aryan and Dravidian languages, these tongues did not develop a written script early on. Oral traditions were recorded by Christian missionaries only in the 19th century.
* **Cultural Links:** The Ahom term *burunji* (family tree) and the Maithili term *panji* are likely linked to Tibeto-Burman roots.

---

#### **3. Dravidian Family**

This family covers almost all of South India and parts of northeastern Sri Lanka.

* **Origins:**
* **Genetic View:** Migration from the Middle East roughly **30,000 years ago**.
* **Linguistic View:** Migration from **Elam** (south-western Iran) around **4000 BC**.


* **Key Languages:** Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, and Malayalam. **Tamil** is considered the most distinctively Dravidian.
* **Other Forms:**
* **Brahui:** The earliest form of Dravidian speech, still spoken in Baluchistan (Pakistan), Iran, and Afghanistan, suggesting the migration route.
* **Oraon/Kurukh:** Spoken by tribes in Jharkhand and central India.



---

#### **4. Indo-Aryan Family**

A branch of the **Indo-European** family. Genetic signals connect these speakers to the steppe people of Central Asia, suggesting migration to India.

* **Sub-branches:** The eastern "Arya" branch split into Indo-Iranian, Dardic, and Indo-Aryan.
* **Evolution:**
* **Old Indo-Aryan:** Vedic Sanskrit.
* **Middle Indo-Aryan (500 BC – AD 1000):** Covers **Prakrit**, **Pali**, and **Apabhramsha**.
* **Modern Indo-Aryan:** Developed from Apabhramsha in medieval times (Hindi, Bengali, Marathi, Gujarati, Punjabi, etc.).


* **Interaction:** Indo-Aryan speakers interacted with Munda and Dravidian groups, leading to mutual borrowing of words (e.g., Munda/Dravidian words in the *Rig Veda*).

---

#### **Ethnic Groups and Language Families**

In the 19th century, anthropologists classified the Indian population into four racial groups based on physical features, associating them with specific language families.

| Ethnic Group | Physical Features | Region | Associated Language Family |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Negrito** | Short stature, short face, short lips | Andaman & Nicobar, Nilgiris, Kerala | Likely Austric roots |
| **Australoid** | Short stature, dark complexion, body hair | Central & South India | Austric or Munda |
| **Mongoloid** | Short stature, flat nose, scanty body hair | Sub-Himalayan & North-East | Tibeto-Burman |
| **Caucasoid** | Tall, long face, fair skin, narrow nose | North, Central & South India | Dravidian & Indo-Aryan |

* **Note on Mixing:** It is difficult to strictly separate these groups today due to centuries of intermingling. Physical features often overlap across caste and linguistic lines (e.g., Brahmins and Chamars in some areas sharing features).

### **Present Day Context: Census Data**

While Grierson's survey is historical, modern data provides a current snapshot of India's linguistic diversity.

**Census of India (2011) Statistics:**
The census recorded **121 major languages** (those spoken by 10,000+ people).

* **Indo-Aryan Family:** Spoken by **78.05%** of the population.
* *Examples:* Hindi, Bengali, Marathi, Gujarati.


* **Dravidian Family:** Spoken by **19.64%** of the population.
* *Examples:* Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam.


* **Austro-Asiatic Family:** Spoken by **1.11%** of the population.
* *Examples:* Santali, Mundari.


* **Tibeto-Burman Family:** Spoken by **1.01%** of the population.
* *Examples:* Bodo, Manipuri.
