### **Chapter 16: Iranian and Macedonian Invasions**

#### **Iranian Invasion**

In the 6th century BC, while north-east India saw the consolidation of the Magadhan empire, north-west India was politically fragmented.

* **Political State:** Small principalities like Kamboja, Gandhara, and Madra fought one another. There was no unifying sovereign.
* **Strategic Vulnerability:** The region was fertile, rich in resources, and accessible through the Hindu Kush passes.

**Achaemenian Conquest:**

* **Darius (516 BC):** The Achaemenian ruler penetrated north-west India, annexing Punjab (west of Indus) and Sindh.
* **The 20th Satrapy:** This annexed area became the 20th province (satrapy) of the Iranian empire.
* It was the most fertile and populous part of the empire.
* **Revenue:** Paid a massive tribute of **360 talents of gold** (one-third of the total revenue from Asian provinces).


* **Military Service:** Indian subjects were enrolled in the Iranian army. **Xerxes** employed Indians in his long wars against the Greeks.

---

#### **Results of the Contact**

The Indo-Iranian contact lasted for about 200 years and had significant cultural and economic impacts:

1. **Trade:** Impetus to Indo-Iranian commerce. Iranian coins found in the north-west indicate goods exchange.
2. **Script:** Introduction of the **Kharoshthi script** (written right-to-left like Arabic). Used in Ashokan inscriptions in the north-west (3rd Century BC – 3rd Century AD).
3. **Art and Architecture:**
* Maurya sculpture shows Iranian influence.
* Ashokan monuments (bell-shaped capitals) owe debt to Iranian models.
* Preamble of Ashoka’s edicts mirrors Iranian forms (e.g., Iranian *dipi* = Ashokan *lipi*).


4. **Exposure:** The Greeks likely learned about India's immense wealth through the Iranians, fueling Alexander’s ambition.

---

#### **Alexander's Invasion**

In the 4th century BC, **Alexander of Macedonia** destroyed the Iranian empire.

* **Motivation:** Attracted by India's legendary wealth (described by Herodotus), passion for geography (belief that the Caspian Sea continued east of India), and desire to surpass past conquerors.
* **Political Opportunity:** North-west India was divided into independent monarchies and tribal republics.
* **Ambhi (Taxila):** Submitted readily; helped Alexander.
* **Porus (Jhelum to Chenab):** Offered strong resistance but was defeated.



**The Campaign (326 BC):**

* Alexander marched through the **Khyber Pass**.
* **Battle of Hydaspes:** Alexander defeated Porus on the banks of the Jhelum. Impressed by Porus's bravery, he restored his kingdom and made him an ally.
* **The Retreat:** Alexander advanced to the **Beas** river but was forced to stop.
* **Soldier's Refusal:** His army was war-weary, diseased, and homesick after 10 years of campaigning.
* **Fear of Magadha:** They heard of the formidable Nanda army on the Ganges, which far outnumbered them.
* **Alexander's Lament:** *"I am trying to rouse the hearts that are disloyal and crushed with craven fears."*



Alexander remained in India for **19 months** (326–325 BC). Before leaving, he divided his territories under three Greek governors and founded several cities to maintain power.

---

#### **Effects of Alexander's Invasion**

Although Greek possessions were soon lost to the Mauryas, the invasion had lasting effects:

1. **Direct Contact:** Opened four distinct land and sea routes, increasing trade and contact between India and Greece.
2. **Greek Settlements:** Establishment of cities like **Alexandria** (Kabul region), **Boukephala** (Jhelum), and **Alexandria in Sindh**. Greeks continued to live there under Mauryan rule.
3. **Historical Records:** Alexander’s historians left valuable, dated accounts of geography and society:
* **Sati System:** Noted the practice.
* **Sale of Girls:** Poor parents selling girls in marketplaces.
* **Fine Oxen:** 200,000 oxen sent to Macedonia.
* **Carpentry:** Flourishing craft (chariots, boats, ships).


4. **Political Unification:** By destroying petty states in the north-west, Alexander paved the way for the **Maurya empire's expansion** in that area. Chandragupta Maurya supposedly learned about military organization from the Greeks.
