## **The Neolithic Age: First Food Producers and Animal Keepers**

In the global context, the **Neolithic Age** (New Stone Age) began around **9000 BC**. In the Indian subcontinent, it began around **7000 BC**, marking the revolutionary transition from food gathering to food production (farming and herding).

### **1. Mehrgarh: The Earliest Settlement (Baluchistan)**

**Location:** Situated on the bank of the **Bolan river** in the **Kochi plain** (the 'breadbasket' of Baluchistan). It lies on the edge of the Indus plains and is one of the largest Neolithic settlements between the Indus and the Mediterranean.

* **Timeline of Development:**
* **7000 BC:** Earliest settlement. Inhabitants domesticated cattle, sheep, and goats (initially goats predominated). They produced wheat and barley.
* **5500 BC:** Settlement disturbed by floods.
* **5000 BC:** Agricultural activities resumed using stone and bone tools.
* **4500–3500 BC:** Expansion from the Kochi plain into the Indus plain.


* **Key Features:**
* **Granaries:** Compartmented mud-brick structures used for storing substantial quantities of cereals. These granaries were crucial for supporting a settled population.
* **Pottery:** Initially (up to 5000 BC), people did not make pots. After **4500 BC**, the **potter's wheel** (likely introduced from West Asia) led to the mass production of painted pots.


* **Legacy:** 47 Later Neolithic settlements in the dried basin of the **Hakra** paved the way for the rise of the Harappan culture.

### **2. Regional Neolithic Settlements (Three Groups)**

Neolithic people used polished stone tools, particularly **stone axes**. The specific shape of the axe distinguishes the three main settlement areas in India:

| Region | Axe Characteristics |
| --- | --- |
| **North-Western** | Rectangular axes with curved cutting edges. |
| **North-Eastern** | Polished axes with rectangular butt; occasional shouldered hoes. |
| **Southern** | Axes with oval sides and pointed butt. |

> **Note on Dating:**
> * **Vindhyas (North):** Some sites are as old as **5000 BC** (e.g., Koldihwa/Mahagra known for rice cultivation).
> * **South India:** Generally not older than **2500 BC**.
> * **Eastern India:** Some sites are as late as **1000 BC**.

### **3. Distinctive Cultures: Bone Tools & Pits**

#### **Kashmir Valley (North-Western Group)**

This culture is distinguished by **pit dwellings**, a wide range of ceramics, and the absence of microliths.

* **Burzahom ('Place of Birch'):** 16 km NW of Srinagar.
* **Dwellings:** People lived in pits on the lakeside.
* **Economy:** Hunting/fishing economy; acquainted with agriculture. Coarse grey pottery.
* **Unique Practice:** **Domestic dogs were buried with their masters** in graves (a practice unique to this site in India).
* **Date:** Earliest date ~2700 BC.


* **Gufkral ('Cave of the Potter'):** 41 km SW of Srinagar. Practiced both agriculture and animal husbandry.
* **Tools:** Used polished stone tools and numerous **tools and weapons made of bone**.

#### **Chirand (Bihar)**

* **Location:** 40 km west of Patna, at the junction of four rivers (Ganges, Son, Gandak, Ghaghra).
* **Significance:** The only site in India besides Burzahom yielding considerable **bone implements** (made of deer antlers).
* **Context:** A late Neolithic settlement (~2000 BC). The river junction provided open land, but stone was scarce in the riverine tract, necessitating the use of bone.

### **4. South Indian Settlements**

* **Location:** South of the Godavari river, usually on granite hilltops or plateaus near river banks.
* **Density:** Over **850 settlements** (AP, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu) due to easy availability of stone.
* **Time Period:** ~2400 to 1000 BC.
* **Economy:**
* **Cattle-Herding:** Kept large herds of cattle, sheep, and goats (evidenced by fire-baked earthen figurines).
* **Agriculture:** Used stone querns (grinding stones), indicating cereal production (**ragi, horse gram**).


* **Piklihal (Karnataka):**
* **Ash Mounds:** Seasonal camps were surrounded by wooden stakes where dung accumulated. When moving, the entire ground was set on fire, creating distinct ash mounds found today.

### **5. Life in the Neolithic Age**

**Farming & Technology:**

* **Tools:** Polished stone axes (celts), microlith blades, and **digging sticks** fitted with **ring stones** (weighing 1/2 kg) to break the ground.
* **Housing:** Lived in circular or rectangular houses made of mud and reed.
* *Social Insight:* Primitive people in circular houses likely owned property in common.


* **Pottery:** Essential for storing food grains. Started as handmade; later used foot-wheels. Included black-burnished, grey, and mat-impressed ware.

**The Threshold of Civilization:**
The shift from hunting-gathering to cultivation and cattle husbandry was revolutionary. It allowed for settled life, the construction of dwellings, and the development of arts like weaving and house building.

**Limitations:**

* **Dependence on Stone:** They were confined to hilly areas and rock shelters where stone was easily available.
* **Subsistence Level:** Despite great effort, they could rarely produce more than what was needed for bare subsistence.
