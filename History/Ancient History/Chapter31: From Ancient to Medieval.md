# From Ancient to Medieval

## 1. Social Crisis and Agrarian Changes

The transition from ancient to medieval society was primarily driven by the practice of **land grants**.

* **The Cause (Social Crisis):**
* **The Model:** Ancient Varna society relied on Vaishyas (peasants) producing surplus and paying taxes, and Shudras providing labour.
* **The Crisis (*Kaliyuga*):** In the 3rd–4th centuries AD, Puranic texts describe a deep social crisis called *Kaliyuga*. The lower orders refused to perform their assigned functions (paying taxes/providing free labour), leading to *Varnasamkara* (intermixture of classes).


* **The Solution:**
* **Coercion:** Law-books (e.g., Manu) advised strict enforcement of duties.
* **Land Grants:** The state began granting land to priests and officials in lieu of salaries.
* **Benefit:** Beneficiaries were responsible for collecting taxes and maintaining law and order on the spot, effectively managing recalcitrant peasants and integrating tribal areas.



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Kaliyuga:** The concept of *Kaliyuga* mentioned in the text as a historical social crisis is still a dominant theological concept in modern Hinduism, used to explain the moral and ethical decline of the current age.

---

## 2. Rise of Landlords

From the 5th century AD, land grants became frequent, creating a class of landlords and eroding royal authority.

* **Shift in Authority:**
* Beneficiaries gained the right to govern, collect taxes, and punish criminals.
* Royal agents were banned from entering donated villages.
* **King's Status:** Shifted from **Gopati** (owner of cattle in Vedic times) to **Bhupati** (owner of land).


* **Payment Mechanism:**
* **Ancient:** Officials paid in cash (Mauryas, Kushans, Guptas).
* **Medieval:** From the 6th century (post-Harsha), officials were paid in land revenue. 1/4th of royal revenue was earmarked for endowments.



---

## 3. New Agrarian Economy

* **Structure:** Landlords did not cultivate; actual cultivation was done by attached peasants or sharecroppers.
* **Serfdom:** In tribal and backward areas (Orissa/Deccan), peasants were legally attached to the land and forbidden from leaving. This practice spread to the Ganges basin, severely undermining mass mobility.

---

## 4. Decline of Trade and Towns

From the 6th century onwards, India witnessed a sharp economic decline.

* **Trade Collapse:**
* Trade with the Roman Empire ended (3rd Century).
* Silk trade with Iran/Byzantium stopped (mid-6th Century).
* **Arabs** emerged as middlemen, monopolizing export trade before the rise of Islam.


* **Currency Scarcity:** Virtual absence of gold coins for 300 years (6th–9th Century).
* **Urban Decay:**
* Major commercial cities (Pataliputra, Mathura, Vaishali, Kaushambi) declined or were deserted.
* **De-urbanization:** Artisans and merchants migrated to the countryside to take up cultivation (e.g., Silk weavers moving to Mandasor).
* **Self-Sufficiency:** Villages became smaller, self-contained units of production (Jajmani system roots).



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **De-urbanization vs. Urbanization:** The text describes a massive "de-urbanization" where people fled decaying cities for villages. Today, India is witnessing the exact opposite trend, with massive migration from rural areas to urban metros in search of economic opportunity.

---

## 5. Changes in the Varna System

* **Vaishyas Downgraded:** Once free peasants, they were reduced to the level of Shudras due to the rise of landlords.
* **New Hierarchy:**
* In outlying areas, society simplified into two main orders: **Brahmanas** and **Shudras**.
* Status became determined by **Land Ownership** rather than just ritual status.


* **Proliferation of Castes (Jatis):**
* **Origins:** Tribal assimilation (tribes became castes) and occupational immobility.
* **Sub-castes:** People in the same occupation developed different sub-castes based on their specific territory/region.



---

## 6. Rise of Regional Identities

Around the 6th–7th centuries, distinct regional cultural units formed (Precursors to modern states like Karnataka, Maharashtra, Orissa, Bengal).

* **Language:**
* Birth of **Apabhramsha** (AD 600–1000), the bridge between Prakrit and modern vernaculars (Hindi, Bengali, Marathi, etc.).
* **South:** Kannada began to grow; Tamil persisted; Telugu/Malayalam developed later.


* **Scripts:** The uniform Brahmi/Gupta script fractured into distinct regional scripts, making cross-regional communication difficult.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Linguistic States:** The regional identities rooted in language and script that began forming in the 7th century (as described here) eventually formed the basis for the **States Reorganization Act of 1956**, which redrew the map of modern India along linguistic lines.

---

## 7. Trends in Literature

* **Style:** Sanskrit literature became ornate, verbose, and artificial (e.g., Banabhatta’s prose), reflecting the vanity of feudal lords.
* **Themes:** Combination of **Sex and Religion** (Tantrism). Sexual union was equated with divine union.
* **Commentaries:** A period of writing commentaries (*Bhasya*) rather than original texts, strengthening authoritarianism and tradition.

---

## 8. Religion: The Divine Hierarchy

Religion adapted to the feudal social structure.

* **Divine Hierarchy:** Gods were arranged in pantheons mirroring the social hierarchy.
* **Supreme Deity:** (Shiva/Vishnu) depicted largely.
* **Subordinates:** Lesser gods depicted smaller, serving the main deity.


* **Panchayatana:** A temple style with a chief god in the center and four subsidiary shrines around it.
* **Bhakti Cult:** Emphasized total surrender to God, mirroring the tenant's surrender to the landlord.
* **Tantrism:** Arose from the assimilation of tribal rituals. Open to women and Shudras. Focused on magic rituals for material gain.

---

## 9. Summary: The Transition

* **Comparison with Europe:**
* Both India and the Roman Empire faced invasions (Hunas) and urban decay in the 6th century.
* **Difference:** Europe turned to slavery/serfdom with lords providing protection; India did not have large-scale slavery but developed a system of landlords and attached peasantry via land grants.


* **Key Shift:** The emergence of **Landlords** as the dominant class shaped the society, economy, and culture of Medieval India.

## 10. Chronology (AD)

| Year | Event |
| --- | --- |
| **3rd Century** | End of trade with Roman Empire. |
| **3rd–4th Century** | **Kaliyuga Crisis:** Social unrest and breakdown of Varna duties. |
| **5th Century** | Land grants become frequent; Rise of Landlords. |
| **6th Century** | Decline of Silk trade; Urban decay begins. Rise of **Tantrism**. Birth of Regional identities. |
| **600–900** | **Apabhramsha** language develops (Transition to modern languages). Virtual absence of gold coins. |
| **7th Century** | Feudalism fully established; Proliferation of Castes; Spread of Bhakti. |
