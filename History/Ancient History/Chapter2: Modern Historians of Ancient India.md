## Modern Historians of Ancient India

The study of ancient Indian history has evolved through several distinct phases, often driven by the political and social needs of the time.

### 1. Colonialist Views and Their Contribution

Modern research into ancient Indian history did not begin out of mere curiosity; it started in the second half of the 18th century to serve the administrative needs of the British colonial government.

**Origins in Administration:**

* **The Problem:** When the East India Company took over Bengal and Bihar (1765), they struggled to administer the Hindu law of inheritance.
* **The Solution:**
* **1776:** The *Manu Smriti* was translated into English as *A Code of Gentoo Laws*.
* **Judicial System:** Pandits were associated with British judges for Hindu civil law, and Maulvis for Muslim law.



**Institutional Development:**
The effort to understand Indian laws and customs led to the founding of key institutions:

* **Asiatic Society of Bengal (1784):** Established in Calcutta by **Sir William Jones**.
* Jones was the first to suggest that Sanskrit, Latin, and Greek belonged to the same language family.
* Translations included *Abhijnanashakuntalam* (1789) and the *Bhagvadgita* (translated by Wilkins, 1785).


* **Global Spread:** The Bombay Asiatic Society (1804) and the Asiatic Society of Great Britain (1823) followed. Chairs in Sanskrit were established across Europe.

**The Max Mueller Era & Generalizations:**
The revolt of 1857 forced the British to realize they needed a deeper understanding of Indian social systems.

* **F. Max Mueller:** A German-born scholar based in England who gave the greatest impetus to Indological studies.
* **Sacred Books of the East:** A massive 50-volume series of translated scriptures published to help missionaries and administrators understand (and critique) the "natives."

**Colonialist Narratives (Justifying Rule):**
Western scholars, including Max Mueller and **Vincent Arthur Smith**, made several generalizations to justify colonial rule:

* **Lack of History:** Claimed Indians had no sense of chronology or history.
* **Despotism:** Argued Indians were accustomed to autocratic rule ("Autocracy is the only form of government" suitable for India).
* **Other-Worldliness:** Claimed Indians were too focused on the spiritual world to care about the present one, implying they needed British management for practical affairs.

> **Note on V.A. Smith:** His book, *Early History of India* (1904), was the first systematic history. It was pro-imperialist, devoting 1/3rd of the space to Alexander’s invasion to emphasize foreign influence.

### 2. The Nationalist Approach

Indian scholars, distressed by colonial distortions and the state of Indian society, took on the challenge of reconstructing history. Their goal was twofold: to reform society and to prove India’s fitness for self-government.

**Rationalists and Social Reformers:**

* **Rajendra Lal Mitra (1822–91):** A rationalist who proved that ancient people ate beef, countering conservative views.
* **R.G. Bhandarkar (1837–1925):** Reconstructed the history of the Satavahanas and Vaishnavism. He advocated for widow remarriage and castigated caste evils.
* **V.K. Rajwade (1869–1926):** A dedicated researcher who journeyed village-to-village in Maharashtra. He wrote a classic history on the institution of marriage in India.
* **P.V. Kane (1880–1972):** Wrote the monumental *History of the Dharmasastra* (5 volumes), an encyclopaedia of ancient social laws and customs.

**Political Historians (Proving Administrative Expertise):**

* **D.R. Bhandarkar:** Published books on ancient Indian political institutions.
* **H.C. Raychaudhuri:** Reconstructed history from the Mahabharata war (10th C. BC) to the Gupta empire. He criticized V.A. Smith.
* **K.P. Jayaswal (1881–1937):** **Exploded the myth of Indian despotism.** He proved that ancient India had republics with self-government in his classic *Hindu Polity* (1924).

**Regional History (South India):**

* **K.A. Nilakanta Sastri:** The premier historian of South India (*A History of South India*). While dependable on facts, he emphasized the cultural supremacy of brahmanas and harmony in society, views which are sometimes questioned.

---

### 3. Move Towards Non-Political History

A shift occurred from focusing solely on dynasties and kings to studying society, economy, and culture.

**A.L. Basham (The Cultural Shift):**

* **Work:** *The Wonder That Was India* (1951).
* **Approach:** A sympathetic survey of culture and civilization, free from V.A. Smith's prejudices.

**D.D. Kosambi (The Materialist Shift):**

* **Work:** *An Introduction to the Study of Indian History* (1957).
* **Approach:** Followed a **materialist/Marxist interpretation**. He viewed history as the development of forces and relations of production (tribal and class processes) rather than just political events.

---

### 4. The Communal Approach (Contemporary Issues)

Since 1980, a struggle has emerged between **Rationalism/Professionalism** and **Communalism/Irrationalism**.

**Characteristics of the Communal Approach:**

* **Identification with Hinduism:** Treats ancient India as synonymous with Hinduism.
* **Myth as History:** Argues for the historical existence of legends (e.g., Rama’s Ayodhya) without evidence.
* **Indigenous Aryans:** Rejects the migration theory; claims Aryans were indigenous and Painted Grey Ware was Vedic.
* **Exclusion:** Labels Muslims and Christians as "foreigners."
* **Uncritical:** Censures critical studies of social inequity (caste) supported by Manu.

**Conclusion:** Historians must remain wedded to objective, scientific criteria to counter these irrational generalizations.

### Chronology

**The Era of Colonial Research**

* **1765:** Bengal/Bihar under East India Company.
* **1776:** *Manusmriti* translated as *Code of Gentoo Laws*.
* **1784:** Asiatic Society of Bengal founded (Calcutta).
* **1785:** *Bhagvadgita* translated into English.
* **1904:** V.A. Smith publishes *Early History of India*.

**Key Works by Indian/Modern Scholars**

* **1924:** *Hindu Polity* by K.P. Jayaswal (Debunking despotism).
* **1926:** *History of the Institution of Marriage* by V.K. Rajwade.
* **1951:** *The Wonder That Was India* by A.L. Basham.
* **1957:** *An Introduction to the Study of Indian History* by D.D. Kosambi.

**Prominent Scholars**

* **1837–1925:** R.G. Bhandarkar
* **1880–1972:** P.V. Kane
* **1888–1980:** R.C. Majumdar
* **1892–1975:** K.A. Nilakanta Sastri
