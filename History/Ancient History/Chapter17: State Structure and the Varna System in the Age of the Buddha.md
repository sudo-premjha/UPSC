# State Structure and the Varna System in the Age of the Buddha

## 1. Second Urbanization & Material Life

This era marks a significant shift in material life in north India (specifically eastern UP and Bihar), reconstructed from Pali texts, Sanskrit Sutras, and archaeology.

### **The NBPW Phase (Northern Black Polished Ware)**

* **Time Period:** Began around **500 BC**.
* **Characteristics:** Glossy, shining pottery made of fine material; likely tableware for the rich.
* **Associated Finds:** Iron implements (crafts/agriculture) and the beginning of metal money.
* **Settlement Growth:**
* Burnt bricks and ring wells appear in the middle phase (c. 3rd Century BC).
* Settlements in the *doab* expanded significantly (thrice the area of the previous Painted Grey Ware phase).
* **Population Explosion:** As settlements expanded, population density increased drastically.



### **Emergence of Towns**

This period is termed the **Second Urbanization** (the first being the Harappan civilization, which disappeared c. 1900 BC).

* **Criteria for Towns:** Large settlements required crafts, coins, trade, and agricultural surplus to evolve into towns.
* **Key Locations:** Kaushambi, Shravasti, Ayodhya, Kapilavastu, Varanasi, Vaishali, Rajgir, Pataliputra, Champa.
* **Infrastructure:**
* Mud structures and wooden palisades (e.g., Patna).
* Wood and mud brick were common but disintegrated over time due to the moist climate.



> **Present Day Connection:**
> **The Legacy of Wood:** The text notes that wooden structures from this era disintegrated. Today, in places like Himachal Pradesh and Kerala, traditional wooden architecture still survives, but in the Gangetic plains, brick and concrete have almost entirely replaced the mud-and-wood constructions described here.

## 2. Urban Economy: Crafts and Trade

### **Organization of Work**

* **Guilds:** Artisans and merchants were organized into guilds headed by a chief. We hear of 18 guilds (e.g., smiths, carpenters, leather workers).
* **Localization:** Artisans lived in fixed localities (e.g., a street of ivory workers in Varanasi; a potters' settlement in Vaishali).
* **Specialization:** Crafts became hereditary; fathers trained sons.

### **Trade and Transport**

* **Routes:** Major cities were on river banks and trade routes.
* **North-South:** Mathura to Taxila.
* **West:** Mathura to Ujjain and the Gujarat coast.
* **East:** Shravasti to Rajgir and Champa via the Ganges.


* **Goods:** Fine textiles, ivory, pots.

### **Currency**

* **Evolution:** Moved from barter/cattle currency to metal money.
* **First Coins:** **Punch-marked coins** (mostly silver, some copper) appeared in the age of the Buddha. They bore symbols like hills, trees, bulls, and elephants.
* **Adoption:** Money was used for wages and buying goods, becoming universally accepted.

## 3. Rural Economy

Urbanization was supported by a strong rural base providing taxes and food.

### **Village Structure**

* **Nucleated Settlements:** People settled in clusters with fields outside—the model for the typical Indian village today.
* **Types of Villages:**
1. **Typical Village:** Mixed castes, headed by a *Bhojaka* (headman).
2. **Suburban/Craft Village:** Specialized (e.g., carpenters' village), serving as markets.
3. **Border Village:** Hunters and fowlers living near forests.



### **Agriculture & Technology**

* **Iron Technology:** Crucial for clearing hard-soil areas in the mid-Gangetic basin.
* **Paddy Transplantation:** The shift from sowing seeds in watery areas to transplanting seedlings revolutionized production, vastly increasing rice yields.
* **Crops:** Rice (staple), barley, pulses, cotton, sugarcane.
* **Taxation:** Peasants paid **1/6th of produce** to the king. No intermediate landlords existed; collection was direct.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **The "Gramini":** The text mentions the village headman was called *Gramini* or *Gramabhojaka*. This role is the direct ancestor of the modern **Sarpanch** or **Gram Pradhan** in the Indian Panchayati Raj system. The title *Gramini* is still used in Sri Lanka today.

---

## 4. Administrative System (Monarchies)

**Key States:** **Magadha** and **Koshala** emerged as the most powerful.

* **The King:** A hereditary warlord (Kshatriya) commanding high status.
* **Officials (*Mahamatras*):** Ministers, commanders, judges, accountants.
* **Kinship Decline:** Officials were recruited based on ability (often Brahmanas), undermining the old clan-based polity of Vedic times.
* **Village Administration:** The *Gramini* (headman) assessed/collected taxes and maintained law and order.

---

## 5. Army and Taxation

### **Military Power**

* **Standing Army:** Large, long-service professional armies replaced tribal militias.
* **Composition:** Nanda rulers maintained 200,000 infantry, 20,000 cavalry, and 6,000 elephants.
* **Elephants:** Gave Magadha a distinct military edge over north-western states that relied on horses.

### **Fiscal System**

* **Taxpayers:** Burden fell on **Vaishyas** and **Grihapatis** (peasants). Kshatriyas and Brahmanas were exempt.
* **Bali:** Transformed from a voluntary offering (Vedic period) to a compulsory tax.
* **Tolls:** Collected by officers (*Shaulkika*) on merchandise.

---

## 6. The Republican Experiment

While monarchies grew, republics (*Ganas* or *Samghas*) existed in the Indus basin and Himalayan foothills.

### **Differences: Republic vs. Monarchy**

| Feature | Monarchy (e.g., Magadha) | Republic (e.g., Lichchhavis/Shakyas) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Ruler** | Single hereditary King (*Ekaraja*). | Tribal Oligarchy (Many *Rajas*). |
| **Army** | Standing army controlled by the King. | Each Raja had his own little army. |
| **Revenue** | King was sole recipient. | Each Raja claimed revenue. |
| **Brahmanas** | Great influence. | No place in early republics. |
| **Assembly** | Replaced by small *Parishad*. | Ruled by assemblies in Motehalls. |

* **Decline:** Monarchies were stronger. Ancient thinkers viewed kingship as the standard, citing the need for a leader to protect property and family (origin of the state theory in *Digha Nikaya*).

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **"Ganarajya":** India today is a "Republic" (Hindi: *Ganarajya*). While the ancient *Ganas* were oligarchies (rule by a few elite clans) and modern India is a democracy (rule by all citizens), the terminology and the spirit of non-monarchical rule trace their linguistic roots to these ancient experiments.

## 7. Social Orders and Legislation

### **The Varna System**

Society was divided into four orders, with law codes (*Dharmasutras*) discriminating based on Varna.

1. **Brahmanas:** Priests/Teachers (purest, highest status).
2. **Kshatriyas:** Rulers/Fighters.
3. **Vaishyas:** Peasants/Taxpayers (*Gahapatis*).
4. **Shudras:** Labourers/Artisans.
* **Disabilities:** Denied the sacred thread (*upanayana*), imposed with severe penal laws, and relegated to serving the twice-born.



* **Untouchability:** Crafts like Chandalas were termed *hina* (despicable).
* **Law:** Criminal law was based on "an eye for an eye," but severity depended on caste.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Constitutional Reversal:** The social disabilities described here (untouchability, caste discrimination) became deeply entrenched over centuries. However, the **Constitution of India (1950)** explicitly reversed this ancient legal structure. **Article 17** abolished Untouchability, and **Article 15** prohibits discrimination on grounds of caste, directly challenging the *Dharmasutra* laws mentioned in this chapter.

### Comparison: First vs. Second Urbanization in India

This table highlights how the "Second Urbanization" was not just a revival of cities, but a completely distinct phase driven by different technologies (Iron) and located in a different geography (Ganges Valley).

| Feature | First Urbanization (Harappan Civilization) | Second Urbanization (Gangetic/Age of Buddha) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Timeline** | c. 2600 BC – 1900 BC | c. 600 BC onwards (Peak approx. 3rd C. BC) |
| **Primary Metal** | **Bronze & Copper** (Chalcolithic/Bronze Age) | **Iron** (Iron Age) |
| **Geography** | **Indus River Valley** (North-West India & Pakistan) | **Middle Gangetic Plains** (Eastern UP & Bihar) |
| **Key Technology** | Bronze tools, burnt brick technology. | Iron ploughshare (for deep ploughing), Paddy transplantation. |
| **Pottery** | Red Ware with black designs (Black-on-Red Ware). | **Northern Black Polished Ware (NBPW)** (Glossy, deluxe pottery). |
| **Currency** | Barter system; possibly seals used for trade identity. | **Punch-marked coins** (Metal money introduced). |
| **Writing** | Harappan Script (Pictographic, **undeciphered**). | Brahmi Script (evolved later, **deciphered**). |
| **Construction** | Extensive use of **burnt bricks**; advanced drainage. | Initial use of **wood & mud**; burnt bricks became common only in the later NBPW phase (3rd C. BC). |
| **Political Unit** | Likely centralized; uniformity suggests strong central authority. | **Mahajanapadas**; split between **Monarchies** (e.g., Magadha) and **Republics** (e.g., Lichchhavis). |
| **Major Cities** | Harappa, Mohenjo-Daro, Lothal, Dholavira. | Pataliputra, Rajgir, Vaishali, Shravasti, Varanasi. |

### Key Takeaway

The **Iron Plough** was the "game changer" for the Second Urbanization. While the Harappans relied on the softer alluvial soil of the Indus which could be tilled with wood or copper, the thick forests and hard soil of the Gangetic plains required iron technology to be cleared and cultivated, leading to the massive agricultural surplus that supported cities like Pataliputra.
