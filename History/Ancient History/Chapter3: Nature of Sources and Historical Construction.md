## **Nature of Sources and Historical Construction**

History is reconstructed from two broad types of sources: **Material Remains** (archaeology) and **Written Sources** (literature, inscriptions, etc.).

### **1. Material Remains (Archaeology)**

Archaeology helps recover material remains from ancient times, crucial for studying prehistory (periods with no written records).

* **Prehistoric vs. Historical Sites:**
* **Prehistoric:** No written records. Found on hill slopes, plateaus, and river banks. Contain fossils (human, plant, animal) and stone tools.
* **Proto-historic:** Cultures that knew writing but it hasn't been deciphered (e.g., Harappan culture, Chalcolithic age).
* **Historical:** Decipherable writing exists (e.g., from **3rd Century BC** with Ashokan inscriptions).



**Excavation Methods:**
Remains lie buried in **mounds** (elevated land covering old habitations).

* **Vertical Excavation:** Lengthwise digging to reveal the chronological sequence of cultures. Most common method.
* **Horizontal Excavation:** Digging the whole mound to get a complete picture of a specific period. Expensive and rare.

**Preservation:**

* **Arid climates:** (Western UP, Rajasthan) preserve antiquities better.
* **Humid climates:** (Gangetic plains, Deltaic regions) cause corrosion, even of iron. Only burnt brick or stone structures survive well here.

**Key Findings:**

* Villages established around **6000 BC** in Baluchistan.
* Material culture of Gangetic plains in the **2nd millennium BC**.
* **Megaliths:** Stone-encircled graves in South India (Deccan) from the Iron Age, revealing tools, weapons, and pottery.

**Scientific Dating Methods:**

* **Radiocarbon Dating (C14):** Measures the decay of radioactive Carbon-14 in organic objects.
* **Half-life of C14:** **5568 years**.
* Can date objects up to **70,000 years** old.


* **Pollen Analysis:** Examines plant residues to reconstruct climate and vegetation history (e.g., agriculture in Rajasthan/Kashmir c. **7000–6000 BC**).
* **Metal Analysis:** Determines the source mines and technological stages.

### **2. Coins (Numismatics)**

Ancient currency was metal (copper, silver, gold, lead), not paper.

* **Hoards:** Coins were often stored in earthenware or brass vessels. Large hoards (including Roman coins) have been found.
* **Information Derived:**
* **Dynastic History:** Names and dates of kings (e.g., **Indo-Greeks** who ruled in 2nd–1st C. BC).
* **Economic History:**
* **Trade:** Guild-issued coins indicate thriving commerce.
* **Prosperity:** Largest number of coins (including gold) from **post-Maurya** and **Gupta** periods suggests flourishing trade.
* **Decline:** Scarcity of post-Gupta coins indicates a decline in trade.


* **Art & Religion:** Depictions of gods and religious symbols.
* **Cowries:** Used as low-value currency, especially in post-Gupta times.



---

### **3. Inscriptions (Epigraphy)**

Inscriptions are carved on stone, copper plates, seals, temple walls, etc. Their study is **Epigraphy**, and the study of old writing is **Palaeography**.

* **Earliest Inscriptions:**
* **Harappan (c. 2500 BC):** Undeciphered pictographic script.
* **Ashokan (3rd C. BC):** Earliest deciphered Indian inscriptions. Written in **Brahmi** (left to right) and **Kharoshthi** (right to left). Deciphered by **James Prinsep** in **1837**.


* **Language Evolution:**
* **3rd C. BC:** Prakrit.
* **2nd C. AD:** Sanskrit begins to appear.
* **4th–5th C. AD:** Sanskrit becomes widespread.
* **9th–10th C. AD:** Regional languages appear.



**Types of Inscriptions:**

1. **Royal Orders:** Administrative decisions (e.g., Ashoka's edicts).
2. **Votive Records:** Marks of devotion by followers of Buddhism, Jainism, etc.
3. **Eulogies (Prashastis):** Praising kings (e.g., **Allahabad Pillar** of Samudragupta).
4. **Donative Records:** Gifts of money, cattle, or land.
5. **Land Grants:** Copper plates recording land/revenue grants. Crucial for understanding the land system and administration.

### **4. Literary Sources**

Manuscripts were written on birch bark, palm leaves, sheep leather, and wooden tablets.

**Religious Texts:**

* **Vedas (c. 1500–500 BC):** *Rig Veda* (prayers), Later Vedas (rituals/magic), *Upanishads* (philosophy).
* **Vedangas:** Supplements to Vedas (phonetics, grammar, astronomy, etc.). **Panini’s Grammar (*Ashtadhyayi*, c. 450 BC)** provides rich social/cultural data.
* **Epics:**
* ***Mahabharata*** (Vyasa): Older epic. Evolved from 8,800 to 100,000 verses. Reflects 10th C. BC – 4th C. AD.
* ***Ramayana*** (Valmiki): Evolved from 6,000 to 24,000 verses. Composition started 5th C. BC.


* **Puranas:** Encyclopaedic texts providing dynastic history up to the Guptas.
* **Sutras (c. 600–300 BC):** *Shrautasutras* (public sacrifices), *Grihyasutras* (domestic rituals), *Sulvasutras* (geometry/altar measurement).

**Buddhist & Jaina Texts:**

* **Buddhist (Pali):** Compiled in **1st C. BC** (Sri Lanka). **Jatakas** (birth stories of Buddha) illuminate social/economic conditions (5th–2nd C. BC).
* **Jaina (Prakrit):** Compiled in **6th C. AD** (Valabhi). Refer to trade and political history of Bihar/Eastern UP.

**Secular Literature:**

* **Dharmashastras:** Law books (*Dharmasutras* and *Smritis*). Prescribe duties, laws of marriage, property, and punishment. **Manusmriti** is a key text.
* ***Arthashastra*** (Kautilya): Important for polity and economy (Maurya period).
* **Creative Literature:** Works of **Kalidasa** (*Abhijnanashakuntalam*), Bhasa, etc., mirror the Gupta period.
* **Sangam Literature (Tamil):** Earliest Tamil texts (c. **AD 300–600**).
* **Corpus:** 30,000 lines, 8 anthologies (*Ettuttokai*).
* **Nature:** Secular, heroic poetry praising chiefs/kings.
* **Historical Value:** Corroborates trade with **Yavanas** (Greeks/Romans) and existence of settlements like **Kaveripattanam**.



---

### **5. Foreign Accounts**

Accounts by Greek, Roman, and Chinese visitors supplement indigenous sources.

* **Greek/Roman:**
* **Megasthenes:** *Indika* (Maurya administration/society).
* ***Periplus of the Erythrean Sea*** (AD 80–115): Roman trade in the Indian Ocean.
* **Ptolemy:** *Geography* (c. AD 150).
* **Pliny:** *Naturalis Historia* (1st C. AD).


* **Chinese:**
* **Fa-hsien (5th C. AD):** Gupta age.
* **Hsuan Tsang (7th C. AD):** Age of Harsha.



---

### **6. Historical Construction**

**Historical Sense:**
Ancient Indians are often accused of lacking a historical sense compared to Greeks.

* **Puranas:** Provide dynastic lists but mix myth with history.
* **Inscriptions:** Ashoka and Kharavela recorded events with dates, showing historical awareness.
* **Biographies:** *Harshacharita* (Banabhatta) and ***Rajatarangini*** (Kalhana, 12th C. AD) show a developed historical tradition. *Rajatarangini* is considered the first true history book of India.

**Modern Approach:**

* **Integrating Sources:** Constructing history requires combining texts, coins, inscriptions, and archaeology.
* **Critical Analysis:** Texts (e.g., Epics, Puranas) must be critically examined against archaeological evidence.
* *Example:* Tradition dates Rama to 2000 BC, but Ayodhya excavations show no settlement then.
* *Example:* *Mahabharata* and *Ramayana* contain layers from different periods and cannot be used for a single "Epic Age."


* **Priority:** **Archaeology, Inscriptions, and Coins** are generally more reliable for dating than literary traditions/myths.
