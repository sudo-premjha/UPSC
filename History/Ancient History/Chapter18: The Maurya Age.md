# The Maurya Age

## 1. Chandragupta Maurya: Founder of the Dynasty

Chandragupta Maurya founded the dynasty, likely hailing from the ruling clan of the little republic of **Pipphalivana** (near Gorakhpur), despite brahmanical traditions calling him a shudra born of the Nanda court.

* **Rise to Power:** With the help of **Chanakya (Kautilya)**, he overthrew the Nandas. This political intrigue is famously dramatized in the play *Mudrarakshasa* (9th Century AD).
* **Conflict with Greeks:** Chandragupta liberated north-western India from **Seleucus** (Alexander’s general).
* **Result:** A peace treaty was signed. In exchange for 500 elephants, Seleucus gave Chandragupta eastern Afghanistan, Baluchistan, and the area west of the Indus.


* **Extent of Empire:** The empire covered virtually the entire subcontinent (except Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and parts of the North-East), plus areas of Afghanistan that were not even part of the British Indian empire.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Geopolitical Reach:** The text notes that the Mauryas ruled areas like Kandahar and Herat (modern Afghanistan). Today, these regions are distinct sovereign nations, but in the Mauryan age, they were politically integrated with Pataliputra (Patna), showing a level of trans-regional unification rarely seen since.

## 2. Imperial Organization

We know about the Mauryan administration primarily from two sources:

1. **Indika:** Accounts by **Megasthenes**, the Greek ambassador sent by Seleucus to Chandragupta's court.
2. **Arthashastra:** A treatise on statecraft by **Kautilya**.

### **The Capital: Pataliputra (Patna)**

Megasthenes describes Pataliputra (calling it *Palibothra*) as a massive city:

* **Defenses:** Bounded by a deep ditch and a wooden wall with 570 towers and 64 gates. (Archaeological finds of timber palisades in Patna support this).
* **Dimensions:** 9.33 miles long and 1.75 miles broad.
* **Administration:** The city was run by **6 committees** of 5 members each (30 members total), handling sanitation, foreigners, birth/death registration, weights/measures, etc.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Patna's Geography:** The text notes that Megasthenes' dimensions tally with modern Patna, which is still "all length with little breadth" due to its location along the banks of the Ganga.

### **Military and Central Administration**

* **The King:** An autocrat who concentrated all power but claimed his happiness lay in the happiness of his subjects.
* **The Army:** A massive force managed by a board of 30 officers (6 committees).
* **Size:** 600,000 foot soldiers, 30,000 cavalry, 9,000 elephants, and 8,000 chariots (according to Pliny).
* **Navy:** They also maintained a navy.


* **Economy & Taxes:** The state controlled almost all economic activities (mining, liquor, arms). Taxes were **1/4 to 1/6** of the produce.

## 3. Ashoka (273-232 BC)

Chandragupta was succeeded by Bindusara, and then by his son **Ashoka**, considered the greatest Mauryan ruler.

### **Ashokan Inscriptions**

Ashoka is the first Indian king to speak directly to the people through inscriptions.

* **Names Used:** Most inscriptions refer to him as **Devanampiya** (Dear to Gods) and **Piyadasi** (Good looking). The name "Ashoka" appears only four times (in Minor Rock Edicts in Karnataka/MP).
* **Scripts and Languages:**
* **India:** Prakrit language in **Brahmi** script.
* **North-West:** Aramaic language and **Kharoshthi** script.
* **Afghanistan:** Aramaic and Greek scripts.



### **The Kalinga War & Transformation**

Ashoka fought only one major war—the conquest of **Kalinga** (modern Odisha).

* **The Toll:** 100,000 killed, 150,000 taken prisoner.
* **The Change:** Deeply moved by the slaughter, Ashoka abandoned *Bherighosha* (physical conquest/sound of war drums) for ***Dhammaghosha*** (cultural conquest).
* **The Policy:** He did not disband the army or return Kalinga, but he stopped waging new wars, focusing instead on internal consolidation and welfare.

## 4. Ashoka's Dhamma (Dharma)

Ashoka converted to Buddhism but his "Dhamma" was not a narrow sectarian faith; it was a code of conduct for social order.

* **Core Teachings:**
* Obedience to parents and respect for teachers/Brahmanas/monks.
* Compassion for animals and slaves.
* Loyalty to the king (*Dridha Bhakti*).
* Tolerance between different sects.


* **Administration of Dhamma:**
* Appointed **Dhammamahamatras** to propagate dharma among all social groups (including women).
* **Rajukas** were officers vested with authority to reward and punish.


* **Social Reforms:** He banned animal slaughter in the royal kitchen, forbade certain sacrifices, and discouraged "gay social functions" involving excess revelry.

> **Present Day Connection:**
> **Secularism and Ethics:** Ashoka's *Dhamma* resembles a modern secular code of ethics more than a religion. His instruction for "amity between sects" is an early precursor to the modern Indian constitutional value of secularism and religious tolerance.

---

## 5. Ashoka's Place in History

Ashoka is celebrated not just as a king, but as a missionary ruler.

1. **Political Unification:** He bound the country with one dharma, one language (Prakrit), and one script (Brahmi).
2. **Cultural Conquest:** He sent missionaries to Sri Lanka, Central Asia, and West Asia (Greek kingdoms).
3. **Pacific Policy:** He is compared to the Egyptian king **Akhnaton** (14th Century BC) for his pacifism, though Ashoka was unaware of him. Unlike Akhnaton, Ashoka's pacifism was backed by a powerful standing army that maintained the empire during his lifetime.
4. **Legacy:** While his policy brought internal peace, the empire began to disintegrate after his retirement in 232 BC.
